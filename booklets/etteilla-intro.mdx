# Reading the Etteilla — An Introduction

*The shared companion for the three Etteilla decks in the Recursive Tarot
library: Etteilla I (Livre de Thot lineage), Etteilla II (the "Egyptian"
pattern), and Etteilla III (Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames).*

## The first deck designed for divination

The Etteilla holds a genuine historical distinction: it is, as far as the
record shows, the **first tarot designed for divination rather than play**.
Jean-Baptiste Alliette — "Etteilla" is his name reversed — was a Paris print
seller who began publishing on cartomancy in 1770 and, in 1788–1789, issued his
own reformed deck. Where the Marseille was a game pack that occultists later
*reinterpreted*, the Etteilla was *built* for reading from the start.

That makes these decks honest in a way collectors sometimes miss: using an
Etteilla for divination is not a projection onto the cards — it is the cards'
original job description.

## What's printed on the cards

Etteilla solved the beginner's problem typographically. Most cards carry:

- a **number** (Etteilla renumbered the sequence 1–78, breaking with the
  Marseille trump order),
- an **upright keyword** at the top, and
- a **reversed keyword** at the bottom.

You read the keyword that is upright *from your point of view* when the card is
laid. That is the entire basic method: the deck is its own little book. The
booklet you are holding adds context, not secret meanings.

## A note on "Egyptian wisdom"

Etteilla embraced Court de Gébelin's 1781 claim that tarot encoded the Book of
Thoth — ancient Egyptian teaching. **The historical record does not support
this**: tarot is 15th-century Italian, and nothing Egyptian is in its
documented ancestry. We keep Etteilla's Egyptian framing visible on the cards
because it is part of *his* system's history — the founding myth of French
cartomancy — while labelling it as myth. (The library's research files trace
what the evidence actually shows.)

## The three decks, briefly

- **Etteilla I** — the pattern closest to Alliette's own 1788–89 design,
  carried on by his students after his death in 1791.
- **Etteilla II** — a 19th-century revision with stronger "Egyptian" imagery,
  produced as the system passed to successor publishers.
- **Etteilla III (Oracle des Dames)** — the late-Victorian mass-market form,
  lithographed c. 1865, pitched to the drawing-room public. Furthest from
  Alliette's hand, and the version most surviving antique decks belong to.

Reading method is essentially the same across all three; imagery and numbering
details differ.

## A simple way to begin

1. Shuffle, letting cards turn freely (reversals matter here).
2. Draw three: **situation — obstacle — direction**.
3. Read each card's upright-facing keyword, in order, as a plain sentence.
4. Only then look longer at the images and ask what they add.

Etteilla's own method built up to large spreads (his *grand jeu* uses dozens of
cards), but the keyword-sentence is the system's atom, and it was designed so a
newcomer could start the same evening the deck was bought.

## Reading responsibly

These are historical instruments of reflection. The library presents them as
primary sources from the tradition that invented French cartomancy — not as
prediction machines. Whatever your relationship to divination, the honest frame
is Etteilla's own market: a structured way to think out loud about a question.
