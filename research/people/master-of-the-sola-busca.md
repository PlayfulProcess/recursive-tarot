# Master of the Sola Busca (anonymous engraver, c. 1491)

## At a glance

The **Master of the Sola Busca** is the conventional name for the anonymous
**North Italian engraver** who produced the **Sola Busca tarot** (c. 1491) —
the **first known fully pictorial 78-card tarot deck**, meaning every card including
the pip minors carries a narrative scene rather than geometric suit symbols.
Several cards bear the monogram **"MS"** which is presumed to indicate the
engraver's initials, but no historical individual has been securely identified
[@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_ciompi].

## Life & context

The stylistic evidence points to a **North Italian engraver trained in the Venetian or
Ferrarese tradition** of the 1480s–1490s: confident cross-hatching, classicising figure
types, and a command of complex multi-figure compositions unusual for playing cards.
The iconographic programme — Roman military figures named on the trumps, allegorical
figures drawn from ancient history, mythology and romance for the courts — suggests the
engraver worked to a **humanist programme** (possibly a written brief from the patron),
rather than composing the imagery independently.

**Proposed attributions and commissioner hypotheses** (all speculative):
- The "MS" monogram has been tentatively linked to a **Milanese or Venetian engraver**
  active in the 1490s, but no match has been convincingly demonstrated
- The commissioner has been hypothesised as a **Venetian noble family**: the Orsini,
  the Cornaro, the Soranzo, or the Sola Busca family itself (who owned the deck by the
  19th century) [@web_sb_ciompi; @web_sb_wp]
- The Ferrarese court has also been proposed given the deck's overlap with Este humanist
  culture and its Roman-military iconographic register
- The date 1491 is inferred from a **hand-coloured exemplar** at the Pinacoteca di Brera,
  Milan, which includes an inscription datable to that year [@web_sb_wp]

## What they made / did

- **The Sola Busca tarot** (c. 1491, 78 copperplate engravings): 22 trumps (each named —
  MATO, PANFILIO, POSTULO…, ALLEXANDRO M., etc.), and 56 fully pictorial minors across
  four suits (cups, coins, batons, swords). No other tarot from this period depicts a
  narrative scene on every pip card — this innovation did not return to the mainstream
  until **Pamela Colman Smith's 1909 RWS deck**, which Smith almost certainly saw
  (the Sola Busca was exhibited in London in 1907 at the British Museum) [@web_sb_wp;
  @web_pcs_wp].
- The major arcana trumps carry **Roman, biblical and legendary named figures** — a
  humanist programme unique in tarot; there is no Popess or Hanged Man, only personal
  names from antiquity.
- The minor arcana depict **allegorical figures, processions, struggles, and still-lifes**
  of suit objects — some compositional motifs (notably the Three of Swords piercing a
  heart) appear to have directly influenced Smith's RWS compositions.

## The claim vs. the record

- **The "MS" monogram**: unresolved. No engraver with those initials active in Northern
  Italy c. 1491 has been definitively identified in archival records [@web_sb_ciompi].
- **Pamela Colman Smith connection**: strongly supported by circumstantial evidence —
  the British Museum exhibited the Sola Busca's hand-coloured photographs in 1907, two
  years before the RWS was published, and several RWS pip compositions show close
  parallels to Sola Busca cards [@web_pcs_wp; @web_sb_wp]. This is now the
  mainstream scholarly view but was not widely known until the 2000s–2010s.
- **The commissioner**: unknown. The "Sola Busca" name comes from the aristocratic
  Milanese family who owned the deck in the 19th century; they may not have commissioned it.
