# Antoine Court de Gébelin

## At a glance

**Antoine Court de Gébelin** (c. 1725 – 1784) is the hinge of the entire "occult turn." In
**1781**, in volume 8 of his vast encyclopedia *Le Monde primitif*, the Protestant pastor and
Freemason published an essay declaring the Tarot de Marseille a survival of the ancient
Egyptian **Book of Thoth**. He offered **no evidence**, and was **wrong** — but he converted a
card game into a "sacred text" and launched two centuries of esoteric tarot. Bundled with his
essay was a second piece by the **Comte de Mellet**, which first proposed actually *reading*
the cards and tying them to the Hebrew alphabet [@decker1996; @web_wopc_monde].

## Life & context

Court de Gébelin was born c. 1725 (sources vary; commonly given as 1719 or 1725), the son of
**Antoine Court** (1696–1760), the Huguenot minister celebrated as the "Restorer of
Protestantism in France." The son trained and served as a **Protestant (Huguenot) pastor**,
then retired from active ministry to literary and antiquarian pursuits in Paris, where he was
**initiated as a Freemason (1771)** and moved in the lodge *Les Neuf Sœurs* — the Enlightenment
circle that also drew Benjamin Franklin, a subscriber to his encyclopedia. He died in Paris in
1784 [@web_gebelin_wp; @decker1996].

His life's work was *Le Monde primitif, analysé et comparé avec le monde moderne* — a
nine-volume comparative project (published c. 1773–1784) arguing that a single primeval
civilisation underlay all languages, myths and symbols. The tarot essay is one small chapter of
volume 8 (1781), where the cards become, for him, one more relic of that lost golden age
[@web_gebelin_wp; @decker1996].

## What they made / did

- **The tarot essay in *Le Monde primitif*, vol. 8 (1781).** Gébelin's *"Du Jeu des Tarots"*
  asserts that the trumps encode Egyptian religious doctrine and that the very name derives from
  (invented) Egyptian roots (*Tar* = "way/road," *Ro/Ros* = "royal" → the "royal road of life").
  The story he tells is that he encountered the game being played in a Paris salon (associated
  with **Mme Helvétius / Mme la C. d'H.**) and instantly "recognised" Egyptian allegory in the
  cards [@decker1996; @web_wopc_monde].
- **`court-de-gebelin-tarot` — his redrawn plates.** Accompanying the essay were engraved
  plates that "corrected" the Marseille images toward what he *imagined* the Egyptian originals
  looked like (e.g. re-Egyptianised figures). These are **interpretive redraws**, not a
  historical deck, and should be flagged as such [@decker1996; @web_wopc_monde].
- **He hosted, not authored, Mellet's essay.** The second tarot piece in the same volume —
  *"Recherches sur les Tarots"* by the **Comte de Mellet** — is a separate author's work; the
  two essays differ (see *Connections* and the comte-de-mellet dossier) [@decker1996;
  @web_place_mellet].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Myth: tarot is the ancient Egyptian "Book of Thoth."** **The record: FALSE.** Tarot is a
  15th-century North-Italian invention (the *trionfi* / tarocchi card game, first documented in
  the courts of Milan and Ferrara). Gébelin wrote in 1781, **four decades before Champollion
  deciphered hieroglyphs** (Lettre à M. Dacier, 1822); his "Egyptian" reading was pure
  imagination projected onto Italian Christian imagery. The verdict of the document-first
  scholarship is unequivocal: the Egyptian genealogy is **invented, not recovered**
  [@dummett1980; @decker1996].
- **Myth: he recovered "suppressed" esoteric knowledge.** **The record:** he announced his
  theory *openly*, under his own name, in a prestigious subscription encyclopedia read by the
  Parisian elite. The occult overlay was born not in secrecy but as a **high-status
  Enlightenment fad** — fashionable erudition about an imagined Egypt, not a transmitted
  tradition [@decker1996].
- **Important caveat.** Debunking Gébelin removes a false *pedigree*; it does **not** debunk the
  real, later history of divinatory practice (Etteilla onward) or its persecuted practitioners.
  Separate the false antiquity from the genuine 18th-century practice [@decker1996].

## Connections

- **Gébelin + `comte-de-mellet` → `etteilla`.** The two 1781 essays are the seedbed: Gébelin
  supplies the Egyptian myth, Mellet supplies the reading-method and Hebrew-letter idea, and
  **Etteilla** (within a few years) turns both into a marketed deck and school [@decker1996].
- **Gébelin → `eliphas-levi` → `oswald-wirth`.** The Egyptian framing and the impulse to read
  tarot symbolically pass down the 19th-century French esoteric line — Lévi reorganises it
  around the Hebrew alphabet (1850s), and Wirth/de Guaita illustrate it (1889) [@decker1996;
  @decker2002].
- **Distinguish Gébelin from Mellet.** Their trump-to-Hebrew assignments and orderings are
  *not* identical; do not conflate the two essays even though they share a volume [@decker1996;
  @web_place_mellet].

## How they appear in this collection

Gébelin is the maker of the deck slug **`court-de-gebelin-tarot`** (the 1781 redrawn plates),
which the grammar should treat strictly as **origin-myth**, dated 1781, and **never** as
evidence about the cards' real 15th-century origin. In a "people of tarot" set he is the figure
on whom the **myth-vs-record** split is most load-bearing: state the Egyptian claim, then the
Dummett-spine correction, on his card and on any card descended from his plates (esp. those he
re-Egyptianised). He pairs naturally with the **comte-de-mellet** card as the two halves of the
1781 turn.
