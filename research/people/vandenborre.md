# F. I. Vandenborre

## At a glance

**F. I. Vandenborre** was a **Brussels** card-manufacturer whose name attaches to the
standard **Belgian / Flemish tarot** (*Tarot Flamand*), conventionally dated **c. 1780**
(sources give 1770 or 1780; the firm was active **c. 1762–1803**) [@web_wopc_vandenborre;
@web_belgian_wp]. The deck is famous for its **anticlerical substitutions**: card II, the
**Popess (La Papesse)**, is replaced by **the Spanish Captain** (*Le Spagnol Capitano
Eracasse* / Captain Fracasse), and card V, the **Pope (Le Pape)**, by **Bacchus** (*Bacus*),
the god of wine astride a barrel [@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_th_spanish_captain]. Like the
Marseille deck it has illustrated trumps and **pip** minor cards [@web_wopc_vandenborre].
Vandenborre is a **firm/imprint** more than a richly documented individual; treat as a
maker-node (confidence: medium).

## Life & context

The Vandenborre tarot is a Brussels product of the late 18th century, part of the **Belgian /
Rouen-Brussels** card tradition rather than the French Marseille line [@web_belgian_wp].
Vandenborre **took over this type of card by c. 1780**, after which the **Flemish/Belgian
tarot became a standard Belgian design** through the 18th century; the cards were also marketed
as *"Cartes de Suisses"* [@web_wopc_vandenborre]. The pattern itself is older than the firm —
it descends from the **Rouen-Brussels** tradition whose French ancestor is **Jacques Viéville**
(c. 1650) and whose earliest dated maker is **Gérard Bodet of Liège, 1693** — so Vandenborre
**standardised and published**, rather than invented, the type [@web_belgian_wp;
@web_th_rouen_bruxelles].

## What they made / did

- **`belgian-tarot`** — the **Tarot Flamand**, 78 cards (22 trumps + 56 pips), issued under
  the Vandenborre imprint c. 1780, woodcut and stencil [@web_wopc_vandenborre]. Its defining
  features are the **Bacchus-for-Pope** and **Spanish-Captain-for-Popess** substitutions
  [@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_th_spanish_captain].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Claim: Vandenborre "created" the Belgian tarot.** **The record:** he **published and
  standardised** an existing **Rouen-Brussels** pattern; the type predates the firm (Viéville
  c. 1650; Bodet 1693) [@web_belgian_wp; @web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- **The substitutions are pattern features, not Vandenborre inventions.** The
  **Bacchus/Spanish-Captain** replacements belong to the Belgian tradition this deck carries,
  not uniquely to Vandenborre's hand; they are usually read as a **secular/anticlerical**
  softening of the two ecclesiastical trumps (the Popess being theologically awkward, the Pope
  politically so), echoing the Besançon line's Jupiter/Juno swap for the same two cards
  [@web_th_spanish_captain; @web_belgian_wp]. (confidence: medium on the *motive* — the swap is
  documented; the precise reason is interpretive.)

## Connections

- **Ancestral French maker:** `jacques-viéville` / `jacques-vieville` — the c. 1650 Paris
  ancestor of the Rouen-Brussels tradition Vandenborre's deck belongs to [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- **The Marseille-line contrast:** `nicolas-conver`, `jean-noblet` — the **Marseille** makers,
  against whom Vandenborre's **Belgian** line is the principal Northern divergence (Pope/Popess
  replaced rather than retained) [@web_belgian_wp].
- **Document-historian:** `michael-dummett`, who classes the Belgian/Brussels tarots with the
  non-Marseille (Bologna → Piedmont/Savoy) tradition [@dummett1980].

## How they appear in this collection

Vandenborre is the maker of **`belgian-tarot`**. The two cards to feature are exactly the
substitutions: **II — the Spanish Captain** (replacing the Popess) and **V — Bacchus**
(replacing the Pope) — the clearest single illustration in the collection of how a regional
pattern could **edit the ecclesiastical trumps out**. Link the deck back to `jacques-vieville`
(its tradition's ancestor) and contrast it with the Marseille makers.
