# Louis-Raphaël-Lucrèce de Fayolle, comte de Mellet

## At a glance

The **Comte de Mellet** — full name **Louis-Raphaël-Lucrèce de Fayolle** (1727–1804) — wrote
the *second*, often-overlooked tarot essay bundled into Court de Gébelin's *Le Monde primitif*,
vol. 8 (1781). While Gébelin supplied the Egyptian *origin myth*, it was **Mellet** who first
in print (a) explicitly paired the **22 trumps-plus-Fool with the 22 letters of the Hebrew
alphabet**, (b) read the trumps in an **inverted order** (from the World down to the Fool) as
the Three Ages of the World, and (c) described a concrete **divination method** — arguably the
oldest published tarot *spread*. He is the quiet hinge between Gébelin's theory and Etteilla's
practice [@decker1996; @web_place_mellet; @web_hrmtc_first].

## Life & context

Mellet was born in **1727 in Périgueux** (Nouvelle-Aquitaine). He had a substantial military
and court career: musketeer; commander of the bodyguard corps of the **King of Poland**;
**Knight of the Order of Saint-Louis**; officer of the French king's bodyguard; **governor of
the provinces of Maine and Perche**; lieutenant-general of the king's armies; and *maréchal de
camp*. He died in 1804. He was an **amateur** student of antiquity and symbolism, not a
professional scholar like Gébelin — which is part of why his contribution was long
under-credited [@web_mellet_search; @web_place_mellet].

(Confidence note: the identification of the anonymous "M. le C. de M." with Louis-Raphaël-
Lucrèce de Fayolle, comte de Mellet, and the dates 1727–1804, rests on convergent secondary
scholarship rather than a primary archival source seen here — but it is consistent across
Decker–Depaulis–Dummett-derived accounts and Robert M. Place's research.)

## What they made / did

- **The essay *"Recherches sur les Tarots, et sur la Divination par les Cartes des Tarots"*
  (in *Le Monde primitif*, vol. 8, 1781), signed only "par M. le C. de M."** Its specific,
  durable contributions:
  - **Hebrew-alphabet correspondence.** Mellet is "responsible for the mystical connection of
    the Tarot's 21 trumps and the Fool with the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet" — the idea
    that Éliphas Lévi would later systematise and that became the spine of esoteric tarot
    [@web_place_mellet; @decker1996].
  - **Inverted trump order / Three Ages.** He read the trumps **backwards**, from **the World
    down to the Fool**, mapping them onto the Classical **Ages of Gold, Silver, and Iron**
    [@web_hrmtc_first; @web_place_mellet].
  - **A divination method.** His essay carries "the earliest discussion of a divination
    technique for the Tarot" — often cited as the **oldest recorded tarot spread** in print
    [@web_hrmtc_first; @web_place_mellet].
  - Detailed treatment of the **minor suits** as well as the trumps [@web_hrmtc_first].
- **No deck.** Mellet designed no cards; his contribution is textual. (Modern reconstructions —
  e.g. Robert M. Place's *The First Occult Tarot* — illustrate *his* scheme, but those are
  21st-century objects, not Mellet's own [@web_hrmtc_first].)

## The claim vs. the record

- **Myth: the Hebrew-letter correspondence is an ancient transmission.** **The record:** it is
  an **18th-century construction**. Mellet *proposed* the 22-trumps ↔ 22-Hebrew-letters pairing
  in 1781 as an interpretive overlay on a 15th-century Italian card game; there is no medieval
  Kabbalistic tarot behind it. The idea is influential and internally elegant, but it is
  **invented in print by Mellet (and Gébelin's circle)**, not recovered [@decker1996;
  @dummett1980].
- **Misattribution of the "reading-the-cards" idea to Gébelin.** Popular accounts credit Court
  de Gébelin with founding tarot divination. The record: the *method* and the *Hebrew/divination*
  material are largely **Mellet's**, appended to Gébelin's origin essay; the two should not be
  merged [@web_place_mellet; @decker1996].
- **Identity once disputed.** "M. le C. de M." was for a time mysterious; the consensus
  identification with the comte de Mellet (de Fayolle) is now standard but still worth treating
  as well-supported rather than incontrovertible (confidence: medium-high on the identity)
  [@web_place_mellet; @web_mellet_search].

## Connections

- **`court-de-gebelin` (host/collaborator).** Mellet's essay shares volume 8 (1781) with
  Gébelin's; Gébelin reportedly had Mellet's text in hand when writing his own and was
  influenced by it. They are the **two halves of the 1781 turn** — myth (Gébelin) + method
  (Mellet) [@decker1996; @web_place_mellet].
- **Mellet → `etteilla` (successor in practice).** Etteilla, within a few years, turned
  Mellet's reading-method and Gébelin's Egyptian framing into a marketed deck, dictionary, and
  school [@decker1996].
- **Mellet → `eliphas-levi` → `oswald-wirth`.** Mellet's trump-to-Hebrew pairing is the germ
  that **Éliphas Lévi** rebuilt into a full Kabbalistic system in the 1850s and that **Oswald
  Wirth** then printed onto cards (1889) — though each later author *changed* the specific
  assignments, so the systems must not be conflated [@decker1996; @decker2002].

## How they appear in this collection

Mellet makes no deck, so he carries no deck slug; in the grammar he is a **person/idea node**,
paired with **`court-de-gebelin`** on the 1781 origin of the occult reading. He should feature
wherever the collection narrates the **Hebrew-letter correspondence** and the **first tarot
spread** — i.e. as the under-credited author behind a system usually attributed to Gébelin or
Lévi. A "people of tarot" card for Mellet should foreground "the man who first paired the 22
trumps with the 22 Hebrew letters, in 1781."
