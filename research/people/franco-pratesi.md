# Franco Pratesi (b. 1939)

## At a glance

**Franco Pratesi** is a retired Florentine physicist who became one of the most
important **archival researchers in tarot history** — not through synthesis or theory,
but through direct excavation of **Italian Renaissance account books, guild records,
and inventories**. His discoveries in Florentine, Ferrarese, and Bolognese archives
have repeatedly pushed back or complicated the accepted chronology of early tarot,
and his work has been essential to the careers of every subsequent tarot historian
[@pratesi_marziano; @pratesi_ferrara; @web_pratesi_site].

## Life & context

Pratesi trained as a physicist (University of Florence), retired early, and devoted
himself to systematic searches through **Renaissance archival materials** — the kind of
slow, specialist work that requires fluency in early Italian script and knowledge of
guild and court record-keeping conventions. He has published primarily in the **Journal
of the International Playing-Card Society (IPCS)** and later on his own website, making
his findings accessible to non-specialists.

His relationship to the tarot history community is that of an **independent primary
researcher**: he does not build interpretive systems or theories but surfaces facts
that others then interpret. Michael Dummett acknowledged Pratesi's contributions
extensively in later editions of his work.

## What they made / did

- **The Marziano da Tortona discovery** (or more precisely, the systematic scholarly
  analysis and dating of the Marziano treatise), establishing c. 1418–1425 as the likely
  date of the earliest documented tarot trump programme [@pratesi_marziano].
- **Florentine documents** (1989 and subsequent): Pratesi found 1440s–1450s Florentine
  merchant account books (the *Ricordanze* of Francesco di Matteo Castellani) recording
  the purchase of *trionfi* (tarot cards), providing some of the earliest dated Florentine
  evidence for tarot alongside the Milanese and Ferrarese records.
- **Bolognese guild records**: excavation of the Bologna cardmakers' guild regulations
  explaining why the **Tarocchino di Bologna** is a 62-card deck — guild statutes
  mandated removing specific cards to comply with local sumptuary law.
- Numerous smaller archival finds scattered across IPCS journal volumes (1981–present),
  collectively constituting the **primary evidence base for 15th-century tarot history**.

## The claim vs. the record

- **Pratesi's contributions are primary research, not interpretation**: he finds documents;
  others interpret them. Where Dummett or other historians make interpretive claims about
  tarot origins, Pratesi's documents are often the evidence base — but the interpretive
  conclusions are not always Pratesi's own.
- **His findings sometimes complicate neat narratives**: Florentine evidence alongside
  Milanese and Ferrarese evidence suggests tarot spread quickly across multiple Italian
  courts in the 1440s–1460s, making any single "origin city" claim overly simple.
