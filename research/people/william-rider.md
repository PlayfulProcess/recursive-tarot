# William Rider & Son (publisher)

## At a glance

**William Rider & Son** was the London publishing house that issued the **Rider-Waite-Smith tarot
deck in 1909** and **A. E. Waite's *Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (1910/1911)** — the "**Rider**" in
the deck's tripartite name. In the early twentieth century it was **the foremost British publisher
of occult, mystical and esoteric literature**, and its commercial release of the Waite–Smith deck
is the moment esoteric tarot crossed from the secret lodge into the open marketplace. The firm
**commissioned neither the artwork nor the symbolic scheme** — those were Smith's and Waite's; its
contribution was publication and distribution [@web_rws_wopc; @web_rider_wp; @web_pkt_wp].

## Life & context

The **Rider** imprint took shape in **1908**, when the firm absorbed the occult publisher **Philip
Wellby**; for some three decades its list was directed by **Ralph Shirley** (a director of the firm
c.1892–1925 and founder-editor of *The Occult Review* from 1905), making Rider the natural home for
authors such as **Éliphas Lévi (in translation), A. E. Waite, Franz Hartmann** — and, famously,
both the **Rider-Waite tarot** and Bram Stoker's *Dracula*. Its London premises included **164
Aldersgate Street** and later **Paternoster Row**. The imprint long outlived its Edwardian heyday
and survives today as **Rider**, now within **Ebury / Penguin Random House** [@web_rider_wp].

## What they made / did

- **Published the Waite–Smith tarot deck (1909).** The first edition was issued by **William Rider &
  Son** from **late 1909** (with the "Roses & Lilies" back), followed by a larger 1910 printing.
  Rider manufactured and sold the cards commercially and openly [@web_rws_wopc].
- **Published the reading book.** *The Key to the Tarot* (1910, bundled with the deck) and the
  illustrated *Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (**1911**), both by Waite with Smith's drawings
  [@web_pkt_wp; @waite1910].
- **Did not originate the deck's content.** Rider was the **publisher**, not the designer or
  artist; the firm holds no authorship of the symbolism (Waite) or the art (Smith). *(Its `made`
  list is therefore empty — it published, it did not make the cards.)* [@web_rws_wopc].

## The claim vs. the record

- **The name problem.** Calling the deck "**Rider**-Waite" foregrounds the *publisher* and the
  *scheme-author* while omitting the **artist, Pamela Colman Smith**, who drew all 78 cards. The
  collection's preferred **Rider-Waite-Smith** restores her; "Rider" should be read as the
  *publisher's* mark, not a creative credit [@web_rws_wopc; @web_pcs_wp].
- **Copyright caution.** Rider's original UK copyrights have lapsed in many jurisdictions (UK public
  domain), but the situation is **uneven internationally** and later editions/reissues complicate it
  — keep the repo's "reference, don't necessarily reproduce" caution [@web_rws_faq].

## Connections

- **`william-rider` published `a-e-waite` + `pamela-colman-smith`** — the 1909 deck and the
  1910/1911 *Pictorial Key* [@web_rws_wopc; @web_pkt_wp].
- **Downstream of `golden-dawn`:** the content Rider printed is the Order's Book-T system made
  public, ultimately resting on **`eliphas-levi`'s** correspondences [@decker2002].
- **House author overlap:** Rider also published Lévi in English translation (via Waite), tightening
  the line from French esotericism to the London occult market [@web_rider_wp].

## How they appear in this collection

Rider is an **institution node**: the publisher that put esoteric tarot on sale. In any RWS-derived
context (including the `golden-dawn-book-t-tarot` grammar's borrowed imagery), Rider should be named
as the **publisher of record** for the 1909 deck and the *Pictorial Key* — explicitly distinct from
the *maker* (Smith) and the *scheme's author* (Waite), and used to anchor the deck's date and
copyright story.
