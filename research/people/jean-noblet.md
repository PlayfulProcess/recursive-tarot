# Jean Noblet

## At a glance

**Jean Noblet** was a Paris cardmaker whose pack — surviving in a **single copy in the
Bibliothèque nationale de France** — is the **oldest known near-complete Tarot de
Marseille**, conventionally classed as **"Type I"** [@web_bnf_noblet; @web_wopc_noblet].
Working *"au faubourg St Germain"* around the **mid-17th century (c. 1650; the BnF dates the
surviving deck 1659, and his activity c. 1659–1681)**, Noblet made the earliest object that
conforms to the graphic type later called *tarot de Marseille* because Marseille came to
specialise in it [@web_bnf_noblet]. He is **barely documented as a person** — known mainly
through his name and address printed on the cards themselves — so this dossier stays short
and the biography is floruit-only (confidence: medium).

## Life & context

Noblet worked in Paris in the **second half of the 17th century**, at a time when the tarot
game was **declining in the French capital** — his is among the *last* Parisian tarots as
well as, by graphic type, the *oldest Marseille* one [@web_bnf_noblet]. His name and address
are signed **on the cards**: *"Iean Noblet Dm.t Av Favbovr St Germain"* on the Two of Coins,
his name and address on the Two of Cups, and his monogram **"I. N."** on the Chariot's crest
[@web_wopc_noblet]. The deck is the **smallest known** Marseille-type pack (about **92 × 55
mm**), with a notably broad colour palette [@web_bnf_noblet]. Beyond the cards, secure
biographical facts (birth, death, workshop history) are not established in the sources
consulted (confidence: low on the person; high that the deck is his and is the oldest).

## What they made / did

- **`noblet-tarot`** — the **c. 1650 Paris tarot**, 78 cards, wood-engraving coloured by
  stencil; the **single surviving copy is in the BnF** [@web_bnf_noblet; @web_wopc_noblet].
  It is **nearly complete**, missing only a short run of Sword pips (the Six through Ten of
  Swords are absent in the surviving set) [@web_wopc_noblet]. As the **oldest surviving
  Marseille-pattern** deck, it is the practical archetype of **Type I** — earlier and in
  details distinct from the later, canonical **Conver Type II** [@web_bnf_noblet;
  @dummett1980].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Claim: "the oldest Tarot de Marseille."** **The record supports this** with the
  qualifier *surviving*: Noblet's is the oldest **extant near-complete** deck of the Marseille
  graphic type, but the **pattern is named for Marseille only later** (18th c.), and Noblet
  himself worked in **Paris** — so "Marseille" here is a type-label, not a place of origin
  [@web_bnf_noblet; @dummett1980].
- **Type I vs Type II.** Noblet anchors the older **Type I** Marseille; the **Conver 1760**
  deck is the canonical **Type II**. The two-type division is a modern descriptive scheme,
  not a maker's category [@dummett1980; @depaulis1984].

## Connections

- **The Marseille-line cardmakers:** `nicolas-conver` (the canonical later Type II) — Noblet
  is the Type-I forerunner of the same line [@dummett1980].
- **Near-contemporary, divergent line:** `jacques-vieville`, another Paris cardmaker working
  c. 1650 in the *same neighbourhood*, but whose pack belongs to a **different (Belgian /
  "Rouen-Brussels") tradition**, not the Marseille line [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- **Document-historian:** `michael-dummett`, who frames the Marseille type and its early
  witnesses [@dummett1980].

## How they appear in this collection

Noblet is the maker of **`noblet-tarot`**, the deck to cite whenever the collection needs
the **oldest surviving Marseille** witness or the **Type I** archetype. In prose, pair him
with `nicolas-conver` (Type I → Type II) and contrast him with `jacques-vieville` (same city
and decade, different pattern). The self-identifying cards — the **Two of Coins / Two of
Cups** (address) and the **Chariot** ("I. N." monogram) — are the ones that physically carry
his signature.
