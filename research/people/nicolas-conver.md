# Nicolas Conver

## At a glance

**Nicolas Conver** is the name signed on the most famous single witness of the **Tarot de
Marseille**: a woodblock-and-stencil pack **dated 1760** and printed in **Marseille**, which
became the canonical **"Type II"** Marseille pattern — the reference layout copied by
French cardmakers for more than a century [@web_camoin_conver1760; @web_vs_conver_heritage;
@dummett1980; @depaulis1984]. His official style was *maître cartier* (master cardmaker) of
Marseille [@web_camoin_conver]. **The catch:** the man documented as "Nicolas Conver,
cartier" in the **BnF authority record lived 1784–1833** — making it physically impossible
for *that* Nicolas Conver to have engraved blocks in 1760 [@web_bnf_conver]. The 1760 date
is therefore either inherited from older blocks, or attaches to an earlier/different Conver;
the standard practice was for cardmakers to buy or inherit pearwood moulds, erase the
previous *cartier*'s name, and substitute their own while **leaving the original date**
[@web_vs_conver_heritage]. So "1760" is a real engraving date; "Nicolas Conver, its author"
is the contested part.

## Life & context

Conver was a **Marseille cardmaker**, and Marseille in the 18th century was *the* city that
specialised in producing this tarot pattern — which is why the pattern bears the city's name
[@dummett1980; @depaulis1984]. The Conver workshop's blocks passed down through the trade:
the firm continued printing from the **same pear-wood templates** through the 19th century,
and the **Conver house eventually became the Camoin firm** through the marriage of
Jean-Baptiste Camoin into a Conver heir [@web_camoin_conver; @web_vs_conver_heritage]. The
**Bibliothèque nationale de France's authority file** records a Nicolas Conver, cartier of
Marseille, with dates **1784–1833** [@web_bnf_conver] — a documented man who cannot be the
1760 engraver, which is the heart of the dating problem (confidence: medium — the
relationship between the dated blocks and the named man is unresolved in the literature).

## What they made / did

- **`tarot-de-marseille-conver`** — the **1760 Marseille tarot** bearing Conver's name: 78
  cards, engraved woodblocks coloured by stencil, the canonical **Marseille "Type II"**
  [@web_camoin_conver1760; @web_vs_conver_heritage]. It is **not** the first Marseille-pattern
  deck — it follows earlier Marseille makers (e.g. **Chosson**, whose deck bears a date in the
  1670s–1730s that is itself debated) — but it became the **standard** witness
  [@dummett1980; @depaulis1984].
- **The reference woodblocks.** The Conver blocks were reused for over a century and became
  the prototype behind most later French Marseille decks, down to **Paul Marteau's** Grimaud
  *Ancien Tarot de Marseille* (c. 1930) [@depaulis1984]. Surviving Conver cards and the
  blocks are held / claimed by public collections and the successor **Camoin** firm; the
  **BnF (Gallica)** holds Conver-pattern tarots [@web_gallica_conver; @web_camoin_conver].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Claim: "Nicolas Conver engraved the gold-standard tarot in 1760."** **The record:** the
  blocks *are* dated 1760, but the BnF-documented Nicolas Conver (1784–1833) was not yet born
  [@web_bnf_conver]. The Camoin firm's own narrative asserts he engraved them in 1760
  [@web_camoin_conver1760]; the document-minded reading is that he (or his workshop)
  **inherited and re-signed older blocks that kept their 1760 date** [@web_vs_conver_heritage].
  Flag the date as belonging to the *object*, and the *authorship* as contested.
- **Claim: the Conver deck is the "original" Tarot de Marseille.** **The record:** it is the
  most renowned and most-copied witness, but the Marseille *pattern* predates it (Noblet
  c. 1650 is older; Chosson is earlier in the Marseille line) — Conver is canonical, not
  primordial [@dummett1980; @depaulis1984].

## Connections

- **The Marseille-line cardmakers:** `jean-noblet` (the oldest surviving Marseille, Type I)
  and — at the divergent edge — `jacques-vieville`. Conver is the late, canonical Type-II
  terminus of the line whose earliest survivor is Noblet [@dummett1980].
- **Down-stream esoteric reception:** the Conver/Marseille pattern is the very pack
  `court-de-gebelin` allegorised as Egyptian in 1781 and that `etteilla` adapted to
  divination [@decker1996].
- **Successor firm:** Camoin (Conver → Camoin), the keeper of the woodblock claim
  [@web_camoin_conver].

## How they appear in this collection

Conver is the maker of **`tarot-de-marseille-conver`** and the human anchor for the
repo's already-written Marseille deck dossier (`08-tarot-de-marseille.mdx`). Wherever the
grammar names him, render the date as *"dated 1760"* with the authorship hedge, and
distinguish the **Marseille pattern** (broad type) from the **Conver 1760 edition** (this
specific witness). Cards worth featuring are the signature trumps of the Type-II pattern
(e.g. Le Bateleur, La Maison Dieu, Le Monde), but the per-card pass is owned by the deck
dossier.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Resolve the Conver identity.** Is the 1760 engraver a distinct, earlier Conver, an
  inherited block re-signed by the 1784–1833 Nicolas Conver, or a misread date? The BnF
  authority dates (1784–1833) and the block date (1760) must be reconciled
  [@web_bnf_conver; @web_vs_conver_heritage].
- **Chosson date.** The relationship to Chosson (1672? / 1736?) is part of dating the
  Marseille line and is itself contested [@depaulis1984; @decker1996].
