# The Visconti & Sforza of Milan (ducal patrons)

> **A family node, not one person.** This dossier covers three linked patrons of the
> mid-15th-century Milanese court. They **paid for**, rather than painted, the early luxury
> tarots — hence `made: []`. The painting is credited (with debate) to
> `bonifacio-bembo`'s workshop.

## At a glance

The oldest surviving tarots were **commissioned by the ruling house of Milan**: **Filippo
Maria Visconti**, last Visconti Duke; his only (illegitimate but legitimised) daughter
**Bianca Maria Visconti**; and the *condottiero* she married, **Francesco Sforza**, who
became the first Sforza Duke of Milan [@web_vs_wp; @web_filippo_wp; @web_bianca_wp;
@web_francesco_wp]. Their patronage produced the hand-painted, gold-leafed **trionfi** packs —
the **Cary-Yale / Visconti di Modrone** and the **Pierpont-Morgan–Bergamo (Visconti-Sforza)**
decks — that anchor the entire history of surviving tarot, and whose dating is unusually
secure *because* the dynastic heraldry is painted right onto the cards [@web_vs_wp;
@web_wopc_vs].

## Life & context

- **Filippo Maria Visconti (1392–1447)**, Duke of Milan, died **without a male heir**, leaving
  the duchy contested [@web_filippo_wp]. He was a notable patron and the commissioner behind
  early luxury card sets at the Milanese court [@web_vs_wp].
- **Bianca Maria Visconti (1425–1468)**, his daughter, married Francesco Sforza in **1441**;
  the marriage was the dynastic hinge by which the Sforza inherited Visconti Milan
  [@web_bianca_wp].
- **Francesco Sforza (1401–1466)**, a brilliant and controversial *condottiero* who had fought
  both **for and against** Milan, became **Duke of Milan in 1450** and founded the Sforza
  ducal line [@web_francesco_wp]. As Filippo's successor and son-in-law, he is co-commissioner
  of the surviving Visconti-Sforza pack [@web_vs_wp].

The milieu is the Quattrocento Milanese court: dynastic insecurity answered by **spectacles of
culture and patronage**, of which luxury *trionfi* cards — miniature allegories of virtue and
authority, in tempera and tooled gold — were one expression [@web_vs_wp; @web_wopc_vs].

## What they made / did

They **commissioned**, did not make, the decks:

- **`cary-yale-visconti-tarot`** — the **Visconti di Modrone / Cary-Yale** pack, the most
  lavish of the luxury sets, commonly tied to the **1441 wedding** of Bianca Maria and
  Francesco; its 67 surviving cards are in Yale's Beinecke (Cary Collection)
  [@web_beinecke_visconti; @web_cary_yale].
- **`visconti-sforza-tarot`** — the **Pierpont-Morgan–Bergamo (Colleoni-Baglioni)** pack,
  c. 1450–1460, commissioned around the Visconti/Sforza ducal succession; held across the
  **Morgan Library** and the **Accademia Carrara / Colleoni collection, Bergamo**
  [@web_vs_wp; @web_wopc_vs; @morgan_visconti].

Both are attributed (with the Bembo/Zavattari debate) to `bonifacio-bembo`'s workshop
[@web_vs_wp].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Claim: a "secret" or esoteric origin for these cards.** **The record:** this is
  **conspicuous dynastic display**, not clandestine ritual — the patrons' **heraldry is
  literally painted on the cards**, and the figures are standard Italian Christian-allegorical
  types. They were a **game** (*trionfi*) at court, not a divinatory instrument; any occult
  reading is a much later (18th–19th c.) overlay [@web_vs_wp; @web_wopc_vs; @dummett1980].
- **Claim: the deck commemorates the 1441 wedding.** **The record:** this is the
  **most-discussed theory** for the Cary-Yale specifically, and a reasonable one given a
  gold-leaf pack as a dynastic gift — but it is a hypothesis, and the various Visconti-Sforza
  sets span c. 1441–1460; treat exact occasions as proposed, not proven
  [@web_beinecke_visconti; @web_vs_wp].

## Connections

- **Their painter:** `bonifacio-bembo` (workshop), the credited maker of both decks — with the
  `Zavattari` workshop as the rival attribution [@web_vs_wp].
- **Their iconographer:** `gertrude-moakley`, who read the trumps as a Petrarchan triumph
  procession and titled her 1966 study around Bembo painting "for the Visconti-Sforza family"
  [@moakley1966].
- **Their historian:** `michael-dummett`, who establishes the game-first context of these
  *trionfi* [@dummett1980].

## How they appear in this collection

This node is the **patron anchor** for the two earliest deck slugs,
**`cary-yale-visconti-tarot`** and **`visconti-sforza-tarot`**. They never appear as "makers"
(`made: []`); in the grammar they are the **commissioners** whose heraldry and dynastic story
explain *why* the decks exist and *how* they are dated. Cards that carry Visconti/Sforza
heraldic devices, and any trumps modelled on family members in period dress, are where this
patron node is most visible.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Which deck for which occasion.** The 1441-wedding tie is firmest for the Cary-Yale; the
  internal dating and exact commission of each of the ~15 related sets remains debated
  [@web_vs_wp].
- **Bianca's status.** Bianca Maria was Filippo's legitimised natural daughter; phrase
  succession claims accordingly [@web_bianca_wp].
