# Andrea Mantegna (c. 1431–1506) — the misattribution

## At a glance

Andrea Mantegna was one of the foremost painters of the early Italian Renaissance, court
artist to the Gonzaga in Mantua. His name survives in tarot history only through an
**error**: the set of 50 engravings long called the **"Mantegna Tarocchi"** was neither
engraved by Mantegna nor a tarot. The collection catalogues that deck honestly under that
traditional-but-wrong name, then debunks it.

## Life & context

Born near Padua c. 1431, died in Mantua 1506. A master of perspective and antiquarian
classicism, he had no documented connection to the engraved series that bears his name.

## What they made / did

In tarot terms: **nothing.** His genuine output is paintings and a few autograph engravings
unrelated to the so-called Tarocchi.

## The claim vs the record

- **Myth:** Andrea Mantegna engraved a "tarot" of 50 cards.
- **Record:** The **"Mantegna Tarocchi"** (c. 1465, two series — the earlier *E-series* and a
  copy *S-series*) is a didactic set of **50 engravings in five decades** (estates of man,
  Apollo and the Muses, the liberal arts, the genii/virtues and sciences, the planets and
  spheres) — **a ladder of being, not a card game**, and **not by Mantegna**. It is attributed
  to an anonymous Ferrarese or Bolognese hand (the "Master of the E-series"). The Mantegna
  label is a traditional convenience, repeatedly rejected by scholarship [@dummett1980;
  @web_mantegna_wopc; @web_mantegna_wp].

## Connections

The misattribution sits beside the **"Gringonneur"** misattribution (the Charles VI deck) as
one of the collection's two classic "famous name wrongly attached to a deck" cases. Scholarly
correction follows **michael-dummett**.

## How they appear in this collection

As the **debunked** namesake of `mantegna-tarocchi`. The deck grammar keeps the familiar name
for findability but states plainly that it is neither Mantegna's nor a tarot. See
`research/decks/mantegna-tarocchi.md`.
