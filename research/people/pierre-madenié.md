# Pierre Madenié (active c. 1709–1736, Dijon)

## At a glance

**Pierre Madenié** was a Dijon cardmaker whose **1709 Tarot de Marseille** (complete
deck, signed and dated) is one of the **earliest intact surviving examples** of the
Tarot de Marseille pattern — the woodcut-engraved, stencil-coloured printed tarot that
became the standard European form. The Madenié deck is a critical document in the history
of **TdM standardisation**: it shows the trump iconography in a relatively early form,
predating the 1760 Conver deck that the collection uses as the canonical TdM reference
[@web_tdm_wp; @web_tdm_wopc; @dummett1980].

## Life & context

Madenié worked in **Dijon (Burgundy)**, which — along with Marseille, Lyon, Avignon, and
Paris — was one of the major French centres of professional cardmaking in the early
18th century. French cardmaking was a **guild trade** regulated by municipal authorities:
cardmakers (cartiers) registered their marks, operated within guild rules, and produced
decks for both local sale and export. The Tarot de Marseille pattern circulated across
this network, with Dijon and Marseille producing closely related but not identical versions.

The **1709 deck** survives intact (52 or more cards) and is now considered a landmark in
tarot bibliophily. Several tarot historians (Jean-Claude Flornoy, Thierry Depaulis) have
used it as a reference point for reconstructing the early TdM tradition.

## What they made / did

- **1709 Tarot de Marseille** (signed "Pierre Madenié, Dijon, 1709"): a complete 78-card
  deck in the TdM pattern. The trump imagery closely follows the established Milanese-
  derived French tradition but shows regional variations in posture, costume, and
  colouring compared to the later Conver standard.
- His work represents the **intermediate step** between the earliest French TdM productions
  (late 17th century, Marseille and Lyon) and the highly standardised Conver 1760 edition
  that became the dominant 20th-century reference [@web_tdm_wopc].

## The TdM standardisation chain

The Tarot de Marseille pattern was not designed by one person but **converged gradually**:
- **Milan → Lyon → Marseille** (16th–17th century): the standard woodcut trump programme
  travelled from Lombardy through Lyon to the Provençal cardmaking trade
- **Dijon workshops** (early 18th century, including Madenié): one branch of the French
  tradition, producing close variants
- **Nicolas Rolichon, Lyon** (c. 1672): an important early survivor
- **Nicolas Conver, Marseille, 1760**: the edition that became the template for virtually
  all subsequent TdM reprints and the standard against which the pattern is defined

## The claim vs. the record

- **"Nicolas Conver invented the Tarot de Marseille."** **The record**: Conver's 1760 deck
  *standardised* an already-existing iconographic tradition. Madenié's 1709 deck shows
  that the TdM pattern was already stable fifty years earlier. Conver's version became
  canonical because of its quality and because 20th-century occultists (Papus, then the
  entire esoteric tradition) reprinted from it — not because he invented it.
