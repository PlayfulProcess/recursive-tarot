# L. A. Mayer (Leo Aryeh Mayer, 1895–1959)

## At a glance

Leo Aryeh Mayer was an Israeli scholar of Islamic art who, in the 1930s, recognised a set
of richly decorated cards in the Topkapı Sarayı, Istanbul, as a **Mamluk-era playing pack** —
the *Mulūk wa-nuwwāb* ("Kings and Deputies"). His identification supplied the missing link
between Chinese money-suited cards and the European four-suit pack that became tarot.

## Life & context

Born 12 January 1895, died 6 April 1959. He studied Oriental and Islamic art at Vienna,
Lausanne and Berlin, and served as rector of the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. A museum of
Islamic art in Jerusalem (the L. A. Mayer Memorial Institute) is named for him. He examined
the Topkapı cards around 1931 and first published them in 1939.

## What they made / did

- Identified and described the Mamluk pack at the Topkapı Palace Museum (first published
  1939; full monograph **_Mamluk Playing Cards_**, E. J. Brill, 1971, posthumous)
  [@mayer1971].
- Established the pack's four suits — cups, coins, swords, **polo-sticks** — each with ten
  pips and three court cards (malik + two nāʾib), all **aniconic** per Islamic norms against
  figural imagery. This is the structural skeleton Europe inherited.

## The claim vs the record

Mayer's careful description corrected loose talk of "Saracen" or "gypsy" card origins with a
dated, physical object. Note the survivor-count nuance his work and its readers established:
the Topkapı set is **almost complete (~48 cards)**, of which **~43 belong to the original
pack** and a handful are cruder later replacements; the complete pack held 52. (confidence:
high on the structure; the exact survivor count is reported variously as 43/47/48 across
sources — the dossier states the reconciled figure.)
