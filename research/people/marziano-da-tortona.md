# Marziano da Tortona

## At a glance

**Marziano da Tortona** (fl. early 15th century) was a **humanist secretary** to
**Filippo Maria Visconti**, Duke of Milan, and the **earliest named tarot designer in
history** — perhaps the earliest named designer of any card trump series. Around
**c. 1415–1425** he wrote a Latin treatise, *Tractatus de deificatione sexdecim heroum*,
describing a set of **sixteen trump cards** he had designed for the duke's personal use,
each depicting a Roman god above a bird sacred to that deity [@dummett1980;
@pratesi_marziano; @web_marziano_wp].

## Life & context

Almost nothing is known of Marziano's biography beyond his role at the Visconti court.
He was a **learned man** — the treatise is written in humanist Latin and reflects
considerable knowledge of classical mythology — and he served Filippo Maria as a
personal secretary, the kind of educated courtier who straddled administration and
literary culture. The treatise is addressed to the duke himself [@pratesi_marziano].

The context is the **Milanese ducal court of the early Quattrocento**: Filippo Maria
Visconti (1392–1447) was an obsessive, reclusive ruler who surrounded himself with
humanist scholars and is documented as an avid card-game player. The commission of a
bespoke trump series reflects both the duke's wealth and the early tarot's status as
a luxury object for the aristocratic intelligentsia.

## What they made / did

- **A set of sixteen trump cards** arranged in four groups of four: **gods** (Jupiter,
  Apollo, Mercury, Diana), **virtues** (Juno, Pallas, Venus, Ceres), **riches** (Vulcan,
  Mars, Aeolus, Neptune), and **pleasures** (Bacchus, Pluto, Saturn, Cupid). Each god
  was paired with a bird: Jupiter with the eagle, Venus with the dove, etc.
  [@dummett1980; @pratesi_marziano].
- This is **not the standard tarot trump sequence** — there is no Papess, no Death, no
  Tower. It is a humanist parallel invention: a classicising trump programme ordered by
  mythological hierarchy rather than Christian allegory. Scholars debate whether it
  represents an **alternative tradition** or a one-off experiment that left no successors.
- The physical cards **do not survive**. Only the treatise survives (in several manuscript
  copies), describing the programme and its iconography in detail.

## The claim vs. the record

- **The claim**: Marziano da Tortona designed the first tarot trumps.
- **The precise record**: He designed *a* trump series — documented, named, classicising —
  that is certainly the **earliest tarot programme we can attach to a named individual**.
  Whether it predates or influenced the standard triumph/trionfi sequence is unknown.
  Franco Pratesi has argued the Marziano deck was made **c. 1418–1425** on the basis of
  manuscript dating [@pratesi_marziano]. Michael Dummett discusses it at length in
  *The Game of Tarot* as evidence for the early Milanese origin of tarot trumps
  [@dummett1980].
- **The key uncertainty**: did the standard tarot trump hierarchy (Fool, Magician, Popess…
  World) derive from experiments like Marziano's, or did both arise independently from a
  broader Milanese court culture of luxury card games? The honest answer is: unknown.

## Why this matters for this collection

Marziano's treatise shifts the **origin of the tarot trump** from anonymous folk tradition
to **documented humanist commission**. The earliest tarot was not a folk object — it was
**invented for a duke by his secretary**, grounded in classical learning, and probably
produced by the same Lombard court painters who made the Visconti-Sforza packs.
This matters for every deck in this collection: the intellectual ambition visible in
Visconti-Sforza iconography has a named ancestor.
