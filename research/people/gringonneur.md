# Jacquemin Gringonneur (fl. 1392) — the misattribution

## At a glance

Jacquemin Gringonneur is a real but shadowy figure: a Paris painter recorded in a **1392**
royal account paying him **56 sols** for **"three packs of cards, gilded and coloured and
ornamented with various designs, for the amusement of our lord the king"** (Charles VI of
France). Those were **ordinary playing cards**, now lost. His name became famous through a
**19th-century error** that tied this payment to a surviving hand-painted tarot — a deck that
is actually **Italian, c. 1450–80**, not French and not from 1392.

## Life & context

Known only from the 1392 treasury entry (recorded by Charles Poupart, treasurer to Charles
VI). Nothing else about him is securely documented [@web_wopc_handpainted; @decker1996].

## What they made / did

- **1392:** three card packs for Charles VI — **standard playing cards**, not tarot (tarot
  did not yet exist; it appears in Italy only in the 1440s) [@dummett1980].

## The claim vs the record

- **Myth:** Gringonneur invented/painted the tarot in 1392 for Charles VI (the "Charles VI"
  or "Gringonneur" tarot).
- **Record:** In the mid-19th century a card historian linked the 1392 payment to a set of
  17 hand-painted tarot trumps in the **Bibliothèque nationale de France**. On stylistic
  grounds those cards are **Italian (Ferrara/B-order), c. 1450–80 — about a century too late**
  to be Gringonneur's, and from the wrong country. The deck is misnamed but the name has
  stuck [@web_charlesvi_th; @web_wopc_handpainted; @dummett1980].

## Connections

The paradigmatic tarot misattribution, paired with **andrea-mantegna**'s. The corrective is
**michael-dummett**'s document-first dating.

## How they appear in this collection

As the **debunked** namesake of `charles-vi-tarot`. The deck grammar keeps "Charles VI
(Gringonneur)" for findability and states the real provenance (Ferrarese, c. 1450–80). See
`research/03-charles-vi-gringonneur.mdx`.
