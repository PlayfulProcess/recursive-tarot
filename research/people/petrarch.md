# Francesco Petrarca ('Petrarch', 1304–1374)

## At a glance

**Francesco Petrarca** (Petrarch) was the defining figure of early Italian humanism —
poet, scholar, collector of ancient manuscripts, and the author of the *Trionfi*
(*Triumphs*), an allegorical poem sequence whose **procession of allegorical figures**
is almost certainly the **primary literary source** for the tarot major arcana
[@moakley1966; @dummett1980; @web_petrarch_wp].

Petrarch had no connection to tarot cards, which emerged roughly 50 years after his
death. But his *Trionfi* created the **cultural grammar** — a hierarchical parade of
cosmic forces in which higher powers capture and defeat lower ones — that the tarot
trump designers translated into visual form.

## The *Trionfi* and tarot

The *Trionfi* (written c. 1356–1374, left unfinished at Petrarch's death) depicts a
sequence of allegorical triumphal processions, each conquering the previous:

1. **Trionfo d'Amore** (Love conquers all)
2. **Trionfo della Pudicizia** (Chastity conquers Love)
3. **Trionfo della Morte** (Death conquers Chastity)
4. **Trionfo della Fama** (Fame overcomes Death — great names outlast death)
5. **Trionfo del Tempo** (Time conquers Fame)
6. **Trionfo dell'Eternità** (Eternity conquers Time)

This sequence — **Love, Chastity, Death, Fame, Time, Eternity** — maps recognisably onto
the tarot major arcana's thematic logic: the Lovers, the Pope (spiritual chastity), Death,
the World (eternal order), the Hermit (time), the Fool (transcendence). **Gertrude Moakley**
argued the case systematically in *The Tarot Cards Painted by Bonifacio Bembo* (1966),
and it has been broadly accepted [@moakley1966; @dummett1980].

The literal word *trionfi* (triumphs) became the **standard Italian name for tarot trump
cards** — from at least the 1440s, tarot trumps were called *trionfi* or *carte da trionfi*
in Italian documents, echoing the Petrarchan literary tradition [@dummett1980].

## Life & context

Petrarch was born in **Arezzo** (his family exiled from Florence), grew up in **Avignon**
at the papal court, and spent his later life between **Padua, Venice, and Arquà**. He was
the first major Italian humanist to systematically recover and study ancient Latin texts,
and his vernacular Italian lyric poetry (*Canzoniere*) established the Petrarchan sonnet
tradition that defined European love poetry for centuries.

His allegorical mode — cosmic forces personified as regal figures in triumphal processions
— was directly visible to the Milanese and Ferrarese courts where tarot was born. The
iconographic tradition of **triumphal processions** (drawn from ancient Roman *triumphi*)
was already a living visual language in the 15th-century Italian courts.

## The claim vs. the record

- **The claim**: Petrarch "invented tarot."
- **The correct framing**: Petrarch provided the **literary framework** — the allegoric
  triumph procession with a fixed hierarchy of cosmic forces — that tarot trump designers
  adapted. He did not design or know about tarot cards. The tarot is a *reception* of
  his allegory, not his creation.
- **The scholarly consensus** (Moakley, Dummett, Kaplan): the *Trionfi* connection is
  well-supported and explains both the word *trionfi* for trump cards and the thematic
  logic of the major arcana sequence. But tarot also incorporated **Christian allegory,
  astrological symbolism, and court-game traditions** not found in Petrarch — it was not
  a simple translation of the poem.
