# Jean-Baptiste Alliette — "Etteilla"

## At a glance

**Jean-Baptiste Alliette** (1 March 1738 – 12 December 1791), who reversed his surname to
sign himself **"Etteilla,"** was the first person known to history to earn his living by
reading cards, the author of the first self-instruction manuals for it, and the maker of the
**first tarot deck purpose-built for divination** (1788–89). Where Court de Gébelin supplied a
*theory* (the cards as an Egyptian "Book of Thoth") and the Comte de Mellet sketched a
*method*, Etteilla industrialised both into a working practice, a printed deck, and a
correspondence *school*. His system outlived him as a 19th-century commercial brand through two
posthumous editions (Etteilla II and III) produced by his followers and later publishers
[@decker1996; @tarot_heritage].

## Life & context

Etteilla was born in Paris on 1 March 1738; his father was a *maître rôtisseur* (caterer) and
his mother a seed-merchant, and Alliette himself worked for a time as a **seedsman and
print/engraving dealer** before turning to cards full-time. He married Jeanne Vattier in 1763
and separated some six years later [@web_etteilla_wp; @web_villarevak_etteilla].

He published his **first cartomancy manual in 1770**, *Etteilla, ou manière de se récréer avec
un jeu de cartes* — "the first self-instruction book on cartomancy," teaching divination with a
**32-card piquet pack** (plus an added "Etteilla" card), already using laid-out *spreads* and
**fixed upright/reversed meanings** for every card. This pre-tarot work established the
machinery — spread, reversal, dictionary of meanings — that he would later carry onto the
tarot itself [@web_etteilla_wp; @web_benebell_petit; @tarot_heritage]. He died in Paris on
12 December 1791.

## What they made / did

- **First cartomancy manual (1770)** — *Etteilla, ou manière de se récréer avec un jeu de
  cartes*, on the 32-card piquet pack: spreads, reversals, and a fixed meaning-dictionary
  [@web_etteilla_wp; @web_benebell_petit].
- **Theoretical works on the tarot (1783–85)** — the four-*cahier* series usually titled
  *Manière de se récréer avec le jeu de cartes nommées tarots*, in which he applied his
  cartomantic system to the 78-card tarot and wove in Gébelin's Egyptian "Book of Thoth"
  framing [@web_etteilla_wp; @decker1996].
- **`etteilla-i-livre-de-thot` — the *Livre de Thot* deck (1788–89).** The **first tarot
  deck designed from scratch for divination.** It does not map cleanly onto the Tarot de
  Marseille: trumps are renumbered (e.g. Death moved away from XIII), querent/significator and
  the four elements are encoded, and upright/reversed keywords are printed on the cards. Treat
  it as a new system, **not** a normalised Marseille [@decker1996; @web_pdr_etteilla].
- **`etteilla-ii-egyptian` — Grand Etteilla II / *Grand livre de Thot* (c. 1838–40).** A
  **posthumous** follower re-edition. Its instruction booklet appeared under the pseudonym
  **"Julia Orsini,"** widely attributed to the Lille printer **Simon-François Blocquel**
  (1780–1863); it altered the titling and imagery of Etteilla's deck (confidence: high on the
  Blocquel attribution per multiple secondary sources; primary not personally inspected)
  [@web_grand_etteilla; @web_druidcraft].
- **`etteilla-iii-oracle-des-dames` — Grand Etteilla III / *Grand jeu de l'Oracle des Dames*
  (c. 1865–70).** A later **posthumous** mass-market lithograph edition, first printed
  c. 1865 (G. Régamey), later by Lequart et Mignot (1872–1890) and ultimately **B. P.
  Grimaud**; sold as respectable parlour amusement "for the ladies" [@web_grand_etteilla;
  @web_druidcraft].
- **The school: *Société des Interprètes du Livre de Thot* (founded 1788).** A network of
  French-speaking correspondents with Etteilla as "correspondent general," through which he
  disseminated his teaching — arguably the first organised body devoted to esoteric tarot
  [@web_etteilla_wp; @decker1996]. His **successor M. d'Odoucet** continued the system after
  his death, publishing the three-volume *Science des signes* (1804–07), which expounds
  Etteilla's cartomancy in his style [@web_dodoucet; @decker1996].

## The claim vs. the record

- **Myth: the deck transmits ancient Egyptian wisdom (the "Book of Thoth").** Etteilla took
  this framing wholesale from Court de Gébelin (1781) and marketed his deck under it. **The
  record:** the Egyptian pedigree is invented. Tarot is a 15th-century North-Italian card game;
  hieroglyphs were undeciphered until Champollion (1820s). Etteilla *founded a real and durable
  divinatory practice* but sold it under a *false antiquity* — both are true, and the dossier
  honours the practice while flagging the pedigree [@decker1996; @dummett1980].
- **Myth: "the first tarot fortune-teller is ancient/timeless."** **The record:** the
  documentary trail for systematic tarot divination begins, concretely, with Etteilla's printed
  books (1770 for cards, 1783–85 for tarot) and his 1788–89 deck — a dated 18th-century
  invention, not a recovered tradition [@decker1996].
- **Etteilla II and III are *not* his work.** They are posthumous continuations by his school
  and by later commercial publishers (Blocquel/"Orsini"; Régamey/Lequart-Mignot/Grimaud).
  Attributing them to Alliette himself is a common error [@web_grand_etteilla; @web_druidcraft].

## Connections

- **`court-de-gebelin` and `comte-de-mellet` → Etteilla.** Etteilla built directly on the two
  essays in *Le Monde primitif*, vol. 8 (1781): Gébelin's Egyptian-origin myth and Mellet's
  proposal that the cards could be *read* and tied to the Hebrew alphabet. Etteilla turned their
  theory into a marketed practice [@decker1996].
- **Rivalry/distance from Gébelin's circle.** Etteilla advanced his *own* numbering and
  symbolism rather than simply illustrating Gébelin; later scholars (Decker–Depaulis–Dummett)
  treat him as the commercial populariser who diverged from the salon theorists [@decker1996].
- **Etteilla → his school → `eliphas-levi` and the later occult revival.** His
  Société and successors (d'Odoucet) kept esoteric tarot alive into the 19th century, the
  milieu that Éliphas Lévi (1850s) would reorganise around the Hebrew alphabet and that
  `oswald-wirth` would later illustrate [@decker1996; @decker2002].
- He is reported to have influenced the celebrated card-reader **Marie-Anne Lenormand**
  [@web_etteilla_wp].

## How they appear in this collection

Etteilla is the maker behind three deck slugs in this repo: **`etteilla-i-livre-de-thot`**
(his own 1788–89 deck), **`etteilla-ii-egyptian`**, and **`etteilla-iii-oracle-des-dames`**
(posthumous). He should feature prominently on the cards his system *renumbered or invented* —
above all the **querent/significator and element cards** that have no Marseille parent, and the
**Death** card (renumbered away from XIII), where his departure from the Marseille tradition is
sharpest. Any "people of tarot" card should frame him as **the first professional cartomancer**
and the bridge from Gébelin's theory to a working deck.
