# Etteilla III — Le Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames

> Part of the Etteilla lineage — hub at `10-etteilla.mdx`; see also `10a` and `10b`.

## At a glance

The **third-generation** Etteilla and the one most collectors actually own: *Le Grand Jeu
de l'Oracle des Dames*, lithographed by **G. Regamey** and printed by **Haugard-Meuge**
around **1865**. Furthest from Alliette's original imagery, it is the **late-Victorian
mass-market** face of the Etteilla system and shows cartomancy fully normalised as parlour
culture.

## Origin & dating

First printed **c.1865** (circulating to **c.1870**). Produced by Alliette's "school" lineage,
**not** his own hand. The marketing tell is in the title — ***des Dames***, "for the
ladies" — explicitly pitching divination as fashionable **feminine drawing-room
entertainment**.

## Provenance & evidence

Many surviving lithographed decks (Regamey/Haugard-Meuge editions); among the more common
antique Etteilla packs on the market. It carries **still more variations** from Etteilla I
in titling and imagery.

## Game or divination?

**Divination** — a retail oracle, the end-state of the Etteilla brand.

## The fear question

The title says it all: by 1865 tarot fortune-telling is **respectable lady's parlour
amusement**, sold under a name that invites *women* to play with fate at home. This is the
**de-stigmatised** end of the spectrum — Christmas-and-Halloween fortune games, not
clandestine sorcery. (The criminalised version persists only for the *itinerant*,
*paid*, often Romani reader — the same class line drawn throughout this library.)

## Counter-voices

The "Oracle des Dames" framing is a useful corrective to a romantic idea: 19th-century
cartomancy's mainstream was **bourgeois domestic leisure**, gendered feminine and sold as
fun. The genuinely persecuted and the genuinely marginal practitioners were a *different*
population from the buyers of this pretty lithographed boxed set — a distinction the
"suppressed occult tradition" narrative should keep in view.
