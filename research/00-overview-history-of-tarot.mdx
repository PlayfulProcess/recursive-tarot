# A History of Tarot — Overview

## At a glance

Tarot begins as a **card game**, not an oracle. Sometime in the 1430s–40s, in the
courts of northern Italy (Milan, Ferrara, Bologna, Florence), players added a fifth
suit of allegorical picture-cards — *trionfi*, "triumphs" — to the ordinary four-suit
pack. These trumps gave us the word *trump* and the structure we now call the Major
Arcana. For roughly **340 years** the cards are documented only as a game. The idea that
they encode secret Egyptian, Kabbalistic, or Hermetic wisdom appears suddenly in
**1781** (Court de Gébelin) and is turned into a working fortune-telling system from
**1788** (Etteilla). Everything "occult" about tarot is younger than the United States.

## The documentary spine (the skeptical default)

The modern history rests on Michael Dummett and his collaborators, who read the
account-books, sumptuary edicts, card-makers' guild records, and surviving objects
rather than later legend:

- **Invention as a game, c.1440s.** The earliest unambiguous references — Ferrara and
  Milan court accounts — describe *trionfi* as cards to be played, won, and lost.
- **No occult use before the 1780s.** Across four centuries of records there is no
  reading-the-cards-for-divination with the tarot trumps. Cartomancy with *ordinary*
  cards is glimpsed earlier, but tarot-specific divination is an 18th-century French
  invention.
- **Three trump orders (A/B/C).** Dummett showed the early decks fall into three
  regional orderings — A (Bologna/Florence), B (Ferrara), C (Milan/the West) — which is
  how we trace descent. See `14-genealogy-tree.mdx`.

This is the position the whole repo defaults to, and it is well-evidenced. The honest
caveat: "absence in the record" is not the same as "proof of absence," and the people
most likely to leave *no* paper trail were the poor, the illiterate, and the
persecuted. That caveat is where the counter-voices below live.

## The fear question, era by era

The user-facing question this library keeps asking: *would a person have openly said
they were doing this, or would they have been afraid?* The answer flips depending on
whether the cards are a **game** or a **divinatory/occult** practice, and on where you
stand.

**Renaissance Italy (game era, 1440s–1700s): mostly unafraid, but cards were
moralised.** The painted decks were luxury objects commissioned by dukes; playing them
was a courtly leisure, openly recorded. What drew fire was **gambling**, not divination.
Reforming friars — most famously **Bernardino of Siena**, who preached against cards at
Bologna in 1423 and attributed their invention to the devil — staged "bonfires of the
vanities." Cities passed anti-gaming statutes; some explicitly *exempted* trionfi as a
game of skill. So the fear in this period attaches to losing money and to clerical
scolding, not to sorcery.

**Early-modern cartomancy (1500s–1700s): quietly risky.** Telling fortunes with cards
existed at the folk level and surfaces in Inquisition and witch-trial records in Italy,
Spain and the Low Countries — but the Inquisition's remit was formally limited (after
Alexander IV, 1258) to divination *linked to manifest heresy*. Practitioners were
typically poor women and were vulnerable; this is exactly the layer that left little
documentation and that the counter-voices say was written out of history.

**Enlightenment France (birth of tarot divination, 1781–1791): surprisingly open.**
Court de Gébelin published his Egyptian theory in a respectable scholarly encyclopedia.
**Etteilla** ran a "New School of Magic," advertised his courses in the Paris papers,
sold decks, and signed his books — hardly the behaviour of a man in hiding. Fortune-
telling in pre-Revolutionary Paris was fashionable salon entertainment as much as
clandestine craft. Fear here is muted for the literate Parisian entrepreneur.

**19th-century Britain (occult revival + criminalisation): a double life.** While
gentlemen-magicians of the **Golden Dawn** explored tarot privately and called it
philosophy, *commercial* fortune-tellers were prosecuted under the **Vagrancy Act 1824**
(building on the Witchcraft Act 1735, which recast "witchcraft" as fraud). Joseph
Powell was convicted for fortune-telling as early as 1807. So the same decade could see
tarot as a respectable drawing-room curiosity for the educated and a jailable offence
for a working-class woman doing it for money. **Class, gender and money decided who was
afraid** — not the cards.

## Counter-voices: was an occult history suppressed?

The repo's stance is document-first, which some practitioners and scholars argue is
itself a bias. Stated fairly, the counter-position runs:

- **The Egyptian/Hermetic claim.** Court de Gébelin and his heirs insisted tarot
  preserved Egyptian temple wisdom. *Scholarly verdict:* unsupported — the imagery is
  demonstrably Italian Christian-allegorical, and the "hieroglyphs" were imagined before
  Champollion could read real ones. This claim is a **myth**, not a suppressed truth.
- **The Romani ("Gypsy") transmission claim.** A popular 19th–20th-century story held
  that Roma carried tarot (and Egyptian wisdom) into Europe. *Scholarly verdict:*
  chronology and evidence don't support Roma *introducing* tarot. **But** Romani people
  genuinely did become Europe's most visible cartomancers, and were simultaneously
  **exoticised and criminalised** for it. Romani writers today argue that an authentic,
  living divinatory tradition — theirs — was both appropriated by white esotericists and
  erased by hostile authorities and tidy academic histories. This is the strongest form
  of the "suppression by colonial/state forces" argument, and it deserves to be heard:
  the absence of Roma from the archive is partly an *effect of persecution*, not proof
  they weren't there.
- **The methodological critique.** Document-first history privileges what literate,
  propertied institutions chose to record. Oral, folk, feminine and colonised
  knowledges leave thin archives. So "no evidence of divination before 1781" can mean
  "the people doing it weren't allowed pens," not "nobody did it."

**How this library holds the tension.** We keep Dummett's chronology for the *objects*
(the decks really were made as games; the Egyptian story really is an 18th-century
invention) while refusing to let "no document" flatten into "no people." We label the
Egyptian and Roma-origin *origin myths* as myths, and separately honour the *real,
documented* history of Romani cartomancers and the *real* state persecution of
fortune-tellers. Suppression is genuine in the second sense even though the Egyptian
genealogy is false.

## The shape of the family

```
Ordinary 4-suit pack (Islamic world → Europe, c.1370s)
        │  + a fifth suit of allegorical "triumphs"
        ▼
Italian trionfi (c.1440s)  ── the game ──────────────┐
  ├─ A-order (Bologna/Florence): Tarocchino · Minchiate · Siciliano
  ├─ B-order (Ferrara): "Charles VI" · d'Este
  └─ C-order (Milan/West): Visconti / Cary-Yale → Tarot de Marseille
                                                   │  the occult overlay (post-1781)
                                                   ▼
                       Court de Gébelin → Etteilla → Lévi → Wirth → Golden Dawn → Rider-Waite-Smith
  (Sola Busca = sui-generis offshoot; Mantegna "Tarocchi" = not a tarot at all)
```
