# Court de Gébelin's Tarot — the 22 trumps, card by card

**Source of imagery:** uncoloured copperplate engravings in Antoine Court de Gébelin,
*Le Monde primitif*, vol. VIII (Paris, 1781), reproducing the **Tarot de Marseille** pattern
with deliberate "corrections" toward Gébelin's imagined Egyptian originals [@decker1996;
@web_robertplace_firstoccult].

**Parent deck:** [tarot-de-marseille-conver](../decks/tarot-de-marseille-conver.md). Every
"Changed from parent" line below is the diff against the Marseille trump and *why* — i.e. how
Gébelin's (or Mellet's) Egyptian thesis re-reads it.

**Two voices — keep them distinct.** *Gébelin* = the allegorical/Egyptian reading in "Du Jeu
des Tarots." *Mellet* = the appended "Recherches sur les Tarots": Hebrew letters, reverse-order
three-Age cosmogony, divination. Where they differ on a card, both are given.

## 0 — The Fool (Le Mat)
- **Depicts:** A traveller in motley with a staff and bundle, a beast tearing at his leg — the Marseille Fool, copied into the plate [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Folly** (*la Folie*) — the senseless man, set apart from the sacred sequence; unnumbered, the head/zero of the series. Gébelin pointedly places the Fool *first*, as the numberless card [@trionfi; @web_trionfi_gebelin].
- **Mellet's reading:** "The Fool, which does not produce anything, is without number; it is the **zero** of this calculation" — the cipher that begins (read backwards) the human, Iron-Age series [@web_tarotforum_mellet].
- **Changed from parent:** Same image; the change is conceptual — the Marseille's nameless wandering jester is fixed as **"zero,"** the head of an alphabet-of-22, giving the Fool the structural prominence every later occult deck grants it [@web_trionfi_gebelin].
- **Sources:** [@trionfi], [@web_trionfi_gebelin], [@web_tarotforum_mellet]
- **Confidence:** high

## 1 — The Magician (Le Bateleur)
- **Depicts:** A figure at a table of cups, coins and knives, a wand/baton raised [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** The **Cup-player / Juggler** holding "the wand of Jacob or of the Magi"; the active, material first principle — life as "a perpetual game of hazard," creation as a juggling of elements. Gébelin re-reads the table's objects as the four **elements** [@web_robertplace_firstoccult; @web_villarevak_gebelin].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's street-performer *bateleur* is elevated from fairground conjuror to a quasi-priestly figure of cosmic agency, his cups/coins/swords/baton turned into element-symbols — the first step of the long transformation of this card into the occult "Magician" [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Sources:** [@web_robertplace_firstoccult], [@web_villarevak_gebelin]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 2 — The High Priestess (La Papesse)
- **Depicts:** A veiled, enthroned woman in a tiara with a book [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** Not a scandalous "popess" but the **High Priestess of Isis** — sacred wisdom in female form. This is one of the **three cards Gébelin reads as Isiac** (with the Moon and the Star) [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille *Papesse* — an awkward Christian image (a female pope) often explained away as Pope Joan or a heresy — is re-titled and re-sexed into **Egyptian priestess-goddess**, removing the Christian embarrassment and making the card the keystone of the Isis reading [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis], [@web_robertplace_firstoccult]
- **Confidence:** high

## 3 — The Empress (L'Impératrice)
- **Depicts:** A crowned woman with sceptre and eagle shield [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** A female sovereign principle. Gébelin does not name her an Egyptian goddess outright; commentators note that because he identifies the **Emperor with Osiris**, the Empress is by implication a sovereign/Isis-like consort (an inference, not an explicit label) [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Changed from parent:** Modest — the feudal Empress is folded into the Egyptian royal frame as Osiris's counterpart; the change is the *context* (Egyptian sovereignty) rather than the image (confidence: medium — Gébelin's text is least explicit here).
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 4 — The Emperor (L'Empereur)
- **Depicts:** A bearded sovereign in profile with sceptre and shield [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** Temporal sovereignty re-read as **Osiris**, the Egyptian king — the male sovereign principle [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Changed from parent:** The Holy Roman Emperor becomes an **Egyptian** king-god (Osiris), anchoring the Isis/Osiris/Typhon family Gébelin scatters across the trumps [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 5 — The Hierophant (Le Pape)
- **Depicts:** A tiara'd pontiff blessing two acolytes [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** The **Grand Hierophant / chief of the hierophants** — the high priest of the Egyptian mysteries, not the Roman Pope [@web_villarevak_gebelin; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Changed from parent:** The Catholic Pope is de-Christianised into the **pagan high priest of the mysteries**, exactly paralleling the Papess→Isis-priestess move; this is where the modern title "Hierophant" originates [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Sources:** [@web_villarevak_gebelin], [@web_robertplace_firstoccult]
- **Confidence:** high

## 6 — The Lovers (L'Amoureux)
- **Depicts:** A youth between two women beneath a winged Cupid drawing his bow [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** Gébelin adds the caption **"Marriage"** [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Mellet's reading:** It is **Mellet** (not Gébelin) who reads it as a **choice between Vice and Virtue** — Hercules at the crossroads, a "trial of moral strength" [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's love-triangle-under-Cupid is first narrowed to **"Marriage"** (Gébelin), then moralised into the **Choice / Vice-and-Virtue** allegory (Mellet) that later cartomancers inherited — though the earliest cards show simple love, not moral choice [@web_benebell_lovers]. *Correction to existing grammar: the Vice/Virtue–Hercules reading is Mellet's, not Gébelin's.*
- **Sources:** [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** high

## 7 — The Chariot (Le Chariot)
- **Depicts:** A crowned prince in a canopied chariot drawn by two horses [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** Victory and dominion — the triumphant king/**Osiris** in his war-chariot (consistent with his Osiris identification of the Emperor) [@web_robertplace_firstoccult; @web_villarevak_gebelin].
- **Changed from parent:** The generic Renaissance triumph-chariot is given an **Egyptian royal/Osirian** gloss; image unchanged, frame Egyptianised (confidence: medium on the explicit "Osiris" label for this specific card).
- **Sources:** [@web_robertplace_firstoccult], [@web_villarevak_gebelin]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 8 — Justice (La Justice)
- **Depicts:** An enthroned woman with sword and balance [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** One of the **four cardinal Virtues** the Egyptians (he claims) set among the sacred cards — here **Justice** [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** Little visual change; Gébelin gathers Justice (VIII), Strength (XI), Temperance (XIIII) and the re-read Hanged Man as Prudence (XII) into the **complete set of four cardinal Virtues**, presenting their presence as proof of a designed wisdom-system [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Sources:** [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** high

## 9 — The Hermit (L'Hermite)
- **Depicts:** A cloaked old man with a staff and a shielded lantern [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** The sage/seeker; Gébelin reads the lantern-bearer as **Diogenes**, who carried a lamp by day searching for an honest man / for virtue — the philosopher seeking truth [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's *Hermite* (sometimes read as Father Time / Old Age) is recast as the **searching philosopher (Diogenes)** — a wisdom-seeker rather than a figure of mortality [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Sources:** [@web_robertplace_firstoccult]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 10 — Wheel of Fortune (La Roue de Fortune)
- **Depicts:** A wheel with ascending and descending animal figures and a beast/monster at the summit (a crowned, winged creature) [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** The wheel of **destiny / fickle Fortune** — fate as the Egyptian priests taught it [@web_villarevak_gebelin].
- **Changed from parent:** Essentially unchanged in meaning; **note for accuracy** — the later Sphinx-couchant / Typhon-descending / Hermanubis-rising figures belong to **Lévi and Waite's** reconstructions, **not** Gébelin's plate, which keeps the Marseille's nondescript beasts. Do not import them into the 1781 reading [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Sources:** [@web_villarevak_gebelin], [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 11 — Strength (La Force)
- **Depicts:** A woman calmly closing/parting a lion's jaws [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Fortitude** — the second of the four cardinal Virtues among the sacred cards [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** Image and meaning essentially as in the Marseille; the change is its enrolment in Gébelin's four-Virtues scheme [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Sources:** [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** high

## 12 — The Hanged Man (Le Pendu)
- **Depicts:** A youth suspended by one foot from a frame — but in the plate reproduced **inverted** (right-side-up) relative to the Marseille [@web_researchgate_prudence; @web_alamy_gebelin].
- **Gébelin's reading:** His most famous "correction": Gébelin argues the card was **printed upside-down by ignorant card-makers**, and that rightly turned it shows **Prudence** — a man lifting one foot to test the ground before he sets it down, supplying the **fourth cardinal Virtue** otherwise missing from the pack [@web_researchgate_prudence; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Changed from parent:** A genuine **visual** change, not just an interpretive one: the figure is flipped so that punishment/suspension becomes **deliberation/Prudence**. This is the clearest case of a plate "corrected" to fit the thesis [@web_researchgate_prudence].
- **Sources:** [@web_researchgate_prudence], [@web_robertplace_firstoccult], [@web_alamy_gebelin]
- **Confidence:** high

## 13 — Death (La Mort)
- **Depicts:** A skeleton with a scythe among the fallen — the card left **unnamed** in the Marseille [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Death** — the great passage. Gébelin treats Death as feminine and notes the number **thirteen** as unlucky; he reads the Marseille's leaving the card nameless as superstitious dread [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's deliberately **anonymous** trump XIII is named outright as Death and folded into an Egyptian discourse on mortality and the unlucky number [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Sources:** [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 14 — Temperance (Tempérance)
- **Depicts:** A winged figure pouring liquid between two vessels [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Temperance** — the third cardinal Virtue; as traditionally, she dilutes/mixes (pouring water into wine to make it less potent) [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** Unchanged in image and meaning; enrolled in the four-Virtues scheme [@web_benebell_lovers].
- **Sources:** [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** high

## 15 — The Devil (Le Diable)
- **Depicts:** A horned, winged figure over two bound captives [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Typhon** — "Brother of Osiris and Isis, the evil Principle, the great Demon of Hell"; the destroyer of Egyptian theology set against Osiris [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_villarevak_gebelin].
- **Changed from parent:** The Christian **Devil** is swapped for the Egyptian **Typhon (Set)**, completing the Osiris/Isis/Typhon triad and de-Christianising the card's evil principle [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis], [@web_villarevak_gebelin]
- **Confidence:** high

## 16 — The Tower (La Maison-Dieu)
- **Depicts:** Figures fall from a tower struck by fire from the sky [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **"Maison-Dieu, ou Château de Plutus"** — the House of God / **Castle of Plutus** (god of wealth): the temple of pride or avarice struck down, calamity and the fall of riches [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's *Maison-Dieu* is glossed as the **Castle of Plutus / fall of avarice**, an allegory of toppled wealth rather than a literal lightning-struck tower [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis]
- **Confidence:** high

## 17 — The Star (L'Étoile)
- **Depicts:** A naked woman kneeling, pouring two urns onto land and water, beneath one great star and seven small ones [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** His signature reading: **"wholly Egyptian."** The great star is **La Canicule — the Dog-Star, Sirius (Sothis)**, whose heliacal rising heralds the **flooding of the Nile**; the kneeling woman is **Isis** (Sovereign of the Skies / Isis-Sothis) pouring out the inundation that renews Egypt. One of his three Isiac cards [@web_traditionaltarot_star; @web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's generic star-and-bathing-woman becomes a precise **Egyptian astronomical calendar image** — Sirius + Nile flood + Isis — the most fully "Egyptianised" card in the essay [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_star], [@web_traditionaltarot_isis]
- **Confidence:** high

## 18 — The Moon (La Lune)
- **Depicts:** A moon dripping drops over two dogs and a crayfish between two towers [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** Egyptian through and through: the falling drops are the **tears of Isis**; the scene depicts the **Nile inundation**; the **two dogs** are the Egyptians' guardians of the tropics, set to keep the stars from wandering; the **crayfish** is the zodiac sign **Cancer**, referring to the Nile's floods. The third of his Isiac cards [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_wopc_mondeprimitif].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's eerie moonscape is decoded as an **Egyptian astronomical/agricultural** allegory (tropics-guardians, Cancer, the flood) rather than a scene of night-fears [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis], [@web_wopc_mondeprimitif]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 19 — The Sun (Le Soleil)
- **Depicts:** A radiant sun, its rays dropping, over two children by a wall [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** The **Sun** as source of Egyptian life; Gébelin calls the falling drops **"tears of gold and of pearl,"** and reads the two children gambolling/running hand in hand below the enclosing wall as a scene of generation/innocence under the sun [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_villarevak_gebelin].
- **Changed from parent:** Image as in the Marseille; the gloss makes it an **Egyptian solar** emblem (and supplies the "tears of gold and pearl" phrase later writers reused) [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis], [@web_villarevak_gebelin]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 20 — Judgement (Le Jugement)
- **Depicts:** An angel trumpets while the dead rise from a tomb [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** He argues the card "misnamed The Last Judgement" actually shows the **Creation of mankind**, not the reanimation of the dead — an Egyptian cosmogonic origin rather than a Christian eschatology [@web_robertplace_firstoccult; @web_benebell_lovers].
- **Changed from parent:** The Christian **Last Judgement** is flipped into its opposite — a scene of **Creation** — to fit an Egyptian-cosmogony reading [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Sources:** [@web_robertplace_firstoccult], [@web_benebell_lovers]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 21 — The World (Le Monde)
- **Depicts:** A dancing figure in a wreath/mandorla, the four beasts in the corners [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Gébelin's reading:** **Time, or the Universe** — misnamed "the World." The central figure is the **Goddess Isis** standing in an **oval/egg of Time**, the wreath being the wreath of Time; the four corner beasts are the **four seasons** (commonly: eagle=Spring, lion=Summer, ox=Autumn, the youth=Winter) — the whole created world/cycle of the year [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_wopc_mondeprimitif].
- **Mellet's reading:** Mellet opens his reverse-order **Golden Age** here at Trump XXI — Isis at the centre of the egg of time, the four seasons in the corners — the first card of his Egyptian cosmogony [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Changed from parent:** The Marseille's *Monde* (a dancer in a mandorla with the four evangelist-beasts) is re-identified as **Isis / Time / the Universe**, the four Christian living-creatures becoming the **four seasons** — the capstone of the Egyptian reading and Mellet's Golden-Age starting point [@web_traditionaltarot_isis].
- **Sources:** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis], [@web_wopc_mondeprimitif], [@web_robertplace_firstoccult]
- **Confidence:** high

## Suit note (the four suits)
Gébelin treats the four suits as mirroring the **four classes/conditions of society** (the
conventional European reading: cups→clergy, swords→nobility, coins→merchants, batons→peasantry),
folded into his Egyptian frame; **Mellet** describes the minor suits in detail and uses them in
his divination method. The plates reproduce the Marseille suit signs unchanged
[@web_robertplace_firstoccult; @web_tarotforum_mellet].
- **Confidence:** medium
