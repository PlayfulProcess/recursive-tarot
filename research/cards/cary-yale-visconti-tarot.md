# Cary-Yale Tarot (Visconti di Modrone) — card by card

**Source of imagery:** the **67 surviving hand-painted cards** in the Cary Collection,
Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library, Yale, traditionally attributed to the **workshop of
Bonifacio Bembo** (Zavattari proposed as the rival hand) [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights;
@web_bembo_wp; @web_vs_wp; @kaplan_caryyale_1984]. The surviving 67 = **11 trumps** (8 standard
triumphs + the 3 theological virtues) + **17 court cards** + **39 pips**
[@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone].

> **Game, not divination.** These are *carte da trionfi* — pieces of a trick-taking court game,
> not a cartomantic instrument [@dummett1980]. The blocks below give **iconography** and the
> **triumph/figure** each card represents. Any upright/reversed "meaning" is a later (18th–19th c.)
> overlay this deck does not natively carry, so it is not asserted here. The **"Changed from
> parent"** line is omitted: the Cary-Yale is a **root deck** with no tarot predecessor to diff
> against.

> **Note on the six-rank courts.** Each suit's court is built from **six** ranks — King, Queen,
> (male) Knight, **Horsewoman** (female Knight / "Lady on horseback"), **Knave** (male Page), and
> **Damsel** (female Page / Maid). Only **17** of the 24 reconstructed court cards survive, so the
> suits below carry an **uneven** set of ranks — this reflects survival, not the original design
> [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].

## Trumps (the *trionfi*) — 11 surviving

## 1 — The Emperor (L'Imperatore)
- **Depicts:** An enthroned, bearded emperor in gold brocade, holding an orb, the imperial eagle displayed, attendants at his feet [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** Temporal authority — the masculine throne the procession of triumphs serves; dynastic propaganda in gold for the Visconti house [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]
- **Confidence:** high

## 2 — The Empress (L'Imperatrice)
- **Depicts:** A crowned empress enthroned in brocade, bearing a shield and sceptre, ladies in attendance [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** Sovereign authority in its feminine register — part of the deck's marked attention to courtly women (the same impulse behind its female knights and pages) [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]
- **Confidence:** high

## 3 — Love / The Lovers (L'Amore)
- **Depicts:** A couple beneath a draped pavilion clasp hands while a blindfolded Cupid hovers above on a column; a dog and attendants frame the scene [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** Union as dynastic alliance — among the most celebrated surviving cards, **very probably a portrait of the 1441 Visconti-Sforza wedding** the deck is thought to honour (a hypothesis, not documented fact) [@web_th_modrone_meneghello; @web_cary_yale]. (confidence: medium for the wedding identification)
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@web_th_modrone_meneghello], [@web_cary_yale]
- **Confidence:** high (image); medium (wedding reading)

## 4 — The Chariot (Il Carro)
- **Depicts:** A crowned noblewoman rides a canopied triumphal car drawn by two white horses, attendants alongside [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** The **triumph** that names the game (*trionfo*); here driven by a **woman**, in keeping with the deck's foregrounding of female figures [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]
- **Confidence:** high

## 5 — Fortitude / Strength (La Fortezza)
- **Depicts:** A figure in courtly dress overpowers a lion, subduing or wrenching open its jaws — the Herculean image of fortitude [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** The **cardinal virtue** Fortitude; in the Cary-Yale the cardinal virtues sit alongside the rare **theological** virtues, an unusually complete moral programme [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone]
- **Confidence:** high

## 6 — Death (La Morte)
- **Depicts:** A skeletal figure (mounted or standing) with a great bow, riding across the gold over fallen bodies [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** The great leveller — the end that rides through every estate, given the dignity of full gold-ground treatment rather than a grotesque [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]
- **Confidence:** high

## 7 — Judgement (Il Giudizio)
- **Depicts:** Trumpeting angels above; below, the dead rise from their tombs at the summons, hands lifted [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** The Last Judgement — the most overtly Christian of the triumphs, a natural companion to the deck's theological virtues [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone]
- **Confidence:** high

## 8 — The World (Il Mondo)
- **Depicts:** A crowned figure (Fame, or a lady) presides above a landscape with a walled city, knights, and a ship — the world spread out to be surveyed and won [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **Represents:** The highest triumph; here a **panorama of earthly glory** rather than the later cosmic wreath — fitting for a dynastic deck [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights], [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]
- **Confidence:** high

## 9 — Faith (La Fede)
- **Depicts:** A woman holding a chalice and cross, sometimes with a kneeling figure — the personification of Faith (Fides) [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Represents:** The first **theological virtue**, found in **no other tarot** but the much later Minchiate; evidence the deck was conceived as a complete moral cosmos (four cardinal + three theological) [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @web_th_modrone_meneghello].
- **Sources:** [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone], [@web_th_modrone_meneghello]
- **Confidence:** high

## 10 — Hope (La Speranza)
- **Depicts:** A woman gazing upward with hands clasped in prayer, often with an **anchor** — the personification of Hope (Spes) [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Represents:** The second **theological virtue**; the anchor is Hope's ancient attribute, joining the tarot only here [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Sources:** [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone], [@web_th_modrone_meneghello]
- **Confidence:** high

## 11 — Charity (La Carità)
- **Depicts:** A woman nurses or tends children, sometimes with a flame — the personification of Charity (Caritas), the classic Caritas image carried over from devotional painting [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Represents:** The third and **greatest** theological virtue, named by tradition the chief of the three; unique (with the other two) to this deck among early tarots [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @web_th_modrone_meneghello].
- **Sources:** [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone], [@web_th_modrone_meneghello]
- **Confidence:** high

## Suit of Coins (Denari, Earth) — 9 pips + 5 surviving courts

The pip cards are **decorative**, not scenic: gold-and-colour suit-emblems on a tooled gold
ground. The **Coin pips reproduce the gold florin of Filippo Maria Visconti**, which is the basis
of Berti's c. 1442–1447 dating [@web_th_modrone_meneghello; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].

## 12 — Ace of Coins
- **Depicts:** A single gold coin-emblem with scrolling decoration on a tooled gold ground [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Represents:** The root of the suit of Coins (Earth) — money, body, provision; a game pip [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **Sources:** [@kaplan_caryyale_1984], [@web_th_modrone_meneghello]
- **Confidence:** high
