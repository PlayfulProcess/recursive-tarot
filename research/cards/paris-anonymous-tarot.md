# Anonymous Tarot de Paris — card-by-card

**Source of imagery:** the single BnF pack, `ark:/12148/btv1b105109624`, via the public-domain
Gallica scan [@web_gallica_paris], described here through secondary iconographic accounts
(Pollett; Tarot Heritage; WOPC; Arnell Ando) since WebFetch was blocked this pass.
**This is a game deck — no native upright/reversed meanings.** Each "Changed from…" line
measures the picture against the *later* Tarot de Marseille standard, purely as a familiar
point of comparison; the Paris deck has no Marseille parent. Captions are given in the deck's
own broken period spelling where reported. **Trumps first (the iconographically distinctive
part), then the four suits in brief.**

---

## I — Le Bateleur (the conjurer)
- **Depicts:** a mountebank/conjurer at his table — but here **wearing ass's ears**, playing a
  game with **bearded grown men** (not the children of some early Italian versions); under the
  table a **monkey scratches a dog's back** [@web_pollett_paris; @web_spiritone_paris].
- **Changed from the Marseille standard:** the Marseille Bateleur is a lone tradesman behind
  his cups-and-balls table; the Paris card turns him into a **satirical gaming scene** with
  ass's ears and animals — a comic, carnival reading absent from Marseille [@web_pollett_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris], [@web_spiritone_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## II — La Papesse
- **Depicts:** the Popess holding **her book away from her body, at her fingertips**, as though
  it were something distasteful she dislikes to touch [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** Marseille's Popess cradles book and reads calmly; the Paris
  gesture is **pointedly aversive**, an editorial flourish unique to this deck.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris], [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## III — L'Impératrice
- **Depicts:** an enthroned empress with regalia (shield/sceptre), in the broad trump line.
  (Detail thin in secondary sources.) [@web_pollett_paris]
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable subject; specific staging not securely
  described here.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## IIII — L'Empereur (captioned *LAN PEREUT*)
- **Depicts:** the Emperor in **full plate armour**, reading almost like an over-armoured
  suit-card King [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille Emperor sits in robes with an eagle shield; the
  Paris Emperor is **militarised, mailed head-to-foot** [@web_pollett_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## V — Le Pape
- **Depicts:** the Pope/Hierophant enthroned with tiara and the gesture of benediction, in
  the standard subject. (Secondary detail thin.) [@web_pollett_paris]
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable; staging not securely described here.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## VI — L'Amoureux (the Lovers)
- **Depicts:** a couple — **the elder/parent figure who usually accompanies them is absent**,
  and (per secondary reports) no overhead Cupid of the Marseille type [@web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille "L'Amoureux" stages a choice between figures under
  a shooting Cupid; the Paris card **strips out the elder figure**, leaving the pair
  [@web_th_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## VII — Le Chariot
- **Depicts:** a triumphant figure with **laurel crown and sceptre** in a car drawn **not by
  horses but by geese (or swans)** — one goose **ridden by a child** [@web_th_paris;
  @web_pollett_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** Marseille's Chariot is horse-drawn (two steeds); the Paris
  **goose-drawn chariot with a child rider** is one of the deck's most famous oddities
  [@web_th_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris], [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium-high.

## VIII — La Justice
- **Depicts:** Justice with scales/sword — but with **two faces, a woman and a bearded man**,
  on the one figure [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** Marseille's Justice is a single seated woman; the Paris card's
  **double (Janus-like) face** is unique [@web_pollett_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## VIIII — L'Hermite (captioned *LER MITE*)
- **Depicts:** the hooded old hermit with a light/lantern, in the standard subject; the broken
  caption *LER MITE* is itself a noted feature [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable figure; the **garbled caption** is the
  distinctive note.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low-medium.

## X — La Roue de Fortune
- **Depicts:** the wheel with figures rising and falling, in the standard subject. (Specific
  Paris staging not securely described in the snippets seen.) [@web_pollett_paris]
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable; details owed from the scan.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## XI — La Force
- **Depicts:** Strength — reported "**without any noticeable detail**" beyond the standard
  subject (figure subduing a beast / breaking a column tradition) [@web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** unremarkable relative to the deck's wilder cards; staging owed.
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## XII — Le Pendu (the Hanged Man)
- **Depicts:** the man hung by one foot — reported "**without any noticeable detail**"
  [@web_th_paris; @web_pollett_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** comparable to the standard inverted hanging figure; nothing
  exotic noted.
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** low-medium.

## XIII — La Mort (Death)
- **Depicts:** Death (skeleton with scythe). Reported distinguishing feature: it **lacks the
  scattered severed heads of kings and popes** seen in some other traditions
  [@web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille XIII reaps a field of hands/heads/crowns; the
  Paris Death **omits the royal/papal heads** [@web_th_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## XIIII — Tempérance (captioned *A TREMPANCE*)
- **Depicts:** Temperance pouring between two vessels, in the standard subject; the broken
  caption *A TREMPANCE* is the noted feature [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable figure (angel/woman pouring); distinctive
  for the **garbled caption**.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low-medium.

## XV — Le Diable (the Devil)
- **Depicts:** a Devil — secondary descriptions report a **Devil with drums before a
  hell-mouth**, but this overlaps the description given for trump XVI *La Foudre*; the two may
  be **conflated** in the snippet sources [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** Marseille's Devil stands on a pedestal with two bound imps; the
  Paris Devil scene is uncertain here — **disambiguate from the scan**.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris], [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## XVI — La Foudre ("the Lightning"; the Tower)
- **Depicts:** **not a struck tower but a hell-mouth** — a confused mass of demons and cowering
  people swirling toward the black jaws of Hell at the card's edge; titled *LA FOUDRE*
  ("the lightning"), not *La Maison-Dieu* [@web_pollett_paris; @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** Marseille's XVI is a tower losing its crown to a lightning-bolt
  with two falling figures; the Paris card replaces it with a **Hell-mouth Last-Judgment
  scene** and keeps the older "Foudre/Lightning" title rather than "Maison-Dieu"
  [@web_th_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris], [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## XVII — L'Étoile (captioned *LES TOILLES*)
- **Depicts:** **an astronomer/astrologer seated at a writing desk, holding calipers
  (dividers) up to the sky** — not the kneeling water-pourer of the later Marseille
  [@web_th_paris; @web_pollett_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille Star is a nude woman pouring two ewers beneath
  stars; the Paris Star is **a scholar measuring the heavens** — a wholly different scene
  [@web_th_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris], [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium-high.

## XVIII — La Lune (the Moon)
- **Depicts:** **a man with a harp serenading a naked woman on her balcony** beneath the moon —
  a courtship scene [@web_pollett_paris; @web_spiritone_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille Moon has two dogs/towers and a crayfish in a pool;
  the Paris Moon is **a romantic serenade**, unrelated to the Marseille emblem
  [@web_pollett_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris], [@web_spiritone_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## XVIIII — Le Soleil (the Sun)
- **Depicts:** **a long-haired woman looking into a mirror held up by a monkey**, with what
  appears to be an **Ace of Batons** behind her, under the sun [@web_pollett_paris;
  @web_th_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille Sun shows two children (or a child on a horse)
  beneath a rayed sun; the Paris Sun is **a vanity/mirror scene with a monkey** — entirely
  different [@web_pollett_paris].
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.

## XX — Le Jugement (Judgement)
- **Depicts:** the Last-Judgement subject — an angel/trumpet with the dead rising. (Secondary
  detail thin for the Paris staging.) [@web_pollett_paris]
- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable subject; specifics owed from the scan.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## XXI — Le Monde (the World)
- **Depicts:** the World — the standard subject of a figure within a mandorla/wreath with the
  four living creatures (man, lion, ox, eagle) at the corners; the Paris staging is reported
  to follow the imported "naked figure in a vesica" type rather than the Marseille dancer
  [@web_th_paris; @web_pollett_paris].
- **Changed from Marseille:** the Marseille World is a dancer in an oval wreath flanked by the
  four beasts; the Paris card hews to the **older mandorla/four-evangelist-beasts** type
  [@web_th_paris]. (Confidence: medium — exact Paris figure not securely described here.)
- **Sources:** [@web_th_paris], [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low-medium.

## — Le Mat (the Fool, unnumbered)
- **Depicts:** the wandering fool/madman, unnumbered, standing outside the trump count, in the

- **Changed from Marseille:** broadly comparable vagabond figure; specific Paris detail owed.
- **Sources:** [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** low.

## The four suits (Italian/Latin suits) — in brief

The 56 suit cards run **Ace–Ten + Valet, Cavalier, Reine, Roi** in each of **Deniers (Coins),
Coupes (Cups), Bâtons (Batons), Épées (Swords)** [@web_gallica_paris; @web_wopc_paris]. They
are **non-divinatory game cards**; only their distinctive design is noted here.

- **The four Aces are figural and unique:** each shows an **animal bearing a flag** with its
  suit sign — reported as a **lion, a deer/stag, a griffon and a unicorn** across the four
  suits (the exact animal↔suit pairing varies between secondary accounts; **owed from the
  scan**) [@web_wopc_paris; @web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium on the device, low on the
  pairing.
- **Deniers (Coins):** the pips carry **emblems of French cities** — a striking localising
  touch absent from the Marseille's plain repeating coins [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:**
  medium.
- **Coupes (Cups):** **each cup is an individually different vessel**, not a repeated stock
  shape [@web_th_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.
- **Bâtons (Batons) & Épées (Swords):** drawn so the **batons and swords intersect / cross
  one another**, in neither the straight-parallel Italian nor the curved Spanish manner; an
  "active," interlaced look [@web_th_paris; @web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.
- **Courts:** Valet, Cavalier, Reine, Roi per suit; the **trompe-l'œil folded-edge border**
  runs across the pack [@web_pollett_paris]. **Confidence:** medium.
