# Visconti-Sforza Tarot — Card by Card

**Source of imagery:** the **Pierpont-Morgan–Bergamo (Colleoni-Baglioni)** pack, c. 1451,
attributed to the **workshop of Bonifacio Bembo** (Zavattari proposed as a rival hand)
[@moakley1966; @web_met_bembo; @web_vs_wp]. Tempera and tooled gold leaf; the cards bear **no
numbers, no titles, no reversals, no astrology** — they are a **game** pack. Each block below
reads the **iconography** and the **Petrarchan triumph** the trump represents (per Moakley), not a
fortune-telling meaning, which this deck does not natively have [@moakley1966; @dummett1980;
@web_moakley_magus].

**Composite pack — flags used below:**
- **Original hand (Bembo workshop):** painted c. 1451.
- **Later-hand replacement:** one of the **six trumps repainted c. 1465–80** by a second,
  stylistically distinct hand — **Fortitude, Temperance, Star, Moon, Sun, World** — to replace
  lost/damaged originals (popularly, but doubtfully, attributed to Antonio Cicognara; the
  attribution rests on a discredited 19th-c. forgery) [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_morgan_vs_ms].
- **Lost:** four cards never recovered — **The Devil, The Tower, the Knight of Coins, the Three of
  Swords** [@web_greer_devil; @web_usgames_vs].

The trump numbering follows the conventional C-order reconstruction; the originals were unnumbered
[@dummett1980; @web_vs_wp].

## The Trumps (Trionfi)

## 0 — The Fool (Il Matto)
- **Depicts:** A ragged man in a tattered tunic, bare-legged, with feathers standing in his hair
  and a club over his shoulder — the village madman, painted in the same gold as kings. In
  Moakley's reading he stands outside the count as the personification of **Lent** answering the
  Carnival King [@moakley1966; @web_moakley_magus]. Survives (Morgan portion).
- **Triumph represented:** The wild card outside the procession — innocence and folly walking at
  the edge of the parade of triumphs.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_morgan_vs_ms], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 1 — The Magician / Mountebank (Il Bagatto / Il Bagatella)
- **Depicts:** A young man seated at a table set with small objects, a wand or knife in hand — a
  market conjuror or sleight-of-hand trickster. *Bagatella* means "a trifle"; he is the lowest
  triumph, the everyman who opens the parade. Moakley identifies him as the **Carnival King**
  himself [@moakley1966; @web_moakley_magus].
- **Triumph represented:** The opening figure of the triumphal procession — the worldly, ordinary
  human at the bottom of the ascent.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 2 — The Popess (La Papessa)
- **Depicts:** A crowned woman in the **brown habit of a nun** (with a knotted cord belt), a
  triple papal tiara on her head and a book in her lap. She is widely identified as **Sister
  Maifreda da Pirovano**, a Visconti kinswoman elected "popess" by the heretical **Guglielmite**
  sect and burned at the stake in Milan in **1300** [@web_th_vs_popess; @web_greer_maifreda].
- **Triumph represented:** Spiritual/feminine authority — the most genealogically pointed image in
  the deck, a buried family memory rendered in gold.
- **Note:** The identification is **debated** — the figure wears a Franciscan Poor Clare habit,
  not the white Umiliati habit Maifreda would have worn (confidence: medium for the Maifreda
  identification specifically) [@web_th_vs_popess].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_vs_popess], [@web_greer_maifreda], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high (the card), medium (the Maifreda reading).

## 3 — The Empress (L'Imperatrice)
- **Depicts:** A crowned, enthroned woman bearing a shield emblazoned with the **imperial eagle**
  of the Visconti — worldly feminine sovereignty wedded to the dynasty's heraldry.
- **Triumph represented:** Earthly female rule — the house made flesh.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 4 — The Emperor (L'Imperatore)
- **Depicts:** A bearded sovereign enthroned, the **imperial eagle** on his shield, orb or sceptre
  in hand — temporal masculine power, a portrait of dynastic legitimacy.
- **Triumph represented:** Earthly male rule, answering the Empress.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 5 — The Pope (Il Papa)
- **Depicts:** A pope in the triple tiara, hand raised in blessing, the crossed keys of Saint
  Peter near him — spiritual authority answering the temporal Emperor.
- **Triumph represented:** The sanctioned hierophant of the established Church, the spiritual
  counterpart to the Emperor's worldly power.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 6 — The Lovers (L'Amore)
- **Depicts:** A couple stand hand in hand beneath a canopy while a **blindfolded Cupid** takes
  aim from a pedestal above. Often read as a **Visconti-Sforza marriage portrait** (possibly
  Francesco Sforza and Bianca Maria Visconti) [@moakley1966; @web_th_vs_visit].
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Love** — here, love as dynastic alliance.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_th_vs_visit].
- **Confidence:** high (Love-triumph); medium (the specific marriage identification).

## 7 — The Chariot (Il Carro)
- **Depicts:** A crowned lady stands in a triumphal car (sometimes drawn by winged horses),
  holding orb and sceptre — the Roman-style **trionfo**, the victory parade from which the very
  word "trumps" descends.
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Fame/Chastity** procession — victory carried
  forward in parade; the emblematic "triumph" of the whole game.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_moakley_magus].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 8 — Justice (La Giustizia)
- **Depicts:** An enthroned woman holding an upraised **sword** and a **balance**; above her a
  small armoured **knight rides** across the gold — a Visconti flourish. In this pre-occult C-order
  Justice sits at **VIII** (the Golden Dawn would much later swap it with Strength).
- **Triumph represented:** The cardinal virtue Justice — measure and reckoning in the procession of
  virtues.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 9 — The Hermit / Time (Il Vecchio / Il Tempo)
- **Depicts:** A stooped, long-bearded old man holding aloft not a lantern but an **hourglass**,
  wrapped in heavy robes. Originally **Time (Il Tempo)** — the Old Man — not yet the lamp-bearing
  Hermit of later decks.
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Time** — age and the slow accounting of hours
  that no triumph escapes.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_moakley_magus].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 10 — Wheel of Fortune (La Ruota della Fortuna)
- **Depicts:** A blindfolded **Fortuna** turns a great wheel; four figures cling to it, some
  sprouting asses' ears and tails as they rise and fall, mottoes streaming from their mouths —
  the medieval *regnabo / regno / regnavi / sum sine regno* ("I shall reign / I reign / I have
  reigned / I am without reign").
- **Triumph represented:** Fortune's turning — the one law that levels emperor and fool alike.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 11 — Strength / Fortitude (La Fortezza) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** A standing figure subdues a **lion** in a Hercules-like display of mastery.
- **Triumph represented:** The cardinal virtue Fortitude — courage that masters the beast by
  steady will.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps**, repainted by a stylistically distinct second
  hand c. 1465–80 (popularly attributed to Antonio Cicognara, but that attribution is rejected by
  modern scholarship) [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_morgan_vs_ms].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@web_morgan_vs_ms], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 12 — The Hanged Man / The Traitor (Il Traditore / L'Appeso)
- **Depicts:** A youth hangs **upside down by one ankle** from a wooden frame, hands bound behind
  his back, the free leg crossed — a ***pittura infamante***, the shaming portrait Italian cities
  painted of traitors and debtors. The sacrificial reading is a much later occult overlay.
- **Triumph represented:** The traitor's punishment — the world seen inverted; in the procession,
  the shamed man hung up for public scorn.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_wopc_vs1460].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 13 — Death (La Morte)
- **Depicts:** A gaunt **skeletal figure** on foot, drawing a great **bow** (or bearing a scythe)
  across barren ground. Unnamed and unnumbered in the original — the card too plain to need a
  label.
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Death** — the great equaliser of the *danse
  macabre*, making no exception for rank or gold.
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_moakley_magus].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 14 — Temperance (La Temperanza) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** A **winged woman pours liquid between two vessels** in an unbroken stream — one of
  the oldest stable tarot images.
- **Triumph represented:** The cardinal virtue Temperance — moderation as a living art, mixing
  without spilling.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps** (second hand, c. 1480); the Morgan catalogues
  its Temperance among the ca.-1480 illuminations [@web_morgan_vs_ms; @web_th_vs_replacements].
- **Sources:** [@web_morgan_vs_ms], [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 15 — The Devil (Il Diavolo) — *LOST*
- **Depicts:** **No Bembo card survives.** One of the four cards never recovered. It is genuinely
  debated whether Bembo's workshop **ever painted a Devil** for this pack — no early Milanese
  example survives [@web_greer_devil]. Later decks in the tradition show a horned, clawed figure
  over bound captives; the Visconti-Sforza original (if it existed) is simply gone.
- **Triumph represented:** In the standard trump sequence, the infernal power between the virtues
  and the celestial cards — here present only as an absence.
- **Sources:** [@web_greer_devil], [@web_usgames_vs], [@web_vs_wp].
- **Confidence:** high (that it is missing); medium (whether it ever existed).

## 16 — The Tower (La Torre / La Casa del Diavolo) — *LOST*
- **Depicts:** **No Bembo card survives.** The second permanently lost trump, and likewise subject
  to the question of whether it was ever painted for this pack [@web_greer_devil]. In the broader
  tradition the Tower — "the House of the Devil," or "the Fire / House of God" — shows a struck
  tower and falling figures.
- **Triumph represented:** Sudden collapse / the breaking of false structures — present in this
  deck only as a gap.
- **Sources:** [@web_greer_devil], [@web_usgames_vs], [@web_vs_wp].
- **Confidence:** high (that it is missing); medium (whether it ever existed).

## 17 — The Star (La Stella) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** A woman stands holding aloft a single great **eight-pointed star** against the gold
  — a simple votive figure, far from the kneeling water-pourer of the Marseille and Waite decks.
- **Triumph represented:** Among the celestial triumphs (Star–Moon–Sun) that rise after Death —
  the fixed light to steer by.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps** (second hand, c. 1465–80)
  [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 18 — The Moon (La Luna) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** A robed woman holds up a **crescent moon**, serene — the eerie dogs-and-crayfish
  landscape of later Moon cards is a much later invention; here the Moon is simply held, like an
  attribute.
- **Triumph represented:** The celestial triumph of the Moon — the borrowed light governing tides
  and dreams.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps** (second hand) [@web_th_vs_replacements;
  @web_vs_wp].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 19 — The Sun (Il Sole) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** A **winged child (putto)** floats above a hilly landscape, lifting a radiant
  human-faced **sun** overhead — one of the most charming surviving compositions in the restored
  hand.
- **Triumph represented:** The celestial triumph of the Sun — vitality and the lit world.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps**; the Morgan catalogues its Sun among the
  ca.-1480 illuminations [@web_morgan_vs_ms; @web_th_vs_replacements].
- **Sources:** [@web_morgan_vs_ms], [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 20 — Judgement (Il Giudizio)
- **Depicts:** **God the Father with attendant angels** sounds the trumpet above; below, the dead
  rise naked from their tombs — a straightforward Last Judgement, the most explicitly Christian of
  the trumps.
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Eternity** approached through the Resurrection
  — the call and the reckoning.
- **Note:** **Original Bembo hand — *not* a replacement card.** (The repo grammar tags this as a
  "second hand" card; the corroborated list of six replacements does **not** include Judgement.
  See the deck dossier's corrections.) [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp].
- **Sources:** [@moakley1966], [@web_th_vs_replacements].
- **Confidence:** high.

## 21 — The World (Il Mondo) — *later-hand replacement*
- **Depicts:** Two winged **putti hold up a great orb** in which a walled city floats above water
  and hills, crowned by a star or a standing figure — the last and highest triumph.
- **Triumph represented:** Petrarch's **Triumph of Eternity** — completion, the cosmos held up to
  be seen, the triumph that overcomes all other triumphs.
- **Note:** **One of the six replacement trumps** (second hand), and the highest of them
  [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp].
- **Sources:** [@web_th_vs_replacements], [@moakley1966].
- **Confidence:** high.

## The Minor Arcana (by suit)

The four Latin suits run **Ace–Ten plus four courts (King, Queen, Knight/Cavallo, Page/Fante)** —
**56 cards, standard 4-court structure** (not the six-court Cary-Yale) [@web_vs_wp;
@web_wopc_vs1460]. The pips are **non-scenic**: the suit-emblems are arranged on a **punched gold
ground** with gold-leaf scrollwork and Visconti-Sforza heraldic flourishes; there is no narrative
picture (scenic pips are a later Sola-Busca / RWS development) [@web_wopc_vs1460; @web_th_vs_visit].
As a game pack these carry **no divinatory meanings** [@dummett1980].

## Coins (Denari) — Earth
- **Depicts:** Ace through Ten show the corresponding number of **coins/disks** laid out on tooled
  gold; the courts (King, Queen, Knight, Page) are richly dressed Milanese-court figures bearing a
  coin. The **Knight of Coins is LOST** — one of the deck's four missing cards [@web_usgames_vs;
  @web_greer_devil].
- **Sources:** [@web_wopc_vs1460], [@web_usgames_vs], [@web_th_vs_visit].
- **Confidence:** high.

## Cups (Coppe) — Water
- **Depicts:** Ace–Ten show the number of **chalices/cups**; the four courts hold a cup. All
  fourteen survive.
- **Sources:** [@web_wopc_vs1460], [@web_th_vs_visit].
- **Confidence:** high.

## Swords (Spade) — Air
- **Depicts:** Ace–Ten show **curved swords/blades** interlaced (the early Italian "spade" arc
  into a lattice on the higher pips); the four courts bear a sword. The **Three of Swords is
  LOST** — one of the deck's four missing cards [@web_usgames_vs; @web_greer_devil].
- **Sources:** [@web_wopc_vs1460], [@web_usgames_vs].
- **Confidence:** high.

## Batons (Bastoni) — Fire
- **Depicts:** Ace–Ten show **batons/staves** crossed or laid parallel; the four courts hold a
  baton. All fourteen survive.
- **Sources:** [@web_wopc_vs1460], [@web_th_vs_visit].
- **Confidence:** high.
