# Ganjifa — the eight Mughal suits

Ganjifa is a game, not an oracle: its cards carry **no native cartomantic meanings**, so the
card-by-card unit is the **suit**. The standard Mughal pack has **8 suits × 12 cards = 96**: each
suit runs 1–10 in pips plus two court cards — a **king** (*shah*) and a **minister** (*wazir*)
[@web_ganjifa_map], [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean]. The "Changed from parent" line is omitted: ganjifa
is a **cousin** of the tarot line with no `derives_from`; what matters is that it has *eight*
suits where the Latin pack has four, the structural mark of a separate branch.

The pack divides into two halves (*bhedas*) with **opposite pip ranking** — a "strong" half where
ten beats one, and a "weak" half where one beats ten [@web_ganjifa_map]. Suit names below follow
the Mughal pattern as given by the Ashmolean and MAP Academy [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean],
[@web_ganjifa_map].

## 1 — Taj (تاج · Crown)

- **Depicts:** A crown — the regalia suit.
- **Means:** None (game suit).
- **Branch note (vs. the four-suit Latin pack):** One of *eight* suits; no Latin-suit
  counterpart, illustrating ganjifa's expansion beyond the four-suit template.
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 2 — Safed (سفید · Silver Coin)

- **Depicts:** A silver coin (the "white" money suit).
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** A coin suit — the one suit-idea ganjifa *shares* with the Mamluk/Latin line,
  reflecting their common probable money-suited ancestry [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 3 — Shamsher (شمشیر · Sword / Sabre)

- **Depicts:** A curved sword or sabre.
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** A sword suit echoes the Mamluk/Latin *swords* — again a shared suit-idea,
  cousin not ancestor [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 4 — Ghulam (غلام · Servant)

- **Depicts:** A servant or slave.
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** No Latin counterpart; a social-rank suit peculiar to the ganjifa pattern.
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 5 — Chang (چنگ · Harp)

- **Depicts:** A small harp.
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** No Latin counterpart; in the *kambar* (weak) bheda of the standard ranking
  [@web_ganjifa_map].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 6 — Surkh (سرخ · Gold Coin)

- **Depicts:** A gold/red coin (*ashrafi*).
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** A second money suit — ganjifa carries *two* coin suits (silver and gold),
  unlike the single coins suit of the Latin pack [@web_ganjifa_map].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 7 — Barat (برات · Document / Draft)

- **Depicts:** A cheque, bill or draft (a written money-order).
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** A commercial-instrument suit with no Latin counterpart — a reminder that the
  ganjifa "economy" of suits is richer and more mercantile than the European four
  [@web_ganjifa_map].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

## 8 — Qimash (قماش · Merchandise / Textiles)

- **Depicts:** Material wealth, usually textiles/cloth goods.
- **Means:** None.
- **Branch note:** No Latin counterpart; completes the eight-suit set that distinguishes the
  Mughal pack from the four-suit European deck [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean].
- **Sources:** [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_map]
- **Confidence:** medium

### The court ranks (shared across all eight suits)

- **Wazir (وزیر · Minister):** the lower court card; "the minister" [@web_ganjifa_map].
- **Shah (شاه · King):** the higher court card, also called *padishah* or *mir*; the court cards
  of each suit are the strongest in play [@web_ganjifa_map].

### Hindu variant note — Dashavatara

The **Dashavatara** ganjifa replaces the 8 Mughal suits with **ten suits / 120 cards**, one suit
for each *avatar* of Vishnu — a thoroughly Indianised re-skinning of the same circular pack, and
further evidence of the tradition's independent development away from the European line
[@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_wiki]. (Dating of its creation varies in the literature;
confidence: low pending a primary source.)
