# Tarot de Marseille (Conver 1760)

## At a glance

The **canonical printed standard** of Western tarot — woodblock-and-stencil cards whose
22-trump sequence (C-order) became *the* reference layout `[Dummett1980] [Depaulis1984]`.
Nicolas Conver's 1760 Marseille edition is the most renowned single witness
`[Conver1760-obj] [Depaulis1984]`. Born a **game** deck, it is the exact pack the
18th-century occultists picked up and re-read as an Egyptian oracle `[CdG1781] [DDD1996]`.

## Origin & dating

The "Marseille" pattern stabilised in 17th–18th-century French and Swiss card-making
`[Dummett1980] [Depaulis1984]`. **Nicolas Conver** signed and printed his Marseille deck in
**1760** from engraved woodblocks, coloured by stencil `[Conver1760-obj] [Depaulis1984]`.
It was not the first Marseille-pattern deck — it follows earlier Marseille cardmakers such
as **Chosson** (the well-known Chosson deck bears the date **1736**, though that date is
itself debated in the scholarship) `[Depaulis1984] [DDD1996]`. The Conver woodblocks were
reused for over a century, and the pattern was later re-standardised for the modern era by
**Paul Marteau's** Grimaud *Ancien Tarot de Marseille* (c.1930), the prototype for most
20th-century French decks `[Marteau1949] [Depaulis1984]`.

## Provenance & evidence

Surviving Conver decks are held in public collections (e.g. the **Bibliothèque nationale
de France** and the **British Museum**, which hold Marseille-pattern cards and sheets), and
the Conver woodblocks are claimed by the successor **Camoin** firm `[BnF-obj] [BM-obj]
[Camoin-T]`. The lineage **Chosson (1736?) → Conver (1760) → Grimaud/Marteau (c.1930)** is
documented in the standard catalogues `[Depaulis1984] [Dummett1980]`.

## Game or divination?

**Game first.** The Tarot de Marseille was printed and sold for the trick-taking game
*tarot* — a living game still played in France — and the documentary record treats it as a
game pack for its entire early life `[DummettMcLeod2004] [Dummett1980]`. **But** this is the
hinge deck: because the Marseille was the common, available pattern in 1780s Paris, it is
the deck **Court de Gébelin** allegorised as an Egyptian "Book of Thoth" in 1781, and the
one **Etteilla** and his followers adapted into a divination system from the later 1780s
`[CdG1781] [Ett1785] [DDD1996]`. So the *object* is a game deck and the *occult tradition*
is a later use grafted onto it; on the documentary evidence, no divinatory reading of these
trumps is attested before 1781 `[Dummett1980] [DDD1996]`. **The grammar should label the
deck "game," and the divinatory meanings as a documented later overlay, not an origin**
`[Dummett1980] [DDD1996]`.

## The fear question

**No fear as a game** — the Tarot de Marseille was an ordinary, regulated commercial
product of the French card trade, subject to the state playing-card tax and its control
stamp `[Depaulis1984]`. The instructive twist comes later: by the 19th century the *same
physical pattern* could be innocent leisure in one hand and, in another, the working tool
of a prosecuted fortune-teller — prosecution that ran on vagrancy and anti-fraud law, not
on anything about the cards themselves (see the fear-question discussion and statutes cited
in `00-overview-history-of-tarot.mdx`) `[Dummett1980]`. The deck did not change; the **use
and the user's social position** did.

## Counter-voices

From **Éliphas Lévi** onward, esoteric writers treated the Marseille's "purity" as evidence
of preserved ancient (Egyptian/Kabbalistic) symbolism `[Lévi1856] [DDD1996]`. The
document-first reading is that the imagery is a worn, simplified descendant of the Italian
trionfi, and that its apparent "perfection" is **printing-tradition conservatism** — block
copied from block — rather than transmitted doctrine `[Dummett1980]`. That said, the
Marseille's role as the **canvas onto which modern esoteric tarot was built** is real and
historically central: it is the genuine bridge object between game and oracle `[DDD1996]`.

## References

**[P] Primary sources** (period texts and objects)

- `[CdG1781]` Antoine Court de Gébelin, *Le Monde primitif analysé et comparé avec le monde
  moderne*, vol. VIII (Paris, 1781) — the essay declaring the tarot an Egyptian Book of
  Thoth; describes the very Marseille-type cards he saw in play. *(public domain)*
- `[Ett1785]` Etteilla (Jean-Baptiste Alliette), *Manière de se récréer avec le jeu de
  cartes nommées tarots* (Paris, 1783–1785) — the first tarot-divination method-books.
  *(public domain)*
- `[Lévi1856]` Éliphas Lévi, *Dogme et rituel de la haute magie* (Paris, 1854–1856) — the
  19th-century source of the tarot–Kabbalah "ancient symbolism" claim. *(public domain)*
- `[Marteau1949]` Paul Marteau, *Le Tarot de Marseille* (Paris: Grimaud, 1949) — the
  standardiser's own account of the Grimaud pattern.
- `[Conver1760-obj]` The Nicolas Conver Tarot de Marseille, Marseille, 1760 — the deck
  itself (signed/dated exemplars in public collections).
- `[BnF-obj]` Bibliothèque nationale de France, Département des Estampes — Marseille-pattern
  tarot holdings (primary objects).
- `[BM-obj]` British Museum — Marseille-pattern playing-card sheets and cards (primary
  objects; see collection online).

**[S] Secondary scholarship that reads the primaries** (prioritised)

- `[Dummett1980]` Michael Dummett, *The Game of Tarot: From Ferrara to Salt Lake City*
  (London: Duckworth, 1980) — the foundational document-based history; establishes the
  game-first chronology and the A/B/C trump orders.
- `[DDD1996]` Ronald Decker, Thierry Depaulis & Michael Dummett, *A Wicked Pack of Cards:
  The Origins of the Occult Tarot* (London: Duckworth, 1996) — traces the 1781→19th-c.
  occult overlay through the primary French sources (de Gébelin, Etteilla, Lévi).
- `[Depaulis1984]` Thierry Depaulis, *Tarot, jeu et magie* (Paris: Bibliothèque nationale,
  1984) — exhibition catalogue grounded directly in the BnF's primary holdings (Marseille
  cardmakers, Conver, the tax stamps).
- `[DummettMcLeod2004]` Michael Dummett & John McLeod, *A History of Games Played with the
  Tarot Pack* (Lewiston: Edwin Mellen Press, 2004) — the definitive account of tarot as a
  *game*, including the French game still played today.
