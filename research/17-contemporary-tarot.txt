## The under-crediting of Pamela Colman Smith, and the broader pattern

**The core facts (high confidence).** The Rider–Waite deck — released in 1909, now the standard set with more than 100 million copies in circulation — was illustrated by Pamela Colman Smith ("Pixie"). Waite supplied concepts and the structure of the Major Arcana; she was paid a small one-time fee for many months of work and research, with no royalties. The naming itself encodes the erasure: the publisher was William Rider & Son and the commissioner was Waite, so the deck became "Rider–Waite," leaving the artist out entirely. Artnet: for more than a century, Smith went wholly uncredited for her contribution, her claim cemented only by her serpentine monogram embedded in every card. She died in 1951 destitute and was buried in an unmarked grave; the burial records were later destroyed in a fire, so the grave has never been located.

**Why her contribution mattered so much (high).** The single most consequential design choice was hers: before Smith, most decks showed the pip cards as simple arrangements of cups, swords, wands, or coins; she turned them into little narrative scenes — of struggle, joy, loss, and hope — and the Minor Arcana were left almost entirely to her. Those fully-illustrated pips are precisely what make the deck legible and teachable to beginners — arguably a structural cause of tarot's later mass adoption. (Important nuance, medium confidence: she was not absolutely first — the obscure 15th-century Sola-Busca deck illustrated all pips, and she borrowed from it — but she was first for a mass-market deck.)

**The broader pattern of shadowed deck artists (high).** Smith is the emblem, not the exception. The other pillar of modern tarot, Crowley's Thoth deck, was painted by **Lady Frieda Harris**, who spent five years painting and repainting the cards — The Magus alone redone seven times — and even paid Crowley a regular stipend for instruction, yet the deck is still routinely called "the Crowley deck." Her case is more complicated than Smith's: she insisted on her own anonymity on the cards, while also at one point exhibiting them publicly under her own name, which infuriated Crowley. Crowley did credit her, writing that she devoted her genius to the Work. Scholars now estimate the Thoth is the second most popular deck after the Waite–Smith — meaning the two most-used decks in the world were both visually realized by women whose names got subordinated to the male occultists who commissioned them. Behind them lies an even deeper layer of anonymity: the Tarot de Marseille pattern was the product of largely uncredited journeyman cardmakers, and Smith's own deck was pirated in the U.S. by L.W. de Laurence without giving her credit at all.

## The deeper historiography (this is what your hypothesis rests on)

The Dummett-school record is unusually clean here. Tarot crystallized as a card game in northern Italy by the 1440s, with trump images drawn from conventional medieval and Renaissance allegory; divinatory use was a late-18th-century invention. For roughly 340 years it was simply a game. The occult overlay can be dated precisely: in 1781 Antoine Court de Gébelin claimed in Le Monde Primitif that tarot derived from the Egyptian Book of Thoth — admitting he had no evidence — and this became the foundation of modern tarot mythology. Then Etteilla (Jean-Baptiste Alliette) became the first professional card reader and the first to design a deck solely for divination; all modern divinatory interpretations for the pip and court cards derive from his work. Dummett's own dating is blunt: it was only in the 1780s, after fortune-telling with ordinary playing cards had been established for two decades, that anyone used the tarot pack for cartomancy. After Etteilla came Lévi (Kabbalah), de Guaita (initiatory sequence), the Golden Dawn, Waite–Smith, and Crowley–Harris — each rewriting the meaning. And crucially, the trump order varied significantly across early decks, which undermines the idea of a single hidden initiatory sequence. The meaning was never even internally stable.

## Your hypothesis: "meaning attributed after the images, by many hands → an open license to create"

**Assessment: strongly supported on the history, and a genuinely good partial explanation — but not a complete one, and worth distinguishing from competing mechanisms.**

What the evidence vindicates: the images really did come first and stayed relatively fixed (the Marseille pattern, then Smith's scenes), while the *meaning* was a late, retroactive, multi-author overlay — de Gébelin → Mellet → Etteilla → Lévi → de Guaita → Golden Dawn → Waite/Smith → Crowley/Harris → today's deck-makers. No single author or institution ever held canonical authority over what the cards *mean*. That is exactly the structural condition that produces an open "license to create": when meaning is attributed rather than authored, and attributed by many competing hands, re-attribution is normal rather than heretical. The community itself describes it in these terms — re-drawing the cards as a powerful reclaiming, with tarot reflecting the democratization of the cards themselves, a toolkit for all to participate and see ourselves in. Smith's narrative pips amplify this: each card is a small, ambiguous scene that *demands* interpretation rather than dictating it, which is itself an invitation to the next maker.

Where I'd push back or refine, since you prefer the honest version:

1. **The mechanism is necessary-feeling but not sufficient.** Plenty of open, multiply-attributed symbol systems (runes, Lenormand, the I Ching) did *not* surge the way tarot has. The "open license" explains *why tarot is unusually generative once adopted*, but the post-2015 *timing* is better explained by the distribution layer — short-form video plus print-on-demand crowdfunding — which lowered the cost of both consuming and *producing* re-attributions to near zero. Your hypothesis describes the latent affordance; TikTok and Kickstarter are what cashed it in.

2. **Astrology is also openly re-attributable** (postcolonial astrology, queer astrology, Co-Star's tone) yet remains app-centralized and arguably *more* doctrinally rigid (fixed planetary correspondences). So "open license" isn't unique to tarot — but tarot's openness is unusually *visual and physical*: the unit of re-creation is a 78-image deck you can redraw and sell, not a paragraph. That material substrate may matter as much as the semantic openness.

3. **There's a sharper version of your thesis available.** It's not just that meaning came after the images; it's that the images were authored by *shadowed, decentralized, non-authoritative* hands (anonymous cardmakers; an uncredited Smith; a self-anonymizing Harris). The absence of a sacralized single author is itself part of the license. A tradition whose most influential artist died nameless in a pauper's grave is structurally hard to gatekeep — which connects to your own interest in gatekeeping: tarot is close to the pure "no effective gate" case, and its history shows both the generativity *and* the costs (uncredited labor, piracy, market saturation) of that openness.

So: I'd call the hypothesis **true as stated and explanatorily real, but incomplete** — it identifies the standing affordance (meaning-after-image, many hands, no canon) that makes tarot endlessly re-makeable, while the recent *surge* needs the distribution-and-production layer to explain its timing.

## "Is that all there is?" — the skew is real but underdetermined

The generic part is genuine. Pew's numbers show women and LGBT adults over-index on *both* astrology and tarot, so some of what you're seeing is just "marginalized-and-women-heavy spaces look marginalized-and-women-heavy." But the magnitude for tarot specifically is striking — 33% of LGBT adults consult tarot versus 11% of all adults, a 3× gap, and 15% do so at least monthly — and the *reasons* people give differ by group. LGBT adults are the most likely of any subgroup to say they do it for helpful insights rather than just for fun (29%), which already tells you it isn't only entertainment for these communities. So "is that all there is" — no. There are at least three deeper mechanisms, and the third is yours.

## Mechanism 1: occultism has been a *sanctuary of authority* for the excluded for ~175 years

This isn't new with TikTok. In the 19th century, spiritualism gave women public authority that institutional religion denied them: in a culture where women had limited routes to social power, they were drawn to spiritualism because it revered them for their ability to commune with the unseen, and because power was decentralized and there was no rigid hierarchy, anyone with the ability could stand before a crowd and lead — mediumship circumvented the structural barriers that excluded women from religious leadership. The occult revival movements — Spiritualism, Theosophy, and the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn were popular with feminists because they rejected the patriarchy of Christianity and sometimes worshipped a female deity. The Golden Dawn (founded 1888) admitted women as initiatory equals — which is how Pamela Colman Smith was in the room at all. The Lady Science piece on spiritualism is even titled "Vulgar Women, Queer Men, and Unruly Spirits," flagging that the séance was also a haven for gender-nonconforming men coded as "passive" — though that male-queer thread is thinner in the sources than the women's-authority thread. Confidence: high for women, medium for the explicitly queer-male dimension.

The structural point: tarot inherits a tradition whose *selling feature*, for the marginalized, is that it has no clergy, no ordination, no gatekeeper who can be a bishop telling you you don't belong. You authorize yourself. That's exactly what someone excluded from sanctioned institutions needs.

## Mechanism 2: "rejected knowledge" and kinship through shared abjection

This is the academic spine. The scholar Wouter Hanegraaff argues that the very category of "esotericism" is the product of a polemical process — knowledge constructed as antithetical to true religion, sound science, and Enlightenment rationality: literally "rejected knowledge." If your *self* has also been constructed as antithetical to the norm, there's an obvious affinity. Sydney Sheedy's 2022 ethnography of queer occult practitioners in Montréal names it precisely: queer practitioners claim kinship with the occult through a perceived shared abjection, deeming it an inherently queer resource for self- and community empowerment. The cards and the people share a status — cast out of the legitimate, surviving on the margins. That's not a metaphor the communities resist; it's one they actively claim. Confidence: high — this is the explicit self-description in both the scholarship and the popular reporting.

## Mechanism 3 — yours: the subjunctive / "as-if" / play structure

Here's where your thesis is strongest, because there's a whole ritual-theory literature that says exactly what you're saying, and you can cite it. Adam Seligman and colleagues, in *Ritual and Its Consequences: An Essay on the Limits of Sincerity* (Oxford, 2008), argue that ritual, like play, creates "as if" worlds — a subjunctive universe — rooted in the imaginative capacity of the mind, and that this subjunctive "third space" is similar to Winnicott's transitional space, a place where we create and share alternative realities and reflect on normative orders and hierarchies. Crucially, they oppose the subjunctive to *sincerity*: ritual imagines the world "as if," in opposition to the way practitioners know the real world to be — there is incongruity, not coherence, and they argue modernity has over-invested in sincerity (mean what you say, *be* what you are) "at the expense of ritual."

Now connect that to identity. The "sincere" mode — *you are, essentially and verifiably, this fixed thing* — is precisely the mode that has been weaponized against queer and trans people (the demand to have a stable, legible, normatively-readable self). The subjunctive mode lets you operate in the conditional: *as if*, provisionally, in play. You can lay a card and try on a self without being pinned to it. That maps directly onto Judith Butler's account of gender as performative — a repeated, stylized act rather than an inner essence — and onto camp (Sontag's "life as theater," the love of artifice and exaggeration) as a historically queer sensibility. Tarot is a machine for the subjunctive: an ambiguous image, laid down "as if" it speaks to your situation, interpreted by you. Pew's own headline finding — most people engage "just for fun" rather than from literal belief — is empirical evidence that the dominant register *is* the subjunctive, not the sincere. So your instinct that tarot has become "a play of theater, of ritual, of fiction" is not loose talk; it's the Seligman thesis, and the data support it.

## On "queer" and Tolkien — your instinct is half-wrong on the surface and right underneath

The factual correction first: "queer" is not Tolkien's, and not primarily a fiction word. It entered English around 1513 meaning "strange, odd, peculiar, eccentric", probably from Low German/German *quer*, "oblique, off-center," related to *quer* "oblique, perverse," from a Proto-Indo-European root meaning "to twist" (compare Latin *torquere* and English "thwart"). The same-sex sense is *late* — it began describing gay people in the late 19th century, with an 1894 letter by the Marquess of Queensberry as a canonical early citation — and the reclaimed, affirmative sense is later still: from the late 1980s, in the AIDS-crisis context, prominently by Queer Nation (1990) and alongside queer theory in the early '90s. Tolkien (writing 1937–1955) used "queer" constantly — Bilbo is "queer," the folk are "queer" — but in the *old* "strange/odd" sense that was still standard British usage. He's a famous recent user of the older meaning, which is exactly why a modern ear notices it; he didn't coin or own it.

But underneath, you're onto something real and better than the Tolkien claim. The etymological core of "queer" is **oblique** — off the straight line. The OED gives senses like "directed sideways, especially surreptitiously," and "(of opinion and behaviour) at odds with others". "Straight" is the literal antonym, and the hetero norm is, of course, "straight." So queerness *is* obliqueness-to-the-straight. And here's the payoff: divination is also oblique knowledge — it approaches the truth *sideways*, in the conditional, not head-on and declaratively. It's "rejected knowledge" that runs crosswise to sanctioned science and religion. So the single word "oblique" unifies three things at once: queer people (oblique to the norm), tarot's epistemic status (oblique knowledge), and the subjunctive "as-if" (an oblique, sideways relation to the real). That's a genuinely tight synthesis, and it's the defensible version of what you were reaching for with Tolkien.

One bonus, since you'll enjoy it: "queer" also carried a 19th-century cant sense of **counterfeit** — "the queer" was counterfeit money, and "to shove the queer" (1859) meant to pass counterfeit coin. So the word's semantic field already contains *trick*, *fake-but-operative*, *the not-quite-real-that-still-works* — which is uncannily close to your game → trick → play arc.

## Your game → trick → play thesis: would I stand by it?

Mostly yes, with sharpening and three caveats.

The arc is real and you can make each stage precise:

- **Game.** Tarot is literally a *trick-taking* card game (tarocchi) from the 1440s — so "game" and "trick" aren't even sequential at the origin; the game is *made of* tricks. Play in the competition-plus-chance register.
- **Trick (the con/fiction sense).** The occult turn supplies the "trick" in the other sense. Court de Gébelin's 1781 Egyptian pedigree was a fabrication he admitted he couldn't evidence — a *fiction* retrofitted onto the images — and Etteilla then built the working cartomantic *device* on top of it. So I'd refine your attribution: de Gébelin is the fiction/con; Etteilla is the engineer who turned the con into a usable method. The "trick" is both.
- **Play (theater/ritual/fiction).** The contemporary phase — performance on TikTok, deck-as-art-object, the subjunctive ritual. This is the Seligman "as-if," and it's where the queer fit becomes structural rather than incidental.

And "play is an identifier for queer communities" — defensible if stated carefully, because of performativity and camp. But here's where I push back, since you want the honest version, not the flattering one:

1. **Play isn't *only* queer.** Huizinga's whole argument in *Homo Ludens* is that play is a human universal underlying all culture. So play can't be a *unique* marker of queerness. The accurate claim is narrower and stronger: queer cultures have a distinctively *self-aware, reclaimed* relationship to play, performance, and artifice — partly *because* the sincere/declarative mode ("you are essentially X") was used against them. They didn't invent play; they were forced into a more conscious relationship with it.

2. **Much queer and diasporic tarot is *not* playful — it's sincere.** The Black feminist strand is explicitly about healing and ancestral reconnection, not camp; recall the Swarthmore scholar's line that for Black women it ultimately comes down to healing — not just bodies but spirits, and Pew's finding that LGBT users lead on doing it *for insight*. So the "play" frame captures one genuine register and misses another. The richest formulation is that tarot lets people *move between* the playful-subjunctive and the sincere-devotional at will — and that mobility is exactly what someone burned by institutional religion needs: you can engage without being doctrinally captured, and you can be sincere without submitting to a gatekeeper.

3. **It's one pillar, not the whole building.** The demographic skew is overdetermined — the historical sanctuary, the rejected-knowledge kinship, the wellness/therapy reframing, *and* the play structure all push in the same direction. Your thesis is the most interesting and most underrated of these, and I'd defend it as a real causal pillar. But if you present it as *the* explanation rather than the keystone that ties the others together, a critic will rightly note that astrology is also subjunctive and also queer-skewed yet far more app-centralized and arguably more doctrinally rigid — which suggests the *visual, physical, redrawable* substrate of tarot (the thing your "license to create" hypothesis was about last time) is doing independent work alongside the play structure.

So the version I'd stand behind: **tarot fits these communities because it is oblique knowledge with no gatekeeper, inherited from a tradition that has sheltered the excluded since the 1850s, and structured as a subjunctive "as-if" device that lets a self be tried on in play rather than declared in sincerity — and that play/sincerity *mobility*, not play alone, is the queer-resonant feature.** That keeps everything you're reaching for, including the Seligman backing and the "oblique" etymology, while not overclaiming that play is uniquely or exhaustively queer.

If you want to take it further, the two most productive next moves would be: Butler's *Gender Trouble* on performativity laid directly against Seligman's subjunctive (they're almost the same argument in different vocabularies), and the question of whether tarot's *physical redrawability* is the thing astrology lacks — i.e., testing your play thesis against your earlier "license to create" thesis to see which is load-bearing.

## The correction you need to hear: performativity is *not* performance

This is where Butler would stop you, and it's the most valuable thing in the piece, because the "play / theater" framing we inherited from Seligman quietly smuggles in the wrong picture. ## Campbell's "discovered": myth as found, not made

Campbell's whole stance is that the mythmaker doesn't invent so much as *uncover*. Following Jung, he treated myths as expressions of pre-existing structures of the psyche — the archetypes of the collective unconscious — which is why he held that the Hero's Journey appears in nearly every culture's mythical framework. If the pattern is already latent in everyone, the artist is less an author than a dowser: they strike a vein that was there all along. Campbell's own term for what the artist does is *mythologization* — keeping the latent pattern alive by re-rendering it for the present, because, as he argued in *The Power of Myth*, modern Western culture has lost its living mythology, producing what he called a wasteland. Note the politics of the metaphor: you discover gold; you don't invent it. "Discovered" implies the thing pre-exists the finder, and the finder is interchangeable. Hold that thought — it comes back and it bites.

And crucially, for Campbell myth is not *false*. It's real as metaphor, and it turns into a lie only when read literally. His running enemy in the Moyers conversations is fundamentalism — the mistaking of the metaphor for fact. So a myth is "real" not by correspondence to events but by *function*: it orients a life. That's already your subjunctive in a different vocabulary — real in the "as-if," dead in the "as-is."

## Star Wars: yes — and the paradox you're circling

Campbell unambiguously claimed it. After Lucas screened the original trilogy for him in one day at Skywalker Ranch, Campbell said he'd thought real art had stopped with Picasso, Joyce, and Mann, and now he knew it hadn't; in *The Power of Myth* he treats Star Wars as a metaphor that conforms to classical mythological legends, and the book is full of references to its characters. So: a modern myth, fully credentialed.

But here's the twist your question is hunting for. *Was it discovered?* Lucas's own account makes it neither pure discovery nor pure invention. In Campbell's authorized biography he says he was already researching fairy tales and folklore, and "in reading The Hero with a Thousand Faces I began to realize that my first draft of Star Wars was following classic motifs, so I modified my next draft according to what I'd" learned. So the first draft was *discovered* (he'd hit the vein instinctively); the later drafts were *engineered* (he reverse-engineered the rest of the pattern from the book). It's **found-by-making** — and a careful source flags that Lucas's debt to Campbell is widely cited and occasionally overstated; he found in Campbell a framework for what he was already working toward, a way to understand what he was doing instinctively. There's even a perfect detail on the "meaning attributed after" front: the composer John Williams said that until Campbell explained what Star Wars meant, they'd regarded it as a Saturday-morning space movie. The mythic meaning was, in part, conferred *after* the artifact — by a third party, retrospectively. That's your de Gébelin structure, wearing a tuxedo.

So Campbell would say Star Wars is discovered (it touches the real pattern). Lucas's drafts say it was also built to fit. The honest answer is that the discovered/invented binary doesn't survive contact with the case. What gets "discovered" is not a fixed story but a *capacity to make worlds that land* — and the landing is the realness.

## Old tarot: two "discoveries" collide

Run your distinction through Campbell and it splits cleanly, which is why it's so useful. There are two completely different "discoveries" in tarot's history, and only one is real.

The *images* were genuinely discovered in Campbell's sense. The Major Arcana — the Wheel of Fortune, Death, the Tower, the Star, the Devil, the Hanged Man, the Fool — were drawn from a living medieval and Renaissance allegorical vocabulary that already circulated in the culture. The deck's makers in the 1440s weren't inventing archetypes; they were cataloguing ones the culture already carried. So the old tarot's imagery is Campbell-true: found, not fabricated.

The *meaning*, by contrast, was a fake discovery. De Gébelin's claim to have *uncovered* an ancient Egyptian Book of Thoth was, in Dummett's account, a fabrication with no evidence — a pseudo-discovery dressed as recovery. And here's the unsettling part: **Campbell wouldn't much care that it was fake.** Because he privileges function over provenance, he'd say the Egyptian story works mythically whether or not it's historically true — the way he read the famous "Chief Seattle" speech in the series, which was actually written by a screenwriter in 1972, not by Chief Seattle. Campbell used it anyway, because it *functioned*. That's the generative and the dangerous face of his method at once: he'll bless a forged genealogy if it moves the psyche. Old tarot is exactly that — real archetypes, fake pedigree, and a theory of myth that doesn't flinch at the difference.

## New tarot: the Star Wars move, performed on a deck

New tarot — the indie, queer, and diasporic decks, and Smith's narrative pips before them — is the Lucas operation applied to cards. It is self-conscious re-mythologization: makers deliberately re-cite the archetypes and re-attribute meaning for a community the old deck didn't see. In Campbell's frame this is the living function of myth doing its job — the artist mythologizing the environment for a new people. In Butler's frame from the last piece it's resignification: re-citing signs that have no original. The two descriptions are the same act seen from two angles, which is the whole payoff: **Campbell's "discovered" and Butler's "cited-with-no-origin" are describing one phenomenon from opposite ends.** Campbell says the pattern was always there to be found; Butler says there was never an original, only iterations. Found-by-making reconciles them: what's "always there" is not a fixed myth but the human capacity to iterate worlds, and each new deck both discovers that capacity and exercises it.

## Where is fiction real?

Now the synthesis, because every thread in this conversation converges on this one answer. Fiction is real **at the point of enactment** — where it is performed, ritualized, played, drawn, cited, lived. Not real as correspondence to fact; real as efficacy, as the thing that organizes a self or a people.

Lay the four frameworks side by side and they say the same sentence:

Campbell: myth is real as *function* — "public dreams" that orient a life — and false only when literalized. Seligman: ritual is real in the *subjunctive* — the "as-if" world that is known to be made and still does work. Winnicott: real in the *transitional space* — the third place, neither inner fantasy nor outer fact, where culture itself lives. Butler: the performative *makes* the fiction real — "it's a girl" names a thing that didn't prediscursively exist and, through iteration, becomes the most materially real fact of a life.

So: the tarot card is fiction; the account of yourself it occasions is real. Star Wars is fiction; the orientation it gave a generation is real. Gender, in Butler's account, is a fiction with no original and the most concrete thing about a body. The fiction is real *in the doing of it* — and it becomes a lie only at the moment it's mistaken for fact-out-there rather than world-made-here. That's Campbell's fundamentalism, Seligman's "sincerity," and Butler's naturalized norm: three names for the single error of forgetting that the fiction was made. Fiction is real where it is enacted as fiction; it dies where it forgets it is one.

## Campbell's "discovered": myth as found, not made

Campbell's whole stance is that the mythmaker doesn't invent so much as *uncover*. Following Jung, he treated myths as expressions of pre-existing structures of the psyche — the archetypes of the collective unconscious — which is why he held that the Hero's Journey appears in nearly every culture's mythical framework. If the pattern is already latent in everyone, the artist is less an author than a dowser: they strike a vein that was there all along. Campbell's own term for what the artist does is *mythologization* — keeping the latent pattern alive by re-rendering it for the present, because, as he argued in *The Power of Myth*, modern Western culture has lost its living mythology, producing what he called a wasteland. Note the politics of the metaphor: you discover gold; you don't invent it. "Discovered" implies the thing pre-exists the finder, and the finder is interchangeable. Hold that thought — it comes back and it bites.

## Old tarot: two "discoveries" collide

The *meaning*, by contrast, was a fake discovery. ## Campbell: the critics who say the template eats everything

The "forces too much into one normative framework" charge against Campbell comes in four distinct flavors, with named owners:

**The empirical/methodological critique — Alan Dundes.** The folklorist's complaint is that the universal isn't actually universal; Campbell cherry-picks and commits category errors. Dundes's signature example: to prove his "Belly of the Whale" motif, Campbell cites the fish-swallowing-a-hero motif as worldwide, but it isn't — it's absent in sub-Saharan Africa, for one — and his examples lump Jonah (a hero, in a myth, swallowed by a whale) together with Little Red Riding Hood (a heroine, in a fairy tale, swallowed by a wolf). Different genre, different gender, different animal, same bin. That's the Procrustean bed in miniature.

**The unfalsifiability critique.** The monomyth is so elastic it can't be wrong — and missing elements get reread as significant rather than disconfirming. As one sharp version puts it, the scheme has more escape hatches than the lair of a James Bond villain; if an element is missing, we're told that's significant, not a contradiction, and the unalterable essentials reduce to "stories tend to have a beginning, a middle, an end, and a protagonist who does interesting things" — which, as the same critic notes via Vonnegut's "Man in a Hole," is not news.

**The normative critique — and this is where your word lands exactly.** Campbell's framework isn't merely descriptive; it *prescribes* a shape of the good life ("follow your bliss"), and that shape is individualist and male. The feminist pushback (Maureen Murdock's *The Heroine's Journey*, written partly because Campbell reportedly told her women don't need the journey since they're the destination the hero seeks) and the Le Guin/Haraway line argue for a collective of heroes rather than a single hero, on the understanding that myths are contextual and particular to specific cultures, times, and places, against Campbell's universalising discourse. Campbell is genuinely *normative* — that's the right word for him, and it's the fair charge.

**The ideological/political critique — Robert Ellwood and Brendan Gill.** Ellwood's *The Politics of Myth: A Study of C.G. Jung, Mircea Eliade, and Joseph Campbell* (SUNY, 1999) argues the universalizing mythologists promised a "gnostic salvation," a saving wisdom telling a universally important secret that would cure the malaises of modernity — and that this carries reactionary, anti-modern freight. Brendan Gill's "The Faces of Joseph Campbell" (NYRB, 1989) and Maurice Friedman's pointed essay on how Campbell's psychologizing of myth precludes the Holocaust as a touchstone of reality push the hardest version: that dissolving history into universal archetype isn't neutral — it can launder away the particular atrocity. (Robert Segal's *Joseph Campbell: An Introduction* is the fair-minded scholarly middle if you want a balanced anchor.)

## Lévi-Strauss: same accusation, different crime

Here's the twist. Lévi-Strauss is *also* charged with cramming everything into one frame, but because he's a structuralist rather than a romantic, the charge mutates on almost every axis.

The shared part: both are accused of arbitrary, Procrustean universalizing. But then it diverges, and the divergences are the interesting bit for you:

**He's not normative — he's anti-humanist.** Campbell tells you how to live; Lévi-Strauss programmatically refuses to. His notorious line is that the purpose of the human sciences is not to constitute man but to dissolve him. So the critique can't be "he prescribes a life"; it's the opposite — that he *erases the subject* entirely. Where Campbell's universal makes the individual maker interchangeable (the archetype was always there to be found), Lévi-Strauss's universal is more radical: it dissolves the maker into the structure. **He is the extreme case of the maker-erasure you've been tracking since Pamela Colman Smith** — not "the artist is interchangeable" but "there is no author, only the system speaking."

**The killing critique is ahistoricism, not ideology.** Structuralism is attacked for being ahistorical and for favouring deterministic structural forces over the ability of people to act — it brackets history, contingency, and agency in favor of timeless synchronic grids. This is what eventually proved fatal to it academically; the politics of 1968 and then deconstruction made the frozen, agency-less structure untenable.

**The interpretive rival — Clifford Geertz.** Where Campbell gets the folklorist Dundes, Lévi-Strauss gets the interpretive anthropologist: Geertz objected to structuralism's etic, universalist decoding of symbols, in favor of local, "thick" meaning. Same instinct as the postcolonial pushback on Campbell, aimed at the formal grid instead of the spiritual template.
