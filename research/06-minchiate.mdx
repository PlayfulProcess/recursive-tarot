# Minchiate

## At a glance

A Florentine super-sized tarot game: **97 cards** with **40 trumps** instead of 22 —
adding the zodiac, the four elements, and the remaining theological/cardinal virtues.
Despite all that cosmic imagery it is a **game**, hugely popular for three centuries,
and a flagship of the **A-order** branch.

## Origin & dating

First recorded as **germini** in **1506** (named for the Gemini card, highest of the new
trumps). The expanded 97-card pack so outsold the older form that the older name
*minchiate* migrated to the big deck in the 1600s. From Florence it spread across Italy
and into France through the 17th century.

## Provenance & evidence

Many printed editions survive (e.g. the well-known 18th–19th-century Florentine
*Minchiate Fiorentine*). Rules are preserved in gaming literature; pagat.com documents
the game's structure. The expansion is the headline evidence: trumps nearly doubled to
absorb astrological and elemental figures.

## Game or divination?

**Game.** This is the deck that most tempts an esoteric misreading — zodiac! elements!
virtues! — yet every record treats those cards as **trump ranks in a trick-taking
game**, not oracle cards. The cosmology is decorative/encyclopedic, the same Renaissance
visual vocabulary as the Mantegna prints, not a divinatory key.

## The fear question

**No fear.** A mainstream parlor and gambling game for centuries. The familiar caveat
applies — moralists disliked *gambling* — but Minchiate as such was respectable
entertainment, openly printed and sold.

## Counter-voices

Because Minchiate looks like a "complete" astrological tarot, occult writers sometimes
present it as a survival of the *true*, larger sacred system that the 22-trump decks
supposedly censored. The evidence runs the other way: the 22-trump form is older and the
40-trump Minchiate is an **expansion for richer play**, not a recovered esoteric
original.
