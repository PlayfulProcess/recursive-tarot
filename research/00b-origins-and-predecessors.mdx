# Origins — Why Tarot Was Invented, and What Came Before

> Deep-dive companion to `01-visconti-sforza.mdx`. The Visconti decks are the oldest
> *objects*; this file is the *why* behind them — the game world tarot was born into, and
> the question of whether any divination tradition can honestly claim to predate it.

## At a glance

Tarot was not invented to tell fortunes, and it was not invented from nothing. It was a
**bolt-on to an existing card game**: sometime around the 1430s–40s, in the courts of
northern Italy, someone added a fifth suit of allegorical "triumphs" (*trionfi*) to the
ordinary four-suit pack that had reached Europe only ~60 years earlier. The triumphs gave
players a permanent suit of trumps. That is the whole invention. Everything mystical came
350 years later.

## Step 1 — Playing cards reach Europe (the 1370s)

Cards appear in European records **suddenly** in the 1370s–80s, as if imported whole:

- **1371 Catalonia**, **1377 Florence** (a described game), **1377 Basel** (a monk calls
  cards a *new* arrival), **1377 Paris** (a decree restricting card-play on work days).
- The source is the Islamic world. The European pack descends from the **Mamluk**
  *Mulūk wa-nuwwāb* ("kings and deputies") deck of Egypt/Syria — **four suits** (coins,
  polo-sticks, cups, swords), each with ten pip cards and court cards. Europe kept the
  structure, swapped polo-sticks (unfamiliar) for batons/clubs, and so produced the
  **Latin suits** (coins, cups, swords, batons) that tarot still uses.
- For the deeper roots — Tang-China money-cards behind the Mamluk deck, and whether any
  of these ancestral cards were used for divination — see `00c-islamic-and-chinese-card-origins.mdx`.

So by c.1380 Europe has a four-suit gambling pack and a robust gaming culture — and,
immediately, **moral panic about it** (the Paris and Basel notices are essentially
complaints). The raw material for tarot exists; the trumps do not yet.

## Step 2 — The "fifth suit" of triumphs (c.1420s–1440s)

The decisive innovation was adding a fixed hierarchy of picture-cards that **beat** the
ordinary suits — a permanent trump line. The trail:

- **c.1418–1425, Milan:** Duke **Filippo Maria Visconti** commissions a deck of *classical
  gods* from **Michelino da Besozzo**, described in a text by **Marziano da Tortona**
  (*"triumphorum genus"*). This is a proto-trump experiment — gods as triumphs — not yet
  the standard 22.
- **16 September 1440, Florence:** the first dated use of *trionfi* for cards, in the
  notary **Giusto Giusti's** records (a deck given as a gift).
- **1440s–60s, Milan:** the **Visconti-Sforza** and **Cary-Yale** decks fix something
  close to the standard trump sequence (with local experiments — extra courts, the
  theological virtues).

The word matters: *trionfi* → *trumps*. Tarot literally **named** the trick-taking concept
of trumping.

## Step 3 — *Why* those images? Three feeders

The 22 triumphs are not random. They draw on the shared visual language of 15th-century
Italian elite culture:

1. **Petrarch's *I Trionfi*** (written partly at the Visconti court): a procession in
   which **Love → Chastity → Death → Fame → Time → Eternity** each "triumphs" over the
   last. The very logic of a card outranking the previous one mirrors the poem.
2. **Christian moral allegory:** Pope, Popess, the Virtues (Justice, Temperance,
   Fortitude), Death, the Devil, the Last Judgement, the cosmos (Sun, Moon, Star, World).
3. **Civic festival culture:** Carnival and religious processions used *trionfi* —
   allegorical floats. The cards are a pocket version of a parade everyone already knew.

So the honest answer to "why was tarot invented" is: **to enrich a fashionable card game
for a courtly audience, using the off-the-shelf allegory of triumph-processions, virtues,
and the cosmos.** It is humanist party culture, not a temple cipher.

## Step 4 — Was there a divination tradition that came *before* tarot?

This is the user's sharpest question, and it needs a careful two-part answer.

**Yes — divination long predates tarot, but not *tarot* divination.** Europe and the
Mediterranean had a deep, continuous divinatory toolkit centuries before any card:

- **Astrology** — the prestige system, Babylonian → Hellenistic → Islamic → Renaissance.
- **Geomancy** (Arabic *ʿilm al-raml*, "the science of the sand") — a portable, dot-based
  system imported with other Arabic learning; arguably the closest *methodological*
  ancestor of later cartomancy (random marks → fixed figures → interpretation).
- **Sortes / lot-books** — bibliomancy and "books of fate" where you cast lots or dice and
  read off a pre-written verse. Hugely popular in manuscript and early print.
- **Dice, dream-books, palmistry, and folk omen-reading** — the everyday layer.

**But none of these is tarot, and tarot did not grow out of them.** When card-divination
(cartomancy) does appear, the documentary trail puts it at the **earliest in the 16th
century, using *ordinary* playing cards**, not the tarot trumps — and tarot-specific
divination only crystallises in **1781–1788** in France (de Gébelin's theory, Etteilla's
practice). For ~340 years the tarot pack and the divining traditions ran on **separate
tracks**: people read the stars, the sand, and the lot-books; they *played* with the
tarot.

**The honest caveat (and where the counter-voices live).** "No surviving record of tarot
divination before 1781" is an argument from documents, and the people most likely to read
cards informally — folk practitioners, poor women, and later Romani cartomancers — were
exactly those least likely to leave written evidence and most likely to be punished for
it. So we can say with confidence that tarot was *designed* as a game and that the
*systematic* occult tarot is an 18th-century construction, **without** claiming that no
human being ever laid the cards out superstitiously before someone wrote it down. The
divinatory *impulse* is ancient; the tarot-divination *system* is modern; the gap between
them is partly real and partly an artifact of who got to keep records.

## Predecessors at a glance

| Layer | What it was | Roughly when | Relation to tarot |
|------|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Mamluk 4-suit pack | Imported gambling cards | reaches Europe c.1370s | **Ancestor** (the suits) |
| Karnöffel & early trick games | European 4-suit games | 1420s+ | **Sibling** game culture |
| Marziano / Michelino deck | "Gods" proto-trumps, Milan | c.1418–25 | **Prototype** of trumps |
| Trionfi (Visconti etc.) | The fifth suit added | c.1440s | **Tarot itself (as a game)** |
| Astrology / geomancy / sortes | Divination systems | ancient–medieval | **Parallel, not parent** |
| Cartomancy w/ ordinary cards | Card fortune-telling | 16th c.+ | Parallel; pre-dates *tarot* cartomancy |
| Court de Gébelin / Etteilla | Tarot divination invented | 1781 / 1788 | **The overlay** |

## Open questions / corrections owed

- The exact decade the *standard 22* stabilised (vs. local experiments) is still argued —
  keep "c.1440s" soft.
- Karnöffel and other early trick games deserve a note as the *gameplay* context; not yet
  filed here.
- The "ordinary-card cartomancy in the 16th c." claim rests on scattered references — flag
  as suggestive, not firmly systematised, until the 18th century.
