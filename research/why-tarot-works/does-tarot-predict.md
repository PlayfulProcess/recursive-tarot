# Does Tarot Predict? — the evidentiary case for "no"

*A focused dossier, spun out of `REPORT.md`'s §2, to ground one specific claim with citable
primary sources: **there is no reliable evidence that tarot predicts the future or accesses
information a person doesn't already have.** Written to be cited directly from
`course/how-tarot-works.mdx` ("The thing the cards cannot do").*

**Date:** 2026-07-07 · **Method:** targeted search + fetch per source, not a fan-out
review. Every source below was independently confirmed to exist (title, authors, venue,
year, and a stable link or DOI) before being added to `research/bibliography.bib`.
Provenance markers follow the house convention from `REPORT.md`: **✔︎** confirmed via
search/fetch this run · **○** standard reference fact, well-corroborated but not
independently re-derived from the primary text · **◆** synthesis/interpretation.

---

## The one-paragraph answer

Three independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion. **(1) Tarot has been
tested directly, twice, and failed both times** — people cannot pick their own reading out
of a stack of others, and cannot tell a real personalized reading from one built out of
randomly drawn cards. **(2) The feeling of "how did it know" has a name and a mechanism** —
the Barnum/Forer effect and its deliberate cousin, cold reading — that produces the exact
sensation of uncanny accuracy from statements that are, provably, generic. **(3) The
broader claim tarot would need to be true — that a person can access information they have
no ordinary way of knowing — has been tested far beyond tarot, at far greater expense and
rigor than any tarot study ever attempted, and has never produced a replicable result.**
None of this proves prediction is *impossible*. It shows that **no one has yet shown it
possible**, under conditions designed to catch it if it were there — which is the correct,
honest strength of the claim, argued in full at the end of this dossier.

## 1. Tarot tested directly

### Blackmore (1983) — the first controlled test
Parapsychologist Susan Blackmore, then a working researcher testing her own paranormal
beliefs against evidence, ran the most direct experiment on tarot as divination that
exists: she gave real tarot readings to 10 subjects, keeping her procedure as close to a
normal reading as the controls allowed, then had each subject try to rank *all ten*
anonymized readings to find their own. If a reading carries real information specific to
the person it's for, people should recognize their own well above the 1-in-10 chance rate.
They did not — across the study's three experiments, subjects' rankings did not exceed
chance in two of the three. **✔︎** [Blackmore, S. (1983). *Divination with Tarot cards: An
empirical study*. Journal of the Society for Psychical Research, 52,
97–101.](https://www.susanblackmore.uk/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/JSPR-1983.pdf)

**Honest complication, not smoothed over:** a 1988 reanalysis by Markwick found that the
*first* of the three experiments remained statistically significant under the corrected
analysis. This is exactly the kind of detail a dossier owes the reader — the finding is not
a spotless zero, it is "did not replicate across the set, with one component result that
survived reanalysis and was not itself independently replicated again." That is a
meaningfully weaker claim than "proven false," and a meaningfully weaker claim than
"proven." ○

### Ivtzan & French (2004) — real reading vs. random-card control
Two decades later, a more targeted design: give one group of subjects a *real* reading (the
cards they actually drew, interpreted for them) and a *control* reading (an interpretation
built from a randomly assembled set of cards that has nothing to do with what they drew),
and see if people can tell which is theirs. They could not — and, in an ironic twist noted
in the secondary literature, self-identified tarot *believers* did no better at telling real
from random than skeptics did, and in some analyses did worse. **○** (conference
proceedings, not a peer-reviewed journal — flagged at lower confidence than Blackmore
accordingly) [Ivtzan, I. & French, C. (2004). *Testing the validity of tarot cards: Can we
distinguish between a real and a control reading?* Proceedings of Presented Papers,
Parapsychological Association 47th Annual
Convention.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/266041454)

### Ivtzan (2007) — the review that ties them together
A later literature review by the same researcher lays the two live explanations for a
tarot reading's apparent accuracy side by side — the paranormal explanation (the cards
access real information) and the non-paranormal explanation (the Barnum effect and cold
reading) — and finds the evidence sits with the second. **✔︎** [Ivtzan, I. (2007). *Tarot
cards: A literature review and evaluation of psychic versus psychological explanations*.
Journal of Parapsychology, 71(1),
139–150.](https://www.parapsychologypress.org/jparticle/jp-71-1-139-149)

## 2. The mechanism behind "how did it know"

This is the part that matters most for honesty: a reading landing hard doesn't need an
explanation stranger than well-documented psychology.

- **The Barnum/Forer effect.** In 1949, Bertram Forer gave 39 students an identical,
  generic personality sketch (assembled from a newsstand astrology column) under the
  pretense that it was individually written for each of them. Students rated it, on
  average, **4.3 out of 5** for how accurately it described them. **✔︎** [Forer, B. R.
  (1949). *The fallacy of personal validation: A classroom demonstration of gullibility*.
  Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology, 44(1), 118–123.] Thirty-five years of
  follow-up study confirmed the effect holds up broadly, not as a one-off classroom
  trick. **✔︎** [Dickson, D. H. & Kelly, I. W. (1985). *The 'Barnum effect' in personality
  assessment: A review of the literature*. Psychological Reports, 57(2),
  367–382.](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2466/pr0.1985.57.2.367)
- **Cold reading.** Where Barnum/Forer explains why a *generic* statement feels personal,
  cold reading is the deliberate craft of producing that feeling on purpose — a set of
  conversational techniques (broad statements, watching for reaction, letting the other
  person supply the specifics) that psychologist Ray Hyman documented in detail after
  years of studying working psychics, mediums, and mentalists. **✔︎** [Hyman, R. (1977).
  *"Cold reading": How to convince strangers that you know all about them*. The Zetetic,
  1(2),
  18–37.](https://skepticalinquirer.org/1977/04/cold-reading-how-to-convince-strangers-that-you-know-all-about-them/)
- **Subjective validation, confirmation bias, apophenia.** Covered at length in
  `REPORT.md` §2 already — the same looseness that makes a Barnum statement feel personal
  lets you pour your own specifics into it; hits get remembered, misses fade;
  under low control, people see patterns in noise more readily (Whitson & Galinsky,
  *Science*, 2008). Not repeated in full here — see `REPORT.md`.

## 3. The broader claim, tested at scale, and its collapse

Tarot-specific studies are thin. The claim tarot would need for prediction to work —
that a person can access information about the future, or about facts they have no
ordinary route to — has been tested **far** more rigorously, at far greater expense, by
researchers with every incentive to find it, and it has never held up.

- **Bem (2011), "Feeling the Future."** A tenured, methodologically careful
  psychologist published nine experiments in a top mainstream psychology journal (not a
  parapsychology venue) claiming evidence that people could sense future, randomly
  determined events before they happened — the most serious modern attempt to demonstrate
  precognition under standard experimental psychology's own rules. **✔︎** [Bem, D. J.
  (2011). *Feeling the future: Experimental evidence for anomalous retroactive influences
  on cognition and affect*. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 100(3),
  407–425.](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021524)
- **It did not replicate.** Three independent, pre-registered attempts to exactly repeat
  Bem's "retroactive facilitation of recall" experiment all came back with nothing
  (combined p = .83). **✔︎** [Ritchie, S. J., Wiseman, R., & French, C. C. (2012).
  *Failing the future: Three unsuccessful attempts to replicate Bem's 'retroactive
  facilitation of recall' effect*. PLoS ONE, 7(3),
  e33423.](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0033423) A separate, larger effort — 7
  experiments, 3,289 participants — found a meta-analytic effect size of **d = 0.04**,
  indistinguishable from zero. **✔︎** [Galak, J., LeBoeuf, R. A., Nelson, L. D., & Simmons,
  J. P. (2012). *Correcting the past: Failures to replicate psi*. Journal of Personality
  and Social Psychology, 103(6),
  933–948.](https://repository.upenn.edu/oid_papers/232/) Bem's paper itself was never
  formally retracted, which is itself worth being precise about — this is a collapse
  under independent replication, not a retraction.
- **The most-studied paranormal paradigm, contested honestly.** The **ganzfeld**
  telepathy-transfer design is the single most meta-analyzed paradigm in parapsychology.
  Milton & Wiseman's 1999 meta-analysis found no significant effect across 30 studies.
  **✔︎** [Milton, J. & Wiseman, R. (1999). *Does psi exist? Lack of replication of an
  anomalous process of information transfer*. Psychological Bulletin, 125(4),
  387–391.](https://koestlerunit.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/milton-wiseman-1999.pdf)
  **This is genuinely disputed, not a clean verdict** — Storm & Ertel (2001) challenged
  the study-inclusion criteria and reported a residual effect on a revised database. Cited
  here deliberately with its dispute attached: after decades of the most rigorous testing
  parapsychology has produced, the field's own flagship paradigm remains an argument, not
  a settled finding either way. ○
- **The standing prize nobody has claimed.** The James Randi Educational Foundation's
  **One Million Dollar Paranormal Challenge** offered a mutually-agreed, scientifically
  designed test to anyone who could demonstrate *any* paranormal ability — over a thousand
  people applied across the challenge's run (informally from 1964, formalized 1996); none
  passed the preliminary test; the challenge was retired in 2015. **✔︎**
  [Wikipedia: One Million Dollar Paranormal
  Challenge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Million_Dollar_Paranormal_Challenge)
- **An adjacent rigorous design, for pattern, not tarot itself.** Shawn Carlson's 1985
  double-blind test of natal astrology — professional astrologers trying to match natal
  charts to the correct one of three personality profiles — found no support for the
  practice, published in *Nature*. **✔︎** [Carlson, S. (1985). *A double-blind test of
  astrology*. Nature, 318, 419–425.](https://doi.org/10.1038/318419a0) Also genuinely
  contested — a 2009 reanalysis by Ertel of the same raw data reported a small, significant
  match in two of the three tests. Included here not as proof about tarot, but as the same
  design pattern applied to an adjacent divinatory/character-reading claim, with its own
  dispute flagged rather than hidden. ○

**The pattern across all three sections:** every time a divinatory or paranormal claim has
been tested with a design rigorous enough to catch a real effect if one were there, the
result is either nothing, or something small enough that it collapses under independent
replication, or a result contested enough that neither side has closed the argument. Tarot
has had far less direct testing than telepathy, precognition, or astrology — and what
testing it has had (Blackmore; Ivtzan & French) landed in the same place.

## 4. Grounding the "ways it can help" side

The mechanisms `how-tarot-works.mdx` claims for *why a reading can be useful even though it
doesn't predict* also have their own, largely independent evidence trails:

- **Ritual.** The claim that a bounded ceremony — shuffling, the spread, the held question
  — genuinely reduces anxiety and restores a felt sense of control, independent of any
  supernatural mechanism, is reviewed at length across social psychology, cognitive
  science, anthropology and neuroscience. **✔︎** [Hobson, N. M., Schroeder, J., Risen, J.
  L., Xygalatas, D., & Inzlicht, M. (2018). *The psychology of rituals: An integrative
  review and process-based framework*. Personality and Social Psychology Review, 22(3),
  260–284.](https://doi.org/10.1177/1088868317734944) A companion single-study finding —
  that engaging in rituals after a loss (a breakup, a death, even a lost lottery ticket)
  measurably reduces reported grief via restored feelings of control — comes from Norton &
  Gino (2014). **Flagged, not withheld:** Francesca Gino's later, separate papers (2012–
  2020) were found to contain fabricated data and were retracted following a Harvard
  investigation; *this* paper is not among the retracted or flagged set as of this
  writing, but it is cited here as corroborating support alongside Hobson et al., not as
  the sole basis for the claim. **○** [Norton, M. I. & Gino, F. (2014). *Rituals alleviate
  grieving for loved ones, lovers, and lotteries*. Journal of Experimental Psychology:
  General, 143(1), 266–272.](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0031772)
- **Productive randomness.** Omar Khayyam Moore's classic 1957 account of Naskapi hunters
  reading cracked caribou shoulder-blades to choose a hunting direction argued the ritual's
  real function was to *randomize* the hunt away from the hunters' own predictable habits
  — divination as a deliberate randomizer, five decades before Oblique Strategies made the
  same move for artists. **✔︎ (with a caveat)** [Moore, O. K. (1957). *Divination — a new
  perspective*. American Anthropologist, 59(1),
  69–74.](https://doi.org/10.1525/aa.1957.59.1.02a00060) His specific *game-conservation*
  hypothesis (that randomizing hunts protected caribou populations from overhunting) has
  since been critiqued by later scholarship as empirically underdetermined; the
  descriptive point this collection actually relies on — that a divinatory procedure can
  function as a working randomizer, not a fate-teller — survives that critique intact. ◆
  Modern behavioral economics finds the identical mechanism completely outside any
  divinatory frame: Steven Levitt's large-scale coin-toss experiment found that ~22,500
  people who were stuck on a hard life decision and let a coin toss push them toward
  change were more likely to actually make the change, and reported greater happiness
  months later, than those the coin told to stay put. **✔︎** [Levitt, S. D. (2021). *Heads
  or tails: The impact of a coin toss on major life decisions and subsequent happiness*.
  Review of Economic Studies, 88(1),
  378–405.](https://doi.org/10.1093/restud/rdaa016) Evans-Pritchard's classical
  ethnography of the Azande poison oracle documents the same structure as a working,
  socially load-bearing decision-procedure (consulted before hunts, journeys, marriages,
  legal disputes) rather than superstition. **○** [Evans-Pritchard, E. E. (1937).
  *Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic Among the Azande*. Oxford: Clarendon Press.]
- **Re-narration.** The underlying move of naming a stuck feeling as an external image and
  telling its story has an independent evidence trail in expressive-writing research:
  people instructed to write about their most difficult experiences for a few days showed
  measurably better physical-health outcomes at follow-up than those who wrote about
  trivial topics, a finding later meta-analyses confirm at a smaller but real effect
  size. **✔︎** [Pennebaker, J. W. & Beall, S. K. (1986). *Confronting a traumatic event:
  Toward an understanding of inhibition and disease*. Journal of Abnormal Psychology,
  95(3), 274–281.](https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.95.3.274)

## 5. Sources examined and excluded — and why

Being explicit about what was *ruled out* is as important as what was kept.

- **Brooks, Schroeder, Risen, Gino, Galinsky, Norton, & Schweitzer (2016), "Don't stop
  believing: Rituals improve performance by decreasing anxiety,"** Organizational Behavior
  and Human Decision Processes, 137, 71–85 — **EXCLUDED.** This is the paper the ritual
  section would most obviously reach for. It was **retracted in November 2024**: the
  author team requested the retraction themselves after an internal data audit found they
  no longer had confidence in the integrity of the data, and could not produce raw data
  for Studies 1a/1b to the journal's retraction committee. Not cited anywhere in this
  dossier or the course. The ritual/anxiety claim is instead grounded in Hobson et al.
  (2018), an independent review, per §4 above.
- **Any claim resting solely on Ivtzan & French (2004) at high confidence** — downgraded
  to ○ throughout, because it is conference-proceedings work, not a peer-reviewed journal
  article. Cited as corroborating, not load-bearing on its own.
- **The specific numeric "significant match" figures from Storm & Ertel (2001) or Ertel
  (2009)** were not independently re-derived this run — mentioned only as "a contested
  reanalysis exists," not quoted with numbers, since those numbers weren't fetched from
  primary sources here.
- **General web claims of "tarot has been scientifically proven to work/not work"** —
  common in blog-level sources found during search, uniformly excluded; none pointed to a
  citable primary study beyond the ones already listed above.

## 6. The honest limits of this claim

**What this dossier does *not* argue:** that tarot's inability to predict has been
*proven*, in the sense a mathematical theorem is proven. Proving a universal negative
("no procedure of this kind can ever access hidden information") is not something any
finite set of experiments can do. What the evidence above supports is the narrower,
correct claim: **no one has yet demonstrated it, under any test designed rigorously enough
to catch it if it were there** — and the harder, more expensive, more motivated attempts
to demonstrate the underlying claim (precognition, telepathy, astrology) have fared no
better, several outright collapsing under independent replication.

Two structural reasons to hold that claim honestly rather than overstate it:

- **Absence of evidence is not, by itself, evidence of absence** — but it stops being a
  merely rhetorical point once the search has actually been made, repeatedly, by people
  motivated to find a positive result, using methods designed to detect a real effect. That
  describes the parapsychology literature reviewed above. The claim earns its strength from
  the *volume and rigor of the search*, not from the absence alone.
- **Publication bias runs in parapsychology's favor, if anything, which makes the null
  result more meaningful, not less.** A field built around demonstrating an anomalous
  effect has every incentive to publish a hit and bury a miss — and still, its most
  studied, most expensive, most carefully designed experiments (ganzfeld, Bem's
  precognition studies) have either failed to replicate outright or remain contested after
  decades. If the file-drawer problem were hiding a real effect, four decades of a field
  built specifically to look for it should have surfaced something more durable by now.

This is why the course text says **"there is no reliable evidence"** rather than
**"it is impossible"** — that is the accurate strength of the claim these sources support,
and overclaiming past it would be its own kind of dishonesty, of exactly the kind this
project asks its readers to notice in a bad tarot reading.
