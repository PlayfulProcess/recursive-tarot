# Recursive Tarot — Research Library

A catalogued set of scholarship notes, one file per deck (per "tarot book"), plus an
overall history and the genealogy. Written to support the grammars in
`tarot/<slug>/grammar.json`. Each file follows the same structure so historians can
scan, cite, and **correct** quickly.

## How each file is organised

Every deck file carries frontmatter (`era`, `tradition`, `order`, `function`,
`dating`, `confidence`) and these sections:

1. **At a glance** — the one-paragraph honest summary.
2. **Origin & dating** — what the documents actually show.
3. **Provenance & evidence** — surviving objects, archives, who holds them.
4. **Game or divination?** — the repo's core distinction, stated per deck.
5. **The fear question** — *would people have admitted what they were doing, or hidden it?* — read against the law and Church of that time and place.
6. **Counter-voices** — where a "suppressed occult history" or marginalised-tradition claim exists, it is stated fairly and then weighed.
7. **Open questions / corrections owed.**
8. **Sources & further reading.**

## The canonical spine

The skeptical, document-first position this library defaults to rests on three works,
all by or with **Michael Dummett**:

- Dummett, *The Game of Tarot: From Ferrara to Salt Lake City* (1980).
- Decker, Depaulis & Dummett, *A Wicked Pack of Cards: The Origins of the Occult Tarot* (1996).
- Dummett & McLeod, *A History of Games Played with the Tarot Pack* (2004).

Their core finding: tarot was invented as a **trick-taking card game** in 15th-century
northern Italy; the occult/divinatory reading is an **overlay imposed after c.1781**
(Court de Gébelin) and c.1788 (Etteilla). This library treats that as the baseline and
flags every place a deck's own tradition disputes it.
