# Etteilla — Lineage Hub

**Jean-Baptiste Alliette** ("Etteilla," 1738–1791) turned Court de Gébelin's 1781 *theory*
into the first working divinatory *practice* and the first deck *built* to tell fortunes.
His system outlived him as a 19th-century commercial brand in three main generations,
each split into its own file:

| File | Edition | Date | What it is |
|------|---------|------|------------|
| `10a-etteilla-i-livre-de-thot.mdx` | **Etteilla I** — *Livre de Thot* | 1788–89 | Alliette's own deck — the **first purpose-built divination tarot** |
| `10b-etteilla-ii.mdx` | **Etteilla II** — *Grand Livre de Thot* (Julia Orsini) | c.1838–40 | Follower re-edition; altered titling/imagery |
| `10c-etteilla-iii-oracle-des-dames.mdx` | **Etteilla III** — *Oracle des Dames* | c.1865–70 | Mass-market lithograph; "for the ladies" |

## The throughline

- **1781:** de Gébelin invents the *theory* (Egyptian Book of Thoth) — see `09`.
- **1788:** Etteilla I makes the *object* — a deck designed for cartomancy. Renumbers
  Death to 17, adds element/querent cards, prints upright/reversed meanings. **No clean
  Marseille mapping — do not normalise.**
- **1838 / 1865:** II and III carry the brand forward, drifting further from the original
  art and normalising tarot fortune-telling into respectable bourgeois leisure.

## The fear question, in one line

Etteilla advertised openly in Paris papers; by 1865 the brand was sold as **parlour
amusement "for the ladies."** The literate, commercial, metropolitan end of cartomancy was
**unafraid** — while the itinerant, paid, often Romani reader doing the same thing stayed
exposed to vagrancy law. Class, gender and country, not the cards, set the level of fear.

## Counter-voices, in one line

Etteilla **founded a real, durable cartomantic tradition** *and* sold it under a **false
Egyptian pedigree**. Both are true; honour the practice, flag the myth.
