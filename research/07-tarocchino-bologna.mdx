# Tarocchino di Bologna

## At a glance

Bologna's own shrunken tarot: a **62-card** pack (the 2s–5s removed) for a fast
trick-taking game that has been played continuously for **over 500 years** — one of the
longest-lived card games in Europe. A core witness to the **A-order**, and the deck the
genealogy uses to correct a common error (see below).

## Origin & dating

Emerged in Bologna in the **late 15th / early 16th century**. The 62-card "tarocchino"
("little tarocco") streamlined the pack for play. A celebrated artistic edition is the
copper-engraved deck commissioned c.1660 from **Giuseppe Maria Mitelli** by Count
Filippo Bentivoglio. The modern descendant game, **Ottocento**, still preserves
16th-century features.

## Provenance & evidence

Continuous printed editions, a living game tradition with documented rules, and named
artistic decks (Mitelli). This is one of the best-attested *use* histories of any tarot
family — we know how it was *played*, not just how it looked.

## Game or divination?

**Game** — and an especially clear one, because the playing tradition never lapsed. The
Bolognese context is overwhelmingly recreational.

## The fear question

**No fear.** A civic pastime. Bologna is also, ironically, where Bernardino preached his
1423 anti-card sermon — yet the city's love of the game outlasted the friar by half a
millennium. The pressure was always on **gambling stakes**, never on the cards as
sorcery.

## Counter-voices

The key correction the repo draws from Bologna is **genealogical, not occult**: the
Tarot de Marseille is **not** descended from the Bolognese Tarocchino. They are
**cousins** — Marseille is C-order, Tarocchino is A-order — both children of the common
trionfi root. Popular histories that draw a straight Bologna→Marseille line are wrong.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- Precise emergence date of the 62-card reduction is fuzzy ("late 15th/early 16th").
- The Bolognese trump order has local quirks (e.g., the four "Moors"/Papi) that the
  grammar should capture rather than normalise to Marseille.
