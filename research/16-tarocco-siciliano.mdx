# Tarocco Siciliano

## At a glance

The southernmost surviving tarot game tradition, and one of the most idiosyncratic. A
**63-card** pack still played in parts of Sicily, shaped by Bolognese and Minchiate
influence, and preserving features that died out everywhere else — including
**Portuguese-style suits** and consistently **female knaves**. A pure **game** deck, and
a good test of the genealogy's edges.

## Origin & dating

Tarot was introduced to Sicily **from the mainland (probably Rome) in 1663**, under the
viceroyalty. There it mutated: the pack was shortened to **63 cards** during the 18th
century, and certain trumps were localised — most strikingly, the **Devil was replaced by
a Ship**. It absorbed structure from the **Tarocco Bolognese** and **Minchiate**, placing
it on the **A-order** side of the family.

## Provenance & evidence

A living game with documented rules (pagat.com) and continuous printed editions (e.g.
Modiano). Two features make it a museum of dead conventions:

- **Portuguese Latin suits** — an early suit variation that vanished elsewhere by ~1900
  but survives here.
- **Female knaves (*donne* / "maids")** — unambiguously women, where most decks use a male
  Jack/Page.

## Game or divination?

**Game.** No cartomantic tradition of its own; its interest is ludic and ethnographic, not
occult.

## The fear question

**No fear** — a regional folk *game*. Its quirks (the Ship for the Devil) likely reflect
local taste and perhaps mild discomfort with the diabolical image, not any clandestine
practice. If anything, the Devil→Ship substitution is the opposite of occultism: a
**softening** of the deck's spookiest card.

## Counter-voices

Sicily's distance from the French occult revival is itself instructive: here a tarot
tradition ran for **centuries with no divinatory overlay at all**, right up to the modern
era. It is living evidence that "tarot = fortune-telling" is a *northern, late, imported*
idea, not intrinsic to the cards.
