# Tarot de Besançon & the Swiss 1JJ

## At a glance

A **confessional adaptation** of the Tarot de Marseille for Protestant Europe: it keeps
the whole Marseille structure but **replaces the two clerical Catholic trumps — the Pope
(V) and Popess (II) — with the classical gods Jupiter and Juno.** The 20th-century **Swiss
1JJ** deck (the "JJ" = Jupiter and Juno) is its best-known descendant and was, for many
English-speaking readers, the first tarot they ever handled.

## Origin & dating

The pattern emerges in **Strasbourg in the early 18th century** and is produced across
**Alsace, Colmar, Konstanz, Ulm, Munich** and Switzerland. Cardmakers later moved
production to the eastern French town of **Besançon**, giving the type its name. The
**1JJ** is a later Swiss standardisation (notably the AGMüller/Schaffhouse decks of the
19th–20th centuries).

## Provenance & evidence

Many surviving printed editions; the Jupiter/Juno substitution is the diagnostic feature.
It is squarely a **Marseille (C-order) descendant**, not an independent branch.

## Game or divination?

**Game first**, like the Marseille — but the 1JJ became widely sold in the 20th century as
a *divination* deck (U.S. Games' 1JJ was a gateway product for Anglophone readers). So it
straddles the game→oracle transition in its commercial life.

## The fear question

The most *literal* "fear" deck in the library — but the fear was **religious-political,
not occult.** Renaming to "Besançon" and swapping Pope/Popess for Jupiter/Juno was almost
certainly done **to avoid offending Protestant sensibilities** in confessionally mixed
markets (Catholic clerical figures were unwelcome). The deck was edited not because anyone
feared sorcery, but because card-makers feared losing Protestant customers — commerce and
the Reformation, not the Inquisition.

## Counter-voices

Because Jupiter and Juno are pagan deities, esoteric readers sometimes treat the Besançon
as evidence of a "restored classical/Hermetic" layer beneath the Christian Marseille. The
documentary reading is more mundane: it's a **marketing-and-religion edit** for Protestant
regions, not a recovered antique symbolism.
