# Sola Busca Tarot

## At a glance

The **earliest complete 78-card tarot** known, and the **first** with fully illustrated
number (pip) cards rather than plain suit-signs. Engraved in 1491, its trumps abandon
the standard cast (Pope, Popess, Hanged Man…) for **classical and biblical heroes**.
Genuinely steeped in **hermetic-alchemical** learning — yet still a game pack, and a
one-off rather than a main-line ancestor. It later left fingerprints on the Rider-Waite-
Smith deck.

## Origin & dating

Consensus dates the engravings to **1491**, likely **Ferrara**, with the surviving
painted set colored about a decade later in **Venice**. The engraver is plausibly
**Nicola di Maestro Antonio**; the program's likely inspirer is the hermetic philosopher
**Ludovico Lazzarelli**.

## Provenance & evidence

One complete hand-colored deck (Pinacoteca di Brera, Milan, bought by the Italian state
in 2009 for €800,000) plus ~35 uncolored cards in various museums. Crucially, in **1907**
the Busca-Serbelloni family gave black-and-white photographs of all 78 cards to the
**British Museum** — where **A. E. Waite** and **Pamela Colman Smith** could have seen
them, which is why several RWS minor-arcana scenes echo Sola Busca compositions.

## Game or divination?

**Game** — but with an erudite overlay. Its imagery is "linked in an irrefutable way to
the hermetic-alchemical tradition," yet that means moral-philosophical allegory, **not**
a divination manual. There is no evidence it was used to tell fortunes. It is the
exception that flatters the rule: even a deeply esoteric Renaissance program was for
*play and contemplation*, not cartomancy.

## The fear question

**No fear in its own time** — a costly engraved luxury for a learned patron. The hermetic
content was fashionable high culture in 1490s Italy, openly admired, not occult contraband.

## Counter-voices

Sola Busca is the strongest *good-faith* card in the "tarot was always esoteric" hand:
here is real Renaissance hermeticism, on a real tarot deck, in 1491. Worth stating
honestly. But the program is **alchemical-moral allegory**, not a divinatory system, and
it is **sui generis** — it did not found the occult-tarot lineage; the 18th-century
French did, retroactively. Loose later mappings of its classical figures onto standard
trumps are exactly that: loose, and should not be flattened.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- Ferrara vs. Venice for the engraving stage is still discussed.
- The precise extent of Sola Busca's influence on Waite-Smith is inferred from visual
  parallels, not documented commission notes.
