# Court de Gébelin's Plates (1781)

## At a glance

The hinge of the whole story. In 1781 the Protestant scholar **Antoine Court de Gébelin**
published an essay declaring the Tarot de Marseille a survival of the ancient Egyptian
**Book of Thoth**. He offered **no evidence** — and was wrong — but he converted a card
game into a "sacred text" and launched two centuries of occult tarot. Bundled with his
essay was a second piece by the **Comte de Mellet**, which first proposed *reading the
cards* and linking them to the Hebrew alphabet.

## Origin & dating

Published in volume 8 of de Gébelin's encyclopedic *Le Monde Primitif* (1781). The story
runs that he saw the tarot game being played in a Paris salon (Mme Helvétius's) and
"recognised" Egyptian allegory in the trumps. His redrawn plates "corrected" the
Marseille images toward what he imagined Egyptian originals looked like.

## Provenance & evidence

The text and plates survive and are public-domain. What does **not** exist is any
evidentiary basis: this predates Champollion's decipherment of hieroglyphs (1822), so
de Gébelin's "Egyptian" reading was pure imagination projected onto Italian Christian
imagery.

## Game or divination?

This is the **birth of the divinatory/occult claim** — but as *theory*, not practice.
De Gébelin allegorised; it was the Comte de Mellet (and then Etteilla) who turned the
idea into a method. The grammar should treat de Gébelin as the **origin of the myth**,
explicitly dated 1781, and never as evidence about the decks' actual 15th-century origin.

## The fear question

**No fear at all — the opposite.** De Gébelin published openly, under his name, in a
respectable subscription encyclopedia read by the Parisian elite (Franklin was a
subscriber). Far from hiding "occult" knowledge, he *announced* it as prestigious
ancient wisdom. The 18th-century salon treated this as fashionable erudition, not
dangerous magic. The occult overlay was, at its birth, a **public, high-status
intellectual fad**.

## Counter-voices

This file is where the "suppressed occult history" claim must be weighed most carefully.
De Gébelin is the *source* of the Egyptian-origin belief that many practitioners still
hold. Defenders argue he intuited a real esoteric substrate. The scholarly verdict
(Decker, Depaulis, Dummett) is firm: the Egyptian genealogy is **invented**, not
recovered — a Romantic-Enlightenment fantasy about Egypt, not a transmission from it.
Importantly, debunking de Gébelin does **not** debunk the *real* later history of
divinatory practice or its persecuted practitioners; it only removes a false pedigree.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- Clearly separate de Gébelin's essay from Mellet's appended essay — they differ (e.g.
  trump-to-Hebrew-letter assignments).
- The plates are interpretive redraws; flag them as such, not as historical decks.
