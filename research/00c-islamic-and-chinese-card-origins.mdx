# Before Europe — Chinese & Islamic Card Origins

> Upstream companion to `00b-origins-and-predecessors.mdx`. That file starts when cards
> reach Europe in the 1370s; this one goes back as far as the evidence allows — to Tang
> China and Mamluk Egypt — and asks the user's question directly: **were these ancestral
> cards ever used for divination?** Short answer: the deepest roots of tarot are a
> **gaming and gambling** tradition. Divination cultures existed alongside them but used
> *other* instruments.

## The chain, oldest to newest

```
Tang China (paper money + block printing)
   │  "leaf game", money-suited cards (9th c. reference; firmer evidence later)
   ▼
[ unknown transmission route — the great gap ]
   │  Silk Road / Mongol-era contact?
   ▼
Mamluk Egypt & Syria — kanjifah / Mulūk wa-nuwwāb (by 13th–15th c.)
   │  four suits, abstract court cards
   ▼
Europe (Iberia & Italy, 1370s) — "naipes" / "naibi"
   │  + a fifth suit of triumphs
   ▼
Italian trionfi → tarot (1440s)
```

## 1. The deepest root: China

- **When.** Playing cards most likely originated in **Tang-dynasty China (618–907 CE)**.
  The oft-cited anchor is an **868 CE** account of Princess Tongchang playing a **"leaf
  game" (yezi)** — though whether that "leaf" was a card is debated. The earliest truly
  *unambiguous* reference to card games comes later, in a **1320 Yuan-dynasty** legal
  text. So the 9th-century date is plausible but soft; firm evidence is later.
- **Why cards existed at all.** China had the two prerequisites no one else yet had:
  **paper** and **block printing**. Early cards are bound up with **paper money** — the
  first "money-suited" cards used **currency denominations as suits**: *coins*, *strings
  of coins*, *myriads of strings*, and *tens of myriads*. Cards were, almost literally,
  play-money.
- **What they were for.** **Gaming and gambling.** The money-suit lineage is a betting
  tradition; cards themselves sometimes served as the stakes. (China *also* had separate
  fortune-telling card traditions later, but the ancestral money-cards are a gambling
  technology, not an oracle.)

## 2. The middle link: Mamluk Egypt & Syria

This is the **direct ancestor of the European pack** — the deck Europe copied wholesale.

- **Names.** The game was **kanjifah** (Arabic; cf. Persian/Indian **ganjifa**). The deck
  was **Mulūk wa-nuwwāb** — "**Kings and Deputies**." Until the 15th century Europeans
  called the cards **naibi / naipes**, straight from Arabic **nā'ib** ("deputy"), still
  the **Spanish word for cards (*naipes*)** today.
- **Structure (the template tarot still uses).** **Four suits** — **coins, cups, swords,
  and polo-sticks** — each with **ten pip cards** and **three court cards**: the *malik*
  (king), the *nā'ib* (deputy/lieutenant), and the *nā'ib thānī* (second deputy). Europe
  kept this almost intact; it only turned the unfamiliar **polo-sticks into batons/clubs**
  and later added Queens.
- **The aniconic look.** Mamluk court cards carry **abstract geometric ornament and
  Arabic calligraphy, not human figures** — consistent with Sunni religious caution about
  depicting people. (Europe re-introduced figural kings and knights.)
- **Dating & the surviving object.** Cards are documented in Mamluk Egypt **by the 13th
  century** (some claims push to the 12th). The famous near-complete survivor — the
  **Topkapı deck** in Istanbul, rediscovered by **Leo Mayer in 1931** — is from the **late
  15th century**, i.e. a late example of a pattern already centuries old.
- **The unsolved gap.** *How* cards travelled from China to the Mamluk world is, in the
  literature's own words, **one of the great puzzles of gaming history** — Silk Road trade
  and Mongol-era contact are the usual guesses, but there is no documentary chain.

## 3. Were these cards used for divination?

The user's core question, answered honestly for each layer:

- **Mamluk / Islamic cards — almost certainly not, as far as the record shows.** Cards
  appear as a **game** (and, for some, gambling — though many Muslim families played and
  still play non-gambling card games). Strikingly, **card games barely appear in Islamic
  historical sources at all** — unlike **chess, backgammon, and other board games**, which
  are written about extensively. There is **no attested systematic card-divination** in
  the Mamluk world. The honest caveat: thin documentation plus perishable cards means
  *absence of evidence*, and people everywhere improvise folk fortune-telling with
  whatever is at hand — so informal, unrecorded use can't be ruled out. But a **tradition**
  of reading these cards? Not in evidence.
- **Chinese ancestral cards — a gambling technology, not an oracle.** The money-suited
  cards are tied to currency and betting. China developed fortune-telling card decks too,
  but those are best treated as a **parallel/later** strand, not the gaming money-cards
  that flowed west.
- **So where was the divination?** The Islamic world had an **enormously sophisticated
  divinatory culture** — but it ran on **other instruments**: **geomancy (ʿilm al-raml,
  "the science of the sand")**, **astrology (ʿilm al-nujūm)**, **bibliomancy and fālnāma
  (lot-books, often Qur'anic)**, dream interpretation, and the astrolabe. Divination was
  *everywhere*; it simply **wasn't done with playing cards**. This is the same pattern we
  see in Europe before 1781: rich divination traditions running *next to* a card-gaming
  tradition that stayed secular for centuries.

## Why this matters for the repo

Pushing the genealogy back to its Chinese and Islamic roots **strengthens the repo's core
thesis**, rather than complicating it. The entire ancestral line of tarot —
money-cards in China → naib cards in Mamluk Egypt → naipes/naibi in Europe → Italian
trionfi — is a **gaming and gambling lineage from end to end**. Divination is not at the
root; it is a **late European graft (1781/1788)** onto a 1,000-year-old game.

That also lets the library answer the romantic "ancient Eastern wisdom" intuition fairly:
there **is** a real, deep, *Eastern* origin for the cards — but it is **Chinese paper
money and Mamluk court games**, not Egyptian temple mysteries or a hidden oracular
tradition. The genuine ancient history is more interesting than the invented one, and it
points at commerce and play, not prophecy.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- The **9th-century Tang** card claim rests on the ambiguous "leaf game"; keep it flagged
  as plausible-but-soft, with the 1320 Yuan reference as the firmer anchor.
- The **China→Islam transmission route** is genuinely unknown — do not assert a path.
- "Cards in Mamluk Egypt by the 12th–13th c." spans competing claims; the **late-15th-c.
  Topkapı deck** is the firm surviving object.
- Worth a future note: **ganjifa** in Persia and Mughal India (a cousin branch that
  *stayed* aniconic/figural-circular and never became tarot).
