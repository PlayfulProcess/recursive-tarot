# Visconti-Sforza Tarot

> For *why* tarot was invented at all — the imported card game, the "fifth suit" of
> triumphs, and whether any divination tradition predates it — see the deep-dive
> `00b-origins-and-predecessors.mdx`.

## At a glance

The oldest **near-complete** tarot we have: a luxury, hand-painted deck made for the
ruling house of Milan around 1451. It is a **game** deck — the cards were still called
*trionfi*, not "tarot," and certainly not used for divination. Its trump sequence is the
headwater of the **C-order** branch that flows down to the Tarot de Marseille and, much
later, the occult decks.

## Origin & dating

"Visconti-Sforza" is a collective name for roughly **fifteen** related, incomplete sets
from the mid-15th century. The best-known (the Pierpont-Morgan-Bergamo deck, c.1451) was
commissioned around the marriage-alliance world of **Filippo Maria Visconti**, Duke of
Milan, and his successor and son-in-law **Francesco Sforza**. Much of the painting is
attributed to **Bonifacio Bembo** (or his workshop), an early-Renaissance Milanese
miniaturist. Materials are unambiguously elite: tempera, tooled gold leaf, heraldic
devices of the two dynasties.

## Provenance & evidence

The cards are dispersed across the Morgan Library (New York), the Accademia Carrara and
Colleoni collection (Bergamo), and elsewhere — hence the alternate name
*Colleoni-Baglioni*. Several decks are missing trumps (one later set has only the
Emperor and the Wheel surviving). The heraldry and the documented Visconti/Sforza
patronage anchor the dating better than for almost any other early deck.

## Game or divination?

**Game, unambiguously.** No divinatory apparatus, no fortune-telling text, no
cartomantic tradition attaches to these objects in their own century. They were played
as a trick-taking game by the Milanese court. Any "meaning" beyond the iconography is a
later overlay imported in the 18th–19th centuries.

## The fear question

**No fear.** This is conspicuous *consumption*, not clandestine ritual — a duke's
heraldry is literally painted on the cards. Playing was a marker of status. The only
contemporary anxiety around cards was the friars' campaign against **gambling** (see
Bernardino of Siena), and even that targeted the wager, not the imagery. A 1451 Milanese
courtier would have shown these off, not hidden them.

## Counter-voices

The "secret Egyptian/Hermetic deck" reading has been retro-fitted onto Visconti imagery
by later occultists, but nothing in the patron context, materials, or use supports it.
The figures are standard Italian Christian-allegorical types (Pope, Popess, the Virtues,
Death, the Wheel of Fortune) — courtly and Catholic, not crypto-pagan.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- Exact internal dating and workshop attribution of each of the ~15 sets is still
  debated; "c.1451" is a convenience label for the Morgan-Bergamo deck.
- The original trump *numbering* was not printed on the cards; reconstructed order
  depends on later C-order witnesses.
