# Belgian Tarot — Vandenborre / 'Tarot Flamand' (Brussels, c. 1780)

## At a glance

The **Belgian tarot** — the *Tarot Flamand* ("Flemish tarot") — is the standard 78-card
pack of the **Rouen-Brussels pattern**, conventionally attached to the Brussels
card-manufacturer **F. I. Vandenborre** and dated **c. 1780** [@web_ipcs_belgian;
@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_belgian_wp]. It is a **game deck** through and through — printed
and sold for the trick-taking game of *tarot* — and the cartomantic readings that travel with
tarot belong to the later French occult tradition, not to this Brussels playing pack
[@dummett1980; @web_belgian_wp]. Its historical importance is that it is the most polished
late witness of a tradition **distinct from the Tarot de Marseille**: the Belgian / Rouen-
Brussels line that descends from **Jacques Viéville** (Paris, c. 1650) through the earliest
dated maker **Gérard Bodet of Liège (1693)** [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles; @web_th_bodet;
@web_ipcs_belgian]. Its signature is **two substituted trumps**: card **II**, normally the
**Popess**, becomes **the Spanish Captain** (*L'Espagnol Capitano Eracasse* — "Captain
Fracasse"), and card **V**, normally the **Pope**, becomes **Bacchus** (*Bacus*), the wine-god
astride a barrel — with both clergy removed, the deck could be sold without offence in the
confessionally divided southern Netherlands [@web_ipcs_belgian; @web_th_spanish_captain;
@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp].

## Origin & dating

The pattern is **older than the Vandenborre firm**. Its French ancestor is the **Viéville**
tarot (Paris, c. 1650), and the **earliest dated maker** of the Belgian pattern proper is
**Gérard Bodet of Liège, 1693** — whose deck (restored by Sullivan Hismans) is the oldest
near-complete witness of the Rouen-Brussels line [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles; @web_th_bodet;
@web_ipcs_belgian]. Production ran from the **Prince-Bishopric of Liège**, then **Rouen**, then
the **Austrian Netherlands** from the late 17th to the late 18th century
[@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. **Vandenborre took over this type of card by c. 1780**, after
which his became the standard Belgian design; the firm was active **c. 1762–1803**
[@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_ipcs_belgian]. The date is soft: sources give **c. 1770 or c.
1780** for the tarot, and **"F. I." / "F. J. Vandenborre"** (François-Jean) is variably
expanded — flag both as unsettled (confidence: medium) [@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_belgian_wp].
Several other Belgian printers — **Jean Galler**, **Dupont**, **Jar**, **Adam C. de Hautot** —
made near-identical decks in the mid-to-late 18th century, so Vandenborre **standardised and
published**, rather than invented, the type [@web_tsr_galler; @web_pregebelin_belgian;
@web_thf_dehautot].

**The "Cartes de Suisses" myth.** From the mid-18th century the wrapping paper bore the
inscription ***"Cartes de Suisses"*** ("Swiss cards") [@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. The story
that a heavy late-16th-century French card tax drove cardmakers to emigrate to Switzerland,
where they devised this pack, is **a myth: there is no evidence of such an emigration, and no
evidence this type of tarot was ever made, sold, or used in Switzerland**
[@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. The "Swiss" label is a marketing tag, not a provenance — flag it
as a documented falsehood, not a clue.

## Provenance & evidence

The Belgian pattern is documented through surviving decks and uncut sheets in collectors' and
institutional hands: the **Bodet (Liège, 1693)** sheets, **Galler**, and the **Vandenborre**
editions are the principal witnesses [@web_th_bodet; @web_tsr_galler; @web_wopc_vandenborre].
A trade record fixes the late life of the pattern: in **1849 a pack of "Swiss Tarots, Cartes
de Suisses"** was advertised in Brussels by **"F. J. Vandenborre, cardmaker," comprising 78
cards** — confirming the firm's long persistence and the 78-card composition
[@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. The card images used by this collection's grammar are the
**public-domain "Tarot Belgijski" set on Wikimedia Commons (c. 1780)** — a **trumps-only**
selection at display resolution; clean public-domain scans of the Flemish *minors* have not
yet been located, so the grammar carries the 22 atouts only [@web_aeclectic_flamand]. The
**Spanish Captain (II)** design is reported to derive from an **engraving by Michel Lasne**, a
detail of independent art-historical interest (confidence: medium — single-tier web sourcing)
[@web_ipcs_belgian].

## Structure

A standard **78-card pack** in the Italian-suited tarot family, keeping the **Tarot de Marseille
count and the southern "C" trump order** but with the Belgian-line substitutions and variant
images [@web_ipcs_belgian; @web_belgian_wp; @dummett1980]:

- **22 trumps (atouts).** Numbered **I–XXI** with the additive forms (**IIII, VIIII, XVIIII**),
  plus the **unnumbered Fool — Le Fou** outside the count [@web_aeclectic_flamand;
  @web_ipcs_belgian]. The running order matches the Marseille C-order, with **Justice VIII and
  Strength XI** (the pre-Golden-Dawn arrangement) and the Fool unnumbered
  [@web_aeclectic_flamand; @web_belgian_wp]. The French titles on the cards are misspelt in the
  workshop's idiosyncratic spelling (*Le Bateleux, L'Imperatris, Lepen-du, LE'toille*)
  [@web_aeclectic_flamand].
  - **II — the Spanish Captain** (*L'Espagnol Capitano Eracasse*) replaces **La Papesse**: a
    swaggering Commedia dell'arte braggart-soldier in place of the female pope
    [@web_ipcs_belgian; @web_th_spanish_captain].
  - **V — Bacchus** (*Bacus*) replaces **Le Pape**: the wine-god seated astride a barrel in
    place of the pope [@web_ipcs_belgian; @web_cartes_de_suisses_wp].
  - **XII — the Hanged Man** (*Lepen-du*) hangs **right-side-up** — a Belgian/Viéville-line
    feature, against the inverted Marseille figure [@web_aeclectic_flamand; @web_belgian_wp].
  - **XVI — La Foudre** ("the Lightning"): a **tree (with a shepherd beneath) struck by
    lightning**, not the Marseille's struck **tower** (*La Maison Dieu*)
    [@web_aeclectic_flamand; @web_belgian_wp].
  - **XIII — Death** is named here (*La Mort*), unlike the deliberately unnamed Marseille
    XIII [@web_aeclectic_flamand].
- **56 minors,** four Italian suits (Coins, Cups, Swords, Batons) of **Ace–Ten plus four
  courts** — like the Marseille, with **non-scenic / emblematic pips** [@web_wopc_vandenborre;
  @web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. (The grammar carries only the trumps; the minors are documented
  but not imaged.)

## Game or divination?

**Game — unambiguously.** The Flemish tarot was a **card game** pack, part of the regulated
Brussels card trade [@dummett1980; @web_belgian_wp]. There is no native cartomantic tradition
attached to it; the upright/reversed "meanings" that circulate with tarot are the post-1780s
French occult overlay (Etteilla and his heirs), and they were built on the **Marseille** image,
not this Belgian one [@dummett1980; @decker1996]. The grammar should label the deck **"game"**
and present any meanings as a clearly-marked, foreign overlay.

## What changed from its parent

Its catalogue parent is **`vieville-tarot`** (the c. 1650 Paris ancestor of the Rouen-Brussels
line); the Belgian tarot is its **18th-century continuation and standardisation**, not a fresh
invention [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles; @dummett1980]. The features it **carries forward from
Viéville** are exactly the non-Marseille markers: the **upright Hanged Man**, the **La Foudre
tree-and-lightning** in place of the struck Tower, and divergent celestial/World imagery
[@web_aeclectic_flamand; @web_belgian_wp]. What the **Vandenborre/Belgian** pattern adds (or at
least fixes as standard) on top of the Viéville base are the **two trump substitutions** —
**Bacchus for the Pope** and the **Spanish Captain for the Popess** — which are not present in
Viéville's surviving deck and define the later Belgian pattern [@web_ipcs_belgian;
@web_th_spanish_captain]. Set against the **Marseille** line (the contrast that matters most),
the diff is sharper still: where Conver **retains** the Popess and Pope, the Belgian pattern
**edits both ecclesiastical trumps out** — the single clearest illustration in this collection
of a regional pack rewriting the clergy off the trumps, echoing (but distinct from) the
Besançon line's Jupiter/Juno swap for the same two cards [@web_th_spanish_captain;
@web_belgian_wp; @dummett1980].

## The fear question

**No concealment — a public, marketed product.** The substitutions are usually read as a
**secular / anticlerical softening**: with the awkward female pope and the politically charged
Roman pope both removed, the pack could be sold without offence in a region pulled between
Catholic and Protestant authority [@web_th_spanish_captain; @web_belgian_wp]. But the swaps are
worn openly on the cards, not hidden — and the "Swiss" wrapper is about **commerce, not
secrecy** (and is, in any case, a myth) [@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp]. So the "fear" here is best
read as **commercial prudence**, not the persecuted-occultist fear of the divinatory decks:
the makers edited two cards to widen the market, not to conceal a forbidden practice. The
*motive* for the swap is interpretive (confidence: medium — the substitution is documented; the
precise reason is inferred) [@web_th_spanish_captain].

## Counter-voices

- **"It's just a quirky Marseille variant."** The document-first reading, following Dummett, is
  that this is a **separate tradition** — Belgian / Rouen-Brussels, inherited more or less
  directly from **Bologna via Piedmont and Savoy**, not from the Marseille line. Calling it a
  "Marseille variant" obscures that ancestry [@dummett1980; @web_th_rouen_bruxelles]. (The
  grammar's `branch: "marseille"` metadata reflects the loose family resemblance, but is
  historically imprecise — see grammar errors.)
- **"Vandenborre created the Belgian tarot."** He **published and standardised** an existing
  pattern; Bodet (1693) predates him by most of a century, and Viéville (c. 1650) earlier still
  [@web_th_bodet; @web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- **The "Cartes de Suisses" Swiss origin.** A marketing myth with **no documentary support**
  [@web_cartes_de_suisses_wp].

## People

- **`vandenborre`** — F. I. (François-Jean) Vandenborre, the Brussels *cartier* whose imprint
  standardised the pattern c. 1780; treat as a **firm/imprint node**, person-level detail thin
  [@web_wopc_vandenborre; @web_ipcs_belgian].
- **`jacques-vieville`** — the c. 1650 Paris ancestor of the Rouen-Brussels line this deck
  belongs to (`derives_from: vieville-tarot`) [@web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- Contrast: **`nicolas-conver`** and the Marseille makers — the line against which the Belgian
  pattern is the principal Northern divergence (Pope/Popess replaced, not retained)
  [@web_belgian_wp]. Document-historian: **`michael-dummett`**, who classes the Belgian/Brussels
  tarots with the non-Marseille tradition [@dummett1980].
