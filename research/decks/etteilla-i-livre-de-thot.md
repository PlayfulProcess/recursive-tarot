# Etteilla I — Livre de Thot (Grand Etteilla)

## At a glance

The **first tarot deck designed expressly for divination** — not a game pack later *read* as
an oracle, but an oracle *built* as one. **Jean-Baptiste Alliette** ("Etteilla," 1738–1791)
commissioned the engraver **Pierre-François Basan** to cut a set of plates, and issued the
**Grand Etteilla / *Livre de Thot*** in **1788–89** [@web_pdr_etteilla; @tarot_heritage]. It is
a 78-card pack, but a thoroughly *reimagined* one: the trumps are **renumbered and renamed**
against the order Court de Gébelin and the Tarot de Marseille had fixed; the male and female
**querent significators** are written into the trump sequence; the **four elements** and a
crowd of **astrological correspondences** are encoded onto the first trumps; and — the
single most consequential innovation — every card carries **printed upright and reversed
keyword meanings on its borders**. That last feature is the direct ancestor of all modern
"card meanings" [@tarot_heritage; @decker1996]. Both things are true at once: Etteilla
**founded a real, durable cartomantic practice** *and* sold it under a **false ancient-Egyptian
pedigree** inherited from Gébelin.

## Origin & dating

Alliette read ordinary playing cards professionally first — his 1770 manual already used
spreads, reversals, and a fixed dictionary of meanings on a 32-card piquet pack — then, after
Court de Gébelin's 1781 Egyptian theory, applied the same machinery to the 78-card tarot in his
*cahiers* of **1783–85** [@web_etteilla_wp; @decker1996]. The **deck itself** followed: in
**1788** he had Basan engrave the (uncoloured) sheets; the pack was **published about 1 March
1789** — Etteilla's birthday — on eight large sheets that could be folded into a booklet or cut
into a deck, with a hand-coloured "luxury" edition also produced [@web_pdr_etteilla;
@tarot_heritage]. He founded the **Société des Interprètes du Livre de Thot** in 1788 to teach
the system [@web_etteilla_wp; @decker1996].

> The standard caution of the document-first school: although many of Etteilla's cards are
> "clearly recognizable as reproducing the Trumps from the Tarot of Marseilles, the order of
> the cards … and the astrological associations are altogether novel" [@web_pdr_etteilla].

## Provenance & evidence

The deck and the method-books survive and are well documented. High-resolution scans of the
original pack are catalogued at etteilla.org, which dates the *Le Jeu de Toth* to "around 1791"
and credits Basan with the illustrations [@web_etteilla_org]. Public Domain Review reproduces
the *Livre de Thot* with provenance notes [@web_pdr_etteilla]; Tarot Heritage (Marco Benedetti)
gives a modern reconstruction history [@tarot_heritage]; benebell wen has published a
card-by-card reconstruction of the meanings and correspondences [@web_benebell_etteilla]; and
the **etteillastrumps** translation project renders Etteilla's own trump commentary into English
[@web_etteillastrumps]. The Dummett spine (Decker–Depaulis–Dummett 1996) is the scholarly
backbone for dating and for separating Alliette's own 1788–89 deck from the **posthumous**
Etteilla II (c. 1838–40) and Etteilla III (c. 1865–70) editions [@decker1996].

## Structure

A standard-count **78-card pack** — but rebuilt. The cards are numbered **1 through 78** in
**Arabic numerals** (Etteilla believed Arabic numerals and the zero were Egyptian), with the
title printed on the **left and right borders** so the keyword can be read whichever way up the
card falls [@web_pdr_etteilla; @web_etteilla_org].

- **Trumps (the "majors"): 22 cards.** Numbered **1–21**, *plus* an **unnumbered / zero**
  card, **La Folie** (the Fool), the only trump left outside the running count
  [@web_etteilla_org; @web_pdr_etteilla].
  - **Cards 1–8 — a cosmogony.** A seven-days-of-creation sequence (plus the primordial Chaos):
    **1 Le Chaos, 2 La Lumière (day 1, light), 4 Le Ciel (day 2, the firmament),
    3 Les Plantes (day 3, vegetation), 6 Les Astres (day 4, sun/moon/stars),
    7 Les Oiseaux et les Poissons (day 5, birds and fishes), 5 L'Homme et les Quadrupèdes
    (day 6, land animals and humanity), 8 Le Repos (day 7, the Sabbath / rest)**. The four
    classical **elements** and the twelve **zodiac signs** are folded into these and the
    following trumps rather than appearing as separate cards [@web_pdr_etteilla;
    @web_benebell_etteilla].
  - **Cards 9–12 — the four cardinal virtues:** **Justice, Tempérance, Force, Prudence.**
    **La Prudence (12)** is Etteilla's substitute for the **Hanged Man**: read upside-down with
    the rope re-imagined as a serpent, the figure becomes a goddess of foresight
    [@web_etteillastrumps].
  - **Cards 13–21 — reworked Marseille trumps** (see the diff below).
- **Minors: 56 cards** in four suits of 14 — **Bâtons (Fire), Coupes (Water), Épées (Air),
  Deniers (Earth)** — King, Queen, Knight, Page plus Ace–Ten. The pips carry astrological
  correspondences (e.g. the ten Coins map to the seven planets, the Lot of Fortune and the two
  lunar nodes) [@web_pdr_etteilla; @web_benebell_etteilla].
- **Querent significators are embedded, not added.** The **male querent is Card 1 (Le Chaos)**
  and the **female querent is Card 8 (Le Repos)** — they double as cosmogony cards. There is
  **no separate pair of querent cards beyond the 78**, and **no four extra element cards**: the
  pack is 78, full stop. (This corrects a common repo note that the deck *adds* querent and
  element cards beyond the standard count — see Corrections owed.) [@web_pdr_etteilla;
  @web_benebell_etteilla]

## Game or divination?

**Divination — the first unambiguous case.** Every earlier pack in this collection was a game
deck that *acquired* a divinatory reading; this is the first deck **manufactured to tell
fortunes**, with the reading apparatus (numbered order, suit elements, upright/reversed
keywords) printed into the object itself [@tarot_heritage; @decker1996]. Mark **1788–89** as the
birth of the purpose-built divinatory deck, distinct from Gébelin's 1781 birth of the *theory*.

## What changed from its parent

Its parents are the **Tarot de Marseille** (the trump iconography and the four Latin suits) and
the **Court de Gébelin / Comte de Mellet theory** (the Egyptian framing and the idea that the
cards could be read) [@derives_from]. Against them, Etteilla:

1. **Broke the trump order.** Gébelin and the TdM fixed a sequence (I Bateleur … XIII Death …
   XXI World). Etteilla re-sequenced the trumps to mirror a creation-cosmogony he claimed to
   read off the Temple of Memphis, so that the first eight cards are Chaos + the seven days,
   and the familiar trumps scatter to new numbers [@web_pdr_etteilla; @web_etteillastrumps].
2. **Moved Death off XIII.** In every Marseille deck Death is trump XIII (often *unnamed*).
   Etteilla makes it **Card 17, "Mortalité,"** severing the number-13 association that the whole
   later tradition treats as fixed [@web_aeclectic_etteilla; @web_etteillastrumps]. *(This is
   the famous renumbering. The repo prompt's "13 → a different position" names the **departure
   from**; the destination is 17.)*
3. **Replaced trumps with new concepts.** The Hanged Man becomes **La Prudence (12)**; the
   Lovers become **Le Mariage (13)**, "Marriage/Union"; the Devil becomes **Force Majeure (14)**;
   the Bateleur/Magician becomes **Maladie (15)**, "Illness"; the Hermit becomes
   **Le Capucin (18)**; the Tower becomes **Le Temple Foudroyé (19)**; the Emperor/Chariot
   material is reworked into **Le Despote Africain (21)** [@web_etteillastrumps;
   @web_benebell_etteilla].
4. **Wrote the reading onto the card.** Etteilla "was the first to bake [reversals] into the
   actual design of the deck. Each card included both an upright and a reversed interpretation,
   printed directly on the card face" — the single most influential structural change, copied
   (in spirit) by every modern keyword deck since [@web_aeclectic_etteilla].
5. **Recast the courts as people.** The 16 court cards each signify **a person** in the
   querent's life, coloured by suit; upright they are well-disposed, reversed ill-disposed —
   the origin of the modern "court card = a person" convention [@web_aeclectic_etteilla].
6. **Layered on astrology.** Zodiac and planetary correspondences run through trumps and pips,
   absent from the Marseille game deck [@web_pdr_etteilla; @web_benebell_etteilla].

**Preserve, do not normalise.** Several Etteilla trumps have *no clean Marseille equivalent*
(the cosmogony cards especially), and even where a parent is visible the meaning has migrated.
The card-by-card file flags each mapping's confidence rather than forcing a one-to-one table —
the sources themselves warn that "attempts to correlate Etteilla's cards … can lead to madness"
[@web_pdr_etteilla].

## The fear question

**Conspicuously unafraid.** Etteilla signed his books, advertised in Paris papers, ran a
correspondence *school*, and dated his deck to his own birthday. In late-18th-century Paris,
cartomancy was fashionable and lucrative, and the literate male entrepreneur operated in broad
daylight — the sharpest contrast with the prosecuted, working-class and often Romani
fortune-tellers across the Channel, who faced vagrancy law for the same act [@decker1996;
@tarot_heritage].

## Counter-voices

Etteilla is the *origin* of the "recovered suppressed wisdom" story in tarot: he sincerely (and
profitably) presented his system as ancient Egyptian, a hieroglyphic "Book of Thoth." The
Egyptian pedigree is **false** — tarot is a 15th-century North-Italian game and hieroglyphs were
undeciphered until the 1820s — but the *practice* he founded is real and has lasted 230 years.
Honour the practice; flag the myth [@decker1996; @dummett1980].

A second counter-voice corrects the repo's own earlier notes (and this very prompt): the deck
does **not** "add querent and four element cards" beyond a standard pack. It is a 78-card deck
in which the querent significators *are* two of the trumps (1 and 8) and the elements are
*embedded* in the cosmogony trumps. The genuinely novel additions are conceptual (new trump
identities, printed reversals), not extra physical cards [@web_pdr_etteilla;
@web_benebell_etteilla].

## People

- **`etteilla`** — Jean-Baptiste Alliette, maker, author, and head of the Société.
- **`court-de-gebelin`** — supplied the Egyptian theory (1781) that Etteilla marketed.
- The engraver **Pierre-François Basan** cut the 1788 plates [@web_pdr_etteilla] (no person

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **The grammar's parent-mappings contain errors** (carried into `tarot/etteilla-i-livre-de-thot/grammar.json`):
  Card **13** is mapped to the **Hierophant** but is historically **Le Mariage = the Lovers**;
  Card **21** is mapped to the **Emperor** but is historically the **Chariot** (Etteilla's
  "African Despot"). See the cards file for the corrected mappings.
- **The female-significator label is misplaced.** The grammar tags Card 78 (**La Folie**) as the
  female querent; the female significator is actually **Card 8 (Le Repos)**. La Folie is the
  **Fool / 0**, the unnumbered trump.
- Confirm the first-edition date against a primary (1788 engraving vs. 1789 publication; both are
  defensible and reported together).
- The mid-trump correspondences (Cards 14–16, 19) rest on secondary scholarship and the
  etteillastrumps translation; a primary check of Etteilla's *Troisième cahier* would raise
  several from medium to high confidence.
