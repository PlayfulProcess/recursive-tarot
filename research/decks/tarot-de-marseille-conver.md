# Tarot de Marseille — Nicolas Conver, 1760 (Type II)

## At a glance

The **canonical printed standard** of Western tarot: a 78-card woodblock-and-stencil pack
signed and dated **1760** at Marseille and bearing the name **Nicolas Conver**, whose
"Type II" Marseille pattern became *the* reference layout copied by French cardmakers for
more than a century [@dummett1980; @depaulis1984; @web_camoin_conver1760]. It is, in origin
and through its whole early life, a **game deck** — printed and sold for the trick-taking
game *tarot* — but it is also the **hinge object** of the entire occult tradition: because
the Marseille was the common, available pattern in 1780s Paris, it is the very pack that
**Court de Gébelin** allegorised as an Egyptian "Book of Thoth" in 1781, that **Etteilla**
adapted into a divination system from the later 1780s, and from which **Oswald Wirth** and
the **Golden Dawn** later worked [@decker1996; @web_levi_rota; @web_wirth_wp]. The trumps
catalogued here are therefore the **image** the occultists read, not native divinatory
"meanings"; those are a documented later overlay. The deck carries a genuine **dating
puzzle**: the blocks read 1760, but the man the BnF documents as "Nicolas Conver, cartier"
was born in 1784 [@web_bnf_conver].

## Origin & dating

The "Marseille" pattern stabilised in 17th–18th-century French and Swiss card-making, and
the city of **Marseille** became the centre of its production — which is why the pattern
bears the city's name [@dummett1980; @depaulis1984]. The pack catalogued here is the
**Conver edition dated 1760**, engraved on pear-wood woodblocks and coloured through
stencils [@web_camoin_conver1760; @web_vs_conver_heritage]. It was **not** the first
Marseille-pattern deck. The modern typology — fixed by **Thierry Depaulis** — distinguishes
**Type I** (older, e.g. the Jean Noblet deck, c. 1650) from **Type II**, the slightly later
and far more widely copied family that includes **Pierre Madenié (1709)**, **François Héri
(1718)**, **François Chosson (1736)**, **Claude Burdel (1751)** and **Nicolas Conver (1760)**
[@depaulis1984; @web_th_rouen_bruxelles]. Conver's is the most renowned single witness of
the Type II pattern and the one "from which most subsequent Tarot de Marseille decks were
copied" [@web_vs_conver_heritage; @web_camoin_conver1760].

**The dating puzzle.** The blocks bear the date **1760**, but the **BnF authority record**
documents a *Nicolas Conver, cartier* of Marseille born **31 December 1784** and dying
**1833** — physically unable to have engraved blocks in 1760 [@web_bnf_conver]. His father
**Mathieu Conver (1746–1804)** is likewise too late. The document-minded reading, supported
by the **"V T" engraver's initials on the Chariot's shield**, is that the 1760 blocks were
**acquired or inherited from an earlier craftsman** and kept their original date — standard
trade practice, in which a *cartier* bought or inherited pear-wood moulds, erased the prior
maker's name, substituted his own, and left the date untouched [@web_vs_conver_heritage;
@letarot_assoc]. So "1760" is a real engraving date attached to the **object**; "Nicolas
Conver, its author" is the **contested** part — flag the authorship, not the date. The same
blocks were then printed by the Conver workshop, which passed (via marriage into a Conver
heir) to the **Camoin** firm and kept running the moulds for over a century; the pattern was
later re-standardised for the modern era by **Paul Marteau's** Grimaud *Ancien Tarot de
Marseille* (c. 1930) [@web_camoin_conver; @depaulis1984; @web_vs_conver_heritage].

## Provenance & evidence

Surviving Conver decks and Marseille-pattern sheets are held in public collections — the
**Bibliothèque nationale de France** (which has digitised Conver-pattern tarots on **Gallica**,
incl. a later re-edition at `ark:/12148/btv1b10543309g`) and the **British Museum** among them
[@web_gallica_conver; @bnf_gallica]. The Conver woodblocks themselves are claimed by the
successor **Camoin** firm, whose narrative reproduces and documents the 1760 edition
[@web_camoin_conver; @web_camoin_conver1760]. The lineage **Type I (Noblet c. 1650) → Type II
(… Chosson 1736 → Conver 1760) → Grimaud/Marteau (c. 1930)** is documented in the standard
catalogues [@depaulis1984; @dummett1980]. Card images used by this collection's grammar are
public-domain scans on Wikimedia Commons (Category: Tarot de Marseille — Nicolas Conver 1760).

## Structure

A standard **78-card pack**, in the pattern that became the European norm:

- **22 trumps (atouts / "majors").** Numbered with **Roman numerals I–XXI**, plus the
  **unnumbered Fool — Le Mat** ("the madman"), which carries no number and stands outside the
  running count [@web_fool_wp; @dummett1980]. The order is the **"C" order** (the southern /
  Marseille order) that became the occult standard [@dummett1980]:
  **I Le Bateleur, II La Papesse, III L'Impératrice, IIII L'Empereur, V Le Pape, VI L'Amoureux,
  VII Le Chariot, VIII La Justice, VIIII L'Hermite, X La Roue de Fortune, XI La Force,
  XII Le Pendu, XIII [Death, unnamed], XIIII Tempérance, XV Le Diable, XVI La Maison Dieu,
  XVII L'Étoile, XVIII La Lune, XVIIII Le Soleil, XX Le Jugement, XXI Le Monde.**
  - **Justice is VIII and Strength is XI** — the **pre-Golden-Dawn order**. The Rider-Waite
    tradition later *swapped* the two, making Strength VIII and Justice XI to align the
    images with the zodiac (Leo / Libra); the Marseille keeps the older **Justice VIII,
    Strength XI** [@web_marseille_wp; @web_gd_swap].
  - **Death (XIII) bears its number but no name** — a Marseille convention [@web_tower_wp;
    @dummett1980].
  - Roman numerals use the additive **IIII** (not IV) and **VIIII** (not IX), as on the blocks.
- **56 minors,** four Latin suits of 14: **Deniers (Coins) — Earth; Coupes (Cups) — Water;
  Épées (Swords) — Air; Bâtons (Batons) — Fire.** Each suit runs **Ace through Ten** plus a
  four-card court: **Roi (King), Reine (Queen), Cavalier (Knight), Valet (Page)**
  [@web_marseille_wp; @depaulis1984].
  - The **pips are non-scenic / emblematic** — repeating suit symbols woven into the woodcut's
    symmetrical floral scrollwork, *not* the narrative scenes Pamela Colman Smith would later
    invent for the Rider-Waite minors [@web_marseille_wp; @thetarotreader_marseille]. The
    **Aces** are the most ornamental, the suit symbol filling the card [@web_marseille_wp].
- **Element/suit mapping.** The Coins=Earth / Cups=Water / Swords=Air / Batons=Fire scheme
  used in the grammar is the **later occult correspondence** (Golden Dawn era), not a native
  feature of a 1760 game pack; treat it as a reading convention, not Conver's own
  (confidence: the suits are native; their *elemental* labels are an overlay)
  [@decker2002; @decker1996].

## Game or divination?

**Game first — unambiguously.** The Tarot de Marseille was printed and sold for the
trick-taking game *tarot* (the ancestor of today's French Tarot and the central-European
Tarock games), and the documentary record treats it as a game pack for its entire early life
[@dummett2004; @dummett1980]. On the documentary evidence, **no divinatory reading of these
trumps is attested before 1781** [@dummett1980; @decker1996]. The card-by-card "upright /
reversed" meanings that circulate with this deck are a **documented later overlay**, grafted
on by Etteilla and the occultists from the 1780s onward — they are not part of the object's
original tradition [@decker1996; @dummett1980]. The grammar should label the deck **"game"**
and present the meanings as a clearly-marked overlay, not an origin.

## What changed from its parent

This is a **root / ancestral** deck in the collection: it has **no `derives_from` parent**
within the catalogue. Iconographically it stands at the late, canonical end of the **Marseille
pattern lineage** — descending through the printing tradition from the earlier Marseille
cardmakers (Noblet, Type I; then the Type II line Madenié → Héri → Chosson → Burdel → Conver),
which in turn are worn, simplified, block-copied descendants of the 15th-century Italian
*trionfi* [@depaulis1984; @dummett1980]. There is no "diff against a parent" to draw here;
the relevant lineage is the **printing conservatism** that kept the images stable, block
copied from block, for generations [@dummett1980]. **Conver is the parent**, not the child:
it is the `derives_from` target of **`court-de-gebelin-tarot`**, **`etteilla-i-livre-de-thot`**,
**`oswald-wirth`**, and the **`golden-dawn`** tradition — the image those projects re-read,
re-numbered, and re-symbolised [@decker1996; @web_wirth_wp; @web_gd_swap].

## The fear question

**No fear as a game.** The Tarot de Marseille was an ordinary, regulated commercial product
of the French card trade, subject to the state playing-card tax and its control stamp
[@depaulis1984]. The instructive twist is later and external to the object: by the 19th
century the *same physical pattern* could be innocent leisure in one hand and, in another,
the working tool of a prosecuted fortune-teller — prosecution that ran on vagrancy and
anti-fraud law, not on anything about the cards [@dummett1980]. The deck did not change; the
**use and the user's social position** did.

## Counter-voices

From **Éliphas Lévi** onward, esoteric writers treated the Marseille's apparent "purity" as
evidence of preserved ancient (Egyptian / Kabbalistic) symbolism — and Conver's pattern,
being the most widely available and most "standard," became the canvas for that claim
[@levi1856; @decker1996]. The document-first reading is that the imagery is a worn,
simplified descendant of the Italian *trionfi*, and that its apparent "perfection" is
**printing-tradition conservatism** — block copied from block — rather than transmitted
doctrine [@dummett1980]. Both can be held at once: the Egyptian/Kabbalistic pedigree is
historically **false**, *and* the Marseille's role as the genuine **bridge object between
game and oracle** is real and central [@decker1996].

## People

- **`nicolas-conver`** — the *maître cartier* whose name the 1760 blocks bear, and the human
  anchor of the deck. Render the date as *"dated 1760"* with the authorship hedge (see the
  dating puzzle) [@web_bnf_conver; @web_camoin_conver].
- Downstream readers (each has its own dossier): **`court-de-gebelin`** (1781 Egyptian theory),
  **`etteilla`** (first divinatory adaptation), **Oswald Wirth**, and the **Golden Dawn**
  founders — all worked *from* the Marseille image [@decker1996; @web_wirth_wp].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Resolve the Conver identity.** Is the 1760 engraver a distinct, earlier hand (the "V T"
  initials on the Chariot shield), an inherited block re-signed by the 1784–1833 Nicolas
  Conver / his workshop, or a re-used date? The BnF dates (1784–1833; father Mathieu
  1746–1804) and the block date (1760) must be reconciled [@web_bnf_conver;
  @web_vs_conver_heritage].
- **Chosson "1736"** should be treated as contested, not settled, when dating the Type II line
  [@depaulis1984; @decker1996].
- **Distinguish pattern from edition** in the grammar: the *Marseille Type II pattern* (broad)
  vs. the *Conver 1760 edition* (this specific witness) [@depaulis1984].
- **Elemental suit labels** (Coins=Earth, etc.) are an occult overlay, not native to 1760;
  the grammar carries them as reading aids and should say so [@decker2002].
- Note the Besançon/Swiss **1JJ** variant (Jupiter/Juno for Pope/Popess) as a cousin pattern
  (see `15-besancon-1jj.mdx`).
