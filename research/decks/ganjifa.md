# Ganjifa — Persian & Mughal Circular Cards

## At a glance

Ganjifa is a tradition of **circular, hand-painted, often lacquered** playing cards — Persian
from the early 16th century, then magnificently developed in **Mughal India** and surviving in
folk workshops (Odisha, Karnataka, Maharashtra) to this day. Its very name — *ganjifa* / Arabic
*kanjifah* — is the **same word** as the Mamluk game, and the leading view is that the ganjifa
pattern was itself shaped under the influence of cards from the East (probably money-suited
decks) [@web_ganjifa_map]. **It is a cousin, not a parent, of tarot:** it is the branch of the
Islamic-world card tradition that flourished eastward into India and *never crossed into Europe*
to become the four-suit pack. Filed here, honestly, as adjacent card art — off the line that
became tarot.

## Origin & dating

The leading theory is that the **ganjifa pattern was created in Persia**, most likely under the
influence of playing cards from further east, and was **introduced into India by the Mughals**
[@web_ganjifa_wiki]. The firmest early documentary anchor is **Ahli Shirazi's *Rubaiyat-i-Ganjifa*
(1514–15)**, a poem of 96 verses that names the 8 suits and 96 cards of a ganjifa pack for the
first time, probably composed in Tabriz [@web_ganjifa_wiki]. In **1527 the emperor Babur** sent a
pack of ganjifa cards as a gift to a friend in Sind — an early sign of the game in the Mughal
orbit [@web_ganjifa_wiki]. The word *ganj* is Persian for "treasure, treasury or hoard"
[@web_ganjifa_wiki].

## Provenance & evidence

Surviving and documented sets are held by, among others, the **Ashmolean Museum** (Persian and
Indian playing cards) [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean] and the **Salar Jung Museum**; living craft
traditions are catalogued by the Asia InCH / India InCH encyclopedias [@web_ganjifa_map]. The
images used in this collection draw on **LACMA open access** and public-domain aggregates.

## Structure

The standard **Mughal ganjifa** is **96 cards in 8 suits, twelve cards per suit** — ten pip cards
(1–10) plus two court cards: a **king** (*shah*, also *padishah* or *mir*) and a **minister**
(*wazir*) [@web_ganjifa_map], [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean]. The eight Mughal suits are: **taj**
(crown), **safed** (silver coin), **shamsher** (sword/sabre), **ghulam** (servant), **chang**
(harp), **surkh** (gold coin), **barat** (document/draft), and **qimash** (merchandise/textiles)
[@web_ganjifa_ashmolean].

Two structural peculiarities mark it as a *cousin* rather than an ancestor of the Latin pack:
- **Eight suits, not four** (and the Hindu **Dashavatara** variant has **ten suits / 120 cards**,
  one suit per avatar of Vishnu) [@web_ganjifa_ashmolean], [@web_ganjifa_wiki].
- The pack splits into two halves (*bhedas*) with **opposite ranking**: in the "strong" half the
  ten is the highest pip and the one the lowest; in the "weak" half the order is reversed
  [@web_ganjifa_map]. This day/night, strong/weak division has no counterpart in the European
  four-suit deck.

## Game or divination?

**Game, not divination.** Ganjifa is a **trick-taking game** (commonly three players, played in
rounds), with the court cards strongest and no trump suit [@web_ganjifa_map]. There is no
cartomantic tradition attached to it; like the Mamluk pack, it is a game object, and the rich
divinatory cultures of Persia and India ran on *other* instruments.

## What changed from its parent

Omitted — ganjifa has no `derives_from` in this catalogue. It is best understood as a **sibling
branch** of the same Islamic-world card family as the Mamluk *kanjifah*: it shares the name and a
probable common eastern ancestry (money-suited cards), but it **multiplied the suits (to 8, then
10)**, **went circular**, and **stayed in the Persianate/Indian world**, where the European line
went four-suited, rectangular, and west [@web_ganjifa_map], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## The fear question

**Not applicable.** Ganjifa is openly courtly and, later, a popular folk craft; there is no
"suppressed history" of hidden meaning attached to it.

## Counter-voices

Because ganjifa is old, Eastern, and visually rich, it is sometimes drafted into "ancient wisdom"
genealogies of tarot. The corrective: it is genuinely related to the cards in your hands **as a
cousin**, sharing a probable money-suited ancestor with the Mamluk pack — but it is **not on the
line of descent to the European tarot** [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- The Persian origin and the eastern (money-suited) influence are the *leading* theory, not
  settled fact [@web_ganjifa_map] (confidence: medium).
- The exact relationship of the 8-suit Mughal pack to any 4-suit Persian forerunner is not fully
  documented; the 96-verse Ahli poem (1514–15) is the first naming of the 8 suits
  [@web_ganjifa_wiki].
- Datings for the Dashavatara (10-suit) version's creation vary in the literature (often given as
  17th century) and should be checked against a primary study before assertion.
