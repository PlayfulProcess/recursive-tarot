# The Cary Sheet

## At a glance

An **uncut sheet of Milanese tarot woodcuts**, c. 1500 — the **earliest surviving evidence of the
Tarot de Marseille pattern itself**, roughly a century before Nicolas Conver's standard 1760 edition
[@dummett1980; @web_dummettmondo_milan]. It is the **missing link** between the hand-painted Italian
court decks (Visconti-Sforza) and the printed C-order Marseille standard: the same trump designs,
already woodcut for mass printing [@web_th_carysheet_15c; @web_tm_carysheet]. Held at the **Beinecke
Rare Book & Manuscript Library, Yale** (Cary Collection of Playing Cards), it was a **game** object —
a printed *tarocchi* sheet, with no native divinatory meaning [@dummett1980; @beinecke_cary].
(confidence: medium — the Milan attribution and c. 1500 dating are Dummett's reasoned inference, not
a dated document.)

## Origin & dating

**Michael Dummett first published the sheet, in 1980**, and assigned it to **Milan, c. 1500**, on
the argument that tarot passed from Italy to France in the aftermath of the French invasions —
Charles VIII (1494) and Louis XII (1499) — during which Milan was under French administration for
years [@dummett1980; @web_dummettmondo_milan]. That timing makes a Milanese sheet of c. 1500 the
plausible **proximate ancestor of the French Tarot de Marseille** [@web_dummettmondo_milan;
@web_tm_carysheet]. Some descriptions broaden the bracket to "mid-16th century," but the standard
attribution is **Milan, c. 1500** [@web_occult_carysheet] (confidence: medium — no dated document;
Dummett's stylistic/historical argument).

## Provenance & evidence

- **Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library, Yale — Cary Collection of Playing Cards**; digitised in
  Yale's collections [@beinecke_cary; @web_tm_carysheet].
- First scholarly publication and Milan attribution: **Dummett 1980** (and his *Il Mondo e l'Angelo*,
  ch. 13 on Milan printed cards) [@dummett1980; @web_dummettmondo_milan].
- A **partial** sheet: it preserves several trumps plus a small remnant of suit cards (the last two
  visible cards are **cup** pips) [@web_pollett_carysheet] (confidence: low — exact card inventory
  varies across summaries; see the cards file).

## Structure

A fragment of a printed sheet, so the structure is read off what survives:

- **Trumps in the Marseille arrangement**, *untitled and unnumbered* on the sheet — players already
  knew the sequence [@web_occult_carysheet; @web_tm_carysheet]. Visible trumps reported include the
  **Tower, Devil, Moon, Star, Sun, Temperance, Wheel of Fortune, Hanged Man, Chariot, Lovers,
  Justice, Strength, Magician, Fool**, and an Emperor/Empress/Pope–Popess group [@web_tm_carysheet;
  @web_occult_carysheet] (confidence: low–medium — lists differ; treat as the visible repertoire,
  not a settled count).
- A **small remnant of suit (pip) cards** — the surviving suit fragments are **cups**
  [@web_pollett_carysheet].
- **Woodcut, for mass printing** — the technological break from the hand-painted court packs
  [@web_th_carysheet_15c].

## Game or divination?

**Game.** A printed *tarocchi* sheet for play; no divinatory apparatus, no fortune-telling text. The
Marseille **cartomantic** overlay is an 18th-century addition (Court de Gébelin, Etteilla)
[@dummett1980].

## Why it matters

The Cary Sheet is the **earliest witness to the specific Marseille trump iconography** — already
recognisable in the **lightning-struck Tower**, the **Star woman pouring from two jars**, and the
**Moon with two dogs/towers and a crayfish** [@web_th_carysheet_15c; @web_tm_carysheet]. Notably,
the **Devil and the Tower** appear here when they are absent from the surviving hand-painted
Milanese sets — the Cary Sheet is the earliest place we can see them [@web_th_carysheet_15c]. It is
"obviously older than any surviving Tarot de Marseille" and bridges the Italian predecessors and the
French printed standard [@web_dummettmondo_milan; @web_tm_carysheet].

## The fear question

**No fear.** A commercial printed game sheet; the only contemporary anxiety was the friars' campaign
against **gambling**, not the imagery [@dummett1980].

## Counter-voices

Occult origin-myths attach to the Marseille trumps downstream, but the Cary Sheet shows the pattern
arising as **printed game iconography in Milan c. 1500**, with no esoteric apparatus
[@dummett1980; @web_dummettmondo_milan]. The genuine open questions are the **exact date**, the
**precise card inventory**, and whether the sheet is strictly Milanese or a broader Lombard product.
