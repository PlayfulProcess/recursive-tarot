# Sola Busca Tarot

## At a glance

The **earliest surviving complete 78-card tarot** and the **first fully-engraved, fully-illustrated**
deck — and, decisively, the **first whose 56 number (pip) cards all carry figural scenes** rather
than bare arrangements of suit-signs [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_pdr; @web_sb_wopc]. Engraved on copper
around **1491** in northern Italy (Ferrara/Venice circle), it is a **sui-generis** pack: its 22
trumps abandon the standard tarot cast (Fool, Popess, Death, Tower, World…) for a roll-call of
**named Roman, biblical and legendary figures** — Mario (Marius), Catone (Cato), Nerone (Nero),
Nenbroto (Nimrod), Nabuchodenasor (Nebuchadnezzar) — each labelled in Latin
[@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_trumps; @web_sb_wopc]. It is still a **game** pack (with a learned humanist /
hermetic-alchemical overlay), not a divination manual. Four centuries later its scenic minors
became a **documented model** for **Pamela Colman Smith**, who studied the deck as black-and-white
photographs at the **British Museum (1907/1908)** before drawing the fully scenic minors of the
1909 Rider-Waite-Smith pack [@web_sb_pcs; @web_sb_wp; @web_sb_mayer_pcs]. Read it **in its own
tradition**: do not flatten its classical figures onto Marseille trumps.

## Origin & dating

Scholarly consensus dates the **engravings to 1491** — a date suggested by an inscription on one
of the illuminated cards — and places the engraving stage in **northern Italy, most likely
Ferrara**, with the surviving complete set **hand-coloured (with gold) about a decade later, for
or in Venice** [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_pdr; @web_sb_mayer_hist] (confidence: medium on the precise
Ferrara-engraving / Venice-colouring split — both stages are inferred, and Ferrara vs. Venice is
still discussed).

Two attributions recur, both **hypotheses rather than documented facts**:
- The **engraver/artist** is associated by some scholars with **Nicola di Maestro Antonio of
  Ancona** (documented c. 1465–1511), on stylistic grounds (the Squarcionesque "forced poses,
  nervous profiles, heightened expressiveness") [@web_sb_lazzarelli; @web_sb_wp]
  (confidence: low–medium — a style attribution, not a signature; the named hand is unidentified
  and the grammar's "Master of the Sola Busca" is a convenience label).
- The **conception of the iconographic programme** is attributed to the humanist **Ludovico
  (Lodovico) Lazzarelli** (b. 1447, San Severino Marche), translator of the *Corpus Hermeticum*
  into Latin and author of the *Crater Hermetis*, who stood close to the hermetic culture that
  Marsilio Ficino had made fashionable [@web_sb_lazzarelli; @web_lazzarelli_wp]
  (confidence: low–medium — an attractive and much-repeated hypothesis, not a contracted
  commission).

The deck takes its name from two later owners — **Count Sola** and the **Marchese Busca** — whose
illuminated copy descended in the **Sola-Busca-Serbelloni** family [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_finestre].

## Provenance & evidence

The surviving record is unusually concrete for a 15th-century pack:

- **One complete hand-coloured deck (all 78 cards)** — the unique illuminated exemplar, **bought
  by the Italian state (Ministero per i Beni e le Attività Culturali) for €800,000 in 2009** and
  delivered to the **Pinacoteca di Brera, Milan**, where it now lives and its images are public
  domain [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_finestre; @web_sb_italianways].
- **Uncoloured engraved impressions** survive in scattered museums — the often-cited count is
  **~35 uncoloured cards** across collections; the **British Museum** holds engraved impressions
  (acquired **1845**), including court cards [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_wopc; @web_sb_mayer_hist]
  (confidence: medium on exact counts — secondary summaries vary between "23" and "35"; treat the
  number as approximate pending a primary catalogue check).
- **The 1907 British-Museum photographs.** In **1907** the **Busca-Serbelloni family sent
  black-and-white photographs of the complete deck from Milan to the British Museum**, where they
  were exhibited (some sources say **1908**) alongside the Museum's original engravings — the
  conduit by which **A. E. Waite and Pamela Colman Smith** could study every card
  [@web_sb_pcs; @web_sb_mayer_pcs; @web_sb_wp] (confidence: high on the gift/exhibition; the
  1907-vs-1908 exhibition date is reported both ways and is flagged here).

## Structure

A full **78-card** pack — **22 trumps + 56 minors** — and the **earliest deck known in exactly
this shape**, the structure most later divinatory decks (RWS, Thoth) emulate [@web_sb_wp;
@web_sb_structure]. What is **standard** and what is **peculiar**:

- **Trumps (22):** numbered 0–21 and each inscribed with a **Latin personal name** — Mato,
  Panfilio, Postumio, Lenpio, Mario, Catulo, Sesto, Deo Tauro, Nerone, Falco, Venturio, Tulio,
  Carbone, Catone, Bocho, Metelo, Olivo, Ipeo, Lentulo, Sabino, Nenbroto, Nabuchodenasor. Every
  trump bears a name **except Mato** (the Fool / 0) [@web_sb_trumps; @web_sb_wopc; @web_sb_wp].
  These are **not** the standard triumphs: there is **no Popess, Hanged Man, Death, Devil, Tower
  or World** in the Marseille sense — the sequence is the deck's own invention, "a humanist
  honour-roll of antiquity," with many figures shown in armour (the deck is sometimes called the
  **"warriors' deck"**) [@web_sb_ciompi; @web_sb_wp].
- **Minors (56):** four suits — **Cups, Coins, Batons (Clubs/Sticks), Swords** — each with
  **Ace–Ten plus four courts (King, Queen, Knight, Page)**, i.e. the standard 14-card suit and
  16 courts. The **suit-emblems are stylised**: amphorae for Cups, discs for Coins, clubs for
  Batons; Swords are conventional [@web_sb_ciompi; @web_sb_structure].
- **Decisive peculiarity — scenic pips.** **All 56 minors are fully illustrated figural scenes**,
  not pip-arrays. With the exception of the Aces, each numbered/court card carries a small Arabic
  number (Pages 11, Knights 12, Queens 13, Kings 14) [@web_sb_ciompi; @web_sb_pdr]. The **named
  figures extend into the courts**: Kings, Queens and Knights bear classical names (e.g. the
  Alexander-cycle figures **Amone**, **Olinpia**, **Natanabo**), while the **Pages are unnamed**
  [@web_sb_ciompi] (confidence: medium — court names from a single detailed secondary source,
  consistent with the engraved inscriptions).
- **No divinatory apparatus** — no upright/reversed meanings, astrology or fortune-telling text;
  any "meaning" is a much later overlay.

## Game or divination?

**A game pack — with an erudite overlay, but no native divination.** Its imagery is genuinely
"linked in an irrefutable way to the hermetic-alchemical tradition" of 1490s Italy, but that means
**moral-philosophical and alchemical allegory**, not a cartomantic system; there is no evidence it
was ever used to tell fortunes [@web_sb_pdr; @web_sb_wopc; @web_sb_mayer_alchemy]. It is the
**exception that flatters the rule**: even a deeply learned Renaissance programme was made for
**play and contemplation**, not divination. Because of this, the **card-by-card file describes the
named figure (trumps) and the engraved scene (minors)** — not upright/reversed meanings, which
this deck does not natively have.

## What changed from its parent

**Not applicable — this is a root, sui-generis deck** (`derives_from: []`). The Sola Busca does not
descend from the Visconti-Sforza or Marseille line and is not a parent of the main tarot trunk; it
is a brilliant **one-off**. Its trumps are not a re-titling of standard subjects but a wholly
**different cast**, so there is no trump-by-trump "diff" to draw. The only meaningful derivation
runs **downstream and partial**: its scenic-pip *idea* (not its trump programme) re-surfaces, via
the 1907 photographs, in the Rider-Waite-Smith minors — documented influence, not lineage.

## The fear question

**No fear in its own time.** A costly engraved luxury for a learned patron, its hermetic-alchemical
content was **fashionable high culture** in 1490s Italy — openly admired humanism, not occult
contraband [@web_sb_pdr; @web_sb_wopc]. The named Roman and biblical heroes are the stuff of
Renaissance moral exemplum, not heresy; nothing about the pack invited concealment.

## Counter-voices

Sola Busca is the **strongest good-faith card** in the "tarot was always esoteric" hand: here is
**real Renaissance hermeticism on a real tarot deck, dated 1491** — worth stating honestly
[@web_sb_pdr; @web_sb_mayer_alchemy]. But two qualifications hold the line:
1. The programme is **alchemical-moral allegory**, not a divinatory system, and there is no sign
   it was read as one in its own century.
2. It is **sui-generis** and did **not found** the occult-tarot lineage — the 18th-century French
   (Court de Gébelin, Etteilla) did that, retroactively and from the Marseille pack, not from
   Sola Busca. Later attempts to map its classical figures onto standard trumps (Catone = Death,
   Nerone = Strength, etc.) are **loose modern back-readings** and should not be flattened into
   equivalences [@web_sb_trumps; @web_sb_wp].

## People

- **`pamela-colman-smith`** — *downstream influence, not maker.* Smith almost certainly studied
  the Sola Busca minors among the British-Museum photographs (Waite, who could not draw them
  himself, is reported to have pointed her to them), and several RWS minor scenes echo Sola Busca
  compositions — most often cited the **Three of Swords** (the pierced heart) and the **Ten of
  Wands / Ten of Swords** [@web_sb_pcs; @web_sb_wp; @web_sb_mayer_pcs]. The scenic-pip *innovation*
  on the modern deck is **Smith's own**; Sola Busca supplied the precedent and some specific
  compositions. She is credited here for that downstream link.
- **(hypothesised, not linked as `people/` ids)** — **Ludovico Lazzarelli** as probable author of
  the iconographic programme, and **Nicola di Maestro Antonio** as the stylistically-proposed
  engraver; both are attributions, not documented facts, and are left in the prose rather than
  asserted as makers [@web_sb_lazzarelli; @web_lazzarelli_wp].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Engraving place:** Ferrara vs. Venice for the engraving stage remains discussed; "N. Italy,
  Ferrara/Venice circle" is the safe formulation [@web_sb_wp; @web_sb_mayer_hist].
- **Authorship:** Lazzarelli (programme) and Nicola di Maestro Antonio (engraving) are
  **hypotheses**; do not state either as fact.
- **Uncoloured-card count:** "~35" (and "23 at the British Museum") are repeated secondhand;
  pin against a primary catalogue.
- **1907 vs. 1908:** the photographs were *given* in 1907; the *exhibition* date is reported both
  ways — flag until a primary source settles it.
- **The RWS echoes are inferred from visual parallels**, not a documented commission note; assert
  influence, not instruction, beyond the Three of Swords / Ten of Wands cases.
- **Grammar notes (do not edit here):** the deck grammar's per-card "Scene" texts are **generic
  placeholders** ("a fully engraved figural scene…"), not descriptions of the actual engraving;
  the **court figures are named** in the original (Kings/Queens/Knights), which the grammar does
  not record; and the standard tarot court order is **King–Queen–Knight–Page** (the grammar lists
  Page→King by ascending number). These are corrections owed to the grammar, logged for a
  maintainer pass — not made by this research agent.
