# Visconti-Sforza Tarot

## At a glance

The oldest **near-complete tarot** that survives: a luxury, hand-painted *trionfi* pack made
for the ruling house of Milan around **1451**, traditionally attributed to the **workshop of
Bonifacio Bembo** [@moakley1966; @web_morgan_vs_ms; @web_wopc_vs1460]. It was a **game** deck —
the cards were called *carte da trionfi*, not "tarot," and carried **no divinatory meanings** in
their own century [@dummett1980; @web_vs_wp]. Its trump sequence is a headwater of the
**C-order** branch that flows down toward the Tarot de Marseille and, centuries later, the occult
decks. The surviving pack is **composite**: roughly **74 of the original 78** cards survive,
dispersed across three Bergamo/New-York collections; **four cards are lost** and **six trumps were
repainted by a later hand** a generation or two after the original [@web_th_vs_replacements;
@web_morgan_vs_ms]. Read these images as **Petrarchan triumphs in the merry mood of Carnival**
(Moakley), not as a coded Egyptian or kabbalistic book [@moakley1966; @web_moakley_magus].

## Origin & dating

"Visconti-Sforza" is best used for **one specific pack** — the **Pierpont-Morgan–Bergamo** deck
(also called **Colleoni-Baglioni**), c. 1450–1460, conventionally dated **c. 1451**
[@web_vs_wp; @web_wopc_vs1460]. It is one of roughly **fifteen** related, incomplete hand-painted
sets from the mid-15th-century Milanese court; do not confuse it with the even more lavish
**Cary-Yale / Visconti di Modrone** pack (a separate deck slug, with its anomalous six-court
structure) [@web_cary_yale; @web_vs_wp].

The deck was commissioned in the marriage-alliance world of **Filippo Maria Visconti**, last
Visconti Duke of Milan, and his daughter **Bianca Maria Visconti** with her husband **Francesco
Sforza**, who became the first Sforza Duke in 1450 [@web_filippo_wp; @web_bianca_wp;
@web_francesco_wp]. The painting is **traditionally attributed to Bonifacio Bembo (or his
workshop)**, an attribution enshrined by **Roberto Longhi (1928)** and **Gertrude Moakley (1966)**
[@moakley1966; @web_vs_wp]. The materials are unambiguously elite — tempera, tooled and punched
gold-leaf grounds, and the heraldic devices of the two dynasties — which is precisely why the
dating is so secure: the patrons' arms are painted onto the cards [@web_wopc_vs1460;
@web_th_vs_visit].

## Provenance & evidence

The surviving cards are split across **three holdings**, all corroborated by the Morgan catalogue
and standard references:

- **Pierpont Morgan Library & Museum, New York — 35 cards** (MS M.630.1–35) [@web_morgan_vs_ms;
  @morgan_visconti].
- **Accademia Carrara, Bergamo — 26 cards** [@web_vs_wp; @web_th_vs_visit].
- **Casa Colleoni (Colleoni family private collection), Bergamo — 13 cards** [@web_vs_wp;
  @web_learntarot_vs].

That is **35 + 26 + 13 = 74** surviving cards of an original 78 [@web_vs_wp; @web_usgames_vs].

**Four cards are lost** and have never been recovered: **The Devil**, **The Tower**, the
**Knight of Coins**, and the **Three of Swords** [@web_greer_devil; @web_usgames_vs; @web_vs_wp].
For the two missing pips/courts (Knight of Coins, Three of Swords) the absence is plainly
accidental — a complete *tarocchi* pack has a full Ace-to-Ten plus four courts per suit, so they
certainly existed and were simply lost [@web_usgames_vs]. For the **Devil and Tower** there is a
genuine scholarly question whether Bembo's workshop *ever painted them*: no early Milanese example
of either survives, and some scholars hold the Visconti packs may not have included them
[@web_greer_devil] (confidence: medium — argued from absence; no document settles it).

**Six trumps were repainted by a different, later hand** — visibly different in style from the
first artist — to replace originals that had been lost or damaged: **Fortitude (Strength),
Temperance, The Star, The Moon, The Sun, and The World** [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp;
@web_morgan_vs_collection]. These are the "six replacement cards" the literature warns buyers
about. The Morgan explicitly catalogues at least three of them — **Temperance, Fortitude, and the
Sun** — as illuminated **ca. 1480**, by a second hand [@web_morgan_vs_ms;
@web_morgan_vs_collection].

## Structure

A standard-shape early tarot pack of **78 cards**, with the **standard four-court structure** —
**King, Queen, Knight (Cavallo), and Page (Fante)** in each suit, i.e. **16 court cards**
[@web_wopc_vs1460; @web_vs_wp]. This is worth stating because the related **Cary-Yale** pack is
the anomaly with **six** courts per suit (adding a mounted Lady and a Damsel); the
**Visconti-Sforza is *not* six-court** [@web_cary_yale].

- **Trumps (*trionfi*): 22**, unnumbered and (mostly) unlabelled on the cards themselves —
  the order is reconstructed from later C-order witnesses [@dummett1980; @web_vs_wp]. The Fool
  and Death are the cards "too plain to need a number."
- **Minors: 56** in four Latin suits — **Coins/Denari (Earth), Cups/Coppe (Water),
  Swords/Spade (Air), Batons/Bastoni (Fire)** — Ace–Ten plus the four courts. The pips are
  **decorative, not scenic**: the suit-emblems are arranged on punched gold grounds with
  heraldic flourishes, with no narrative picture (the scenic pip is a much later Sola-Busca / RWS
  invention) [@web_wopc_vs1460; @web_th_vs_visit].
- **No numerals, no titles, no reversals, no astrology** printed on the cards — none of the
  divinatory apparatus that Etteilla would later bake into a deck. The object is a game pack.

## Game or divination?

**Game, unambiguously.** No divinatory apparatus, no fortune-telling text, and no cartomantic
tradition attaches to these objects in their own century; they were played as a **trick-taking
game** at the Milanese court [@dummett1980; @web_vs_wp]. Every "meaning" beyond the iconography is
a later overlay, imported in the 18th–19th centuries with Court de Gébelin and Etteilla. **The
card-by-card file therefore describes iconography and the triumph each card represents — not
upright/reversed fortune-telling meanings**, which this deck does not natively have.

## What changed from its parent

**Not applicable — this is a root deck** (`derives_from: []`). The Visconti-Sforza pack is an
**ancestor**, not a descendant: it stands near the head of the surviving record, and later decks
diff *against it*, not the reverse. (The only "parent" question that arises is internal to the
Visconti family of packs — whether the Cary-Yale precedes or postdates it — and that is a
sibling-dating question, not a derivation.)

## The fear question

**No fear.** This is conspicuous *consumption*, not clandestine ritual — a duke's heraldry is
literally painted on the cards, and playing was a marker of status [@web_vs_wp; @web_wopc_vs1460].
The only contemporary anxiety around cards was the friars' campaign against **gambling** (e.g.
Bernardino of Siena), and even that targeted the **wager**, not the imagery. A 1451 Milanese
courtier would have shown these off, not hidden them. The one card that brushes against danger —
the **Popess**, possibly a memory of the burned heretic Maifreda da Pirovano — is rendered in the
same gold as the kings, a buried family memory displayed rather than concealed
[@web_th_vs_popess; @web_greer_maifreda].

## Counter-voices

The **"secret Egyptian/Hermetic deck"** reading was retro-fitted onto Visconti imagery by later
occultists, but nothing in the patron context, materials, or use supports it. **Gertrude Moakley
dissolved the occult origin-myth on iconographic grounds**: the trumps are intelligible as a
**procession of Petrarchan triumphs** — Love, Chastity, Death, Fame, Time, Eternity — excerpted
and rearranged "in the merry mood of Carnival," with the Mountebank as Carnival King and the Fool
as the personification of Lent [@moakley1966; @web_moakley_magus; @web_pregebelin_moakley]. No
Egyptian or Hebrew key is needed to explain figures that were standard Italian
Christian-allegorical types (Pope, Popess, the Virtues, Death, the Wheel of Fortune) [@dummett1980].

A second counter-voice is **internal to the attribution**. "Bembo" is the conventional label, not
a settled fact: **Giuliana Algeri** argued the painter was instead **Francesco Zavattari**, on
stylistic grounds, and the Metropolitan Museum hedges by cataloguing its cards under "Workshop of
Bonifacio Bembo" [@web_vs_wp; @web_met_bembo]. Likewise the popular attribution of the **six
replacement cards to Antonio Cicognara** is shaky: it rests on a **19th-century literary forgery**
and is rejected by modern scholarship, which proposes an unidentified second hand (sometimes
**Franco dei Russi**) working c. 1465–80 [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp] (confidence: medium
— the "Cicognara" name is still widely repeated, but the document underpinning it is discredited).

## People

- **`bonifacio-bembo`** — Cremonese court painter; the luxury pack is **traditionally attributed
  to his workshop** (Zavattari proposed as the rival hand) [@moakley1966; @web_met_bembo;
  @web_vs_wp].
- **`visconti-sforza-patrons`** — Filippo Maria Visconti, Bianca Maria Visconti, and Francesco
  Sforza, who **commissioned** (did not paint) the deck; their heraldry dates it
  [@web_filippo_wp; @web_bianca_wp; @web_francesco_wp].
- **`gertrude-moakley`** — the NYPL librarian whose 1966 study gave this pack its enduring
  Petrarchan-triumph reading and its (now-hedged) Bembo attribution [@moakley1966].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **The six vs. seven "later hand" cards.** The repo grammar tags **seven** trumps as
  `painter_hand: "second"` — Fortitude, Temperance, Star, Moon, Sun, World **and Judgement**.
  The corroborated list of replacement cards is **six** and **does not include Judgement (#20)**,
  which is an original-hand card. *Correct the grammar* by setting `major-20-il-giudizio`
  `painter_hand` back to `bembo`/original [@web_th_vs_replacements; @web_vs_wp].
- **Cicognara attribution.** The grammar's History notes name **Antonio Cicognara** for the
  replacement hand "(c.1480s–90s)." Keep the name only with a hedge: it rests on a discredited
  19th-c. forgery; modern scholarship rejects it and proposes Franco dei Russi or an unnamed hand
  c. 1465–80 [@web_th_vs_replacements].
- **Did the Devil and Tower ever exist here?** Treat as open — the two lost trumps may never have
  been painted for this pack [@web_greer_devil].
- **Bembo vs. Zavattari.** The individual hand is genuinely debated; assert "Milanese court work,
  mid-15th c.," not a flat "by Bembo" [@web_vs_wp; @web_met_bembo].
- **Exact internal dating** of each of the ~15 related sets remains debated; "c. 1451" is a
  convenience label for the Morgan-Bergamo deck, and the original trump numbering was never
  printed [@dummett1980; @web_vs_wp].
