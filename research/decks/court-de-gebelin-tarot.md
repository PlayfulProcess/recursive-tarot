# Court de Gébelin's Tarot (Le Monde Primitif, vol. VIII, 1781)

## At a glance

This is not a deck made to be played with, and not, strictly, a deck made to be sold: it is a
set of engraved plates illustrating an **essay**. In 1781 the Protestant pastor and scholar
**Antoine Court de Gébelin** devoted a section of volume VIII of his vast comparative
encyclopedia *Le Monde primitif, analysé et comparé avec le monde moderne* to the Tarot,
declaring it the last survivor of an ancient Egyptian **"Book of Thoth"** — a hieroglyphic
priestly encyclopedia carried into Europe by "the Gypsies." The plates reproduce the
ordinary **Tarot de Marseille** pattern but "corrected" toward the Egyptian originals he
imagined: figures relabelled and re-read (the Papess becomes a High Priestess of Isis, the
Star becomes the Dog-Star Sirius heralding the Nile flood, the World becomes Time/Isis, the
Hanged Man is declared to be Prudence printed upside-down). Bundled into the same section was
a second, shorter essay by the **Comte de Mellet**, which went further: it linked the trumps
to the **Hebrew alphabet**, read them in **reverse** as an Egyptian cosmogony of three Ages,
and gave the **first published method of cartomancy** with the tarot pack. Gébelin offered no
evidence and was wrong on every historical point — but this single section converted a card
game into a "sacred text" and launched two centuries of occult tarot. The repo treats it as
the **origin-myth**, never as evidence about the cards' actual fifteenth-century Italian
origin.

## Origin & dating

- **Publication.** The tarot material occupies a section of **volume VIII** (the second
  "tome huitième") of *Le Monde primitif*, **Paris, 1781**, beginning around **page 365**
  [@web_marykgreer_gebelin; @web_villarevak_gebelin]. The encyclopedia as a whole appeared in
  nine volumes between 1773/1775 and 1784 [@decker1996].
- **Two essays, one section.** Gébelin's own essay is **"Du Jeu des Tarots"**; it is followed
  in the same volume by **"Recherches sur les Tarots, et sur la Divination par les Cartes des
  Tarots, par M. le C. de M.***"** — the work of the **Comte de Mellet** [@web_letarot_divination;
  @decker1996]. The two men's claims overlap but **differ** (see *What changed* and the
  card-by-card file); conflating them is the single most common error in popular accounts.
- **The discovery anecdote.** Gébelin tells how he first saw the pack at a salon, in the hands
  of "**Madame la C. d'H.**" (a lady newly arrived from Germany or Switzerland), and
  "recognised" the Egyptian allegory on the spot — "within a quarter of an hour, the pack was
  perused, explained, declared Egyptian" [@web_traditionaltarot_gebelin; @web_villarevak_gebelin].
  The salon is conventionally identified as Madame Helvétius's (confidence: medium — the
  identification is widely repeated but Gébelin's text only gives the initials "C. d'H.").
- **No evidentiary basis.** The essay predates **Champollion's decipherment of hieroglyphs
  (1822–24)** by four decades; the "Egyptian" reading is projection onto Italian Christian
  imagery, and later Egyptologists found nothing in the language to support his etymologies
  [@web_villarevak_gebelin; @decker1996].

## Provenance & evidence

- **What survives.** The printed text and the copperplate engravings of all 22 trumps (plus
  suit illustrations) survive in *Le Monde primitif* vol. VIII and are public-domain; scans
  circulate via Gallica/BnF and Wikimedia Commons [@bnf_gallica].
- **The plates themselves.** They are **uncoloured engravings** that reproduce the **Marseille
  pattern** the essay was written against, with deliberate "corrections" to support the thesis
  (the most famous being the Hanged Man, which Gébelin and Mellet reproduce **inverted** —
  right-side-up relative to the Marseille — to make him "Prudence")
  [@web_alamy_gebelin; @web_robertplace_firstoccult; @decker1996]. The engraver is not securely
  named in the sources consulted (confidence: low — see Open questions).
- **Not a usable deck.** No evidence that these plates were ever issued or used as playing
  cards; they are book illustrations. (The modern "Le Monde Primitif Tarot" / "First Occult
  Tarot" products are twenty-first-century reconstructions, not eighteenth-century objects
  [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].)

## Structure

- **Count.** The standard **78-card** tarot is described, but the essay's argument and plates
  centre on the **22 trumps** (21 numbered *atouts* + the unnumbered Fool, *Le Mat*)
  [@web_wopc_mondeprimitif].
- **Trump order.** Gébelin keeps the familiar Marseille trump sequence but makes a point of
  placing the **Fool at the head** of the series (as the unnumbered card / "zero"), reading it
  as Folly outside the count [@trionfi; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **A peculiarity of order — Mellet's reversal.** The Comte de Mellet reads the trumps
  **backwards**, World → Fool, "because the Egyptians read from right to left," dividing the
  21 numbered trumps into **three groups of seven** mapped to the Ages of **Gold (XXI–XV)**,
  **Silver (XIV–VIII)** and **Iron (VII–I)**, with the Fool as the numberless zero
  [@web_robertplace_firstoccult; @web_tarotforum_mellet].
- **Suits.** Following the conventional European reading, the four suits are taken to mirror
  the **four classes/conditions of society** (cups→clergy, swords→nobility, coins→merchants,
  batons→peasantry); Gébelin folds this into his Egyptian frame [@web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Hebrew letters.** Gébelin *alludes* to the 22 trumps answering to the 22 letters of the
  Hebrew alphabet; **Mellet** is the one who actually assigns specific letters to trumps
  [@trionfi; @web_tarotforum_mellet].

## Game or divination?

This is the **birth of the divinatory/occult claim** — but in two distinct registers.
**Gébelin's** essay is *allegory*: he decodes the trumps as a lost Egyptian wisdom-book; he
does **not** teach fortune-telling. It is **Mellet**, in the appended essay, who supplies the
**first published cartomantic method** for the tarot pack — the oldest recorded tarot
divination procedure — and Etteilla (from 1785) who turns the whole idea into a commercial
practice [@web_letarot_divination; @web_tarotforum_mellet; @decker1996]. The repo should treat
Gébelin as the **origin of the myth** (dated 1781), Mellet as the first to propose *reading*
the cards, and neither as evidence about the deck's actual fifteenth-century use.

## What changed from its parent (the Tarot de Marseille)

The plates *are* the Marseille pattern; what changed is the **frame of meaning** laid over
each image, plus a handful of deliberate visual "corrections." The diff:

- **Relabelling toward Egypt.** Christian/feudal titles are re-read as Egyptian: the **Papess**
  → High Priestess of **Isis**; the **Pope** → Grand Hierophant of the mysteries; the
  **Emperor** → Osiris; the **Devil** → **Typhon**, "the evil Principle"; the **World** →
  **Time / Isis** in the egg of time; the **Star** → the **Dog-Star Sirius (Sothis)** and the
  flooding of the **Nile** [@web_traditionaltarot_isis; @web_villarevak_gebelin; @web_wopc_mondeprimitif].
- **A "corrected" image.** The **Hanged Man** is reproduced **upside-down** relative to the
  Marseille, because Gébelin argued "ignorant card-makers" had inverted a figure that really
  shows **Prudence** lifting a foot to test the ground — supplying the fourth cardinal Virtue
  the Marseille seemed to lack [@web_researchgate_prudence; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].
- **Etymology in place of history.** "Tarot" is derived from invented Egyptian roots —
  **Tar** ("road/path") + **Ro/Ros** ("royal/king"), i.e. the **"Royal Road"** of life;
  Mellet preferred **"Ta-Rosh,"** "the science/road of Thoth (Mercury)"
  [@web_libarynth_taroot; @web_trionfi_gebelin].
- **A migration story.** The pack is said to have been carried west from Egypt by **"the
  Gypsies."** This is in Gébelin's essay and amplified by Mellet; both are wrong — tarot is a
  fifteenth-century Italian invention and the Romani came from Asia, not Egypt/Africa
  [@web_trionfi_gebelin; @decker1996].
- **Numbering rhetoric.** The Fool is foregrounded as the numberless head of the sequence; the
  three cardinal-Virtue trumps (Justice VIII, Strength/Force XI, Temperance XIIII) plus the
  re-read Hanged Man (Prudence XII) are gathered into the **four cardinal Virtues**
  [@web_benebell_lovers; @web_robertplace_firstoccult].

What did **not** change: the basic scenes, the trump order, the suit signs. This is an
**interpretive overlay**, not a redesign — which is precisely why later occultists (Etteilla,
Lévi, Wirth, Waite, Crowley) could keep redrawing "the" tarot while claiming to recover the
same Egyptian original.

## The fear question

**No fear at all — the opposite.** Gébelin published **openly, under his own name**, in a
respectable subscription encyclopedia read by the Parisian elite (Benjamin Franklin, his
fellow member of the masonic lodge **Les Neuf Sœurs**, was among the subscribers)
[@web_trionfi_gebelin; @web_villarevak_gebelin]. Far from hiding "occult" knowledge, he
*announced* it as prestigious ancient wisdom; the salon treated it as fashionable erudition,
not dangerous magic. The occult overlay was, at its birth, a **public, high-status
intellectual fad** of Enlightenment Paris — the antithesis of a suppressed underground.

## Counter-voices

This is the file where the "suppressed occult history" claim must be weighed most carefully,
because Gébelin is the **source** of the Egyptian-origin belief many practitioners still hold.
Defenders argue he *intuited* a real esoteric substrate beneath the cards. The scholarly
verdict (Decker, Depaulis & Dummett) is firm and document-based: the Egyptian genealogy is
**invented, not recovered** — a Romantic-Enlightenment fantasy about Egypt projected onto an
Italian Christian card game, in an era when hieroglyphs could not yet be read [@decker1996;
@dummett1980]. Two fair qualifications, though: (1) debunking Gébelin does **not** debunk the
*real* later history of divinatory practice or its practitioners — it only removes a false
pedigree; and (2) Gébelin's essay is genuinely **foundational** — as the first sustained
symbolic reading of the trumps it is the document from which the entire esoteric tradition
descends, which is a historical fact independent of whether its claims are true.

## People

- **[court-de-gébelin](../people/court-de-gebelin.md)** — Antoine Court de Gébelin
  (b. Nîmes, c. 1719/1725 – d. Paris, 1784), Reformed pastor, comparative mythologist,
  Freemason (Les Neuf Sœurs), founder of the *Musée de Paris*; author of the essay and the
  thesis [@web_museeprotestant_gebelin; @web_trionfi_gebelin].
- **[comte-de-mellet](../people/comte-de-mellet.md)** — **Louis-Raphaël-Lucrèce de Fayolle,
  Comte de Mellet** (b. Périgueux, 1727 – d. 1804), cavalry officer; author of the appended
  *Recherches sur les Tarots*, which first linked the trumps to the Hebrew alphabet, read them
  in reverse as a three-Age Egyptian cosmogony, and published the first tarot divination method
  [@web_robertplace_mellet; @decker1996].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **The engraver of the plates** is not securely identified in the sources consulted; confirm
  from a Gallica scan of vol. VIII or from Decker–Depaulis–Dummett (confidence: low).
- **Gébelin's birth year** is given variously as **1719, 1724, 1725 or 1728**; the grammar
  currently states "1725–1784," defensible but flag the spread (the Musée protestant gives
  "1724 or 1728") [@web_museeprotestant_gebelin].
- **Keep Gébelin and Mellet distinct** at every point: the Hebrew-letter scheme, the
  reverse-order three-Age cosmogony, the "Ta-Rosh" etymology and the divination method are
  **Mellet's**; the "Royal Road" etymology, the Isis/Typhon/Sirius readings and the Prudence
  argument are **Gébelin's**.
- The **primary French text** was not read line-by-line this session (WebFetch unavailable);
  upgrade quotes against a Gallica scan or the Donald Tyson / David Vine translations before
  relying on any single phrasing.
