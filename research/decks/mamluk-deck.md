# Mamluk Playing Cards — Mulūk wa-nuwwāb (Kings and Deputies)

## At a glance

The Mamluk pack is the single most important upstream object in this collection: the
**four-suit pack Europe copied wholesale**. The near-complete survivor — held at the
**Topkapı Palace Museum, Istanbul**, and identified and published by **L. A. Mayer**
(1939; monograph 1971) — is a Mamluk-era set from **roughly the 15th century (c. 1500)**.
It has **four suits — cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks** — each running **Ace–10 plus
three court cards** (a king and two deputies), and its courts are **aniconic**: no human
figures, only abstract ornament and Arabic calligraphy [@mayer1971], [@web_wopc_mamluk],
[@web_pollett_mamluk]. Remove the 21 triumphs and the Fool from any historical tarot and
what is left is a Mamluk-shaped deck. It is the **direct ancestor**, distinct from the
**cousin** branch (Ganjifa) that flowed eastward and never became tarot.

## Origin & dating

Cards are documented in the Mamluk world by the 13th–14th centuries (some claims reach
back further), but the firm surviving object is **late 15th century**. Mayer attributed
the Topkapı cards squarely to the Mamluks and "to the 15th century," noting they show no
greasy fingermarks and so were probably little used [@mayer1971], [@web_wopc_mamluk]. The
game was called **kanjifah** (Arabic; cf. Persian/Indian *ganjifa*), and the deck itself
was **Mulūk wa-nuwwāb — "Kings and Deputies"** [@web00c_islamic_chinese],
[@web_pollett_mamluk]. The cards reached **Iberia and Italy in the 1370s** (as *naipes* /
*naibi*); it is that pattern, plus a later Italian fifth suit of triumphs (1440s), that
became tarot [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Provenance & evidence

The famous near-complete pack is in the **Topkapı Sarayı Museum, Istanbul**, measuring
about 252 × 95 mm. **L. A. Mayer** recognised it as a Mamluk playing pack around 1931,
published it in 1939, and it appeared as the posthumous monograph *Mamluk Playing Cards*
(E. J. Brill, 1971) [@mayer1971], [@la-mayer]. Web summaries report the surviving count
variously as **47 or 48 cards**; the reconciled figure used in this collection is **~48
survive, of which ~43 belong to the original pack** and a handful are cruder later
replacements — the complete pack held **52** (4 suits × 13: ten pips + three courts)
[@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk], [@mayer1971]. (confidence: high on the
structure; the exact survivor count is genuinely reported as 43/47/48 across sources.)

## Structure

**Four suits, each Ace–10 plus three court cards:**

- **Cups** (*ṭūmān*) — note that *ṭūmān* itself means "myriads/ten-thousands," echoing the
  Chinese money-card *wàn* suit, a small fingerprint of the eastern ancestry
  [@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
- **Coins** (*darāhim*, plural of *dirham*) [@web_pollett_mamluk].
- **Swords** (*suyūf*) — straight blades, carried into the European Swords suit
  [@web_pollett_mamluk].
- **Polo-sticks** (*jawkān*) — the *jawkān* (polo mallet), emblem of the most popular
  Mamluk aristocratic sport [@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web_cairoscene_mamluk].

The **three court cards** in each suit are the **malik** (king), the **nā'ib** (deputy /
lieutenant / viceroy), and the **thānī nā'ib** (second deputy) — hence *Mulūk wa-nuwwāb*
[@web_pollett_mamluk], [@mayer1971]. The courts are **aniconic**: no human or animal
figures, only geometric ornament and **Arabic calligraphic inscriptions** naming the rank,
with further blue panels carrying verse — apparently good-luck or courtly-praise maxims
[@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web_th_trzes_mamluk].

## Game or divination?

**A gaming (and likely gambling) object — not divination.** The deepest roots of tarot are
a card-game tradition: Tang-China money-suited cards → Mamluk *kanjifah* → European *naibi*.
Card games barely appear in Islamic historical sources (unlike chess or backgammon), and
there is **no attested systematic card-divination** in the Mamluk world; the rich Islamic
divinatory cultures ran on *other* instruments (geomancy, astrology, fālnāma)
[@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The honest caveat is that thin documentation plus perishable
cards means absence of evidence, not evidence of absence.

## What changed from its parent

The `derives_from` link to **madiao-money-cards** records a **transmission, not a documented
descent**: *how* cards travelled from Tang/Yuan China to the Mamluk world is one of the
great unsolved puzzles of gaming history — Silk Road trade and Mongol-era contact are the
usual guesses, but **there is no documentary chain** [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. (confidence:
low on the specific route — hedge.) What plausibly changed across that gap:

- **From money-denomination suits to object suits.** The Chinese ascending-money suits
  (cash / strings / myriads / tens-of-myriads) gave way to four **named objects**: cups,
  coins, swords, polo-sticks. The coin survived most recognisably; the cup may preserve a
  trace of the *wàn* ("myriads") suit in its very name *ṭūmān* [@web_pollett_mamluk],
  [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. (confidence: low — an attractive but unproven correspondence.)
- **A fixed three-card aniconic court.** The Mamluk pack standardised **malik + two nā'ib**,
  all without figures — the structural skeleton, minus the trumps, that Europe inherited
  [@mayer1971].

## How the suits became European

When the pack reached Iberia and Italy in the 1370s, three suits carried over almost
directly and one was redrawn:

- **Cups → Cups** (*coppe* / *coupes*), **Coins → Coins** (*denari* / *deniers*),
  **Swords → Swords** (*spade* / *épées*) — the Latin suits preserve the Mamluk shapes most
  faithfully [@web_wopc_mamluk_european], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
- **Polo-sticks → Batons / Wands** (*bastoni* / *bâtons*; later French *clubs*). Polo was a
  Mamluk court sport Europeans didn't play, so the unfamiliar *jawkān* was reinterpreted as
  staves — the **clearest single fingerprint of the Islamic origin**
  [@web_cairoscene_mamluk], [@web_th_trzes_mamluk].
- The later **French suits** (hearts / tiles / clovers / pikes) are a downstream
  simplification of the Latin shapes [@web_wopc_mamluk_european].
- The **court** was re-figured: Europe replaced the second deputy with a mounted **Knight**,
  kept the King, and later **added a Queen** [@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web_pollett_mamluk].

The word itself crossed too: Arabic **nā'ib** ("deputy") became the first European name for
the cards (*naib* / *naibi*) and survives as the **Spanish word for cards, *naipes***
[@web00c_islamic_chinese], [@web_pollett_mamluk].

## The fear question

**Not applicable in the divinatory sense.** The Mamluk pack carried no hidden esoteric
program to suppress — it was a courtly game. Whatever religious unease attached to it would
have been the ordinary censure of gaming and gambling, not heresy; and the cards' strict
**aniconism** reflects Islamic norms against figural depiction, a design constraint rather
than a concealment [@web_th_trzes_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Counter-voices

Because the cards are old, Eastern, and richly decorated, they are sometimes drafted into
"ancient Egyptian wisdom" genealogies of tarot. The corrective: there **is** a genuine deep
Eastern origin for the cards — but it is **Chinese paper money and Mamluk court games**, not
temple mysteries or a hidden oracle. The real history points at commerce and play, not
prophecy [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
