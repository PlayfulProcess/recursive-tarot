# Mamluk Playing Cards — Mulūk wa-nuwwāb (Kings and Deputies)

## At a glance

The Mamluk pack is the single most important upstream object in this collection: the
**four-suit pack Europe copied wholesale**. Remove the 21 triumphs and the Fool from any
historical tarot and what is left is a Mamluk-shaped deck. The near-complete survivor — held
at the **Topkapı Sarayı Museum, Istanbul**, and identified and published by **L. A. Mayer**
(1939; posthumous monograph 1971) — is a Mamluk-era set dated to **roughly the 15th century
(c. 1500)**. It has **four suits — cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks** — each running
**Ace–10 plus three court cards** (a king and two deputies), and its courts are **aniconic**:
no human figures, only abstract ornament and Arabic calligraphy [@mayer1971],
[@dummett_abudeeb1973], [@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk]. It is the **direct
ancestor** of the European pack, distinct from the **cousin** branch (Ganjifa) that flowed
eastward and never became tarot.

## Origin & dating

Cards are documented in the Mamluk world by the 13th–14th centuries (loose fragments, below),
but the firm, near-complete surviving object is **late 15th century**. Mayer attributed the
Topkapı cards to the Mamluks and "to the 15th century," noting they bear no greasy
fingermarks and so "it is doubtful whether these cards were used for any length of time"
[@mayer1971], [@web_wopc_mamluk]. The game was called **kanjifah** (Arabic; cf.
Persian/Indian *ganjifa*), and the deck itself was **Mulūk wa-nuwwāb — "Kings and Deputies"**
[@web_spiorad_topkapi], [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The cards reached **Iberia and Italy in the
1370s** (as *naipes* / *naibi*; the Chronicles of Viterbo record a new game "nayb"
introduced by a "Saracen" in 1379); it is that pattern, plus a later Italian fifth suit of
triumphs (1440s), that became tarot [@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Provenance & evidence

### The Topkapı pack — how many cards survive

The famous near-complete pack is in the **Topkapı Sarayı Museum, Istanbul**, measuring about
**252 × 95 mm** — large for the hand [@web_wopc_mamluk]. The reported survivor count differs
slightly between the two scholars who actually examined it, and the discrepancy is now
well understood:

- **Mayer (1931 article; 1939 publication; 1971 monograph)** gave photographs of **46 cards**
  and mentioned a **47th** so badly damaged that virtually nothing could be made out — hence
  the **"47 cards"** figure repeated by most secondary sources [@mayer1971],
  [@web_spiorad_topkapi], [@web_wopc_mamluk].
- **Dummett & Abu-Deeb (1973)** re-examined the cards in the museum and counted **48**. They
  found no trace of a damaged card, but did find a **4 of coins** and a **10 of coins** that
  Mayer had not mentioned; these "bore no surprises" and blend in with the rest of their suit
  [@dummett_abudeeb1973], [@web_spiorad_topkapi].

The reconciled, defensible statement: **47–48 cards survive of an original 52** (4 suits ×
13). Of these, **about 43 belong to the original primary pack**; the remainder are **cruder
replacement cards drawn from two other contemporary packs**, plus a few original pip cards
"promoted" to court rank to fill gaps [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk], [@dummett_abudeeb1973].
(confidence: **high** on count and structure.) The previous catalogue figure of "~48 survive /
~43 original" was therefore essentially correct; what was wrong was the **imagery** (only 7
photographs were held, reused as stand-ins). The full survival map is documented card-by-card
in `research/cards/mamluk-deck.md`.

The replacement / repurposing in detail [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk]: of the eight deputy and
second-deputy slots, **only two originals survive** (both in cups). One is simply missing; two
are imported from another pack; and three are original pip cards "promoted" to deputy — the
**aces of coins and polo-sticks** and the **nine of swords**. The five outright replacement
cards are the **second deputy of coins, second deputy of polo-sticks, ace of polo-sticks, nine
of swords, and ten of swords**. (confidence: high — Gjerde's reconstruction follows the
inscriptions and Dummett/Abu-Deeb.)

A standing caution: the 1972 Aurelia Books facsimile (booklet by Jan Bauwens) followed an
**earlier, mistaken view of Mayer's that each suit had four court cards rather than three**,
and some of its reconstructed cards conform to that faulty theory. Treat that facsimile's
analysis, and any count derived from it, with care [@web_wopc_mamluk]. (confidence: high.)

### Surviving Mamluk cards OUTSIDE Topkapı (new — previously omitted)

The Topkapı pack is not the only survival. Several loose **fragments**, some dated **earlier**
than the Topkapı pack, sit in Western collections and are routinely described as **the earliest
playing cards to survive from the Islamic world**:

- **Keir Collection (formerly Dr Edmund de Unger), on long-term loan to the Dallas Museum of
  Art.** A fragment of an **8 of cups**, Mamluk, delicately painted on soft paper (possibly
  deliberately torn); the cup shape matches the Topkapı form. WOPC dates it "12th–14th century."
  A study of the Keir holdings reports **two fragments of numerical cards whose style points to
  13th-century Egypt** [@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web_keir_dma_fragments]. (confidence: high that they
  exist; **medium/low** on the exact century.)
- **Benaki Museum, Athens** — three fragments reported by **Dalu Jones, March 1973** (relayed
  to Simon Wintle; noted in Dummett's correspondence). Crucially, only **one** is a Mamluk
  card: (a) an uncoloured **3 of Cups that is 15th-c. *Italian*, probably Venetian** — not
  Islamic — matching a Cary Collection sheet at Yale; (b) part of a **2 of Cups of Islamic
  (Mamluk) origin** (cup with a circle above the base and a crescent around the stem); (c) a
  third Islamic fragment that is **probably not a playing card at all** (a Kufic *Allah* is
  legible) [@web_wopc_mamluk]. (confidence: high — note the careful distinction; do not count
  all three as Mamluk cards.)
- **Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA)** — **three fragmentary Mamluk playing cards**,
  Egypt, **13th–14th c.**, ink and colours on paper, in the Madina Collection of Islamic Art
  (accession **M.2002.1.654**, with **M.2002.1.650** and **M.2002.1.651**). LACMA's own
  curatorial note calls them part of "a rare group of extant playing cards produced in Mamluk
  Egypt (1250–1517), representing the earliest such objects to survive from the Islamic world"
  [@web_lacma_mamluk_fragment]. (confidence: high that LACMA holds them; **medium** on dating.)
- A **c. 1300 fragment** (top-right corner, possibly a 4-of-cups but perhaps a 7/8/9 of cups)
  is documented and reconstructed by Tor Gjerde; he stresses the ~1300 date is on
  art-historical grounds and "must be regarded as highly uncertain." It may be the de
  Unger/Keir fragment seen from a different reading, or a related object — the identity is
  not settled here [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk1300]. (confidence: **low** on date and identity.)

The upshot: the surviving Mamluk corpus is the Topkapı near-complete pack **plus** a scatter of
earlier fragments in at least three Western museums — and those fragments, not Topkapı, are the
candidates for the **oldest surviving playing cards anywhere**.

## Structure

**Four suits, each Ace–10 plus three court cards.** Suit and court names are read directly from
the cards' own Arabic inscriptions [@web_spiorad_topkapi], [@mayer1971]:

- **Coins** — *darāhim* (plural of *dirham*, the Mamluk silver coin).
- **Cups** — *ṭūmān*; note that *ṭūmān* itself means "myriads / ten-thousands," echoing the
  Chinese money-card *wàn* ("myriads") suit — a small fingerprint of the eastern ancestry
  [@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. (confidence: low — suggestive, unproven.)
- **Swords** — *suyūf* (straight blades, carried into the European Swords suit).
- **Polo-sticks** — *jawkān* (the polo mallet), emblem of the foremost Mamluk aristocratic
  sport [@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web_cairoscene_mamluk].

The **three court cards** per suit are the **malik** (king), the **nāʾib** (deputy /
lieutenant), and the **thānī nāʾib** (second deputy) — hence *Mulūk wa-nuwwāb* [@mayer1971],
[@web_spiorad_topkapi]. The courts are **aniconic**: no human or animal figures, only geometric
ornament and **Arabic calligraphic inscriptions**. Two kinds of blue inscription panel appear:
**top panels** carry verse (good-luck / courtly-praise aphorisms with no direct bearing on
play), while **bottom panels** — added later — name the card's rank [@web_wopc_mamluk],
[@web_cardsoldno_mamluk].

A genuinely useful structural fingerprint: the distribution of the original top panels (present
on the court cards, on **1–3 of coins and polo-sticks**, and on **8–10 of swords**) shows that
the **coins and polo-sticks suits ranked in reverse — low pips beating high** — exactly as
coins and cups do in early Italian tarot, and as some suits do in Chinese money-suited cards.
This reversed-ranking parallel is among the better structural arguments for the East–West
connection [@web_cardsoldno_mamluk]. (confidence: medium.)

## Game or divination?

**A gaming (and likely gambling) object — not divination.** The deepest roots of tarot are a
card-game tradition, not an oracle. Card games barely appear in Islamic historical sources
(unlike chess or backgammon), and there is **no attested systematic card-divination** in the
Mamluk world; the rich Islamic divinatory cultures ran on *other* instruments (geomancy,
astrology, fālnāma) [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The honest caveat is that thin documentation
plus perishable cards means absence of evidence, not evidence of absence.

The Mamluk game's **rules do not survive**. From the deck's structure (four suits, ten pips,
three courts, reversed ranking in some suits) it is inferred to have been a **trick-taking
game**, broadly like tarot without the trumps — but that is an inference, not a recorded
ruleset [@dummett_abudeeb1973], [@web_th_trzes_mamluk]. (confidence: medium.)

## What changed from its parent

The `derives_from` link to **madiao-money-cards** records a **transmission, not a documented
descent**: *how* cards travelled from Yuan/Ming China to the Mamluk world is one of the great
unsolved puzzles of gaming history — Silk Road trade and Mongol-era contact are the usual
guesses, but **there is no documentary chain** [@web00c_islamic_chinese], [@web_parlett_leafgame].
(confidence: low on the specific route — hedge.) What plausibly changed across that gap:

- **From money-denomination suits to object suits.** The Chinese ascending-money suits
  (cash / strings / myriads / tens-of-myriads) gave way to four **named objects**: coins, cups,
  swords, polo-sticks. The coin survived most recognisably; the cup may preserve a trace of the
  *wàn* ("myriads") suit in its very name *ṭūmān* [@web_pollett_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
  (confidence: low — attractive but unproven.)
- **A fixed three-card aniconic court.** The Mamluk pack standardised **malik + two nāʾib**,
  all without figures — the structural skeleton, minus the trumps, that Europe inherited
  [@mayer1971].

The best structural evidence for the link is not iconographic but procedural: both Chinese
money cards and Western cards share **reversed rank-order in some suits** and the same
**trick-taking idea** (highest card of the led suit wins). John McLeod argues these are hard to
have arisen independently, which is why a common origin remains the standard view despite the
missing paper trail [@web_parlett_leafgame], [@web_pagat_madiao]. (confidence: medium.)

## How the suits became European

When the pack reached Iberia and Italy in the 1370s, three suits carried over almost directly
and one was redrawn:

- **Cups → Cups** (*coppe* / *coupes*), **Coins → Coins** (*denari* / *deniers*), **Swords →
  Swords** (*spade* / *épées*) — the Latin suits preserve the Mamluk shapes most faithfully
  [@web_wopc_mamluk_european], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
- **Polo-sticks → Batons / Wands** (*bastoni* / *bâtons*; later French *clubs*). Polo was a
  Mamluk court sport Europeans didn't play, so the unfamiliar *jawkān* was reinterpreted as
  staves — the **clearest single fingerprint of the Islamic origin** [@web_cairoscene_mamluk],
  [@web_th_trzes_mamluk].
- The later **French suits** (hearts / tiles / clovers / pikes) are a downstream simplification
  of the Latin shapes [@web_wopc_mamluk_european].
- The **court** was re-figured: Europe replaced the second deputy with a mounted **Knight**,
  kept the King, and later **added a Queen** — and re-introduced the figural human images the
  Mamluk cards forbade [@web_wopc_mamluk], [@web_pollett_mamluk].

The word crossed too: Arabic **nāʾib** ("deputy") became the first European name for the cards
(*naib* / *naibi*) and survives as the **Spanish word for cards, *naipes*** [@web00c_islamic_chinese],
[@web_wopc_mamluk].

## The fear question

**Not applicable in the divinatory sense.** The Mamluk pack carried no hidden esoteric program
to suppress — it was a courtly game. Whatever religious unease attached to it would have been
the ordinary censure of gaming and gambling, not heresy; and the cards' strict **aniconism**
reflects Islamic norms against figural depiction, a design constraint rather than a concealment
[@web_th_trzes_mamluk], [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Counter-voices

Because the cards are old, Eastern, and richly decorated, they are sometimes drafted into
"ancient Egyptian wisdom" genealogies of tarot. The corrective: there **is** a genuine deep
Eastern origin for the cards — but it is **Chinese paper money and Mamluk court games**, not
temple mysteries or a hidden oracle. The real history points at commerce and play, not prophecy
[@web00c_islamic_chinese].
