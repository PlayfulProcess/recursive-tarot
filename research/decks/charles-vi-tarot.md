# "Charles VI" Tarot (so-called Gringonneur)

## At a glance

A fragment of **seventeen** hand-painted, gold-ground *trionfi* cards in the **Bibliothèque
nationale de France** — **sixteen trumps and a single Page of Swords** — that for two centuries
bore a **wrong name and a wrong date** [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_tarotgarden_charlesvi;
@web_intomystery_oldest]. Nineteenth-century writers tied them to a real **1392** Paris payment
to the painter **Jacquemin Gringonneur** for cards made for **Charles VI of France**; on
stylistic grounds the surviving cards are in fact **Italian, mid-to-late 15th century**, about a
century too late and from the wrong country [@dummett1980; @web_th_charlesvi_benedetti;
@gringonneur]. This is therefore the repo's **debunk deck**: the name preserves an exploded myth.
It was a **game** pack — *trionfi* / *tarocchi*, played for tricks — with **no native divinatory
meaning**; the card-by-card file describes iconography and the triumph each card shows, not
fortune-telling [@dummett1980].

> **Two attributions are unsettled and flagged throughout.** The repo grammar inherits the older
> **"Ferrarese / B-order"** label. The **modern BnF catalogue** attributes the deck to **Florence**
> — the workshop of **Apollonio di Giovanni and Marco del Buono, c. 1460** — and its **trump order**
> behaves like a **southern (A-type) sequence**, not a clean Ferrarese B. Both points are live
> questions for the Tarot History Forum, kept open below rather than asserted.

## Origin & dating

The legend rests on a genuine document: a **1392** entry in Charles VI's treasury (Charles
Poupart) paying **Jacquemin Gringonneur 56 sols** for **three gilded, coloured packs** for the
king's amusement [@gringonneur; @decker1996]. Those were **ordinary playing cards** — tarot did
not yet exist (it appears in Italy only in the 1440s) — and they are **lost** [@dummett1980]. The
attachment of that entry to the BnF cards was a **19th-century error**; the cards are not French
and not from 1392 [@web_th_charlesvi_benedetti; @gringonneur].

On style, the deck is **Italian, c. 1450–1480** [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_th_charlesvi_tag].
The **place of origin has moved**:

- Older scholarship proposed **Ferrara** (or Bologna, or "Northern Italy" generally) — the label
  the repo grammar still carries, and the basis for calling this a **B-order / Ferrarese** witness
  [@web_th_charlesvi_tag; @web_thf_charlesvi]. The BnF's own older publicity still reads
  *"Italie du Nord, fin du XVe siècle"* [@web_bnf_essentiels_charlesvi].
- The **current BnF Catalogue général record** attributes the deck to the **workshop of Apollonio
  di Giovanni and Marco del Buono**, the busy Florentine *cassone*-painting partnership — placing
  it in **Florence, c. 1460** [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_gallica_charlesvi_fou;
  @web_apollonio_wp]. Sherryl Smith (Tarot Heritage) reports **Florence as the most reliable
  hypothesis as of 2021**, the deck being "the most complete deck of the **Florentine pattern**"
  [@web_th_charlesvi_benedetti; @web_th_charlesvi_tag].

So the safest statement is **"Italian, c. 1450–80, now most often placed in Florence (Apollonio di
Giovanni's workshop), formerly in Ferrara"** (confidence: medium — the Florence attribution is the
catalogue's current position but the deck carries no signature, date, or heraldry to fix it).

## Provenance & evidence

**Seventeen cards** survive — **sixteen trumps + the Page (Knave) of Swords** — each roughly
**180–185 × 90–95 mm**, egg tempera over an ink drawing on a paper support, with stamped
scrollwork over gold and silver leaf [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_intomystery_oldest]. They
descend from the collection of the antiquary **Roger de Gaignières** and are held in the BnF
**Cabinet des Estampes**, shelfmark **Rés. Kh 24** [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_thf_charlesvi]
(confidence: medium on the exact shelfmark — one forum source gives "Kh 24"; not independently
re-verified here). They are digitized on **Gallica** and presented on **BnF Essentiels**
[@web_gallica_charlesvi_fou; @web_bnf_essentiels_charlesvi].

The **sixteen surviving trumps** are: the **Fool**, **Emperor**, **Pope**, **Lovers**,
**Chariot**, **Justice**, **Hermit / Time**, **Strength (Fortitude)**, **Hanged Man**, **Death**,
**Temperance**, **Tower (Maison-Dieu)**, **Moon**, **Sun**, **Judgement**, and **World**
[@web_tarotgarden_charlesvi; @web_intomystery_oldest]. Mapped onto the familiar 22-trump set, the
**six missing trumps** are the **Magician (Bagatto)**, **Popess**, **Empress**, **Wheel of
Fortune**, **Devil**, and **Star** [@web_intomystery_oldest; @web_th_charlesvi_tag]. The rest of an
original 78-card pack — the other three suits and most courts — is lost; the surviving **Page of
Swords** is the lone witness that the suit cards were painted to the same luxury standard as the
trumps [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi].

## Structure

A luxury *trionfi* pack, originally **78 cards** on the standard model, of which only the **16
trumps + 1 court** survive. The cards are **unnumbered and untitled** — there is no painted
numeral or name to fix the trump sequence, which is **reconstructed from later witnesses of the
same regional pattern**, not read off the objects [@dummett1980; @web_pollett_orders]. This is why
the order classification is contested:

- The repo tags the deck **B-order (Ferrarese)** — the eastern family in which **Star, Moon, Sun**
  appear as a graded triad and **Justice** sits high (eighth) [@web_pollett_orders;
  @web_thf_charlesvi].
- But as a **Florentine** deck its sequence behaves like a **southern (A-type)** order: **all four
  virtues sit *below* Death**, and the **Chariot sits *below* the virtues** — a placement Dummett
  uses to separate the pseudo-Charles VI Florentine order from Bologna's (where the Chariot is
  above) [@web_pollett_orders; @dummett1980]. The Florentine line later pushes the Chariot up under
  the Wheel, and ultimately becomes **Minchiate** [@web_pollett_orders].

The honest position: this is a **southern/Florentine** sequence whose assignment to A vs B is a
real scholarly question, **not** a settled "B" [@web_pollett_orders; @web_thf_charlesvi]
(confidence: low on the bare "B-order" label).

Several images are **iconographically distinctive** and worth recording because later decks
diverge from them: the **Moon** shows **two astronomers measuring with compasses** (not the later
dogs-and-towers scene); the **Sun** shows a **woman with a distaff** beneath a great rayed sun;
the **World** shows a **standing female figure holding a globe/orb** (sources also describe a
censer); the **Hanged Man** is a *pittura infamante*, the shaming portrait of a traitor hung by
one foot [@web_th_charlesvi_benedetti; @web_intomystery_oldest].

## Game or divination?

**Game, unambiguously.** These are *carte da trionfi* for a **trick-taking game**; no cartomantic
tradition attaches to them in their own century, and the divinatory overlay is a **post-1781
import** (Court de Gébelin, then Etteilla) read back onto early Italian cards [@dummett1980;
@gringonneur]. The card-by-card file therefore gives **iconography and triumph**, not
upright/reversed meanings, which this deck does not natively possess.

## What changed from its parent

**Not applicable — this is a root deck** (`derives_from: []`). The "Charles VI" pack is an
**ancestor / witness**, standing near the head of the surviving record alongside the
Visconti-Sforza family; later decks diff *against* it, not the reverse [@dummett1980].

## The fear question

**No fear.** Like the Visconti packs, this is **conspicuous princely consumption** — a gilt,
silver-leafed luxury object of a Renaissance court, the kind of thing a patron displayed
[@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_th_charlesvi_benedetti]. The only "deception" in its history is
**scholarly, not social**: the 19th-century mis-dating, not any hidden or forbidden practice.

## Counter-voices

The deck's whole fame is a **counter-voice corrected**. The bogus **1392 French** attribution was
an early piece of romantic over-dating that made tarot look older and more mysterious than the
documents allow — the same impulse that later reached for Egypt [@gringonneur;
@web_th_charlesvi_benedetti]. Restoring the real provenance (Italian, c. 1450–80) is **removing a
19th-century embellishment**, not "suppressing an occult past." A second, **live** counter-voice
runs the other way against the repo's own label: the modern catalogue's **Florence / Apollonio**
attribution and the **southern (A-type) order** both push back on the inherited "Ferrarese
B-order" tag — recorded here as an open question, not silently overwritten
[@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_pollett_orders].

## People

- **`gringonneur`** — Jacquemin Gringonneur, the Paris painter of the **1392** royal cards, is the
  **debunked namesake**: wrongly credited with this deck centuries after his lost, ordinary packs.
  He did **not** make it [@gringonneur; @dummett1980].
- *(Not yet `people/` entries; recorded here for the dossier.)* The **modern attribution** is the
  Florentine workshop of **Apollonio di Giovanni** and his partner **Marco del Buono Giamberti**,
  better known for painted *cassone* (wedding-chest) panels [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi;
  @web_apollonio_wp]. The corrective dating is **Michael Dummett's** document-first work
  [@dummett1980].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **"17 trumps" is wrong — it is 16 trumps + 1 Page.** The surviving total is **17 cards = 16
  trumps + the Page of Swords**, corroborated by the BnF, The Tarot Garden, and Into Mystery
  [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_tarotgarden_charlesvi; @web_intomystery_oldest]. (The repo grammar's
  emergence node "The 16 Surviving Trumps" is correct; any phrasing of "17 trumps" elsewhere should
  be fixed.)
- **Place of origin: Florence vs Ferrara.** The grammar's `tradition: "Ferrarese"` and the prose
  "(Ferrara or possibly Florence)" predate the **current BnF attribution to Florence (Apollonio di
  Giovanni's workshop)**. Update to lead with Florence, keep Ferrara as the superseded hypothesis
  [@web_bnf_cat_charlesvi; @web_th_charlesvi_benedetti].
- **Order family: A vs B.** The deck is a **southern / Florentine** sequence (all virtues below
  Death; Chariot below the virtues). Calling it cleanly **"B-order (Ferrarese)"** is **not safe**;
  Dummett's later analysis leans southern/A. Flag `order` accordingly [@web_pollett_orders;
  @dummett1980].
- **Shelfmark.** "Rés. Kh 24" comes from a forum source; confirm against the BnF catalogue record
  before treating it as canonical [@web_thf_charlesvi; @web_bnf_cat_charlesvi].
- **World card detail.** Sources vary between "holds a globe/orb" and "swings a censer"; the precise
  attributes need a direct look at the Gallica scan [@web_gallica_charlesvi_fou].
- The deck is a **fragment**; the full original trump set and order are **reconstructed, not
  observed** [@dummett1980].
