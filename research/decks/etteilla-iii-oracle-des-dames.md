# Etteilla III — Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames

## At a glance

The **third-generation Etteilla** and the one most people actually mean by "Grand Etteilla":
*Le Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames*, a **mass-market lithograph** edition whose pattern was
designed by **G. Régamey around 1865** and which stayed in print — through Haugard-Meuge,
Delarue, Lequart & Mignot, H. Pussey and finally **B. P. Grimaud** — for the better part of a
century [@web_phantho_egyptian; @web_steve_grandetteilla; @web_mccloskys_etteilla3]. It carries
Alliette's divinatory **system** intact (the same 78-card count, the same cosmogony-then-virtues
trump order, the same printed upright/reversed keywords and "court card = a person" convention),
but **completely re-draws the imagery** in a heavier, ornate, faintly Gothic 19th-century style
"only vaguely related to the original," and **re-titles several trumps** away from Alliette's own
labels [@web_phantho_egyptian; @web_tarotgarden_grandetteilla]. The title is the tell: ***des
Dames*** — "for the ladies" — pitches cartomancy as fashionable bourgeois **drawing-room
amusement**, the fully de-stigmatised, domestic end of the practice Etteilla I sold as recovered
Egyptian wisdom. Under **Grimaud** the pattern became the *standard* "Tarot Égyptien," the form
in which the Etteilla line survived into the 20th century.

## Origin & dating

The **Oracle des Dames** pattern was first issued **c.1865**, circulating to **c.1870**; the
lithographs are credited to **G. Régamey** and the first printings to **Haugard-Meuge** in Paris
[@web_mccloskys_etteilla3; @web_phantho_egyptian]. It is a **follower edition** — produced by the
commercial print trade that inherited Alliette's brand, not by Alliette (d. 1791) or by the
"Julia Orsini"/Blocquel workshop behind Etteilla II — and it is the **furthest** of the three from
Alliette's own 1788–89 drawings [@web_phantho_egyptian; @web_druidcraft]. Sources describe it as
"completely redrawn," with "larger, more ornate imagery" exuding "a 19th-century Gothic aura that
contrasted with the dowdy, rather inept look of the original deck," again "alter[ing] the
symbolism of several of the cards" [@web_phantho_egyptian; @web_tarotgarden_grandetteilla].

The publishing chain is the spine of its history and explains why "Grand Etteilla" is ambiguous
in catalogues:

- **c.1865** — Régamey design, printed by **Haugard-Meuge**; first *Oracle des Dames* sheets
  [@web_mccloskys_etteilla3].
- **c.1872–1890** — published by **Lequart & Mignot** [@web_grandetteilla_chronology].
- **c.1880–1890** — parallel Paris editions by **Delarue** and by **H. Pussey** (the same Pussey
  associated with the 32-card *Petit Etteilla*) [@web_mccloskys_delarue;
  @web_grandetteilla_chronology].
- **c.1890–1891** — **B. P. Grimaud** absorbs the line: "Grimaud increased its catalogue …
  through the purchase of the companies Pussey and then Lequart et Mignot, acquiring … the Grand
  and Petit Etteilla, the Petit Oracle des Dames …" — after which Grimaud's *Grand Etteilla /
  Tarots Égyptiens* becomes the **standard** issue, reprinted through the 20th century (and
  photo-reprinted by **Éditions Dusserre, 1985**) [@web_grandetteilla_chronology;
  @web_steve_grandetteilla; @web_phantho_egyptian].

(Confidence: medium on the exact firm-by-firm dates — these come from antiquarian-trade and
collector listings rather than the Dummett spine directly; the **broad sequence**
Régamey/Haugard-Meuge → Lequart & Mignot/Delarue/Pussey → Grimaud is well corroborated.)

## Provenance & evidence

The deck survives in quantity — it is among the **more common antique Etteilla packs** on the
market, exactly because it was the mass-market lithograph that stayed in print
[@web_mccloskys_etteilla3]. Datable examples are catalogued by antiquarian dealers (McClosky's
lists a c.1865 Régamey/Haugard-Meuge set and a c.1885 Delarue *Oracle des Dames*)
[@web_mccloskys_etteilla3; @web_mccloskys_delarue], and the **British Museum** holds an Oracle des
Dames / Etteilla III pattern set (object group P,1982.U.4598.1–78) (confidence: medium — surfaced
in a museum search result; accession should be confirmed against the BM catalogue when WebFetch
is available) [@web_bm_etteilla3]. The BnF Gallica holds *Le Grand Etteilla* ("tarot égyptien")
sheets under ark:/12148/btv1b105431857 (the Grimaud-line standard pattern)
[@web_gallica_grandetteilla]. Wikimedia Commons maintains a dedicated **Etteilla III** category of
card scans [@web_commons_etteilla3]. The **Dummett spine** (Decker–Depaulis–Dummett 1996;
Decker–Dummett 2002) is the scholarly backbone for separating the three Etteilla types and for the
claim that Etteilla III is a late-19th-century commercial re-drawing, not an independent system
[@decker1996; @decker2002].

## Structure

**A 78-card pack, structurally identical to Etteilla I/II** — the *system* did not change; the
*pictures and a few titles* did [@web_steve_grandetteilla; @web_phantho_egyptian].

- **22 trumps** in Etteilla's order (cosmogony 1–8, cardinal virtues 9–12, reworked Marseille
  trumps 13–21), plus **La Folie** as the unnumbered/0 trump (the deck's running sheet runs to
  78) [@web_steve_grandetteilla; @web_etteillastrumps].
- **56 minors** in four Latin suits (Bâtons/Fire, Coupes/Water, Épées/Air, Deniers/Earth), each
  court read as a **person** in the querent's life (upright well-disposed, reversed ill-disposed)
  — Alliette's convention, retained [@web_steve_grandetteilla; @web_aeclectic_etteilla].
- **Querent significators embedded, not added:** the **male querent is Card 1 (Le Chaos)** and the
  **female querent is Card 8 (Le Repos)**, as in Etteilla I — they double as cosmogony cards, and
  the pack is **78, full stop**. (This corrects the repo grammar, which mislabels **Card 78 / La
  Folie** as the female significator — the same error carried over from Etteilla I. See Open
  questions.) [@web_etteillastrumps; @web_phantho_egyptian]
- Astrological/keyword apparatus printed on the borders is retained from the parent line
  [@web_steve_grandetteilla].

## Game or divination?

**Divination — a retail oracle, the brand's end-state.** Every card is a fortune-telling card by
design; the deck was never a game pack. What changes vs. the parents is not the function but the
*market*: by 1865 this is a boxed, lithographed consumer product with an instruction booklet,
sold as parlour entertainment for ladies [@web_mccloskys_etteilla3; @web_phantho_egyptian].

## What changed from its parent

Its parent is **Etteilla II** (the c.1838–40 Blocquel / "Julia Orsini" *Grand Livre de Thot*),
and behind that **Etteilla I** (Alliette's own 1788–89 *Livre de Thot*). The **delta** is one of
*production, framing, and titling* far more than of *system*:

1. **Wholesale re-drawing of the imagery.** Etteilla III is a fresh lithographic redesign — "larger,
   more ornate," "completely redrawn," with a heavy 19th-century Gothic look "only vaguely related
   to the original," where Etteilla I's engravings looked "dowdy" and "inept." This is the single
   biggest visible change from I and II [@web_phantho_egyptian; @web_tarotgarden_grandetteilla].
2. **The "Oracle des Dames" re-branding.** The pack is sold not as a *Livre de Thot* for adepts but
   as a *Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames* — a **game/oracle "for the ladies."** The framing turns
   Alliette's Egyptian-priestly mystique into **feminine domestic leisure** — the de-stigmatised,
   respectable, parlour end of cartomancy [@web_mccloskys_etteilla3; @web_phantho_egyptian]. A
   companion **Petit Oracle des Dames** (a smaller pack) belongs to the same retail family
   [@web_grandetteilla_chronology].
3. **Trump re-titlings** that drift away from Alliette's own labels and settle the names most
   collectors know (see the cards file for each):
   - **Card 13** is titled **"Le Grand Prêtre" (The High Priest)** — an image-driven re-label of
     what is, by identity, Alliette's **Le Mariage / the Lovers**. (The "High Priest" title is a
     *posthumous* Etteilla II/III drift, **not** Alliette's emphasis.) [@web_etteillastrumps]
   - **Card 14** settles as **"Le Diable" (The Devil)**, foregrounding the Devil image over
     Alliette's abstract *Force Majeure / Force Mineure* reading (which survives as the divinatory
     meaning) [@web_etteillastrumps; @web_steve_grandetteilla].
   - **Card 15** keeps the dual title **"Le Magicien ou le Bateleur"** but its *meaning* remains
     **Illness/Maladie** — the Magician demoted and re-purposed, as in Alliette
     [@web_picryl_etteilla3; @web_steve_grandetteilla].
   - **Card 18** settles as **"Le Capucin"** (the Capuchin/Hermit), with the added "traitor /
     concealment" sense [@web_etteillastrumps].
   - **Card 21**, the reworked **Chariot**, is **"Le Despote Africain" (The African Despot)** whose
     **upright keyword is "Dissension / Discord,"** the Grimaud booklet tying it to **Rehoboam**,
     Solomon's son who split the kingdom — the "re-titling to *Dissension*" the brief flags
     [@web_houndofhecate_despot; @web_benebell_chariot; @web_etteillastrumps].
4. **The Grimaud standardization.** When Grimaud absorbed the line (c.1890–91) it fixed *this*
   pattern as the canonical **"Grand Etteilla / Tarots Égyptiens,"** the version reprinted for the
   rest of the 20th century and the one most people mean by "Grand Etteilla" — completing the
   journey from Alliette's bespoke oracle to a **mass-produced commodity deck**
   [@web_grandetteilla_chronology; @web_steve_grandetteilla; @decker2002].

**What did *not* change:** the 78-card count, the cosmogony→virtues→Marseille trump order, the
embedded querent significators (Cards 1 and 8), the printed upright/reversed keywords, the
element-coded suits, and the "court = a person" convention. The divinatory **dictionary** is
substantially Alliette's, carried through II into III [@web_steve_grandetteilla;
@web_etteillastrumps].

## The fear question

**The de-stigmatised pole of this whole library.** By 1865 a tarot fortune-telling deck is a
*pretty boxed retail product* invited into the bourgeois drawing room under a name that asks
**women** to play with fate at home — "Christmas-and-Halloween fortune games, not clandestine
sorcery." The publishers advertised openly; the buyers were respectable. The criminalised,
precarious version of the same act persisted only for the **itinerant, paid, often Romani**
reader — a *different population* from the purchasers of an Oracle des Dames boxed set. The class
line this library draws throughout is at its starkest here [@web_phantho_egyptian;
@web_mccloskys_etteilla3].

## Counter-voices

- The "Oracle des Dames" framing is a useful corrective to a romantic picture: the **mainstream**
  of 19th-century cartomancy was **bourgeois domestic leisure, gendered feminine and sold as
  fun**, not a persecuted underground. The "suppressed occult tradition" narrative should keep
  that distinction in view [@web_phantho_egyptian].
- The proliferation of Etteilla editions (I → II → III → Grimaud) is evidence not of recovered
  antiquity but of a **successful manufactured tradition** — Alliette's false Egyptian pedigree
  becoming a self-sustaining commercial genre that the Paris print trade kept profitable for a
  century [@decker2002; @decker1996].

## People

- **`etteilla`** — Jean-Baptiste Alliette, originator of the *system* this deck transmits (the
  numbering, the cosmogony order, the printed reversals, the court-as-person rule). He had no hand
  in the III drawings.
- **G. Régamey** — lithographer/designer of the c.1865 *Oracle des Dames* pattern (no person
  dossier yet; candidate, alongside the Etteilla II "Julia Orsini"/Blocquel figures).
- **Publishers/printers** — Haugard-Meuge, Delarue, Lequart & Mignot, H. Pussey, **B. P. Grimaud**
  (the standardizer). Candidates for institution dossiers if the catalogue grows that branch.

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Grammar — female-significator error (carried from Etteilla I):**
  `tarot/etteilla-iii-oracle-des-dames/grammar.json` labels **Card 78 (`La Folie`)** as "The
  Questioner – Female" with keyword `etteilla:significator-female`. La Folie is the **Fool / 0**;
  the female significator is **Card 8 (Le Repos)**, which the grammar names merely "Repos" and
  gives no significator keyword. The significator keyword should move from Card 78 to Card 8.
- **Grammar — trump archetype mappings** mirror the Etteilla I errors:
  - **Card 13 ("Le Grand Prêtre")** is mapped `arcana:the-hierophant` ("exact"). By *identity* the
    card is the **Lovers / Le Mariage**; "Le Grand Prêtre" is the III-era **title of the image**.
    The title is authentic to III, but `mapping_confidence: exact` to the Hierophant overstates a
    re-label as a derivation — flag as needs-review.
  - **Card 21 ("Le Despote Africain")** is mapped `arcana:the-emperor` ("loose"). Historically it
    reworks the **Chariot**, not the Emperor; the Emperor has no clean single trump in Etteilla.
- **Dating** rests on antiquarian-trade listings, not the Dummett spine: confirm c.1865 vs c.1870
  first printing and the firm-by-firm chronology (Haugard-Meuge / Lequart & Mignot / Delarue /
  Pussey / Grimaud) against Decker–Dummett 2002 and a BnF/BM catalogue when WebFetch is restored.
- **Physical specifics flagged but unverified:** one trade description mentions "chromolithograph
  on pasteboard, single black-line borders, blue/red marbled reverses"; a claim of double-headed
  reversible courts could **not** be confirmed and is omitted here (confidence: low).
- The BM accession (P,1982.U.4598.1–78) and the Gallica ark for the Grimaud standard pattern
  should be verified directly.
