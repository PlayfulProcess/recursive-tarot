# Ma Diao (馬吊) — Chinese Money-Suited Cards

## At a glance

Ma Diao (馬吊, often "hanging horse") is the best-documented of the Chinese **money-suited**
card games — a four-player, trick-taking gambling game played with a 40-card pack whose four
suits are **denominations of currency** (cash, strings, myriads, tens-of-myriads). It belongs
in this library as a **deep documented root of the suited pack** — but precision matters here,
because the popular framing overshoots in two ways:

- Ma Diao is **not "older than European cards."** The documented game is **late Ming
  (16th–17th c.)**, *after* cards reached Europe (1370s) and after tarot (1440s). What predates
  Europe is the broader Chinese **paper-card idea** (attested 1294), not Ma Diao itself
  [@web_parlett_leafgame], [@lo2000_madiao].
- Ma Diao is **not "the oldest card game we can still play" in any simple sense.** It is more
  defensibly **the oldest card game whose rules survive in enough detail to reconstruct and
  actually play** — and even those rules were written down only in the early 17th century, well
  after the game's origin, with modern playable versions filling gaps [@web_pagat_madiao],
  [@web_madiao_wiki].

It is a *root*, not a *parent*: no single Ma Diao card descends by a clean line into a tarot
trump, and there is no cartomantic tradition attached to it. (confidence: medium — the money
suits are a genuine deep ancestor of the Western suits, but the China→Islam transmission route
is undocumented.)

## Origin & dating — what is actually attested

The romantic story dates Chinese cards to the **Tang dynasty (618–907)** via the 9th-century
*yezi ge* (葉子戲, "game of leaves"). Modern scholarship is sceptical. **Andrew Lo** ("The Game
of Leaves," *BSOAS* 63/3, 2000) argues that "leaves" most likely meant the **pages of a rules
manual**, and that the game so named was a **board-and-dice game of promotion and demotion**
(something like a complex Snakes-and-Ladders), or a wine-drinking forfeit game — *not* a card
game. The "cards" meaning of *yezi* is a Ming–Qing usage projected back onto the Tang
[@web_parlett_leafgame], [@lo2000_leafgame]. **Ouyang Xiu** (c. 1067) already noted that the
rules of the leaf game were lost in his own day [@web_parlett_leafgame]. (confidence: high that
the Tang "card" claim is disputed; medium that it was specifically a board/dice game.)

The earliest **unambiguous** reference to paper playing cards (*zhǐpái*, 紙牌) is a Yuan
lawsuit: on **17 July 1294** two gamblers (Yan Sengzhu and Zheng "Pig-Dog") were arrested in
Shandong with nine cards and the woodblocks to print them — recorded in the Yuan legal code
completed 1320. But the report gives **no descriptive details and the cards are untraced**, so
it cannot be reconstructed or even confirmed to be suited [@web_parlett_leafgame],
[@lo2000_madiao]. (confidence: high.)

The specifically **money-suited** four-suit pack is described by the Ming scholar **Lu Rong
(1436–1494)**; David Parlett notes money cards are **"not firmly attested before the late Ming
period (1590–1644)."** Ma Diao itself was codified and most popular in the late Ming, its rules
recorded by **Pan Zhiheng** and **Feng Menglong** in the early 17th century [@web_madiao_wiki],
[@web_parlett_leafgame]. A Yuan origin for the game is asserted by the Korean writer Jang Hon
(1759–1828) but is not documented [@web_madiao_wiki]. So: the *form* may be older, but the
*game* is firmly documented only from the late Ming. (confidence: high.)

## Provenance & evidence

The cards illustrated in this collection are the public-domain Chinese holdings at **Skokloster
Castle, Sweden** — a small money-suited East Asian pack, scanned at high resolution and released
via Wikimedia Commons [@web_madiao_themahjong]. Twelve items survive there; the individual
suit/value of each is left un-guessed in the grammar. (The Skokloster pack is a later survival
and a European collection of an East-Asian deck, not a Ming original — it is a *physical
witness* to the tradition, not the Lu Rong deck itself.)

## Structure

Forty cards in **four ascending money suits** [@web_madiao_wiki], [@web_parlett_leafgame]:

1. **Cash / coins** (文, *wén*) — single copper coins, the lowest suit (11 cards in the Ma Diao
   pack: 1–9 plus a half-cash and a zero/empty card). In Ma Diao the **cash suit ranks in
   reverse** — zero highest, nine lowest.
2. **Strings of cash** (索, *suǒ*) — ropes of a hundred coins (9 cards, 1–9).
3. **Myriads of strings** (萬, *wàn*) — ten-thousand units (9 cards, 1–9). In many money-card
   sets this suit was illustrated with the **108 bandit-heroes of *Water Margin* (水滸傳)** —
   "Water Margin cards" (水滸牌) [@web_madiao_themahjong].
4. **Tens of myriads** (十萬, *shí wàn*) — the highest suit, carrying the honour cards (11 cards
   in the Ma Diao pack) [@web_madiao_wiki].

The suits are **units of money, not abstract pips** — the structural signature of the East Asian
tradition [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Game or divination?

**Game — and gambling — never divination.** Ma Diao is a four-player trick-taking gambling game
[@web_madiao_wiki], [@web_pagat_madiao]. There is no fortune-telling tradition attached to the
money-suited cards; they sit here purely as the upstream of the *object*, not of any reading
practice [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## What changed from its parent

**Nothing — this is a root** (no `derives_from`). The interesting diff runs *downstream*: the
**westward mapping** onto the Mamluk and European suits. The standard hypothesis is that the
visual similarity of the Chinese suits to the Islamic suits of **coins, polo-sticks, scimitars
and cups** makes it "likely (but unproven) that the Islamic suits evolved from the Chinese
suits" [@web_suit_wiki]. The strongest evidence is structural rather than visual: John McLeod
points to the shared **reversed rank-order in some suits** and the shared **trick-taking idea**
(highest card of the led suit wins) as hard to have arisen independently East and West
[@web_parlett_leafgame], [@web_pagat_madiao]. The Chinese **coin** suit survived the diffusion
most recognisably, becoming the **coins** suit of the Mamluk and Latin packs
[@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The line therefore runs: Chinese money-cards → (centuries, many
intermediaries) → Mamluk *kanjifah* → Italian/Spanish suits → trionfi/tarot. (confidence:
medium — the structural argument is strong; the documentary chain is absent.)

A crucial chronological caveat: because the documented money cards are late-Ming, they are **far
too late to be the literal source of the European pack**; Parlett stresses this. The Chinese
*contribution* is the **idea and structure** of the suited pack, transmitted at some earlier,
undocumented point — not the specific Ma Diao deck [@web_parlett_leafgame].

## The fear question

**Not applicable** in the European sense. Gambling was periodically restricted under various
Chinese dynasties, but Ma Diao is not a heterodox or hidden object — it is a popular betting
game, openly described in Ming literature [@web_madiao_wiki].

## Counter-voices

The romantic "ancient Eastern wisdom" intuition wants the Chinese root to be oracular and
impossibly old. Neither holds: the genuine deep, *Eastern* origin of the cards is **Chinese
paper money and gambling games** (paper money itself dates to 1023), and the firmly documented
money-suited game is **late, not ancient** [@web00c_islamic_chinese], [@web_parlett_leafgame].
The honest caveats are the **transmission gap** (how cards reached the Mamluk world is "one of
the great puzzles of gaming history") and the **dating gap** (money cards are not attested early
enough to be Europe's literal parent) [@web_parlett_leafgame].

## People

- **la-mayer** — L. A. Mayer, whose *Mamluk Playing Cards* (1971) established the Mamluk pack as
  the middle link between Chinese money cards and the European deck [@mayer1971].
- **michael-dummett** — the document-first spine; Dummett's work on the diffusion of cards from
  China through Islam into Europe frames the whole genealogy [@dummett1980], [@dummett2004].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- The Tang (9th-c.) origin rests on the ambiguous *yezi*; per Lo it is **probably not a card
  game** — keep it flagged, with the firmer anchors being the 1294 *zhǐpái* and the late-Ming
  money cards [@lo2000_leafgame], [@web_parlett_leafgame].
- **Do not call Ma Diao "the oldest card game" or "older than European cards."** The documented
  game is late Ming; cards as objects are older (1294), and European cards (1370s) predate the
  documented Ma Diao [@web_parlett_leafgame].
- The most defensible superlative is "**among the earliest card games whose rules can be
  reconstructed and played**" — and even that rests on early-17th-c. records plus modern
  reconstruction [@web_pagat_madiao], [@lo2000_madiao].
- The China→Islam transmission route is unknown — assert no path [@web00c_islamic_chinese].
- The mapping of which money-suit became which Latin suit beyond *coins* is inference, not
  documentation (confidence: low).
- The Skokloster items are not separately catalogued by suit/value here.
