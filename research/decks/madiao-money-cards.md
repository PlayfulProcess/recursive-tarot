# Ma Diao (馬吊) — Chinese Money-Suited Cards

## At a glance

Ma Diao (馬吊, often "hanging horse") is the best-documented of the Chinese **money-suited**
card games — a four-player, trick-taking gambling game played with a 40-card pack whose four
suits are **denominations of currency**. It belongs in this library as the **deepest root of
the playing-card form**: the standard scholarly view is that money-suited Chinese cards are the
ancestors, by a long and only partly-documented westward diffusion, of the Mamluk pack and
through it of every European suited deck — including tarot. It is a *root*, not a *parent*: no
single Ma Diao card descends by a clean line into a tarot trump, and there is no cartomantic
tradition attached to it. (confidence: medium — the China→Islam transmission route is genuinely
undocumented; what is not in dispute is that paper cards are a Chinese invention.)

## Origin & dating

Paper playing cards are usually traced to **Tang-dynasty China (618–907 CE)**, but the famous
9th-century "leaf game" (*yezi*) reference is disputed and may not describe cards at all; the
earliest unambiguous evidence is later [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The specifically
**money-suited** four-suit pack is described by the Ming scholar **Lu Rong (1436–1494)**, who
recorded a deck of four suits — "cash" (coins), "strings" (of coins), "myriads," and "tens" (of
myriads) [@web_madiao_wiki]. Ma Diao itself was codified and most popular in the **late Ming**
(16th–17th c.) and described in period manuals [@web_madiao_wiki]. So the form is plausibly Tang
in origin but firmly documented only from the Ming.

## Provenance & evidence

The cards illustrated in this collection are the public-domain Chinese holdings at **Skokloster
Castle, Sweden** — a small set of a money-suited East Asian pack, scanned at high resolution and
released via Wikimedia Commons [@web_madiao_themahjong]. Twelve items survive there; the
individual suit/value of each is left un-guessed in the grammar rather than asserted. (Note: the
Skokloster pack is a later survival and a European collection of an East-Asian deck, not a Ming
original — it stands as a *physical witness* to the tradition, not as the Lu Rong deck itself.)

## Structure

Forty cards in **four ascending money suits** [@web_madiao_wiki]:

1. **Cash / coins** (文, *wén*) — single copper coins, the lowest suit (11 cards in the Madiao
   pack: 1–9, plus a half-cash and a zero/empty card).
2. **Strings of cash** (索, *suǒ*) — ropes on which a hundred coins were threaded (9 cards, 1–9).
3. **Myriads of strings** (萬, *wàn*) — ten-thousand units (9 cards, 1–9). In many money-card
   sets this suit was illustrated with the **108 bandit-heroes of the novel *Water Margin*
   (水滸傳)**, so painted "Water Margin cards" (水滸牌) are a famous money-suited variety
   [@web_madiao_themahjong].
4. **Tens of myriads** (十萬, *shí wàn*) — the highest suit, carrying the honour cards
   (in the Madiao pack 11 cards: from 20-myriad up through hundred-, thousand-, and
   myriad-myriad) [@web_madiao_wiki].

The suits are **not abstract pips but units of money** — the structural signature of the East
Asian card tradition, and the reason the cards are sometimes described as almost literally
play-money [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## Game or divination?

**Game — and gambling — never divination.** Ma Diao is a four-player trick-taking gambling game
[@web_madiao_wiki]. There is no attested fortune-telling tradition attached to the money-suited
cards; they sit in this library purely as the upstream of the *object*, not of any reading
practice [@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## What changed from its parent

**Nothing — this is a root.** Ma Diao has no `derives_from`; it stands at (or near) the head of
the documented playing-card line. The interesting diff runs *downstream*: the **westward
mapping** onto the Mamluk and European suits. The standard hypothesis is that the visual
similarity of the Chinese suits to the Islamic suits of **coins, polo-sticks, scimitars and
cups** makes it "likely (but unproven due to lack of evidence) that the Islamic suits evolved
from the Chinese suits" [@web_suit_wiki]. The Chinese **coin** suit is the one that survived the
long diffusion most recognisably, becoming the **coins/dinari** suit of the Mamluk and Latin
packs [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The line therefore runs: Chinese money-cards → (centuries, many
intermediaries) → Mamluk *kanjifah* → Italian/Spanish suits → trionfi/tarot.

## The fear question

**Not applicable** in the European sense. Gambling games were periodically restricted under
various Chinese dynasties, but Ma Diao is not a heterodox or hidden object — it is a popular
betting game, openly described in Ming literature [@web_madiao_wiki].

## Counter-voices

The romantic "ancient Eastern wisdom" intuition wants the Chinese root to be oracular. It is
not: the genuine deep, *Eastern* origin of the cards is **Chinese paper money and gambling
games**, not a hidden divinatory tradition [@web00c_islamic_chinese]. The honest caveat is the
**transmission gap**: how cards reached the Mamluk world from China is "one of the great puzzles
of gaming history," with Silk Road trade and Mongol-era contact the usual (undocumented) guesses
[@web00c_islamic_chinese].

## People

- **la-mayer** — L. A. Mayer, whose *Mamluk Playing Cards* (1971) established the Mamluk pack as
  the middle link between the Chinese money-cards and the European deck [@mayer1971].
- **michael-dummett** — the document-first spine; Dummett's work on the diffusion of cards from
  China through Islam into Europe frames the whole genealogy [@dummett1980].
