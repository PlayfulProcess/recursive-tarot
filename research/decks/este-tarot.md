# d'Este Tarocchi (Ferrara Fragment)

## At a glance

A small **fragment of a hand-painted Ferrarese tarocchi** — only **16 cards survive** (8 trumps +
8 court cards) of what was once a full luxury pack made in the orbit of the ruling **House of Este**
[@web_yale_este; @web_trionfi_este]. It is one of the rare witnesses to **Ferrara's** parallel
early-tarot tradition — the **B-order** branch — as against Milan's Visconti (C-order) decks. Like
all the hand-painted court packs, it was a **game** object (*trionfi*), with no native divinatory
meaning [@dummett1980]. The fragment is held in the **Cary Collection of Playing Cards at Yale's
Beinecke Library** and is fully digitised, open access [@web_yale_este]. (confidence: medium — the
attribution and dating follow standard scholarship but are debated, and the deck's original
composition is unknown.)

## Origin & dating

The cards are attributed to **Ferrara** and the **Este court** on stylistic and provenance grounds
[@web_trionfi_este; @web_pollett_ferrara]. **Dating is genuinely contested**: the conventional
repo/label date is **c. 1450**, placing it among the very oldest surviving tarot alongside the
Milanese Visconti packs; but several sources — including the iconographic study of its "Star" card
— date the fragment to the **1470s, c. 1473**, a period of heavy tarocchi activity in Ferrara
[@web_intomystery_este; @web_pollett_ferrara]. The Yale catalogue itself describes a 15th-century
hand-painted pack but notes the **composition of the deck is unknown** [@web_yale_este]. Treat
"c. 1450" as a convenience label and flag the 1470s alternative (confidence: low — no document
fixes the date; argued from style).

## Provenance & evidence

- **Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library, Yale — Cary Collection of Playing Cards.** 16 cards,
  hand-painted, completely digitised and open access (credit: Yale University Library)
  [@web_yale_este; @beinecke_cary].
- Catalogued and discussed on **trionfi.com** ("16 d'Este cards") and on **Andy Pollett's**
  Tarots-of-Ferrara pages [@web_trionfi_este; @web_pollett_ferrara].

## Structure

A fragment, so the full structure is reconstructed, not observed:

- **8 surviving trumps:** Il Bagatto (Magician), Il Papa (Pope), Temperance, the Star, the Moon,
  the Sun, the World, and Il Matto (the Fool) [@web_yale_este; @web_trionfi_este]. Card names follow
  **Yale's catalogue labels**, not iconographic guesswork. The trumps are **unnumbered**, as
  15th-century hand-painted trumps were [@dummett1980].
- **8 surviving court cards** across the four Italian suits (Swords, Batons, Coins, Cups) — kings,
  queens, a cavalier and a jack [@web_yale_este].
- The pips and most trumps are **lost**; the original count is presumed a standard early tarocchi
  pack, but the catalogue declines to assert it [@web_yale_este].

## Game or divination?

**Game.** A luxury Ferrarese court *trionfi* pack, three centuries before any cartomantic use; no
divinatory apparatus and no fortune-telling tradition attach to it natively [@dummett1980]. The
card-by-card file therefore describes **iconography**, not upright/reversed meanings.

## The fear question

**No fear.** Like the Visconti packs, this is courtly luxury consumption, not clandestine ritual;
the only contemporary anxiety around cards was the friars' campaign against **gambling**, which
targeted the wager, not the imagery [@dummett1980]. An Este courtier would have displayed such a
pack, not hidden it.

## Counter-voices

Any "secret Egyptian/Hermetic deck" reading is an 18th–19th-century retrofit; nothing in the Este
court context supports it. The trumps are standard Italian Christian-allegorical and celestial types
(Pope, Temperance, the celestial lights, the World) [@dummett1980]. The live scholarly questions
here are mundane and document-shaped: the **date** (c. 1450 vs c. 1473) and the deck's **lost
original composition** [@web_intomystery_este; @web_yale_este].

## Why it matters — the B-order home and the Charles VI group

Ferrara is the heartland of the **B-order** trump sequence (Dummett's Eastern branch). Where Milan's
Visconti decks head the **C-order** line toward the Tarot de Marseille, the d'Este cards witness
Ferrara's parallel tradition [@dummett1980; @web_pollett_ferrara]. The fragment is conventionally
grouped with the so-called **"Charles VI" tarot** — itself a North-Italian (likely **Ferrarese**)
hand-painted set of c. 1475–1500, **misnamed** after a 1392 Paris account-book payment to
Jacquemin Gringonneur for cards that do not survive [@web_th_charlesvi]. The d'Este and "Charles VI"
packs are often cited together as **the two principal early witnesses of the B/Eastern order**
[@web_tm_este; @web_th_charlesvi] (confidence: medium — the B-order classification is well
established; the exact d'Este–Charles VI relationship is a grouping by order and region, not a
proven derivation).
