# Jean Noblet Tarot de Marseille (Paris, c. 1650/1659 — "Type I")

## At a glance

The **Jean Noblet tarot** is the **oldest surviving near-complete Tarot de Marseille**: a
small woodcut-and-stencil pack printed in **Paris** around the mid-17th century, surviving in
a **single copy held by the Bibliothèque nationale de France** and digitised on Gallica
(`ark:/12148/btv1b105109641`) [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @web_gallica_noblet;
@web_wopc_noblet]. It is the practical archetype of what **Thierry Depaulis** later named the
**"Type I"** Marseille — the older, more austere graphic family that precedes the canonical
**"Type II"** standardised by **Conver (1760)** and the rest of that line
[@depaulis1984; @web_th_typeI_typeII; @dummett1980]. In origin and through its whole life it
is a **game deck** — printed for the trick-taking game *tarot* in a Paris where that game was
already declining — and it carries **no native divinatory meanings**; the occult reading of
these images is a later overlay invented from the 1780s, more than a century after Noblet
[@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @dummett1980; @decker1996]. Its fame rests on three things: that
it is the **earliest extant TdM** (roughly **110 years before Conver**), that it preserves a
batch of **distinctive Type-I details** (the cape-and-loincloth World, the human-faced Devil's
belly, the full-face Moon, the adult Sun couple), and that it carries the **famously explicit
Fool** — a small, weird, vivid object at the head of the whole printed Marseille tradition.

## Origin & dating

Noblet was a **maître cartier** working *"au faubourg St Germain"* in Paris. A *Jean Noblet,
master cardmaker*, is cited in **two notarial acts of 1659** as living at Saint-Germain-des-Prés
(rue Sainte-Marguerite, parish of Saint-Sulpice), and his documented activity runs roughly
**1659–1681** [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @web_apprendre_noblet]. The deck is conventionally
dated **c. 1650**, while the **BnF dates the surviving object to 1659** and frames the dating
after Depaulis's research [@web_bnf_cat_noblet; @web_apprendre_noblet]. The "c. 1650 vs 1659"
gap is a genuine soft spot — both are in circulation, both are mid-17th-century, and neither
is contradicted by the documentary record; treat the date as **c. 1650/1659**
(confidence: medium on the precise year, high on the mid-17th-century placement)
[@web_bnf_cat_noblet; @dummett1980].

The crucial point is **graphic-typological, not geographic**: Noblet worked in **Paris**, yet
his pack is "the oldest known example conforming to the graphic type conventionally called the
*tarot de Marseille*" — a name the pattern only acquired **later** (18th c.), from the city of
Marseille that came to specialise in printing it [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @dummett1980].
So "Marseille" here is a **type-label applied retrospectively**, not Noblet's own term or place.

## Provenance & evidence

The surviving pack is a **single copy in the BnF**, digitised on **Gallica**
(`ark:/12148/btv1b105109641`); the BnF catalogue notice and the BnF *Essentiels* feature pages
describe and reproduce it [@web_gallica_noblet; @web_bnf_cat_noblet; @web_bnf_essentiels_noblet].
The deck **self-identifies** physically: the **Two of Coins** bears *"Iean Noblet Dm.t Av
Favbovr St Germain"*, the **Two of Cups** carries his name and address, and the **Chariot's
escutcheon** carries his monogram **"I. N."** [@web_wopc_noblet; @web_bnf_essentiels_noblet].
It is **nearly complete**, lacking only a short run of Sword pips — the **Six through Ten of
Swords** are missing from the surviving set [@web_wopc_noblet; @web_th_noblet_vs]. Two
20th/21st-century restorations make the deck legible to modern users and are the basis of most
circulating images: **Jean-Claude Flornoy's** woodcut-faithful restoration (**2007**) and
**Joseph H. Peterson's** facsimile (**Twilit Grotto, 2016**); both had to reconstruct the five
missing Sword pips, and they differ in background tone and palette (Flornoy shifts terra-cotta
toward flesh and lemon-yellow toward deep orange; Peterson cleans the cream ground toward
white) [@web_th_noblet_vs; @web_peterson_noblet; @web_flornoy_noblet].

## Structure

A standard **78-card pack** in the Marseille layout, here in its **oldest extant** witness:

- **22 trumps (atouts).** Numbered **I–XXI** in Roman numerals, plus the **unnumbered Fool**,
  which stands outside the count [@web_aeclectic_noblet; @dummett1980]. The order is the
  **"C" order** (the southern / Marseille order) that became the occult standard, with
  **Justice VIII and Force (Strength) XI** — the **pre-Golden-Dawn** arrangement that the
  Marseille keeps and the Rider-Waite tradition later swapped [@dummett1980; @web_th_typeI_typeII].
  - **Death (XIII) is here actually named — "LAMORT"** — which is *unusual*: in most later
    Marseille decks the thirteenth trump bears its number but **no name**. Several sources call
    Noblet "the only" Marseille deck to title Death (confidence: medium — "only" is a strong
    claim resting on secondary sources) [@web_wopc_noblet; @web_th_noblet_vs; @web_aeclectic_noblet].
  - **The Fool is titled "LE FOV / Le Fou"** — reportedly the only Marseille deck to use *Le
    Fou* rather than *Le Mat* / *Le Fol* (confidence: medium) [@web_th_typeI_typeII;
    @web_aeclectic_noblet].
  - The numerals use the additive woodcut forms (**IIII**, **VIIII**) typical of the tradition
    [@dummett1980].
- **56 minors,** four Italian-suited sets of 14: **Deniers (Coins), Coupes (Cups), Épées
  (Swords), Bâtons (Batons)**, each **Ace–Ten** plus a four-card court — **Roy (King), Dame
  (Queen), Cavalier (Knight), Valet (Page)** [@web_wopc_noblet; @web_aeclectic_noblet].
  - The **pips are non-scenic / emblematic** — repeating suit symbols in woodcut scrollwork,
    not narrative scenes — as throughout the Marseille tradition [@dummett1980;
    @web_aeclectic_noblet].
  - **Smallest known Marseille pack:** about **92 × 55 mm** (the BnF measure; ~9.2 × 5.7 cm),
    with square corners and a notably broad colour palette [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet;
    @web_wopc_noblet; @web_th_noblet_vs].
- **Element/suit mapping.** Any Coins=Earth / Cups=Water / Swords=Air / Batons=Fire labelling
  in the grammar is the **later occult correspondence** (Golden Dawn era), not a feature of a
  17th-century game pack — carry it as a reading aid, not as Noblet's own [@decker1996; @decker2002].

## Game or divination?

**Game — unambiguously, and emphatically so for Noblet.** This is a 17th-century commercial
playing-cards object, printed for the trick-taking game *tarot*; the BnF frames it as made in
a Paris where that game was **declining**, making Noblet's among the *last* Parisian tarots as
well as, by type, the *oldest Marseille* one [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @dummett1980]. No
divinatory reading of these trumps is attested anywhere near 1650 — the occult overlay begins
with **Court de Gébelin (1781)** and **Etteilla** (later 1780s), **130 years downstream**
[@dummett1980; @decker1996]. The grammar should label this deck **"game"** and present any
card meanings strictly as a later overlay, not an origin.

## What changed from its parent

This is a **root / ancestral** deck in the catalogue: it has **no `derives_from` parent**, and
it sits at the **head** of the printed Marseille line. The relevant "diff" is therefore
**forward** — what later decks changed *from Noblet* — and **lateral**, against the uncut
**Cary Sheet** (c. 1500, Milan) which is older evidence of the *pattern itself* but is a
fragmentary sheet, not a deck [@web_th_celebrating_typeI; @dummett1980]. Noblet's images are
worn, simplified, woodblock descendants of the 15th-century Italian *trionfi*; their apparent
"purity" is **printing-tradition conservatism**, not transmitted doctrine [@dummett1980].

**Type I vs Type II — the core comparison.** In **1986 Thierry Depaulis** divided the Tarot de
Marseille into two iconographic families. **Type I** (Noblet 1650, then **Dodal 1701**, **Payen
1713**) differs from the later, far more widely copied **Type II** (Madenié 1709 → … → Conver
1760) on about **ten cards** [@depaulis1984; @web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI;
@web_th_payen_typeI]. The **well-attested** Type-I marks visible in Noblet:

- **The World (XXI):** the central figure wears a **loincloth and a cape**; in Type II she is
  draped only in a scarf, with the **left leg bent** [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].
- **The Devil (XV):** the belly carries a **second human face**; Type II has a smooth belly
  [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].
- **The Moon (XVIII):** shown **full-face**; Type II shows it in **profile**
  [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].
- **The Sun (XVIIII):** the two figures are an **adult man and woman** in scant loincloths;
  Type II reduces them to **two children** [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].
- **The Pope (V):** holds a **bishop's crook**, as on the Cary Sheet, rather than the later
  triple cross [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_th_celebrating_typeI].

Noblet is also reported to carry **seven of the ten** canonical Type-I details, with notable
**exceptions**: its **Emperor** lacks the floating "4" seen on some Type-I decks, and its
**Chariot** carries the maker's "I. N." initials on the shield [@web_th_typeI_typeII]. The
**complete, itemised ten-card list is not fully recoverable from the sources consulted** and
is left flagged (confidence: medium) [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].

## The fear question

**No fear as a game object.** Noblet's pack was an ordinary, taxed product of the Paris card
trade; nothing about a 17th-century *tarot* deck invited concealment, and there is no
divinatory use to hide because there was none yet [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @dummett1980].
The one genuinely transgressive feature is **artistic, not occult**: the **explicit Fool**
(genitals exposed, a cat clawing at them) and the Bateleur's phallic gesture are **bawdy
period imagery**, not esoteric code — the kind of frankness later Type-II printers quietly
sanitised [@web_aeclectic_noblet; @web_th_noblet_vs]. The deck did not need hiding; later
prudery simply cleaned it up.

## Counter-voices

Because Noblet is the **oldest survivor**, esoteric writers sometimes treat its details as
"original" and therefore **doctrinally meaningful** — reading the Devil's belly-face, the
naked Fool, or the cape-clad World as preserved ancient symbolism. The document-first reading
is the reverse: Noblet's images are **early but not primordial** (the Cary Sheet pattern is
older still), and their peculiarities are **the older state of a woodcut tradition** that
Type II later regularised, not a buried Egyptian or Kabbalistic key [@dummett1980;
@web_th_celebrating_typeI; @web_th_typeI_typeII]. Both things hold: Noblet is genuinely the
**archetypal Type I** and a precious witness, *and* its iconography is descended craft, not
revelation.

## People

- **`jean-noblet`** — the Paris *maître cartier* whose name and faubourg-Saint-Germain address
  are printed on the cards (Two of Coins / Two of Cups) and whose **"I. N."** monogram sits on
  the Chariot. Thinly documented beyond the deck; floruit **c. 1659–1681**
  [@web_bnf_essentiels_noblet; @web_wopc_noblet].
- **Type-I kin (downstream, own dossiers where present):** **`jean-dodal`** (Lyon, 1701) and
  **Jean-Pierre Payen** (1713) carry the same Type-I family [@web_th_payen_typeI;
  @web_th_typeI_typeII].
- **Same-city, different pattern:** **`jacques-vieville`** (Paris, c. 1650), a contemporary
  whose pack belongs to the **Rouen-Brussels / Belgian** line, not the Marseille one
  [@web_th_french_connection; @web_th_rouen_bruxelles].
- **Restorers / scholars:** **Jean-Claude Flornoy** (2007 restoration) and **Joseph H.
  Peterson** (2016 facsimile); the typology is **Thierry Depaulis's**; the documentary frame
  is **Michael Dummett's** [@web_flornoy_wp; @web_peterson_noblet; @depaulis1984; @dummett1980].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Pin the date.** "c. 1650" vs the BnF's "1659"; reconcile against the BnF object record and
  the 1659 notarial acts [@web_bnf_cat_noblet; @web_apprendre_noblet].
- **Itemise the ten Type-I cards.** The well-attested five (World, Devil, Moon, Sun, Pope) are
  documented; the full ten-card list, and exactly which seven Noblet shows, need a primary
  Depaulis check [@web_th_typeI_typeII; @web_druidcraft_typeI].
- **Verify the "only named Death" and "only *Le Fou*" claims** against more than secondary
  sources before stating them as fact [@web_wopc_noblet; @web_th_typeI_typeII].
- **Distinguish object from pattern:** the Cary Sheet (c. 1500) is older evidence of the
  *pattern*; Noblet is the oldest surviving *deck* [@web_th_celebrating_typeI; @dummett1980].
