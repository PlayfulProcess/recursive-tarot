# Cary-Yale Tarot (Visconti di Modrone)

## At a glance

The most **lavish outlier** of the surviving hand-painted Milanese *trionfi* packs: an
extravagantly **expanded** game deck, possibly the **oldest tarot material of all**,
notable for two structural anomalies found in almost no other tarot — **six ranks of
court cards** per suit (adding a female Knight and a female Page to the usual four), and
trumps depicting the three **theological virtues** (Faith, Hope, Charity) alongside the
cardinal ones [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].
**Sixty-seven** hand-painted cards survive — gold, silver, tooled grounds and miniature
Renaissance portraiture — held in the **Cary Collection at Yale's Beinecke Library**, which
gives the deck the "Cary" half of its name (the other half, *Visconti di Modrone*, comes from
the commissioning Milanese house and a 20th-century owner) [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights;
@web_yale_modrone_catalog; @beinecke_cary]. Like its sister `visconti-sforza-tarot`, it was a
**game** object — *carte da trionfi*, played at court — with **no divinatory use** in its own
century [@dummett1980]. Read its images as dynastic display and Christian moral allegory, not
as a coded esoteric system.

## Origin & dating

Held at **Yale's Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library** (Cary Collection of Playing Cards)
[@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @beinecke_cary]. **Giordano Berti** dates the pack to
**c. 1442–1447** on **numismatic** grounds: the Coin (*denari*) pips reproduce the gold
**florin struck by Filippo Maria Visconti**, in use only from **1442** until his death in
**1447** [@web_th_modrone_meneghello; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone]. If that argument holds,
the Cary-Yale is **among the very earliest surviving tarot material**, plausibly the oldest.
The repo grammar's headline date of **c. 1442** is the early end of that bracket
[@web_th_modrone_meneghello].

Dating estimates **vary** across the literature — from **c. 1428–1447** at the conservative
extreme to **c. 1450** at the late extreme (the latter sometimes tied to the *tenth-anniversary*
of the Visconti-Sforza marriage rather than the wedding itself) [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone;
@web_cary_yale] (confidence: medium — the florin argument is widely cited but not universally
treated as decisive; "c. 1442" should be read as a convenience anchor, not a settled fact).

The deck is conventionally tied to the **marriage of Bianca Maria Visconti and Francesco
Sforza** — celebrated **24 October 1441** (Cremona) — and the celebrated **Lovers/Love** card
is often read as a portrait of that union [@web_bianca_wp; @web_th_modrone_meneghello;
@web_cary_yale]. This is the **most-discussed theory** for the deck's occasion, and a reasonable
one for a gold-leaf dynastic gift, but it remains a **hypothesis**, not documented fact
[@web_cary_yale] (confidence: medium).

## Provenance & evidence

A **fragmentary** set: **67 cards survive** of an original pack reconstructed at **~86 cards**
[@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @kaplan_caryyale_1984; @web_learntarot_cary]. The surviving 67
break down — corroborated across the Beinecke highlight, Kaplan's booklet, and review sources —
as:

- **11 trumps** (the *trionfi*): the Emperor, the Empress, Love (the Lovers), the Chariot,
  Fortitude (Strength), Death, the World, Judgement, and the three theological virtues
  **Faith, Hope, Charity** [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @web_beinecke_cary_highlights].
- **17 court ("face") cards**, drawn unevenly from the four suits' **six-rank** courts
  [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].
- **39 pip cards** across Coins, Cups, Swords and Batons (the suit-emblem for Batons here is
  drawn as an **arrow**) [@kaplan_caryyale_1984; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone].

That is **11 + 17 + 39 = 67** [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights]. The structural oddities — the
**six courts** and the **theological virtues** — are themselves the key evidence that the early
trump set was **not yet standardised**; different Milanese commissions experimented with how many
triumphs and ranks to include [@dummett1980] (see *Counter-voices*). The painting is
**traditionally attributed to Bonifacio Bembo (or his workshop)**, with **Francesco Zavattari**
proposed by some art historians as the rival hand — the same attribution debate that hangs over
the Visconti-Sforza pack [@web_bembo_wp; @web_vs_wp]. (The U.S. Games reproduction supplies **19
cards recreated by Luigi Scapini in 1983** to fill out the missing slots; these are modern
restorations, not surviving 15th-century cards [@kaplan_caryyale_1984].)

## Structure

A non-standard, **enlarged** early tarot pack. Reconstructed at **~86 cards = 22 trumps + 64
minors**, where the 64 minors arise from **four suits × (10 pips + 6 courts)**
[@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].

- **Six-rank courts (the headline anomaly).** Where a normal tarot suit has four courts — King,
  Queen, Knight, Page — the Cary-Yale has **six**: **King, Queen, (male) Knight, (female) Knight
  — a "Lady on horseback"/Horsewoman, (male) Page/Knave, and (female) Page — a "Damsel"/Maid**
  [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone]. It is, as far as the record shows,
  the **only** historical Western tarot with six court ranks, and the extra ranks are **female**,
  giving the deck an unusual female-courtier emphasis [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone]. These extra
  courts are what swell the pack toward ~86 cards.
- **Theological virtues among the trumps.** The trumps include **Faith, Hope and Charity** — the
  three **theological** virtues — alongside the cardinal virtue Fortitude. The theological
  virtues appear in **no other tarot** except, much later, the **Minchiate** expansion
  [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @web_th_modrone_meneghello]. Whether they **replaced** standard
  trumps or were **added** to them is unsettled [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **Suits.** Four Latin suits — **Coins/Denari, Cups/Coppe, Swords/Spade, Batons/Bastoni** — the
  Batons rendered as **arrows** [@kaplan_caryyale_1984]. The pips are **decorative**, not scenic:
  suit-emblems on tooled gold grounds, no narrative picture.
- **Trump order: C-branch.** Like the Visconti-Sforza, the Cary-Yale stands near the head of the
  **C-order** family from which the Tarot de Marseille line descends; its trumps are unnumbered
  and unlabelled, and the sequence is reconstructed from later C-order witnesses [@dummett1980].

## Game or divination?

**Game, unambiguously.** The expansions are about **enriching play and flattering a dynasty**,
not encoding secret doctrine [@dummett1980]. The virtue cards are conventional **Christian moral
allegory** — the common visual currency of Renaissance courts — and the courtly figures are
dynastic portraiture in gold. No divinatory apparatus, no fortune-telling text, no cartomantic
tradition attaches to the object in its own century; any "meaning" is a much later (18th–19th c.)
overlay. **The card-by-card file therefore describes iconography and the triumph each card
represents, not upright/reversed meanings**, which this deck does not natively have.

## What changed from its parent

**Not applicable — this is a root deck** (`derives_from: []`). The Cary-Yale is an **ancestor**,
not a descendant; later decks diff *against* it, not the reverse. The only "parent" question that
arises is **internal** to the Visconti family of packs — whether the Cary-Yale **precedes or
postdates** the Visconti-Sforza — and that is a **sibling-dating** question, not a derivation. On
the florin argument the Cary-Yale is the **earlier** of the two; either way its six-court,
seven-virtue structure looks like an **un-standardised early experiment** that the later,
four-court Visconti-Sforza had already trimmed back toward the norm.

## The fear question

**No fear** — the same logic as the Visconti-Sforza, only more so. This is **conspicuous
consumption**: elite, heraldic, openly commissioned, drowning in gold and silver. If anything the
Cary-Yale is **more** ostentatious — more gold, more courts, more virtues — which is the opposite
of something hidden [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights]. The only contemporary anxiety around cards
was the friars' campaign against **gambling**, which targeted the **wager**, not the imagery. A
Milanese courtier would have **shown this deck off**, not concealed it.

## Counter-voices

Because the Cary-Yale shares the **theological virtues** with the much later **Minchiate**, some
esoteric readers treat it as evidence of a **lost "complete" sacred system** later censored down
to the canonical 22 trumps. The simpler, **better-supported** reading is the reverse: the early
trump set was **unfixed**, and **standardisation** (not censorship) trimmed and regularised it
over time — the Cary-Yale captures a moment **before** the count settled, not a purged remnant of
a suppressed canon [@dummett1980; @web_th_modrone_meneghello]. The presence of the virtues as a
**complete moral programme** (four cardinal + three theological) fits a **Christian-court
commission**, not a hermetic one.

A second counter-voice is **internal to the attribution**, exactly as for the sister deck:
"**Bembo**" is the conventional label, not a settled fact — **Giuliana Algeri** proposed the
**Zavattari** workshop on stylistic grounds, and museums hedge toward "workshop of" rather than
the master's own hand [@web_bembo_wp; @web_vs_wp] (confidence: medium — individual hand genuinely
debated; assert "Milanese court work, mid-15th c.," not a flat "by Bembo").

## People

- **`bonifacio-bembo`** — Cremonese court painter; the pack is **traditionally attributed to his
  workshop**, with Zavattari proposed as the rival hand [@web_bembo_wp; @web_vs_wp].
- **`visconti-sforza-patrons`** — Filippo Maria Visconti, Bianca Maria Visconti, and Francesco
  Sforza, who **commissioned** (did not paint) the deck; the Coin pips' florin and the dynastic
  imagery date and explain it, and the 1441 marriage is its most-cited occasion
  [@web_bianca_wp; @web_th_modrone_meneghello].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Virtues: replaced or added?** Whether the three theological virtues **displaced** standard
  trumps or were **additional** to a fuller set is unsettled [@web_tarotmysterium_modrone].
- **The date.** The **c. 1442** anchor leans on Berti's florin argument; estimates in the
  literature range **c. 1428 – c. 1450**, and not all scholars treat the florin evidence as
  decisive [@web_th_modrone_meneghello; @web_tarotmysterium_modrone; @web_cary_yale].
- **The 1441-wedding occasion** is the most-discussed theory, not documented fact; the
  Lovers-as-wedding-portrait reading is plausible but a hypothesis [@web_cary_yale; @web_bianca_wp].
- **Bembo vs. Zavattari.** The individual hand is genuinely debated; state the debate, do not
  assert Bembo as fact [@web_bembo_wp; @web_vs_wp].
- **Original count.** "~86" is a reconstruction (22 trumps + 64 six-court minors); the surviving
  count of **67** is firm [@web_beinecke_cary_highlights; @kaplan_caryyale_1984].
