# Etteilla II — Grand Livre de Thot (Égyptien)

## At a glance

The **first successful posthumous re-edition** of Alliette's oracle, and the form in which
"Etteilla" became a mass-market product. Issued **c. 1838–1840**, roughly half a century after
Alliette's death (1791), it is **not by Alliette** — it is the work of the Lille printer-publisher
**Simon-François Blocquel** (1780–1863), who issued the accompanying instruction book *Le Grand
Etteilla, ou l'Art de tirer les cartes* under the **pseudonym "Julia Orsini"** [@web_grand_etteilla;
@web_archive_orsini; @web_druidcraft]. Collectors call it the **"Lismon" Etteilla** (after the later
publisher Z. Lismon, an anagram of Blismon/Blocquel) or simply **Etteilla Type II**
[@web_mccloskys_lismon; @web_druidcraft]. It keeps Alliette's entire divinatory machine — the same
1–78 numbering, the same cosmogony-then-virtues trump sequence, the querent significators, the
printed upright/reversed readings — but repackages it as an explicitly **"Egyptian"** retail object:
**full French titles printed on both sides of every card**, stencil-coloured **blue borders**, a
six-colour palette, and **five redrawn trumps**. The deltas are therefore almost entirely
**cosmetic and commercial**, not systemic; the system is Etteilla I's [@web_etteilla_org;
@web_druidcraft].

## Origin & dating

Blocquel — a Lille printer who ran the family firm and wrote much of its output under a stable of
anagrammatic pseudonyms (**Blismon, Z. Lismon, Zlismon, Buccellos, Milbons, Monblis**, and the
non-anagram **"Julia Orsini"**) — published the *Grand Livre de Thot* / *Grand Etteilla* package
**c. 1838** [@web_grand_etteilla; @web_archive_orsini]. The booklet *Le Grand Etteilla, ou l'Art de
tirer les cartes* "was first published in 1838 and remained in print for almost a century"
[@web_archive_orsini]. The deck is commonly dated **1838 or 1840**; the two dates are reported
together and both are defensible (booklet vs. card issue), so the dossier carries the range
**c. 1838–1840** (confidence: medium — exact first-card-printing date not pinned to a primary)
[@web_druidcraft; @web_grand_etteilla]. The "Julia Orsini" name is **a publisher's pseudonym**, not
a real cartomancer — plausibly a tongue-in-cheek nod to Giulia Orsini (Farnese), mistress of Pope
Alexander VI (confidence: low on the joke, high on the pseudonymity) [@web_grand_etteilla].

> A naming caution. "Etteilla II" is a **collector's / scholar's label** (Type I/II/III) for
> distinguishing Alliette's 1788–89 deck from the 1838–40 Blocquel re-edition and the c. 1870
> "Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames" (Type III). It was sold simply as *Grand Etteilla* /
> *Tarots égyptiens* [@web_druidcraft; @web_steve_grandet].

## Provenance & evidence

Surviving decks and booklets are well attested in the antiquarian trade and reconstruction
literature. The instruction book survives in digitised form (Internet Archive holds an *1850* Orsini
*Le Grand Etteilla* printing) [@web_archive_orsini]. etteilla.org catalogues the deck as **"Etteilla
II: Jeu des 78 tarots égyptiens — Livre de Thot"** [@web_etteilla_org]. Antiquarian dealers
(McClosky's) catalogue surviving **Lismon Type II** packs from c. 1838–50 through c. 1890, describing
the physical type: **French titles on either side of the image, a single black-line inner border,
numbered at all four corners, with upright and reversed meanings at top and bottom**, and
**stencil-painted blue borders** in a six-colour palette (light blue, red, green, yellow, gold,
peach) [@web_mccloskys_lismon; @web_steve_grandet]. Steve P.'s card-by-card pages and benebell wen's
reconstruction notes document the redrawn trumps; the Tarot History Forum has a long-running
"Etteilla Timeline and card Variants" thread [@web_steve_grandet; @web_grand_etteilla;
@web_tarothistory_timeline].

## Structure

**Identical to Etteilla I in every structural respect** — this is the key fact. A **78-card pack**:
22 trumps (numbered **1–21 plus the unnumbered/zero La Folie**) and 56 minors in four suits of 14
(Bâtons/Fire, Coupes/Water, Épées/Air, Deniers/Earth: Roi, Reine, Chevalier, Valet, Ace–Ten). The
trump sequence is Alliette's: cards **1–8** the Chaos-plus-seven-days cosmogony (male significator =
**1 Le Chaos**, female significator = **8 Le Repos**), **9–12** the four cardinal virtues (Justice,
Tempérance, Force, Prudence), **13–21** the reworked Marseille trumps, plus the Fool outside the
count [@web_etteilla_org; @web_druidcraft]. Numbering and the cosmogony order are **unchanged from
Etteilla I**; the divinatory readings are the same Etteilla wordlists.

What is genuinely **new in the object** (not the system):
- **Full titles on both sides of every card**, printed outside the picture frame — where Etteilla I
  carried only short keyword-titles on the left/right borders, Etteilla II adds a proper card
  *name* repeated on each side [@web_druidcraft].
- **Four-corner numbering** and top/bottom upright-vs-reversed meaning blocks [@web_mccloskys_lismon].
- **Stencilled blue borders**, six-colour palette — the visual signature of the "Lismon" type
  [@web_mccloskys_lismon; @web_steve_grandet].
- **Five redrawn trumps (1, 10, 12, 15, 21)** — see *What changed*.

## Game or divination?

**Divination**, wholly — never a game pack. By the 1838–40 of its issue, Alliette's purpose-built
oracle had become a **commercial publishing genre**: a boxed, titled, instruction-bundled retail
product of the French print trade [@web_druidcraft; @decker1996].

## What changed from its parent

Its parent is **Etteilla I — Livre de Thot** (Alliette, 1788–89) [@etteilla-i-livre-de-thot]. The
changes are a **repackaging**, not a redesign of the system, and they are best understood as the
commercial "Egyptianisation" of an already-finished oracle:

1. **Authorship and pedigree.** Etteilla I is Alliette's own deck and book. Etteilla II is
   **posthumous**, made by **Blocquel** writing as **"Julia Orsini"** — a continuation by the
   "school," not the hand [@web_grand_etteilla; @web_druidcraft]. The Egyptian-Book-of-Thoth myth
   Alliette *invented* is here a **marketing brand** ("Tarots égyptiens") [@web_steve_grandet].

2. **Full card titles on both sides.** "The main difference between Etteilla I and Etteilla II is
   that titles have been added to all cards, repeated on both sides of the picture outside the
   frame" [@web_druidcraft]. This is the single most consistent delta and the one Etteilla II
   **shares with Etteilla III — they carry the same titles** [@web_druidcraft].

3. **Five redrawn trumps — 1, 10, 12, 15, 21.** The imagery is "essentially similar to Etteilla I
   but changed in some respects" [@web_druidcraft]; the documented redraws are: **1 Le Chaos** (the
   pre-creation void redrawn *without the sun* — logical, the sun not yet created); **10 La
   Tempérance** (now holding an elephant's **bridle of restraint**, an elephant beside her, *not*
   pouring between two vessels); **12 La Prudence** (a serpent now climbing her arm, a **mirror** in
   her hand, instead of the figure stepping around a snake on the ground); **15 Le Bateleur** (the
   magician moved to the **right** of the table, a small **statue/homunculus** added before him);
   **21 Le Despote Africain** (the chariot now driving **right-to-left**) [@web_steve_grandet;
   @web_benebell_etteilla]. The other ~73 cards are reprintings of Etteilla I's designs in the new
   border/colour style.

4. **One re-title that masks a renumbering history — Card 13 "Le Grand Prêtre."** Etteilla I's Card
   13 was **Le Mariage** ("Marriage/Union," answering the Marseille **Lovers/L'Amoureux VI**).
   Etteilla II/III re-title the same card **"Le Grand Prêtre" ("The High Priest")** — emphasising
   the *officiant* of the marriage rather than the union itself, which is why so many modern keys
   map this card to the Hierophant [@web_steve_grandet; @web_etteillastrumps]. **The card's
   identity (Lovers) and its divinatory sense (marriage/union, counsel, sanction) are inherited
   from Etteilla I; only the printed *title* drifts.** This is the title that misleads the repo
   grammars into an `arcana:the-hierophant` mapping (see *Open questions*).

5. **The reading apparatus is unchanged.** Same 1–78 Arabic numbering, same cosmogony order, same
   embedded significators, same upright/reversed wordlists. Etteilla II adds **no cards, removes
   none, renumbers none** versus Etteilla I [@web_etteilla_org; @web_druidcraft]. The delta is
   surface: titles, borders, colour, five redrawn trumps.

**For contrast (not this deck):** Etteilla III (c. 1870, *Grand Jeu de l'Oracle des Dames* / the
"Egyptian Gypsies" line later issued by Grimaud) has **wholly different, "rather grotesque" imagery**
only vaguely related to the original — a far larger redraw than Etteilla II's [@web_druidcraft]. Keep
the two distinct: Etteilla II is the *conservative* re-edition.

## The fear question

**Zero fear at the publisher's end.** By 1838–40, fortune-telling decks were a **normal retail
product** of the Paris/Lille print trade — printed, titled, sold openly with a how-to booklet, and
profitable enough to keep in print "for almost a century" [@web_archive_orsini; @web_druidcraft].
Blocquel's only "concealment" is the marketing kind: a pen-name on the booklet and an Egyptian
brand on the box, both commercial flourishes, not flight from law or Church. The criminalised,
precarious end of cartomancy remained the *itinerant* reader, not the boxed deck [@decker1996].

## Counter-voices

The Etteilla II/III proliferation is the clearest evidence for a thesis the repo holds against the
"suppressed antiquity" story: a **manufactured tradition** can become a self-sustaining market in two
generations. The "ancient Egyptian tarot" Alliette invented in 1788 was, by 1838, a saleable genre
label — proof of a successful **19th-century commercial myth**, not of recovered Egyptian wisdom
[@decker1996; @dummett1980]. Honour the durable practice; flag the brand.

## People

- **`etteilla`** — Jean-Baptiste Alliette, the *original* author of the system Etteilla II reprints.
  **He did not make this deck** (he died in 1791); it carries his name as a brand.
- **Simon-François Blocquel** (1780–1863), Lille printer, the actual maker, writing as **"Julia
  Orsini"** — no person dossier yet; a strong candidate for one [@web_grand_etteilla].

    "exact"`. The card's *historical identity* is the **Lovers / "Le Mariage"** (Etteilla I);
    "Grand Prêtre" is the **Etteilla II/III title drift**, the officiant of the marriage. Either
    keep the Hierophant *title* mapping but lower confidence and note the Lovers ancestry, or
    re-map. Flag as `medium`, not `exact`.
  - **Card 21 "Le Despote Africain"** is mapped `arcana:the-emperor`. Per the parent dossier the
    closer Marseille source is the **Chariot (VII)**, not the Emperor; `mapping_confidence` already

  - **Card 78 "La Folie"** is named **"(The Questioner — Female)"** and the block reads "the female
    querent (significator)." This is **wrong**, the same error as Etteilla I: La Folie is the
