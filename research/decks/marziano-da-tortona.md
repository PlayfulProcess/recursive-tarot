# Marziano da Tortona — the lost FIRST trump deck (the text survives, the cards do not)

**The earliest documented deck with a fifth, picture suit predates the Visconti-Sforza
trionfi — and we know it only because the WRITING survived while the painted cards were
lost.** This is a research note, not a deck: there is nothing to render here that is an
artifact. Any image of "the Marziano deck" online is a **modern reconstruction** from the
text (the video author made one; so have others). We flag that loudly.

## What survives
Around **1418–1425**, **Marziano da Tortona**, secretary to **Filippo Maria Visconti, Duke of
Milan**, wrote a short Latin treatise — *Tractatus de deificatione sexdecim heroum* ("on the
deification of sixteen heroes") — describing a card game built on Filippo's love of the Greek
gods and Petrarch's hunt poetry. The **physical cards, painted by Michelino da Besozzo, are
lost.** What survives is **Marziano's text** — the description and the order of play —
preserved because **Jacopo Antonio Marcello** sent the treatise (with, he says, the deck) to
**Isabella of Lorraine in 1449**, and the letter + treatise are held at the **Bibliothèque
nationale de France**. So the "first tarot" is reconstructable in *idea* from a manuscript, not
from any surviving object.

## What the text describes
- **16 gods**, ranked, standing in for the trumps-to-come.
- **Four "bird" suits**, each a classical theme, ranked highest→lowest:
  **Eagles = Virtues · Phoenixes = Riches · Turtledoves = Virginities/Continences · Doves = Pleasures.**
- The sixteen gods by suit: **Virtues** — Jupiter, Apollo, Mercury, Hercules; **Riches** — Juno,
  Neptune, Mars, Aeolus; **Virginities** — Pallas (Athena), Diana, Vesta, Daphne; **Pleasures** —
  Venus, Bacchus, Ceres, Cupid.
- **Object of the game:** a *hunt* / card-capture mechanic (take the low cards with higher-ranked
  ones) — an ancestor of the thousands of capture games that followed. (confidence: the structure
  is from the text; fine details of play are debated.)

## Why it matters — and the honest caveat
This is **not a tarot**: no 22 standard trumps, no cups/coins/swords/batons. It is the
**conceptual first move** — a deck where a *themed picture series* is laid over ordinary suits —
that the Milanese **trionfi** (Visconti-Sforza, the Cary-Yale) would, decades later, reshape into
the 22-trump tarot. Treat the Marziano deck as a **documented prototype, not the tarot's direct
parent**, and remember the imagery is interpretive reconstruction.

## Context 1 — Before the trumps: triumphal processions (Cola di Rienzo → Petrarch)
The trump *idea* did not appear from nowhere. After the Black Death, the Roman demagogue
**Cola di Rienzo** (1313–1354) revived antiquity's **triumphal processions** as grand civic
spectacle. His friend the poet **Petrarch** turned the imagery inward in *I Trionfi* (the
*Triumphs*) — a sequence of allegorical processions where **Love is conquered by Chastity, then
Death, then Fame, then Time, then Eternity.** That *ordered procession of allegories that each
overcomes the last* is the template the tarot's trumps echo — the cards read like a triumphal
march held in the hand. (confidence: high on Petrarch's influence as a *cultural source*; the
exact card-by-card mapping is interpretive, not documentary.)

## Context 2 — How the first deck reached France: Marcello's "royal robbery" (1449)
The Marziano deck "would have remained a courtly exclusive," but **Queen Isabella of Lorraine**
wanted one. Her subject **Jacopo Antonio Marcello**, seeking her favour (and protection for his
Venetian estates), obtained the Visconti deck **and its instruction book** and sent them to her
in **1449** — which is precisely **why the text survives at all.** By then the deck was already
out of date: Milan had moved on to the newer **22-trump** decks (the Cary-Yale, c. 1441), so the
old 16-god game was, in effect, the obsolete version that got mailed away and thereby preserved.
