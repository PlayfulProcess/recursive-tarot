# Oswald Wirth Tarot — Les 22 Arcanes du Tarot Kabbalistique

## At a glance

The **first tarot conceived as an explicitly occult, initiatic instrument** — twenty-two
Major Arcana **only**, no minors, no game function. **Oswald Wirth** (1860–1943) drew them in
**1889** under the direction of the Kabbalist **Stanislas de Guaita**, who commissioned him to
"restore the 22 Major Arcana to their hieroglyphic purity" along the lines laid down by
**Éliphas Lévi** [@web_wirth_wp; @decker2002]. The 1889 deck — *Les 22 Arcanes du Tarot
Kabbalistique* — appeared as a small hand-coloured, gilt-edged series and its trumps were
reproduced in **Papus's *Le Tarot des Bohémiens* (1889)** [@web_wirth_wp; @web_22arcanes].
Wirth issued a **revised, more famous version in 1926**, published alongside his treatise
*Le Tarot des imagiers du Moyen Âge* (book dated **1926/1927**) [@web_harrington_wirth;
@wirth1927]. Two things define the deck historically: it **keeps the Tarot de Marseille
iconography and numbering** (including **Strength = XI / Justice = VIII** — Wirth did **not**
adopt the Golden Dawn swap), and onto that frame it **prints a Hebrew letter on each trump**
following **Lévi's** 1850s attribution, fixing the trump↔alphabet marriage that defines
Continental esoteric tarot [@web_gd_swap; @web_levi_rota; @decker2002].

## Origin & dating

Wirth was a Swiss-born occultist, illustrator and Freemason who, after a Theosophical phase,
was **introduced to Stanislas de Guaita in 1887** and became his secretary and collaborator;
both belonged to the **Ordre Kabbalistique de la Rose-Croix** [@web_wirth_wp; @decker2002]. De
Guaita, steeped in Lévi, **commissioned Wirth not only to illustrate his books but to draw the
22 trumps** "restored to hieroglyphic purity" per Lévi's program [@web_wirth_wp;
@web_22arcanes]. The result, **published 1889** as *Les 22 Arcanes du Tarot Kabbalistique*, was
a small run of **hand-coloured, gilt-edged** cards (collector notes commonly cite about
**300 copies**, a figure resting on secondary sources only — *confidence: medium*)
[@web_22arcanes; @web_wirth_wp]. The trumps were reproduced in **Papus's *Le Tarot des
Bohémiens*** the same year, which carried Wirth's drawings to a far wider readership than the
tiny deck edition [@web_wirth_wp].

Decades later Wirth **redrew the 22 trumps** and published the mature version in **1926**,
accompanied by his major interpretive treatise **_Le Tarot des imagiers du Moyen Âge_** (the
book is variously dated **1926 and 1927** in catalogues; both circulate) [@web_harrington_wirth;
@wirth1927; @web_gallica_wirth]. This 1926 edition is the one most people now mean by "the
Wirth Tarot."

## Provenance & evidence

The 1889 deck and the 1926 revision both survive, as do Wirth's writings; the BnF holds copies
and Gallica carries *Le Tarot des imagiers du Moyen Âge* [@web_gallica_wirth]. The grammar's
card images are drawn from the **1889 BnF edition** via Wikimedia Commons. The lineage is
documented and explicit — **Lévi (text, 1850s) → de Guaita (patron/director, 1889) → Wirth
(deck)** — and is treated as the backbone of the Continental occult tarot by the Decker–Dummett
history of the occult tarot [@decker2002]. Marco Benedetti's facsimile work (via Tarot Heritage)
and Peter Harrington's bibliographic description of the 1926/27 book give the modern
collector-grade provenance for distinguishing the two editions [@tarot_heritage;
@web_harrington_wirth].

## Structure

**Twenty-two cards: the Major Arcana only.** There are **no minor arcana, no suits, no court
cards** — this is a trumps-only philosophical/meditative object, not a 78-card pack
[@web_wirth_wp]. The trumps follow the **Tarot de Marseille** sequence and names (Le Bateleur,
La Papesse, L'Impératrice, L'Empereur, Le Pape, L'Amoureux, Le Chariot, La Justice, L'Ermite,
La Roue de Fortune, La Force, Le Pendu, [La Mort], Tempérance, Le Diable, [La Maison-Dieu, which
Wirth titles **Le Feu du Ciel** / "Fire of Heaven"], Les Étoiles, La Lune, Le Soleil, Le
Jugement, Le Monde, and Le Fou/Le Mat). Each trump carries its **French title** and a **Hebrew
letter**, the defining visible innovation over the Marseille game deck [@web_wirth_wp;
@web_22arcanes].

**Trump order — the load-bearing point.** Wirth **retains the Marseille order**, with
**VIII = Justice** and **XI = Strength (La Force)**. He did **not** adopt the **Golden Dawn
swap** (which moved Strength to VIII and Justice to XI to align Strength with Leo and Justice
with Libra). Sources note Wirth deliberately **emphasised the number 8 for Justice** — Justice
as balance, not the Golden Dawn's zodiacal reshuffle [@web_gd_swap]. This is the single most
useful structural fact distinguishing Wirth's Continental scheme from the Anglophone
(Golden Dawn / Waite–Smith) one.

**The Hebrew-letter scheme (Lévi's, not the Golden Dawn's).** Lévi assigned **Aleph to Trump I,
Le Bateleur** ("his body and arms constitute the letter Aleph"), then ran the alphabet **up the
numbered trumps in order**, and placed **the Fool (Le Mat) = Shin**, unnumbered, **between
Trump XX (Judgement) and Trump XXI (The World)** [@hermetics_marathakis;
@web_levi_arrangement]. So the correct Lévi/Wirth correspondence is:

| Trump | Card | Hebrew letter |
|---|---|---|
| I | Le Bateleur (Magician) | א Aleph |
| II | La Papesse | ב Beth |
| III | L'Impératrice | ג Gimel |
| IV | L'Empereur | ד Daleth |
| V | Le Pape | ה He |
| VI | L'Amoureux | ו Vau |
| VII | Le Chariot | ז Zain |
| VIII | La Justice | ח Cheth |
| IX | L'Ermite | ט Teth |
| X | La Roue de Fortune | י Yod |
| XI | La Force | כ Kaph |
| XII | Le Pendu | ל Lamed |
| XIII | La Mort | מ Mem |
| XIV | Tempérance | נ Nun |
| XV | Le Diable | ס Samekh |
| XVI | Le Feu du Ciel (Tower) | ע Ayin |
| XVII | Les Étoiles | פ Pe |
| XVIII | La Lune | צ Tzaddi |
| XIX | Le Soleil | ק Qoph |
| XX | Le Jugement | ר Resh |
| — | **Le Fou / Le Mat** | **ש Shin** *(between XX and XXI)* |
| XXI | Le Monde | ת Tav |

This is the **Continental** scheme: **Aleph follows the Magician, Shin the Fool**. The
**Golden Dawn** scheme is **different** — it puts the Fool first as 0 = Aleph and shifts every
letter down one card; do **not** conflate the two [@web_levi_arrangement; @decker2002].

## Game or divination?

**Neither, primarily — it is esoteric/initiatic.** Where Etteilla (1788–89) built a deck *to
tell fortunes*, Wirth built one *to meditate and initiate upon*. It can be read divinatorily
(de Guaita called it "cartomantic"), but its reason for existing is the **symbolic system**:
the 22 trumps as an initiatory ladder mapped to the Hebrew alphabet and the Kabbalistic Tree of
Life [@web_wirth_wp; @decker2002]. It was never a game deck — there are no cards to play a game
with.

## What changed from its parent

Its parent is the **Tarot de Marseille** (the Conver-pattern trump iconography and numbering)
[@derives_from]. Wirth **preserves the outlines and the order** but **overlays an esoteric
apparatus** and **edits details to carry symbolic meaning**:

1. **Added Hebrew letters and the Lévi correspondence.** The Marseille game deck carried no
   alphabet; Wirth **prints a Hebrew letter on every trump**, fixing **Lévi's** trump↔letter
   scheme onto the cards for the first time. This is the deck's defining departure
   [@web_wirth_wp; @web_22arcanes; @web_levi_arrangement].
2. **Kept the Marseille order — refused the Golden Dawn swap.** Strength stays **XI**, Justice
   stays **VIII**; the Fool keeps **Shin** and sits between XX and XXI (Lévi's placement). The
   contemporaneous English occultists were doing the opposite [@web_gd_swap;
   @web_levi_arrangement].
3. **Imported Lévi's iconographic edits.** Most visibly, the **Chariot is drawn by two
   sphinxes** (one light, one dark) rather than Marseille horses, and the **Wheel of Fortune**
   follows Lévi's "cosmogonical wheel" with a rising figure, a falling figure (Hermanubis /
   Typhon), and **a sword-bearing sphinx in equilibrium above** [@web_th_wheel; @web_th_chariot].
4. **Layered alchemical, Hermetic and Masonic symbolism** onto the figures — colour symbolism,
   elemental and planetary correspondences, the **lemniscate (∞)** over the Magician and over
   Strength — turning each Marseille image into a dense emblem rather than a card-game trump
   [@web_wirth_wp; @web_22arcanes; @wirth1927].
5. **Renamed the Tower.** Wirth titles **La Maison-Dieu** as **Le Feu du Ciel** ("the Fire of
   Heaven"), reframing the struck tower as a higher force shattering false structures
   [@web_22arcanes]. *(confidence: medium — title on the BnF plate; reading per Wirth's text.)*

**1889 vs. 1926 — the same deck, redrawn.** Wirth revised several cards between editions. The
best-documented changes: on **Le Bateleur** the blue/red ends of the wand are inverted and the
table shows **one** object-shield rather than three; **La Papesse** loses her **dark skin and
black-and-red columns** (a fin-de-siècle Egyptianising touch) for **light skin and blue-and-red
columns** in 1926 — the card Wirth most visibly Westernised between editions [@web_cartomancer].
The Hebrew-letter scheme and the Marseille order are **unchanged** across both editions.

## The fear question

**Openly published, inside a fashionable subculture.** Wirth, de Guaita and Papus were public
figures of a bourgeois Parisian occult *movement* — salons, initiatic orders, a real publishing
trade. There was **no legal peril** for them; the "secrecy" was the deliberate mystique of
initiation, not fear of prosecution. The contrast the collection keeps drawing holds here too:
respectable occult *philosophy* was safe and salable, while the jobbing, déclassé
*fortune-teller* doing the same gesture for coins was the criminalised version
[@decker2002; @web_wirth_wp].

## Counter-voices

The deck embodies the esoteric tradition's self-image: that it **restores** an ancient symbolic
tarot. The historical caveat is firm — **Lévi's Hebrew-letter assignment is an invention of the
1850s**, laid over a 15th-century Italian card game, not a medieval survival [@decker2002;
@dummett1980]. But Wirth's deck is comparatively **honest about being a construction**: it
visibly **builds a new symbolic system on the Marseille**, and Wirth's own treatise treats the
trumps as designed emblems. Unlike Gébelin's flat-false Egyptian *antiquity* claim, the Wirth
deck is best recorded as a **well-documented esoteric overlay (1889 / 1926)**, not a recovered
tradition [@decker2002; @web_wirth_wp].

## People

- **`oswald-wirth`** — drew and published the 22 trumps (1889), redrew them (1926), and wrote
  *Le Tarot des imagiers du Moyen Âge* (1926/27).
- **`eliphas-levi`** — supplied the trump↔Hebrew-letter and Kabbalistic correspondence Wirth
  follows; designed no deck himself.
- **Stanislas de Guaita** (1861–1897) — commissioned and directed the 1889 deck; Wirth's
  patron and the bridge from Lévi's text to Wirth's cards (no person dossier yet; candidate for
  one).
- **Papus (Gérard Encausse)** — reproduced Wirth's 1889 trumps in *Le Tarot des Bohémiens*,
  carrying them to a wide audience (candidate for a person dossier).

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **The grammar mis-places the Fool's letter logic.** The grammar lists **Le Fou = Shin at
  sort_order 0** (Golden-Dawn-style position) while elsewhere assigning the numbered trumps in
  Lévi's order. In **Lévi's / Wirth's actual scheme the Fool (Shin) is unnumbered and sits
  between XX Judgement and XXI World** — not at 0. The *letters* the grammar assigns to the
  numbered trumps (Magician = Aleph … World = Tav) are **correct** for Lévi; only the Fool's
  **position** reflects a later convention. Flag this so the grammar does not imply the Golden
  Dawn arrangement (see cards file).
- Confirm the **1889 print run** against a primary or Decker–Dummett (the "≈300 copies" figure
  is collector lore).
- Pin **1926 vs. 1927** for the deck plates vs. the book against the BnF/Gallica record.
- Tabulate **every** image change between the 1889 and 1926 editions beyond Le Bateleur and La
  Papesse.
- Minor: the grammar's per-card "astrological association" mixes planetary and zodiacal
  attributions inconsistently with Lévi's own (e.g. several "traditionally associated with"
  signs) — verify each against Wirth's text before promoting from medium confidence.
