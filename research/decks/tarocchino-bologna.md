# Tarocchino di Bologna

## At a glance

Bologna's own shrunken tarot: a **62-card** pack — the **2, 3, 4 and 5 of every suit are
removed**, leaving 24 pips (Ace + 6–10 in each suit), 16 court cards, 21 numbered/figured trumps
and the Fool — for a fast trick-taking game that has been **played continuously for roughly five
centuries** and is still played in Bologna today [@web_tarocchini_wp; @web_tarocco_bolognese_wp;
@dummett1980]. It is a core witness to Dummett's **A-order** of the trumps, with two famous local
peculiarities: the three Virtues sit **clustered in the middle** of the sequence, and the **Angel
(Judgement) is the highest trump, ranked above the World** [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp;
@web_pollett_orders; @dummett1980]. Its single most distinctive feature is the **four equal-rank
papal cards** — turned by a **1725 papal edict** into the **four Moors** (*Mori*) — which is why
the genealogy treats the Bolognese pack as a touchstone, not as an ancestor of the Tarot de
Marseille [@web_druidcraft_montieri; @web_tarocchini_wp]. Read this as the best-attested **use**
history of any tarot family: we know how it was *played*, not merely how it looked
[@web_pagat_ottocento; @dummett2004].

## Origin & dating

Tarot itself is documented in Bologna **very early** — there are records of *trionfi* in the city
within a few years of the game's appearance (a Bologna document of **1442** is among the earliest
references) [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @web_pratesi_bologna]. The **62-card reduction** that gives
the *tarocchino* ("little tarocco") its name is generally placed in the **early 16th century**;
the diminutive simply refers to the shortened pack, and *tarocchino* is securely documented by
**1550** [@web_tarocchini_wp; @web_tarocco_bolognese_wp] (confidence: medium on the exact decade —
the reduction is inferred, not dated by a surviving document).

A persistent **local legend** ascribes the invention of the shortened pack to **Prince Francesco
Antelminelli Castracani Fibbia (c.1360–1419)**, a Pisan exile in Bologna. The story is **at least
17th-century folklore and is regarded as apocryphal** — Giordano Berti, among others, debunks it
in detail; there is no contemporary evidence and the dates do not fit the documentary record
[@web_forum_fibbia; @web_th_mitelli_berti] (confidence: high that the legend is apocryphal).

The deck has a celebrated artistic edition: the **copper-engraved Tarocchino made by Giuseppe
Maria Mitelli (1634–1718), c.1660–1665**, for Count **Filippo Bentivoglio**; Mitelli's engraving
gives more naturalistic, fluid figures than the usual woodblock cards, and several cards carry
Bentivoglio heraldry and popular mottoes [@web_th_mitelli_berti; @web_mitelli_wp;
@web_giordanoberti_mitelli]. The **modern game, Ottocento**, still preserves 16th-century
structural features, and the standard 19th–20th-century pattern (the double-headed "Al Mondo" /
Dal Negro / Modiano design) is the one whose images this dossier's card file documents
[@web_pagat_ottocento; @web_th_almondo; @web_modiano_tarocchino].

## Provenance & evidence

The Bolognese tradition is unusually well evidenced because it never lapsed: continuous printed
editions from named makers (Mitelli c.1660; the 18th-century *Al Trombetta* reprints; the modern
Dal Negro / Modiano patterns), a **living game with written rules**, and a documentary trail in
the Bologna archives studied by Franco Pratesi [@web_th_mitelli_barattini; @web_pratesi_bologna;
@web_pagat_ottocento]. The **International Playing-Card Society** catalogues the standard Bolognese
design as pattern **PS-11** [@web_ipcs_bolognese]. The Montieri affair of 1725 (below) is itself a
documentary anchor: it produced a papal edict and arrests recorded at the time
[@web_druidcraft_montieri].

## Structure

A **62-card** pack [@web_tarocchini_wp; @web_tarocco_bolognese_wp]:

- **Four Latin suits** — **Coins/Denari, Cups/Coppe, Swords/Spade, Batons/Bastoni** — each reduced
  to **Ace and 6–10** plus the **full four-card court (Re/King, Regina/Queen, Cavallo/Knight,
  Fante/Knave)**. The **2, 3, 4 and 5 are removed** from every suit. That is 6 ranks + 4 courts =
  **10 cards × 4 suits = 40** suit cards (24 pips + 16 courts) [@web_tarocchini_wp;
  @web_ipcs_bolognese].
- **22 trumps** in the count, but iconographically only **20 distinct subjects plus the four
  Moors collapsed onto one rank**: the **Fool** (the *Matto*, the unnumbered Excuse), the
  **Bagattino**, the **four Moors/Papi** (equal rank), then Love, Chariot, the three Virtues,
  Wheel, Old Man, Traitor, Death, Devil, Lightning, Star, Moon, Sun, World, Angel. 40 suit cards
  + 22 trumps = **62** [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @web_pollett_orders].
- **The A-order trump sequence** (Dummett's A): Bagattino · **four Moors (equal)** · Love (5) ·
  Chariot (6) · **Temperance (7) · Justice (8) · Strength/Fortitude (9)** · Wheel of Fortune (10)
  · Old Man/Hermit (11) · Traitor/Hanged Man (12) · Death (13) · Devil (14) · Lightning/Tower (15)
  · Star (16) · Moon · Sun · World · **Angel (highest)**; the **Fool** stands outside the order
  [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @web_pollett_orders; @dummett1980]. Two hallmarks: the **three
  Virtues are grouped together after the Chariot**, and the **Angel outranks the World** — the
  reverse of the Marseille summit [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @web_th_almondo].
- **Numbering is partial and deliberate.** Only trumps **5 to 16 carry printed numbers** (Love
  through the Star); the lowest trumps (Fool, Bagattino, the four Moors) and the highest (Moon,
  Sun, World, Angel) are **unnumbered** — a Bolognese tradition, since their rank is fixed by
  custom [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @web_steve_bolo]. **No card bears a printed title**
  [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp]. *(Note for the grammar: the `sort_order` values in
  `grammar.json` are positional indices 0–21, not the printed Bolognese card-numbers; the printed
  numbers run only 5–16. See Open questions.)*

## Game or divination?

**Game — and an unusually clear one**, because the playing tradition never lapsed. The Bolognese
context is overwhelmingly recreational: a fast partnership trick-taking game (modern **Ottocento**)
with sequences, *cricche*, and a scoring count, documented in continuous rule literature
[@web_pagat_ottocento; @dummett2004]. A minor cartomantic use of the Bolognese pack is attested
later (a "50-card method" circulates), but it is a secondary overlay on a deck that is, first and
last, a game pack [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp]. The card-by-card file therefore describes
**iconography and rank**, not upright/reversed fortune-telling meanings.

## What changed from its parent

The Tarocchino is a **descendant of the common 78-card *trionfi* root**, not of any one surviving
deck (`derives_from: []` — no single parent pack is documented). What the Bolognese tradition
*does* to that inherited 78-card pack is sharp and specific:

- **Strips the low pips.** The 2–5 of every suit are removed, taking the pack from 78 to 62
  [@web_tarocchini_wp].
- **Keeps the A-order**, with the clustered Virtues and the Angel above the World — diverging from
  the C-order that runs toward the Tarot de Marseille [@dummett1980; @web_pollett_orders].
- **Collapses the four "papal/imperial" cards into one equal rank**, and (after 1725) **re-images
  them as the four Moors** — see the next two sections [@web_druidcraft_montieri].

## The four Moors / Papi — the signature peculiarity

In the older Bolognese pack the **Popess, Empress, Emperor and Pope occupied a single equal rank**:
four cards that did not outrank one another, the **last one played winning the trick**
[@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @dummett1980]. The *equal rank* is the structural oddity; it predates
1725.

What 1725 changed was the **imagery**. In that year the priest **Rev. Luigi Montieri** published an
educational *Tarocchino* (a geography/heraldry/politics teaching pack) one card of which described
the government of Bologna as "mixed" — politically offensive, since Bologna was part of the **Papal
States**. The papal legate, **Cardinal Tommaso Ruffo**, had Montieri and the printers arrested,
burned the woodblocks and cards, and threatened large fines and a seven-year jail term for anyone
holding the offending deck [@web_druidcraft_montieri; @web_tarocco_bolognese_wp]. In the wake of
the affair, the legate **decreed that the four contentious papal/imperial cards be replaced by four
turbaned "Moors" (*Mori*)** — keeping their equal rank and keeping the Angel as the highest card.
**From 1725 onward every Bolognese pack shows four Moors instead of Popess/Empress/Emperor/Pope**;
the heretical-looking Popess simply vanishes [@web_druidcraft_montieri; @web_tarocchini_wp]. A later
production quirk made **two of the four Moors identical** (a tradition begun, it is said, when one
of the Moor designs was lost) [@web_tarocchini_wp] (confidence: medium on the "lost-design" cause —
the *two-identical* fact is well attested; the explanation is anecdotal).

## The fear question

**No fear of the cards as sorcery — but a real, documented fear of the cards as politics.** Bologna
is, ironically, the city where **Bernardino of Siena preached his 1423 anti-gambling sermon** — yet
the city's love of the game outlasted the friar by half a millennium, and the pressure was always
on the **wager**, not on the imagery as magic [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp; @dummett1980]. The one
sharp episode of suppression — the 1725 Montieri affair — was about a card that **insulted papal
sovereignty**, not about occultism; the Church's response (arrests, burned blocks, the mandated
Moors) was censorship of a *political* image, and it reshaped the deck permanently
[@web_druidcraft_montieri].

## Counter-voices

The key correction the repo draws from Bologna is **genealogical, not occult**: the **Tarot de
Marseille is not descended from the Bolognese Tarocchino**. They are **cousins** — Marseille is
C-order, Tarocchino is A-order — both children of the common *trionfi* root. Popular histories that
draw a straight Bologna→Marseille line are wrong [@dummett1980; @web_pollett_orders]. A second
counter-voice is the **Fibbia origin myth**, which would make Bologna the *birthplace* of tarot
(c.1419); it is local folklore, debunked by Berti and unsupported by the documents
[@web_forum_fibbia; @web_th_mitelli_berti].

## People

No individual maker is load-bearing for the deck's *identity* (it is a civic, anonymous tradition),
so `people: []`. Two names recur in the record and could earn `people/` stubs later: **Giuseppe
Maria Mitelli** (engraver of the famous c.1660 edition) and **Rev. Luigi Montieri** (whose 1725
teaching-pack triggered the Moors edict); **Cardinal Tommaso Ruffo** is the papal legate behind the
edict [@web_th_mitelli_berti; @web_druidcraft_montieri].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Exact date of the 62-card reduction** is fuzzy — "early 16th century," inferred rather than
  documented; *tarocchino* is firm only from **1550** [@web_tarocchini_wp].
- **Grammar `sort_order` vs printed numbering.** The grammar numbers the trumps 0–21 by position;
  the *actual* Bolognese cards number only **trumps 5–16** (Love–Star), with low and high trumps
  unnumbered. The grammar text should say this rather than imply a full 1–21 numbering
  [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp].
- **The four Moors are four cards, two of them identical** — the grammar's `trump-papa-c` reuses
  the `mor2.gif` image of `trump-papa-b`, which actually *matches* the real "two identical" quirk,
  but the dossier should record that this is a genuine feature, not an image error
  [@web_tarocchini_wp].
- **The 1725 story is richer than "after the 1725 controversy."** It was specifically the
  **Montieri educational pack** and **Cardinal Ruffo's** response; the *equal-rank* papal cards
  predate 1725 — only the **Moor imagery** is the 1725 change [@web_druidcraft_montieri].
- **Cartomantic use.** A secondary "50-card" reading method exists; worth a line, but it does not
  make this a divination deck [@web_tarocco_bolognese_wp].
