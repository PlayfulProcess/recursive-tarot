# Tarot de Besançon / Swiss 1JJ — The Juno & Jupiter Variant

## At a glance

A **confessionally-neutral variant of the Tarot de Marseille**: it keeps the *entire*
Marseille structure — 78 cards, the C-order trump sequence, the non-scenic pips, the four
Latin suits — and changes **exactly two cards**. The Catholic clergy trumps, the **Popess
(II)** and the **Pope (V)**, are replaced by the classical deities **Juno** (with her
peacock) and **Jupiter** (with his eagle and thunderbolt) [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon;
@dummett1980]. The pattern arose in **Strasbourg in the early 18th century**, spread through
**Alsace and southwest Germany into Switzerland**, and took the name **Besançon** when
cardmakers relocated production to that eastern French town in the early 19th century
[@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon]. Its best-known late descendant is the **Swiss 1JJ**
deck — "1JJ" because of the **J**uno and **J**upiter substitution — which in the 20th century
became, for an enormous number of Anglophone readers, the **first tarot they ever handled**:
it was the founding product of **U.S. Games Systems** [@web_swiss1jj_wp; @web_kaplan_usgames_1jj].
It is, in origin and through its whole early life, a **game deck**; the divinatory meanings
are a later overlay inherited (like the Marseille's) from the occult tradition.

## Origin & dating

The diagnostic substitution — Juno and Jupiter for Popess and Pope — appears on packs made
in **Strasbourg in the early 18th century**, then across **Alsace, Colmar, Konstanz, Ulm and
Munich**, with the bulk of production in **Alsace** [@web_besancon_wp; @web_swiss_tarot_wp].
The type is named for **Besançon**, the eastern French town to which cardmakers moved
production in the **early 19th century**; the name stuck to the whole style even though
Besançon was only one of several manufacturing centres [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon].
So the label "Besançon" denotes a **pattern**, not a single edition or maker.

The **Swiss 1JJ** is a later, standardised descendant of this pattern. Encyclopedia and
collector sources trace a specific Swiss line: a version produced **c. 1831–1838** in the
card factory of **Johann Georg Rauch**, continued by his successor **Johannes Müller**
(Diessenhofen) into the mid-19th century, and reprinted in the 20th century by the
Schaffhausen firm **A.G. Müller** (whose cards bear "FABRIQUE DE CARTES À SCHAFFHOUSE")
[@web_swiss1jj_wp; @web_swiss_tarot_wp]. (confidence: medium — the Rauch/Müller/AGMüller
dates rest on encyclopedia and trade sources, not a primary catalogue seen here.) The deck
entered the modern English-speaking world through **Stuart R. Kaplan**, who found it at the
**Nuremberg Toy Fair in 1968**, brought it to New York, and made it the founding deck of
**U.S. Games Systems** (published 1970 with his companion booklet *Tarot Cards for Fun and
Fortune Telling*) [@web_kaplan_usgames_1jj; @web_swiss1jj_wp].

## Provenance & evidence

Many printed editions survive in public and trade collections; the **Juno/Jupiter
substitution is the diagnostic feature** that identifies a deck as Besançon-type
[@web_th_besancon]. The **British Museum** holds a Besançon-type pack
(P,1940.0730.1.1–78) [@web_bm_besancon], and the **World of Playing Cards** documents named
makers such as **Jacob (Jacques) Jerger** working in the Besançon area
[@web_wopc_besancon_jerger]. Tarot Heritage catalogues the family (e.g. the **Miller 1780**
Besançon and the Lequart–Arnoult c. 1880 deck) [@web_th_besancon; @web_th_besancon_tag]. The
card images used by this collection's grammar are public-domain Swiss 1JJ scans on Wikimedia
Commons (Category: Tarot 1JJ), some of which are **trilingually titled** (French / German /
English) — e.g. *Der Herrscher* (Emperor), *Der Teufel* (Devil), *The Chariot*, *La Force*
[@web_aeclectic_1jj; @web_learntarot_1jj].

## Structure

A standard **78-card Marseille pack** — there is **no structural deviation** beyond the two
substituted trumps:

- **22 trumps**, numbered **I–XXI** plus the unnumbered **Fool (Le Mat)**, in the **C
  (southern / Marseille) order** [@dummett1980; @web_besancon_wp]. The sequence is identical
  to the Marseille de Conver, with two relabelled cards:
  **I Le Bateleur, II JUNO (in place of La Papesse), III L'Impératrice, IIII L'Empereur,
  V JUPITER (in place of Le Pape), VI L'Amoureux, VII Le Chariot, VIII La Justice,
  VIIII L'Hermite, X La Roue de Fortune, XI La Force, XII Le Pendu, XIII [Death, unnamed],
  XIIII Tempérance, XV Le Diable, XVI La Maison Dieu, XVII L'Étoile, XVIII La Lune,
  XVIIII Le Soleil, XX Le Jugement, XXI Le Monde** [@web_besancon_wp; @dummett1980].
  - **Justice VIII / Strength XI** — the **pre-Golden-Dawn order**, kept exactly as in the
    Marseille (the Besançon makes no virtue swap) [@web_swiss_tarot_wp; @web_aeclectic_1jj].
  - **Death (XIII)** bears its number but no name, the Marseille convention [@dummett1980].
- **56 minors** in four Latin suits of 14 — **Deniers (Coins), Coupes (Cups), Épées (Swords),
  Bâtons (Batons)**, each Ace–Ten plus **Roi, Reine, Cavalier, Valet** — with **non-scenic,
  emblematic pips** identical in kind to the Marseille [@web_learntarot_1jj; @web_aeclectic_1jj].
- **The only diff from the parent is Juno↔Papess (II) and Jupiter↔Pope (V).** Everything
  else is standard Marseille [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon; @dummett1980].

## Game or divination?

**Game first**, exactly like its Marseille parent: the Besançon pattern was printed and sold
for the trick-taking game *tarot/Tarock* across French, German and Swiss markets
[@dummett2004; @dummett1980; @web_th_besancon]. The substitution itself is **not** a
divinatory or esoteric move — it is a commercial-confessional one (see below). The deck's
**divinatory afterlife** is genuine but later and external: the 20th-century **Swiss 1JJ**
was marketed by U.S. Games as a fortune-telling deck and, through Kaplan's booklet, became a
**gateway oracle** for Anglophone readers [@web_kaplan_usgames_1jj; @web_swiss1jj_wp]. So in
its commercial life the 1JJ straddles the game→oracle transition — but the reading-meanings
are a documented overlay, not native to the object [@decker1996; @dummett1980].

## What changed from its parent

Its `derives_from` parent is the **Tarot de Marseille (`tarot-de-marseille-conver`)**, and
the diff is the **smallest of any deck in this collection**: a **two-card relabelling**.

- **Popess (II) → Juno.** The Marseille's tiara-and-book female "Pope" — already the most
  doctrinally awkward trump — is replaced by the goddess **Juno**, attended by her
  **peacock** [@web_besancon_wp; @web_aeclectic_1jj; @web_th_besancon].
- **Pope (V) → Jupiter.** The Marseille's enthroned, blessing Pope is replaced by
  **Jupiter**, enthroned with his **eagle** (and often a thunderbolt)
  [@web_besancon_wp; @web_aeclectic_1jj].
- **Why:** to let the Marseille pattern travel into **Protestant and confessionally mixed
  markets** (eastern France, Switzerland, the Rhineland) where **Catholic clerical figures
  were unwelcome on playing cards**. Replacing them with neutral classical deities — who,
  conveniently, were the king and queen of the Roman pantheon and so preserved the cards'
  "rank" within the trump hierarchy — removed the offence without disturbing the game
  [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon; @web_swiss1jj_wp]. The numbering, order, virtues, pips
  and suits are otherwise untouched.

This makes the Besançon a clean illustration of the catalogue's thesis that a pattern can
spread by **editing its imagery to fit a new audience** while leaving its game-mechanics
intact.

## The fear question

This is the most **literally "edited-out-of-fear"** deck in the library — but the fear was
**religious-political and commercial, not occult or inquisitorial.** The substitution was
almost certainly made **to avoid offending Protestant (and indeed strongly partisan
Catholic) sensibilities** in mixed-confessional regions, so as not to lose customers who did
not want Catholic clergy on their gaming cards [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon;
@web_aeclectic_1jj]. The driver was **the card trade in a post-Reformation market**, not the
Inquisition and not any suspicion of sorcery: the cards were re-imaged so they would *sell*,
not because anyone feared the cards themselves [@web_th_besancon]. (confidence: high on the
confessional motive as the standard explanation; it is universally described as a reasonable
**inference** — "assumed," "presumed," "surmised" — rather than documented from a maker's own
statement, and should be phrased that way [@web_besancon_wp].)

## Counter-voices

Because Juno and Jupiter are **pagan deities**, some esoteric readers treat the Besançon as
evidence of a "restored classical / Hermetic layer" beneath the Christian Marseille — a
glimpse of older, deeper symbolism [@web_th_besancon]. The document-first reading is far more
mundane: it is a **marketing-and-religion edit** for Protestant regions, made by commercial
cardmakers, **not** a recovery of antique doctrine. The deities were chosen precisely because
they were *uncontroversially secular-classical* substitutes of the right "rank," not because
the makers were transmitting a mystery tradition [@web_besancon_wp; @web_th_besancon].

## People

- The Besançon pattern is the work of **anonymous and trade cardmakers** rather than a single
  named author, so `people: []`. Documented makers in the family — to be promoted to
  `people/` dossiers only if the maintainer wants them — include **Jacob (Jacques) Jerger**
  (Besançon-area cartier) [@web_wopc_besancon_jerger] and the Swiss line **Johann Georg
  Rauch → Johannes Müller → A.G. Müller (Schaffhausen)** [@web_swiss1jj_wp].
- **Stuart R. Kaplan** is the figure who carried the Swiss 1JJ into the modern Anglophone
  market (Nuremberg 1968 → U.S. Games, 1970); he belongs in any later *people* pass on
  20th-century tarot publishing [@web_kaplan_usgames_1jj].

## Open questions / corrections owed

- **Pin the Swiss 1JJ chronology to a primary source.** The Rauch (c. 1831–38) → Müller →
  A.G. Müller (Schaffhausen) line, and the U.S. Games 1970 publication date, currently rest
  on encyclopedia/collector tier; a WebFetch-enabled or print check is owed
  [@web_swiss1jj_wp; @web_kaplan_usgames_1jj].
- **Date the earliest substitution precisely.** "Strasbourg, early 18th century" is the
  consensus, but the *earliest dated* Juno/Jupiter pack should be fixed from a museum
  catalogue [@web_besancon_wp; @web_bm_besancon].
- **Confirm the confessional motive is an inference, not a documented maker's statement** —
  phrase accordingly [@web_besancon_wp].
- **Distinguish pattern from edition** in the grammar: "Tarot de Besançon" (the broad
  Juno/Jupiter pattern) vs. the specific "Swiss 1JJ" 19th–20th-c. edition the images come
  from [@web_th_besancon; @web_swiss1jj_wp].
- Note that the **elemental suit labels** (Coins=Earth, etc.) carried in the grammar are the
  same occult overlay flagged for the Marseille, not native to a Besançon game pack
  [@decker2002].
