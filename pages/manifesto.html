Manifesto

The Recursive Tarot

A living grammar, held in the open.

Tarot has never stood still.

For about six centuries, people have inherited these images, played with them, argued over them, redrawn them, forgotten them, and found new questions to ask through them. The cards have been a card game, a luxury artwork, a philosophical mirror, a spiritual practice, a psychological tool, a collector's prize. No single use has ever exhausted them.

Six centuries is not a long time — and that is worth saying plainly, because so much of tarot's mythology is an attempt to deny it. When the cards were reframed in 1781 as a survival of ancient Egyptian wisdom, the claim was false, and the impulse behind it is the one we want to name: a worry that six hundred years is too thin a pedigree to carry meaning, so a deeper, older, secret origin had to be invented to lend authority. We don't accept the trade. Age is not meaning. A thing that is merely old is merely old. The cards do not need a borrowed antiquity to be worth our attention — they have something better: a real, documented history of people making sense with them, in the open.

So we begin with an observation. The history of tarot is not a history of fixed meanings handed down intact. It is a history of people continually renewing their relationship with the same set of symbols.

That renewal is not a corruption of the tradition. It is the tradition.

We see tarot as a living grammar — a finite set of symbols that can generate an unlimited number of conversations. Like any living language, it survives because people keep speaking it in new ways. Every deck is a dialect. Every spread is a sentence someone proposes. Every reading is a small act of interpretation. Each generation inherits the language, changes it, and hands it on.

And the cards are beautiful. They are an artifact of human imagination — like a painted cave wall, or a Van Gogh — and that is already enough to be worth keeping. We don't read them to be told the future. We read them to see ourselves and our questions more clearly. The card is a mirror, never a verdict. Relate to it; don't obey it.

Our task, then, is not to recover the one true tarot — there isn't one. It is to understand how this grammar has actually evolved, why it keeps inviting participation, and how to add to it honestly.

Honesty means something specific here. We keep public-domain decks not because they are sacred but because they are a shared inheritance, and we treat each tradition in its own terms: we name the artist and the date, we don't dress a fifteenth-century card game in occult meanings it never carried, and we keep the historical record separate from the practices built on top of it. Responsibility, for us, is mostly a refusal to flatten — letting a Marseille deck be a Marseille deck and a Golden Dawn deck be a Golden Dawn deck, without pretending either is the secret key to the other.

This is also why the work is open. Every deck here is public-domain art and public-domain JSON — readable, correctable, translatable, forkable. The living grammar is the idea; the open commons is the practice that makes the idea real. A tradition you cannot edit is not alive; it is embalmed.

This is an invitation.

Study the historical decks. Learn how they differ. Watch meanings emerge, shift, vanish, and return. Then make something of your own. Some people will build private decks for their families; others for classrooms, communities, research, games, therapy, poetry, or purposes no one has tried yet. Some will collaborate, some will publish, some will preserve a tradition that might otherwise be lost. Every one of these continues the same human practice: inheriting symbols, transforming them through experience, and offering them back.

The software exists to support that. Here you can create cards, build symbolic grammars, document their history, discuss interpretations, work with others, and — if you wish — publish your work as an open project that the next people can continue. The aim is not to generate images. It is to keep a living tradition alive.

Recursive Tarot is one grammar inside the larger ecosystem of recursive.eco. We think symbolic systems deserve the care we already give to open-source software, to scientific knowledge, to living ecosystems: they flourish when they are documented, questioned, maintained, shared, and renewed by communities — not when they are guarded by authorities. The future of tarot belongs to no single school, publisher, or philosophy. It belongs to everyone willing to engage with it honestly, creatively, and generously.
