# The Recursive Tarot

*A genealogy across the decks — from a Renaissance card game to an esoteric cosmos.*

Tarot did not begin as fortune-telling. It began in the courts of northern Italy in the **1440s** as a luxury **card game** — hand-painted *trionfi* for the ruling dynasties of Milan and Ferrara. The fortune-telling, the Egyptian myths, the Kabbalah — all of that was **added later**, beginning in 1781. This book follows the cards across five centuries, watching the images stay recognisable while their meaning is rewritten again and again.

But the story starts earlier, and further east.

## Before Tarot — the cards come west

Playing cards are far older than tarot, and they did not begin in Europe. The earliest came from **Tang and Song China** (by the 9th–10th century) — "money-suited" cards whose coin-based suits the Chinese **Mǎ Diào** (馬吊) money-card games still echo. From China, cards travelled west along the trade routes through the Islamic world. The **Mamluk** cards of Egypt and Syria — a near-complete pack survives in Istanbul's Topkapı palace — carried four suits: **cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks**. Those became the European **cups, coins, swords, and batons** still used in the Italian and Spanish (and Marseille) traditions. India's **Ganjifa** is a cousin from the same family tree.

When these cards reached Europe in the **1370s–80s**, they already were a complete four-suit game. Tarot's one innovation, half a century later in Italy, was to add the **trumps** — the 22 allegorical cards laid *on top of* the four suits. So the deck you know is a hybrid: its **suits are an Eastern inheritance**, and only its **trumps are a Renaissance Italian invention**. (This collection preserves three of those ancestors — the Chinese money cards, the Mamluk deck, and Ganjifa — at the root of the tree below.)

## The Lineage

Tarot is not one tree but a family of them — branching, hybridising, and reinventing itself while staying recognisably itself. From the Italian **Roots** it spread into regional families (the Southern, Eastern, and Western ways), was seized by Parisian occultists in the late 18th century, and was redrawn from the ground up by English initiates around 1900.
