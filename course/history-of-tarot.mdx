# The History of Tarot

> *"There is in fact no history prior to the fourteenth century. The deception and self-deception regarding their origin in Egypt, India or China put a lying spirit into the mouths of the first expositors… The true Tarot is symbolism; it speaks no other language and offers no other signs."*
>
> — **A. E. Waite**, *The Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (1910), Parts I–II — the occultist who designed the Rider-Waite-Smith deck, on the cards' invented past and their real substance

Tarot is everywhere right now. New decks are crowdfunded by the thousand; readings fill the social feeds; the cards thread through fashion, music, and therapy. A four-suit card game has become, in the 2020s, a genuine cultural force — the survey data and search trends are laid out in [Tarot Today](course-viewer.html?course=tarot-today); here we ask where it actually came from.

Not where its own mythology points. Much of what people are told about the cards — that they descend from ancient Egypt, that they hold a lost hermetic key — was invented late, in 1781. The documentary record is clear: tarot began as a *game*, a luxurious card game played in the courts of northern Italy in the 1440s. The divination, the Egyptian myths, the promise of secret wisdom were all added afterward, by people, layer upon layer — the container came long before the content. What runs unbroken from that first card table to a phone screen today is not a hidden ancient truth but a recursion: the same pictures, their meaning made and remade, generation after generation. That recursive history is the subject here.

That is what makes tarot worth our attention here at [recursive.eco](https://recursive.eco), which is really a place about one thing: how human beings make meaning *together*, and hand it on, and remake it. The cards are a near-perfect specimen of that process. Because the divination was *added* rather than revealed, it never carried any binding authority — there is no pope of tarot, no scripture, no one who can tell you that you have read a card wrongly. That openness is the whole point. Anyone who picks up the cards becomes, in a small way, an author of meaning.

It is also why the cards can serve us now. There is an arch in how the cards have been held — **game → crystal ball → gate.** Meaning, left alone, tends to harden — into fate, into fear, into the **crystal ball** that claims to tell you what *will* happen. But a game is a safe container, and inside it a card stops being an oracle and becomes a **gate, not a fate**: it asks what is already alive in you rather than dictating what is to come. This is the same move a narrative therapist calls *externalising* — the problem is the problem, the person is not the problem — and the same instinct that puts a crayon in a hand in art therapy. It is why the Tower can be read as a lesson in radical acceptance instead of a prophecy of ruin. Knowing that a game sits underneath it all is precisely what loosens meaning's grip, and lets us put these old pictures to more adaptive use.

There is another way the cards become a gate. Held forward instead of back, a spread is a way to *imagine* a possible future — and a future we can picture, we are likelier to make. That is the self-fulfilling edge of reading: a generous view, practised, tends to come true, the way a hopeful account of a relationship can help bring the relationship about. So the responsibility is ours — to be careful which prophecies we choose to make real. How humans have used lots, oracles, and signs to choose well is its own long story, told in [the divination traditions](course-viewer.html?course=divination-traditions).

So we begin where the cards themselves began — with the journey west — and only then turn to the question every reader brings to them: whether, at bottom, any of it is magic.

## Before Tarot — the cards come west

Long before the first tarot was painted, the cards themselves had already travelled half the world. Most scholars trace them to **Tang or Song China** — perhaps by the ninth or tenth century — as "money-suited" cards, an echo of which may survive in the Chinese [Mǎ Diào](#deck-madiao-money-cards) (馬吊) games; the earliest stages are thinly documented and still debated. From there they moved westward along the trade routes of the Islamic world. The [Mamluk cards](#deck-mamluk-deck) of Egypt and Syria — an incomplete pack of which survives in Istanbul's Topkapı palace — carried four suits: cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks.

Watch those four suits, because they are about to become something familiar. Cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks become the European **cups, coins, swords, and batons** — the very suits still shuffled today. India's [Ganjifa](#deck-ganjifa) is a cousin from the same broad family.

When these cards finally reached Europe, in the 1370s, they arrived not as an idea but as a finished thing: a complete four-suit game that had already crossed deserts and seas and passed between civilisations. The Italians, half a century later, added just one inspired novelty — a second, higher set of cards laid *over* the four suits, like a new layer of life settling over an older one. Those are the **trumps**. Everything else, the suits you hold in your hand, is the older, Eastern inheritance.

## The First Deck — and What Came Before

The trump idea had a prelude. After the Black Plague, the Roman demagogue **Cola di Rienzo** revived antiquity's **triumphal processions** as civic spectacle, and his friend **Petrarch** turned them inward in *I Trionfi* — a sequence of allegorical figures each conquering the last: Love overcome by Chastity, then Death, then Fame, then Time, then Eternity. That *ordered procession of pictures, each triumphing over the one before,* is the mould the trumps were cast in.

The very first deck of this kind is **lost — but its text survives**. Around 1418–1425, **Marziano da Tortona**, secretary to Duke Filippo Maria Visconti of Milan, wrote out a game of **sixteen Greek gods** played over **four "bird" suits** (eagles, phoenixes, turtledoves, doves), its cards painted by Michelino da Besozzo. The cards vanished; the treatise endured only because **Jacopo Antonio Marcello** mailed it to Isabella of Lorraine in **1449** — by then already out of date, since Milan had moved on to the newer 22-trump decks. It was not yet a tarot (sixteen gods, not twenty-two trumps), but it is the documented first move. (Full sources, and the honest "reconstruction, not artifact" caveat: the [research dossier](https://github.com/PlayfulProcess/recursive-tarot/blob/dev/research/decks/marziano-da-tortona.md).)

## A Game, First of All

We have followed the cards west; now watch what people actually *did* with them. The answer, for three centuries, is that they **played**. The game tarot belongs to is the **trick-taking game** — the same broad family as Whist, Bridge and Hearts — in which each player lays a card to the table and the strongest one sweeps the pile. Cards had reached Europe as gambling equipment, and trick-games were the thing they were overwhelmingly used for.

The Italian novelty of the 1440s was a stroke of pure game design: a fifth, permanent suit of pictures that **outranked all four ordinary suits at once**. They called these cards *trionfi* — triumphs; we call them **trumps**, the word simply *triumph* worn smooth — and that single idea proved so useful it spread out of this game into countless later ones. So tarot did not merely *descend* from card games; it handed them one of their most enduring mechanics. Every time you "trump" a trick in any modern game, you are echoing the Italian *trionfi*.

The game itself travelled and split into regional dialects, each on its own pack: the swift **Tarocchino** of Bologna on a shortened 62-card deck, the vast **Minchiate** of Florence on ninety-seven, the French Tarot still dealt in cafés today. The rules drift; the core holds — follow the led suit if you can, and when you cannot, a trump triumphs. And you can still play it: we have built two of these to try in your browser from the public-domain decks in this library — the authentic four-handed **[Tarocchino di Bologna](games/tarocchino.html)**, and a gentler one-on-one introduction, **[Trionfi](games/trionfi.html)** (the whole shelf is on the **[Play](play.html)** page). The family even reaches back past tarot itself: the **Mamluk** game that first carried the four suits into Europe is *lost* — no rules for it survive — but its Chinese ancestor, the money-suited **[Ma Diao](games/madiao.html)**, was written down in Ming-dynasty manuals, so the oldest card game anyone can still deal is playable here too. Knowing the cards as a game first is the surest way to feel how much meaning was *added* afterward. (For how the games were actually scored and played, see John McLeod's [rules at pagat.com](https://www.pagat.com/tarot/ottocento.html) and the deck's [research dossier](https://github.com/PlayfulProcess/recursive-tarot/blob/dev/research/decks/tarocchino-bologna.md).)

## The Lineage

No tradition this old ever travels in a straight line. Observe tarot across the centuries and you see something that behaves less like an invention and more like a living lineage — spreading, dividing into families, hybridising with its neighbours, and yet, astonishingly, remaining recognisably itself the whole way through. From its roots in Italy — the gold-ground [Visconti-Sforza](#deck-visconti-sforza-tarot) cards painted for a Milanese duke — it branched south into Florence and Bologna, east into Ferrara, west toward the workshops of Marseille whose [Conver deck](#deck-tarot-de-marseille-conver) fixed the standard pattern, and at last into the hands of the occultists — from [Court de Gébelin](#deck-court-de-gebelin-tarot) to the [Golden Dawn](#deck-golden-dawn-book-t-tarot) — who would redraw it entirely.

## The three Ways — how the family split

After the Milanese **Roots**, the game travelled, and as it travelled the *order of the trumps* drifted from city to city. Sorting the surviving evidence, the historian **Michael Dummett** found the twenty-two trumps had been numbered in three distinct regional patterns — he labelled them, without ceremony, **A, B, and C**. Those three orders are the three "Ways" branching across the [Tree of Tarot](../viewers/genealogy-tree.html):

- **The Southern Way · Order A · Bologna & Florence** — home of the swift [Tarocchino](#deck-tarocchino-bologna) and the vast ninety-seven-card [Minchiate](#deck-minchiate-florence-tarot).
- **The Eastern Way · Order B · Ferrara** — the court of the d'Este; the so-called ['Charles VI'](#deck-charles-vi-tarot) cards.
- **The Western Way · Order C · Milan, carried west** — into Savoy and France, where the [Tarot de Marseille](#deck-tarot-de-marseille-conver) (the Conver pattern, the [Besançon](#deck-tarot-de-besancon) variant) fixed the order almost every modern deck still follows.

Here is why that small fact about *numbering* matters so much. The occultists who came later treated the sequence of the trumps as a sacred, fixed key — a hidden alphabet whose order *meant* something eternal. But the order was never fixed. Three cities kept three different sequences, disagreeing mostly about where to slot the three Virtues. A thing that varies by town is a **game design, not a revealed cosmology.** The regional drift of the trump order is the single cleanest piece of evidence that tarot was a game that *acquired* meaning — never a book of secrets that always had it.

Two branches grow off to the side of the main trunk:

- **The Occult Reframing** (France → England, from 1781): [Court de Gébelin](#deck-court-de-gebelin-tarot)'s invented Egypt, [Etteilla](#deck-etteilla-i-livre-de-thot)'s fortune decks, then Lévi, Papus, the [Golden Dawn](#deck-golden-dawn-book-t-tarot) and Waite — where the cards were redrawn as a hidden book of the soul. That tradition keeps its own page here, held as *practice*, not history: [Walking the Golden Dawn Path](../pages/course-viewer.html?course=walking-the-golden-dawn-path).
- **Sui Generis & Relatives** — the strange ones not quite in the line: the [Sola Busca](#deck-sola-busca-tarot), the [Mantegna 'Tarocchi'](#deck-mantegna-tarocchi) (not a tarot at all, but an engraved encyclopaedia of the cosmos), and the modern descendants.

And behind all of them, the **Ancestors** — the money-suited [Ma Diao](#deck-madiao-money-cards), the [Mamluk](#deck-mamluk-deck) cards, the Indian [Ganjifa](#deck-ganjifa) — the pre-tarot packs the four suits first rode in on.

## Why a tree

We catalogue all this as a **tree** — a genealogy of descent — because that is how the cards actually behave: a single Milanese root, branches into regional families, hybrids where two branches touch, and an unbroken recognisability the whole way down. A tree shows what an alphabetical list cannot — that the Marseille and the Minchiate are *cousins*, that the occult decks are a graft onto an old trunk, that no deck is the "original" and none the "true" one.

One thing to keep straight, because the words collide. Our **Tree of Tarot** is a *family tree* — who descends from whom, an account of history. The esoteric **Tree of Life** that the Golden Dawn later mapped onto the cards is a different tree entirely — a diagram of the soul, not of the past. The first is evidence; the second is practice. We keep them apart — and you can [walk the second one](../pages/course-viewer.html?course=walking-the-golden-dawn-path) if you like.

## Seeing it from every angle

The tree is only one cut through the cards; there are many, and they are worth playing with. The [Emergence Explorer](../viewers/explorer.html) lets you pivot the whole library by any dimension and watch the patterns surface — *every grouping is an emergence.* Group by **century** and the game's spread across Europe appears; by **order** (A / B / C) and the three Ways separate themselves cleanly; by **function** — *game · divination · esoteric · origin-myth* — and you watch a plaything be slowly made into an oracle; by **suit**, **arcana**, **number**, or **lineage** and still other families come into view. None of these is the "real" structure underneath the others. Each is one true shape among many — which is, in the end, this whole book's argument about the cards themselves.

Each of these decks is a single specimen: a moment in the lineage, preserved. Some are luxurious one-offs, painted in gold for a single Milanese duke; others were cut in wood and printed by the thousand. Oldest first — and every one has its story, and the hands that made it.

## A Few Plates

Before the analysis, a moment simply to look — and one card to watch in particular. Here is **Death** across four and a half centuries: the skeleton holds, from the 1450s to 1909, even as the scene around it is rebuilt every time — a standing archer, then a scythe-mower among severed limbs, then an armoured rider on a pale horse. The picture is a fossil; the meaning is poured in fresh each century. It is much of this book's argument in a single card.

## The Divination Question

The history is on the table; now the question every reader brings to these cards. Was the tarot, in its origins, a thing of magic? The honest answer, the one the evidence gives, is *no*. For its first three centuries the tarot was a game — the point Michael Dummett spent a career establishing — and nothing in the surviving record suggests otherwise. The point of this book is not that the cards were always magical. It is that an ordinary card game was, over time, *made* magical — that meaning was poured into these little pictures until people could read a whole cosmos in them. Here is the case, laid out plainly.

## The Four Suits

Here is the older, quieter half of the deck — and the part that travelled furthest to reach us. For three centuries the numbered cards of each suit were purely abstract: four coins, five swords, arranged like wallpaper, meant to be *counted* in a game and nothing more. And then, slowly, the most extraordinary thing happened to them. Watch a single number — the Five, the Ten — transform from a pattern into a picture, from a quantity into a little scene with a meaning. It is a small, quietly remarkable shift, and an oddly moving one to follow.

## The Numbers

One more pattern hides in the suits — in the numbers themselves. For three centuries a Four was simply four coins, a quantity to be counted. Then the occultists gave every number a fixed meaning that holds across all four suits at once, by laying the ten pips over the ten *Sephiroth* of the Kabbalistic Tree of Life. Watch the Five turn sour in every suit and the Six come good in every suit: it is the number speaking now, not the suit. Read straight from the decks' own words, number by number.

## The Trumps

And now the trumps themselves — the twenty-two great cards the Italians laid over the suits. Follow any one of them across the decks and you will see the central wonder of this whole story in miniature. The picture holds — the skeleton, the falling tower, the dancing figure — almost unchanged for five hundred years. But the *meaning* poured into that picture is replaced over and over: read first as a piece in a game, then as a moral allegory, then as a key to the cosmos itself.

## Tarot today — the recursion continues

The remaking did not stop in 1909, or 1781, or the 1440s. The cards are in the middle of another turn of the same wheel right now — crowdfunded by the thousand, carried by queer, feminist, and diasporic communities as a tool of self-authorship, performed on the social feeds, sold as a wellness object and a party game at once. The boom is not a rise in literal belief, which has barely moved; it is a rise in *practice, visibility, and identity use* — people picking up the same old pictures and making them say something new, for communities the old decks never imagined. The latest dialect of the same living language.

We opened on the present; we close there too. Why tarot returned, who it is for, and what that tells us about how meaning gets made is its own open question — followed, with the sourced evidence laid out, in **[Tarot Today — a living question](course-viewer.html?course=tarot-today)**.
