# The Light of Tarot

> *Read to know yourself, not to be told your fate. A card starts a conversation with what is already alive in you. Relate to the card; never obey it.*

Tolkien argued that fairy-stories are not an escape from reality but one of the ways we return to it. Human beings, he suggested, are *sub-creators*: creatures who inherit a world they did not make and yet are invited to take part in its ongoing creation. We paint, write stories, invent games, compose music, cultivate gardens, build languages, and imagine worlds — not because reality is lacking, but because creativity is one of the ways we belong to it.

## Recovery — seeing the ordinary again

He called one of the gifts of fantasy *Recovery*: learning to see the ordinary again as if for the first time. The sun has illuminated every day of our lives, and feeds the whole biosphere with its energy, and yet we often stop seeing it — perhaps that is why some once worshipped it as Apollo, as a gesture of looking again. A tree becomes "just a tree." Another person becomes familiar enough to disappear into habit. Recovery is the quiet miracle of attention restored.

Modern psychology hints at something similar. Studying couples over a year, Sandra Murray, John Holmes, and Dale Griffin found that the partners who idealized one another — who kept noticing what was alive and good in the other — were not the deluded ones but the ones whose love lasted, and whose generous view tended to come true: [love, they found, is not blind but prescient](https://faculty.washington.edu/jdb/345/345%20Articles/Chapter%2011%20Murray%20et%20al.%20%281996%29.pdf). This is not a denial of reality. It is a disciplined way of perceiving more of it.

## Tarot as a practice of recovery

Tarot, at its best, can become one small practice of recovery. And that practice does not depend on the cards having been invented by some ancient civilisation more versed in magic than we are. It works not because the cards possess hidden powers, but because they interrupt our habits of seeing. A shuffled image asks us to pause. To notice. To imagine another possibility. To look again at ourselves and the world with fresh attention.

It is our nature as sub-creators that lets us imagine a game as a gate onto a world of meaning. Sometimes the cards are for their own playfulness — like a painting, a poem, a myth, or a fairy-story, they are simply artifacts of human imagination. Sometimes they are an exercise in envisioning the different futures we might help co-create — not as a fate, but as a gate. Either way, they remind us that meaning is not merely discovered; it is cultivated through participation.

## The invitation

Perhaps that is why people have kept returning to tarot. Not because tarot has decoded the secrets of the universe, but because that mystery is never quite decoded, and the cards can become a way to dance with it. Every generation inherits these symbols, sees something new in them, and leaves them a little changed for the next.

The invitation of Recursive Tarot is not to uncover a final meaning hidden inside the cards. It is to join the long human practice of tending a symbolic language — one image, one conversation, one act of imagination at a time.
