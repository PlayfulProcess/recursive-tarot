# Games of the Tarot — A Player's Companion

*The companion booklet for the game decks in the Recursive Tarot library:
Minchiate, Tarot de Marseille, Tarocchino di Bologna — and the Mantegna series,
which is something else entirely.*

## The first thing to know

For roughly three and a half centuries, the cards in your hands were **game
equipment**. From the 1440s in northern Italy until the occult reframing of
1781, a tarot pack meant an evening of trick-taking — closer in spirit to bridge
or sueca than to anything mystical. The "meanings" of the trumps, in this
period, were the meanings of chess pieces: rank, capture, strategy.

This booklet describes the games in their own terms. If you want divination,
the library's Etteilla and Golden Dawn decks come from genuine divinatory
traditions — those have their own companion.

## The shape of every tarot game

All historical tarot games share a skeleton (details varied by city and century):

1. **Four suits plus trumps.** The pack is an ordinary Italian-suited deck
   (Cups, Coins, Swords, Batons) with a fifth series added — the *trionfi*, or
   trumps — and a wild card, the Fool (*il Matto*).
2. **Trick-taking.** A player leads a card; others must follow suit if they
   can. A trump beats any suit card. The highest trump (or the highest card of
   the suit led) wins the trick.
3. **The Fool excuses.** In most rules the Matto wins nothing but can be played
   instead of following suit — you keep it, and it scores for you. This is
   arguably the ancestor of the joker.
4. **Counting cards.** Kings, queens, the highest and lowest trumps, and the
   Fool carry point values; most cards count nothing. The hand is won by
   *points captured in tricks*, not by number of tricks.

If you know any modern trick-taking game, you can be playing a recognizable
tarot game in ten minutes.

## Minchiate — Florence's maximalist variant (97 cards)

Florence answered the 78-card tarot by *adding more trumps*: the twelve zodiac
signs, the four elements, and extra virtues, for 40 trumps and 97 cards in all.
Played typically by four in fixed partnerships, Minchiate rewards memory above
all — combinations of trumps (*verzicole*) score bonuses, and players keep a
running tally of what has fallen. It remained a Tuscan passion into the 19th
century before fading; the deck in this library (Florence, 1860–1890) is from
its last flourishing.

## Tarocchino di Bologna — the compact cousin (62 cards)

Bologna shortened the pack (dropping pips 2–5 in each suit) and kept playing —
the Bolognese game survives unbroken to this day, among the oldest continuously
played card games in Europe. Its four equal "Moors" (replacing Popess, Empress,
Emperor, Pope after a 1725 controversy) are a famous local quirk. The game is
fast, partnership-based, and full of signalling conventions.

## Tarot de Marseille — the printed standard

The Marseille pattern was the workhorse: printed by the thousands in Lyon,
Marseille and beyond, exported across Europe, and used for the French game of
*tarot* — which is still played competitively in France today (the Fédération
Française de Tarot organizes tournaments). If you want one historical deck you
can actually take to a living game community, this is it.

## Mantegna — the odd one out (50 cards, not a game)

The so-called "Mantegna Tarocchi" (Ferrara, c. 1465) is neither by Mantegna nor
a tarot: it is a series of fifty instructional engravings — the ranks of man,
the muses, the liberal arts, the virtues, the celestial spheres — arranged in
five ordered groups. Renaissance educators apparently used such series as
visual curricula. We include it because early writers kept confusing it with
tarot, and because it is beautiful. Don't try to play tricks with it; read it
as a map of a Renaissance education.

## Where the cards came from

Playing cards reached Italy through the Islamic Mediterranean in the late
1300s — the Mamluk pack (in this library) carries the four suits that Italy
adapted. The trail continues east: suited money cards were in use in China
centuries earlier. The library's ancestors line (Mamluk, Ganjifa, and the early
European sheets) lets you walk that road card by card.
