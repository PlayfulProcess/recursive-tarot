# How the Cards Can Work

Most people encounter tarot with one question already in mind:

**Does it actually work?**

The answer depends almost entirely on what we mean by *work*.

If we expect the cards to reveal hidden facts about the future, then the evidence is not encouraging. People have searched for reliable evidence that tarot predicts events or reveals information unavailable to the reader, and so far they have not found it. That matters, and we'll come back to it.

But suppose we ask a different question.

Can a deck of symbolic images help us notice assumptions we didn't realize we were making? Can it help us articulate feelings we couldn't quite name, imagine possibilities we hadn't considered, or approach an old problem from a genuinely new perspective?

I think the answer to that question is yes.

Not because the cards possess supernatural powers.

Because human beings possess remarkable symbolic ones.

We are creatures who make meaning from stories, symbols, images, language, chance, and conversation. Long before there were tarot cards, there were myths, poems, rituals, paintings, parables, dreams, and games. Every culture has discovered symbolic ways of thinking that allow us to see ourselves—and the world—with fresh eyes.

This is the idea underneath everything on [recursive.eco](https://recursive.eco).

**We make beliefs in order to make meaning.**

That sentence deserves a careful explanation.

I do **not** mean that beliefs are arbitrary, or that truth doesn't matter. Reality continually pushes back against our interpretations. Some maps describe the terrain better than others. Some stories help us flourish, while others trap us.

But every worldview begins as a way of organizing experience.

A scientist proposes a theory. A child decides what kind of person she wants to become. A family develops traditions. A nation tells stories about its history. Each is attempting to orient itself within a world that is always richer than any single description.

The philosopher Alfred Korzybski famously reminded us that *the map is not the territory.* Every map leaves something out. Yet maps remain indispensable precisely because they simplify reality enough that we can move through it.

Our beliefs work much the same way.

We inherit some from our families and cultures. We construct others ourselves. We revise them whenever life surprises us. Meaning is not something we simply uncover, like buried treasure. It is something we continually cultivate together.

Tarot belongs inside that process.

A reading is not a verdict about the future. It is another symbolic map—one that helps us notice the landscape we are already walking through and imagine where we might go next.

## Why Fiction Changes Real Lives

If there is one writer who shaped the philosophy of this project more than any other, it is J.R.R. Tolkien.

Not because Tolkien believed in tarot—he almost certainly did not—but because he understood something profound about imagination.

In his essay *On Fairy-stories*, Tolkien argues that fantasy is not valuable because it allows us to escape reality. Rather, it allows us to **recover** reality.

Recovery is Tolkien's word for seeing something as though for the first time.

A tree stops being merely "a tree." A friend stops being merely "someone you've always known." The ordinary becomes strange enough to deserve your attention again.

Modern psychology describes something similar.

People who win the lottery eventually adapt to their good fortune. People who suffer devastating injuries often adapt as well. Within surprisingly short periods, both groups tend to drift back toward roughly their previous levels of happiness. Human beings become accustomed to almost everything—even wonder and tragedy.

We stop noticing.

Recovery interrupts that habit.

Sandra Murray, John Holmes, and Dale Griffin found something related while studying long-term romantic relationships. Couples who continued seeing one another generously—not by ignoring flaws, but by continuing to notice what was alive, admirable, and beautiful—tended to build happier and more enduring relationships. Sometimes seeing generously is not self-deception. Sometimes it helps create the very reality it perceives.

A tarot card can perform a similar interruption.

It quietly asks:

> *What if there is another way to see this?*

The card does not reveal hidden information.

It reveals hidden possibilities of attention.

Tolkien also defended what he called **Escape**, though perhaps not in the way people imagine. Critics often accuse fantasy of encouraging people to flee reality. Tolkien replied that there is an important difference between the escape of a deserter and the escape of a prisoner. Sometimes stepping outside a familiar frame is precisely what allows us to return with greater clarity.

A reading can do the same.

For a few moments, it lets us leave the room where we've been rehearsing the same arguments, look through another window, and then return to the same decision with fresh eyes.

That decision remains ours.

The cards never remove our responsibility.

## The One Thing the Cards Cannot Honestly Do

At this point, intellectual honesty matters.

There is currently no reliable evidence that tarot predicts future events or reveals hidden knowledge unavailable to the reader.

Controlled studies have repeatedly struggled to demonstrate that tarot readings contain information uniquely matched to the people receiving them. Likewise, broader claims about precognition have consistently failed to replicate under careful experimental conditions.

That does not prove prediction is impossible.

Science rarely proves universal impossibility.

It simply means that no reliable predictive effect has yet been demonstrated.

Psychology offers another explanation for why readings can nevertheless feel astonishingly accurate.

The **Barnum (or Forer) effect** shows that people readily recognize themselves in descriptions that are actually broad enough to apply to many people. **Confirmation bias** encourages us to remember the impressive "hits" while quietly forgetting the misses. **Subjective validation** allows us to fill symbolic images with our own lives until they feel uncannily personal. **Apophenia** describes our remarkable tendency to perceive meaningful patterns, especially when we feel uncertain or out of control.

These are not flaws in human nature.

They are consequences of being extraordinarily good at making meaning.

Understanding them does not diminish tarot.

It changes how we hold it.

The cards are poor crystal balls.

They are excellent mirrors.

## Why the Cards Can Still Help

If tarot does not carry knowledge *to* us, what does it actually do?

Perhaps a better question is:

**What does any meaningful work of art do?**

A painting does not tell us what to think.

A novel does not dictate its interpretation.

A poem does not solve our problems.

Instead, each becomes a surface upon which our own lives encounter another imagination.

Tarot works similarly.

Every card is deliberately ambiguous. It contains characters, symbols, relationships, tensions, and possibilities. As we engage with them, we inevitably project something of ourselves into the image.

That projection is not a bug.

It is the mechanism.

Therapists working with narrative and expressive arts have long understood that symbolic images often help people articulate experiences that remain inaccessible through direct analysis alone. Writing about painful experiences, for example, has repeatedly been shown to improve psychological and even physical well-being. Stories help organize lives.

Tarot offers stories. Not fixed stories. Possible ones. And randomness contributes something important here as well.

Our minds naturally return to familiar explanations. Random prompts interrupt those habits. Brian Eno's *Oblique Strategies* cards use this principle for creative work. Economist Steven Levitt's famous coin-flip experiment demonstrated that even a random prompt can help people move beyond indecision by revealing how they actually feel about change.

The random card is not wiser than you. It simply asks a question you might never have thought to ask yourself. Or make you wonder about a different answer.

## Gates and Oracles

This distinction changes everything.

There are two ways to approach symbolic systems.

One treats them as **oracles**.

The other treats them as **gates**.

An oracle claims authority over your future.

A gate invites you into deeper reflection.

I believe tarot belongs on the second side.

This is why Tolkien's image of the **palantír** has always struck me as the perfect metaphor.

The seeing-stones in *The Lord of the Rings* never actually lie.

They simply tempt their users into mistaking partial truth for complete understanding.

Denethor sees genuine images and despairs because he mistakes what he sees for the whole story.

Aragorn sees equally real images but refuses to surrender his judgment.

The difference is not the stone.

It is the stance.

The same is true of tarot.

Approached as an oracle, the cards become dangerous because they invite us to surrender responsibility.

Approached as a gate, they invite us to exercise responsibility more consciously.

The cards remain exactly the same.

Only our posture changes.

## The Light Is in the Eyes of the Beholder

Tolkien returned to these themes in his poem *Mythopoeia*, written after his famous conversation with C.S. Lewis about myth.

One line has always stayed with me.

He describes language itself as "divination, judgement, and a laugh."

Whether or not Tolkien intended anything resembling tarot, I don't think he was defending fortune-telling. He was pointing toward something older and deeper.

Human beings do not merely record the world.

We participate in naming it.

Every meaningful name is already an interpretation.

Every metaphor opens one way of seeing while closing another.

Every story highlights some truths while leaving others in shadow.

Tarot joins that ancient human conversation.

It offers seventy-eight images that become meaningful only when someone sits with them honestly.

The meaning is not hidden inside the cardstock.

It emerges in the meeting between image and imagination.

That is why no one owns these cards.

Like every symbolic language, they remain alive only because people continue using them, questioning them, revising them, and sharing them with one another.

The cards do not contain meaning.

They invite it.

# Further Reading

### J.R.R. Tolkien — *On Fairy-stories*

The philosophical foundation of this chapter. Tolkien argues that imagination is not primarily an escape from reality but one of the ways we recover reality itself.

### J.R.R. Tolkien — *Mythopoeia*

A poetic defense of myth, language, and human creativity. Read alongside *On Fairy-stories* to understand Tolkien's view of humanity as a "sub-creator."

### Alfred Korzybski — *Science and Sanity*

Introduces the famous insight that "the map is not the territory," a helpful way of thinking about every symbolic system.

### George Kelly — *The Psychology of Personal Constructs*

Argues that people continually build and revise personal theories about the world, anticipating many modern ideas about meaning-making.

### William James — *The Varieties of Religious Experience*

Still one of the finest explorations of belief, experience, and the psychological significance of religion and symbolism.

### Brickman, Coates & Janoff-Bulman (1978)

The classic study on hedonic adaptation, showing how people gradually adjust to both extraordinary fortune and extraordinary hardship.

### Murray, Holmes & Griffin (1996)

Shows that generous, hopeful ways of seeing a romantic partner can contribute to healthier and more enduring relationships.

### Pennebaker & Beall (1986)

The foundational expressive-writing study demonstrating that putting difficult experiences into words can promote health and well-being.

### Whitson & Galinsky (2008)

Explores how uncertainty and loss of control increase pattern perception, an important reminder whenever we engage with divinatory practices.

### Brian Eno & Peter Schmidt — *Oblique Strategies*

A deck of creative prompts illustrating how structured randomness can help break habitual patterns of thought.

### Omar Khayyam Moore (1957)

A fascinating anthropological account of divination as a practical way of disrupting predictable decision-making rather than predicting the future.

### Susan Blackmore (1983)

One of the clearest controlled tests of tarot reading. Worth reading because intellectual honesty requires taking skeptical evidence seriously.

### Ivtzan & French (2004)

A thoughtful follow-up study examining whether people can distinguish genuine tarot readings from randomly generated ones.

> The purpose of this bibliography is not simply to document sources, but to invite further exploration. Read widely, disagree thoughtfully, and help improve this project. The conversation is always unfinished.
