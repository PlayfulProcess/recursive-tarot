# Tarot Today — a living question

This is the most **unfinished** course in the library, and deliberately so. It is not an answer; it is a question we are holding open, with the evidence laid out so far and an invitation to help us think. **Contributions are warmly welcome** — there is a Claude prompt and a contribute link at the end.

The question is simple to ask and hard to answer: **why is tarot everywhere right now?**

## The phenomenon

A card game invented in 1440s Italy is, in the 2020s, a genuine cultural force. New decks are crowdfunded by the thousand. Reading apps sit in the app-store charts. "Tarot" trends on social platforms; bookstores carry shelves of it; it threads through fashion, music, and design.

It lives, conspicuously, in **two places at once**. It is a folk practice carried by **minority and marginal communities** — queer, feminist, diasporic, neo-pagan — for whom a deck is a tool of self-authorship outside inherited institutions. And it is **thoroughly mainstream** — a wellness-aisle commodity, a brand aesthetic, a party game. Any honest account has to explain *both* at once.

It is tempting to say *tarot has overtaken astrology* — and in some recent windows, and on some platforms, it has. But read honestly: across the **full** 2004–present average, astrology still edges tarot, while **poker** (a game) dwarfs both. What the curve actually shows is a **steep, sustained climb** for tarot from a low base — the shape of something becoming a phenomenon, not something that always was one. *Whether, where, and for whom tarot has truly overtaken astrology is exactly the kind of question this course holds open.* (See the prompts below to dig into the live data yourself.)

## The hypothesis: a license to create

Here is the thesis we are testing — offered as a hypothesis, not a verdict.

Tarot spreads because of **how its meaning was made**. The images came first, as a game; the *meaning* was poured in **afterwards**, by many hands, over centuries — Court de Gébelin's Egypt, Étteilla's cartomancy, the Golden Dawn's correspondences, Jung's archetypes, a thousand modern readers. (That whole arc is the spine of [the history course](course-viewer.html?course=history-of-tarot).) Because no single authority ever owned the meaning, the system carries an implicit **license to create**: the deck is a grammar you are *invited* to speak, not a scripture you must obey. Anyone can make a deck. Anyone can mean with it. That openness — a Western, almost open-source commons of symbols — is, we suspect, a large part of why it travels so far and lands so hard with exactly the communities who most need to author meaning on their own terms.

But "anyone can create in it" must not slide into "the creators don't matter." They matter enormously, and many were **shadowed**. The deck most people picture is the Rider–**Waite**–Smith — and the third name, **Pamela Colman Smith** ("Pixie"), the artist who actually drew all 78 cards in 1909, was for most of a century under-credited and underpaid for the very images that made the system legible to millions. A license to create is not a license to forget who created. Part of holding this question open is **returning the names** — honouring the artists even as we celebrate the openness.

## What we'd investigate next

Open threads, none settled:

- **The numbers.** Is the "tarot > astrology" claim true, and when/where? What do platform-specific data (TikTok, YouTube, app stores), not just web search, show?
- **The industry.** How big is the deck-and-app market, who profits, and how does a commons-of-symbols coexist with a commercial boom?
- **The communities.** Why does self-authorship-outside-institutions land so hard with queer, feminist, and diasporic readers? What's the through-line to other folk meaning-making revivals?
- **The artists.** Beyond Pixie Smith — who else got shadowed, and how would a fairer credit economy for deck artists look?
- **The mechanism.** Does the "meaning-attributed-later → license to create" story actually predict *which* symbolic systems spread and which don't? (Test it against the I Ching, runes, astrology itself.)
