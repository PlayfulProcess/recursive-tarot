# Tarot Today — a living question

This is the most **unfinished** course in the library, and deliberately so. It is not a verdict; it is a question held open, with a first pass of sourced evidence laid out and an invitation to help us think. Where a claim is solid I say so; where the evidence is thin I flag it. **Contributions are warmly welcome** — there is a paste-ready Claude prompt and a contribute link at the end.

The question is simple to ask and hard to answer: **why is tarot everywhere right now?**

## The phenomenon

A card game invented in 1440s Italy is, in the 2020s, a genuine cultural force. New decks are crowdfunded by the thousand. Reading apps sit in the app-store charts. "Tarot" trends on social platforms; bookstores carry shelves of it; it threads through fashion, music, and design.

It lives, conspicuously, in **two places at once**. It is a folk practice carried by **minority and marginal communities** — queer, feminist, diasporic, neo-pagan — for whom a deck is a tool of self-authorship outside inherited institutions. And it is **thoroughly mainstream** — a wellness-aisle commodity, a brand aesthetic, a party game. Any honest account has to explain *both* at once.

## Has tarot overtaken astrology?

Short answer: **no — but it has converged, and now trades places week to week.** "Overtaken" overstates the evidence. Three independent measures agree.

A caution that matters more than it looks: **Google Trends rescales every comparison to 0–100 against the single highest point in that exact basket of terms.** In this five-term chart, the early peak is poker's 2004–05 boom, which compresses everyone else (tarot averages 9, astrology 13). Change the basket and the numbers change — so you can never compare the absolute figures across two different Trends images; only the *shape within one chart* is valid. Read the shape and a clear story emerges: astrology leads cleanly from 2004 to about 2016, the lines **converge around 2016–2018**, and from roughly 2019 they intertwine — both rising into 2024–25, tarot periodically pulling level.

**Actual practice — the strongest source.** [Pew's fall-2024 survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/05/21/3-in-10-americans-consult-astrology-tarot-cards-or-fortune-tellers/) of 9,593 U.S. adults found **28% consult astrology or a horoscope at least yearly, versus about 11% for tarot** — astrology roughly 2.5× larger by participation. And here is the corrective that reframes the whole "surge": **belief has barely moved** — 27% believed in astrology in 2024 versus 29% in 2017 (Gallup found 23–28% back to 1990). So the boom is **not a rise in literal belief**; it is a rise in *content, practice, visibility, and identity use*.

**App infrastructure.** Astrology has a category-defining standalone product; tarot does not. Co-Star reports 30M+ registered users since 2017, with a whole tier behind it (The Pattern, Sanctuary, CHANI, AstroTalk). The leading *dedicated* tarot app, Labyrinthos, serves ~2.25M and is a learning/journaling tool — an order of magnitude smaller, and most astrology apps treat tarot as a bolt-on.

**The honest verdict:** tarot has *caught up to* astrology in search interest and now overlaps it, but astrology remains larger by practice and by app infrastructure. Where tarot arguably **leads** is as a **physical-product and creation phenomenon** rather than a check-the-app habit — which is exactly the thread this course pulls on.

## Why it surged since ~2015

Ranked by how well the evidence supports each:

- **Short-form video** (high, as association). Tarot is visual and performable — ideal for the medium. One trade source cites ~8 million #tarot videos posted on TikTok in 2024 alone; reporting consistently ties the resurgence to WitchTok and YouTube. (Causation is hard to isolate from COVID timing.)
- **COVID-19 as accelerant** (medium-high). Pandemic isolation and a search for control; zodiac/birth-chart searches hit a five-year peak in 2020 — better documented for astrology than tarot specifically.
- **Decline of organized religion / self-directed spirituality** (medium-high). The "nones" grow; tarot fills a meaning-making role. But remember the flat belief numbers — this is about *practice and identity*, not conversion.
- **Democratization of production** (medium). Print-on-demand, Etsy, and crowdfunding lowered the barrier to *making* a deck. The Wild Unknown Tarot's 2012 Kickstarter raised **$118,512 from 3,310 backers** against a $15,000 goal — and opened the floodgates for the indie scene.

## Who it's for — and why

This is where the evidence is genuinely strong, anchored by Pew.

- **Queer** (high). **33% of LGBT adults consult tarot — three times the 11% general rate** — and 15% do so at least monthly. The literature reads tarot as structurally queer-friendly: Cassandra Snow's *Queering the Tarot* (2019); decks like the Black Power Tarot reframe re-drawing the cards as reclamation.
- **Feminist** (high). 43% of women aged 18–49 believe in astrology; tarot skews young and female. The framing is explicit — Black women's use of tarot described as a *feminist reclaiming*.
- **Diasporic / Black / Latinx** (high). Hispanic Catholics (39%) and Black Protestants (33%) are among the most likely to consult astrology. Reporting documents a deliberate reconnection with African, Indigenous, and Latinx ancestral practice — and it is older than the hashtag: Luisah Teish's *Jambalaya* (1984) already speaks of "reclaiming our magic." Rachel True's *True Heart Intuitive Tarot* (2020) is a representative artifact of the representation-driven deck wave.

Why these communities, structurally? Because tarot inherits a tradition whose *selling feature for the marginalized* is that **it has no clergy, no ordination, no gatekeeper** — you authorize yourself. Occultism has been a sanctuary of authority for the excluded for ~175 years: 19th-century spiritualism gave women public authority that churches denied them, and the Golden Dawn (1888) admitted women as initiatory equals — which is the only reason Pamela Colman Smith was in the room at all. The scholar Wouter Hanegraaff calls esotericism *"rejected knowledge"*; Sydney Sheedy's 2022 ethnography finds queer practitioners claiming **kinship through shared abjection** — the cards and the people share a status, cast out of the legitimate, surviving on the margins.

## The artists in the shadow

"Anyone can create in it" must never slide into "the creators don't matter." Many were **shadowed**, and the emblem is **Pamela Colman Smith** ("Pixie"). The Rider–Waite deck (1909, now 100M+ copies in circulation) was illustrated entirely by her: Waite supplied concepts; she drew all 78 cards. She was paid a **small one-time fee, no royalties**. The name itself encodes the erasure — *Rider* was the publisher, *Waite* the commissioner, the artist left out — and she died destitute in 1951 in an unmarked grave whose records later burned.

Her single most consequential choice: before Smith, most decks showed the pip cards as bare arrangements of cups, swords, wands, coins; **she turned them into little narrative scenes** — struggle, joy, loss, hope. Those fully-illustrated pips are precisely what make the deck legible and teachable to beginners — arguably a *structural cause* of tarot's later mass adoption. (Nuance: she was not absolutely first — the obscure 15th-century **Sola-Busca** deck illustrated all pips, and she borrowed from it — but she was first for a mass-market deck.)

She is the emblem, not the exception. The other pillar of modern tarot, Crowley's **Thoth** deck, was painted by **Lady Frieda Harris** over five years (she repainted *The Magus* seven times) — yet it is still called "the Crowley deck." So the **two most-used decks in the world were both visually realized by women whose names were subordinated to the male occultists who commissioned them**, and beneath them lies an even deeper anonymity: the Tarot de Marseille pattern was the work of uncredited journeyman cardmakers. **Returning the names** is part of holding this question open.

## Why tarot, specifically? Two theses

Plenty of open symbol systems exist; few become *this*. Two explanations, honestly weighed.

### 1. A license to create

The Dummett-school record is unusually clean: tarot crystallized as a **card game** in 1440s Italy, its trumps drawn from ordinary medieval allegory. Divinatory use was a **late-18th-century invention** — in 1781 Antoine Court de Gébelin claimed (admitting he had no evidence) that tarot descended from the Egyptian Book of Thoth; **Etteilla** then built the first deck designed for divination, and all modern pip/court meanings derive from his work. Crucially, the **trump order varied** across early decks — so there was never even a single hidden sequence to recover. The meaning was never internally stable.

That is the structural condition that produces an open **license to create**: when meaning is *attributed* rather than authored, by many competing hands, re-attribution is normal rather than heretical. And tarot's openness is unusually **visual and physical** — the unit of re-creation is a 78-image deck you can redraw and *sell*, not a paragraph. The sharper version of the thesis: the images were authored by *shadowed, decentralized, non-authoritative* hands — anonymous cardmakers, an uncredited Smith, a self-anonymizing Harris. **A tradition whose most influential artist died nameless in a pauper's grave is structurally hard to gatekeep.**

### 2. Tarot as play — game → trick → play

The other thesis is that tarot fits these communities because of what it *is*: a machine for **play**, in three precise stages.

- **Game.** Tarot is literally a *trick-taking* game (tarocchi) from the 1440s — competition-plus-chance. The game is made of tricks.
- **Trick** (the con/fiction sense). De Gébelin's 1781 Egyptian pedigree was a fiction he couldn't evidence; Etteilla was the engineer who turned the con into a working method. The "trick" is both senses at once.
- **Play** (theatre / ritual / fiction). The contemporary phase — TikTok performance, deck-as-art-object, the reflective spread.

The academic backbone for the third stage is real. Adam Seligman and colleagues' *[Ritual and Its Consequences: An Essay on the Limits of Sincerity](https://www.academia.edu/6535559/Ritual_and_Its_Consequences_An_Essay_on_the_Limits_of_Sincerity_by_Adam_Seligman_et_al)* (2008) argues that ritual, like play, creates **"as-if" worlds** — a *subjunctive* space close to Winnicott's transitional space — and explicitly opposes the subjunctive to **sincerity** (the demand to *be*, essentially and verifiably, a fixed thing). That sincere mode is exactly what has been weaponized against queer and trans people; the subjunctive lets a self be **tried on** without being pinned to it — which maps directly onto Butler's performativity and onto camp. Pew's own headline (most people engage "just for fun," not from literal belief) is empirical evidence that the dominant register *is* the subjunctive. Tarot is a machine for the as-if.

There's even an etymological key. "Queer" is **not** Tolkien's word (he used the old "strange/odd" sense); it entered English around 1513 from a root meaning **"oblique" — off the straight line**. And that single word unifies three things: queer people (oblique to the norm), divination (oblique knowledge, approached sideways), and the subjunctive (an oblique relation to the real). A 19th-century cant sense of "queer" even meant *counterfeit* — "to shove the queer" was to pass fake coin: *the not-quite-real that still works*, uncannily close to the game → trick → play arc.

### The honest refinement

Neither thesis is the whole building, and the honest version says so:

- **Affordance vs. timing.** Runes, Lenormand, and the I Ching are also open and multiply-attributed, yet didn't surge like this. The "license to create" and the "play structure" describe a *standing affordance*; the post-2015 *timing* is better explained by the **distribution layer** — short-form video plus print-on-demand crowdfunding lowered the cost of consuming *and producing* re-attributions to near zero. TikTok and Kickstarter are what cashed the affordance in.
- **Play isn't only queer**, and much queer/diasporic tarot isn't playful. Huizinga's *Homo Ludens* makes play a human universal, so it can't be a unique marker. And the Black-feminist strand is explicitly about **healing and ancestral reconnection** — sincere, not camp. The richest formulation: tarot lets people **move between** the playful-subjunctive and the sincere-devotional at will — and *that mobility*, not play alone, is what someone burned by institutional religion needs. You can engage without being doctrinally captured, and be sincere without submitting to a gatekeeper.

## What's still thin

Honesty about the gaps:

- **YouTube/TikTok Trends specifically:** not pulled here — claims rest on web Trends plus trade reporting. Treat any platform-specific number as unverified.
- **Market size:** vendor reports disagree by an **order of magnitude** (~$94M by 2027 / ~$150M in 2025 / $0.71B in 2026 / ~$2.5B in 2020), reflecting different scopes and several low-quality syndicated reports. Direction (growth) is reliable; any single dollar figure is not.
- **Viral aggregator stats** ("$150M+ on Kickstarter," "10,000+ decks in print," "51% of Gen Z use tarot"): traced to single low-quality aggregators. Color, not fact.
- **Pamela Colman Smith's heritage and sexuality:** popularly described as mixed-race and queer; the documented genealogy is more contested (American parents; formative years living in and collecting the folklore of Jamaica; queerness inferred from her milieu, not documented). Flag, don't assert.

**Two sources carry most of the load here:** [Pew Research Center — *3 in 10 Americans consult astrology, tarot cards, or fortune tellers* (2025)](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/05/21/3-in-10-americans-consult-astrology-tarot-cards-or-fortune-tellers/) for the practice numbers, and [Seligman, Weller, Puett & Simon — *Ritual and Its Consequences* (2008)](https://www.academia.edu/6535559/Ritual_and_Its_Consequences_An_Essay_on_the_Limits_of_Sincerity_by_Adam_Seligman_et_al) for the subjunctive "as-if" framework.
