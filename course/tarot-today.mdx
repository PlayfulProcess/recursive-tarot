# Tarot Today — a living question

This is the most **unfinished** course in the library, and deliberately so. It is not a verdict; it is a question held open, with a first pass of sourced evidence laid out and an invitation to help us think. Where a claim is solid I say so; where the evidence is thin I flag it, and where I've gone past what any source actually says, I mark it as a guess. Contributions are warmly welcome — there is a paste-ready Claude prompt and a contribute link at the end.

## An unlikely rival

Astrology is one of the oldest continuously practiced systems of meaning-making on Earth. Cuneiform tablets from Mesopotamia were already recording celestial omens — this planet's position foretelling that outcome for the king — in the early second millennium BCE, and the tradition runs in an unbroken line from there through Hellenistic Egypt, the medieval Islamic world, and into a horoscope app on your phone. Tarot, by contrast, is a teenager. It was invented as a card game in the courts of northern Italy in the 1440s, and nobody used it to divine anything for another three hundred and fifty years.

So it is strange that, right now, tarot is closing the gap. Here is the clean version of the story, drawn from a two-term Google Trends pull (U.S. web search, 2004–present):

(A caution worth keeping in view: Google Trends rescales every chart to 0–100 against its own highest point, so the absolute numbers only mean something *within* one chart — the shape is what to trust, not the digits.) The practice data tells the same story from a different angle. [Pew's fall-2024 survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/05/21/3-in-10-americans-consult-astrology-tarot-cards-or-fortune-tellers/) of 9,593 U.S. adults (published May 2025) found that about **30%** of U.S. adults consult astrology, tarot cards, or a fortune teller at least yearly — 28% for astrology or a horoscope, against roughly **11% for tarot specifically** — astrology still bigger, by about two and a half times. (The same survey guards this course's creed for us: most who consult say they do it *just for fun*, and only about **1%** say they rely on it "a lot" for major life decisions — the practice is overwhelmingly play, not obedience.) Astrology also has the infrastructure lead: Co-Star alone reports more than 30 million registered users since 2017, with a whole tier of rivals behind it, while the leading dedicated tarot app, Labyrinthos, serves an order of magnitude fewer. And here is the detail: over the same years, **belief in astrology has barely moved** — 27% in 2024 versus 29% in 2017, and Gallup has it oscillating in the low-to-high twenties since 1990. So none of this is a wave of new believers. It is a wave of *use* — content, practice, identity, objects — arriving under a much older roof.

That a 580-year-old card game is even in the conversation with a divination tradition that predates the pyramids' court astrologers by a thousand years is the fact this course exists to explain. Five things, taken together, do most of the explaining, and one thread running under all five ties them into a single argument. In order: tarot decks are physical objects you can own and collect, and no one collects a horoscope. Crowdfunding and print-on-demand quietly rebuilt the cost of making a deck from "requires a publisher" to "requires a laptop." Tarot is riding the same wave that lifted board games and tabletop role-playing games back into fashion — a wave about tactile, social, imaginative play, not about tarot specifically. Queer, diasporic, and Black-feminist creators have been re-authoring the deck in ways that look, on close reading, less like a single trend than several distinct and serious projects that happen to share a tool. And organized religion keeps shrinking while private, self-administered practices keep growing to fill the room it leaves behind. Underneath all five sits the same hinge: tarot was never handed down with a fixed meaning to obey, so every generation that picks it up is free to make one.

## Something you can hold

Start with the plainest fact and the easiest to underrate: a tarot deck is a *thing*. It has a box, a weight, a feel when you shuffle it, a cover you can photograph and a box you can display on a shelf. You can own three different decks of the same cards and it will not feel redundant, the way owning three different King James Bibles would. Astrology, by contrast, is barely a noun — a horoscope is a reading, an app is a subscription, a birth chart is a printout. There is nothing shaped like a tarot deck in astrology's toolkit, and that asymmetry alone explains a great deal of what makes tarot collectible in a way its older rival simply isn't built to be.

This is not a new story; it's an old one repeating in a new material. Vinyl records, the format everyone left for cassettes and then CDs and then nothing at all, are now outselling both: 2025 was the nineteenth consecutive year of growth in U.S. vinyl sales, and industry revenue crossed a billion dollars for the first time since 1983, with roughly half of new buyers reportedly under 35. Nobody needed vinyl back; streaming is more convenient in every measurable way. What people wanted was an object — something with heft, cover art, a ritual of handling it — in a life otherwise mediated entirely through glass. Tarot decks are their genre's vinyl. A 78-card object you can hold, gift, illustrate, and stack next to the others on a shelf is doing something a phone notification can never do, and the market for it has grown for exactly the same underlying reason records did.

## A cheaper door in

Owning the desire to make one is not the same as being able to. What actually changed after roughly 2012 is that Kickstarter and print-on-demand quietly dismantled the wall between "wants to publish a deck" and "did publish a deck." Before, you needed an occult publisher to take an interest in you. After, you needed a laptop, seventy-eight illustrations, and a funding page.

The mechanism is verifiable even where the eye-catching totals are not. The Wild Unknown Tarot is the emblem of the shift: Kim Krans **hand-drew and self-published** it in 2012 — no occult publisher, no gatekeeper — and its success carried it to a **HarperCollins** edition in 2016 (per her own account at kimkrans.com). That is the whole point in one deck: an illustrator with a laptop, not a crowdfunding jackpot, is what the new infrastructure made possible. Crowdfunding did the same work for others and left an auditable trail — the *Arcana Playing Cards* (a hybrid of standard playing cards and tarot symbolism, not a pure divinatory deck, but drawing the same crowd) [raised $190,592 from 3,199 backers](https://www.kardify.com/2020/01/the-12-most-funded-kickstarter-playing.html) in 2015, the most-funded deck of its year. What I could *not* verify, despite trying, are the industry-wide totals that circulate freely online — "$150 million raised since 2010," "over 1,200 tarot campaigns since 2009." Every version of those numbers I found traces back to the same handful of SEO content-mill sites restating each other, not to Kickstarter's own public statistics or to any auditable source, so I'm not repeating them as fact. What I can point to instead is simpler and more durable: Kickstarter's own live "tarot deck" listings show a steady, ongoing stream of funded projects year after year, and print-on-demand services now let one illustrator ship a deck with no print run, no warehouse, and no gatekeeper's approval. The infrastructure, not any specific dollar figure, is the load-bearing fact here.

## Tarot's cousins are surging too

Zoom out one more level and tarot stops looking like an isolated phenomenon at all. Board games and tabletop role-playing games — Dungeons & Dragons and its many descendants — went through their own pandemic-era boom. [Trade analysis of NPD data](https://icv2.com/articles/markets/view/48827/hobby-game-sales-over-2-billion-2020) found that tabletop RPG sales in the US and Canada grew 31% in 2020, breaking $100 million for the first time; board games saw a comparable jump the same year. (Total market-size figures for the games industry vary by an order of magnitude across vendor reports, the same way tarot's do — direction is reliable, any single dollar figure is not.) None of that is about tarot specifically. It's about a broader hunger, sharpened by lockdown and never fully switched off, for things that are tactile, social, and imaginative in a life otherwise lived through screens. Tarot rode in on that same wave, as one member of a much larger family of resurgent physical, imaginative play — not as a special case.

Here, rather than up front, is what tarot actually is underneath all of this: in the 1440s Italian courts where it began, tarot **was** exactly this kind of thing — a trick-taking card game called *tarocchi*, played for the same reasons anyone plays a card game, three and a half centuries before anyone thought to use it for divination. The game-object identity people are rediscovering now isn't a costume tarot put on for the occasion; it's the original outfit. The full, sourced story of how a card game acquired a fortune-telling reputation it never asked for lives in [The History of Tarot](course-viewer.html?course=history-of-tarot) — this course is about the surge, not the history, so that's where the history belongs.

## Who's re-authoring the deck, and why

This is the strand I find most interesting, and the one I want to be most careful about — because it is easy, writing about "minority communities and tarot," to slide into claiming you know what a whole group of people wants or needs. I don't, and I'm not going to. What follows is anchored in named scholarship and citable reporting; where I go further than any source actually claims, I'll say so.

Start with an erasure worth correcting on the way in. The most reproduced tarot deck in the world, from 1909, is usually called the *Rider-Waite* deck — after the publisher and the man who commissioned it. The artist who drew all seventy-eight cards, gave the pip cards their first fully illustrated scenes, and made the deck legible to beginners in a way no earlier deck had been, was **Pamela Colman Smith**, a mixed-heritage, Jamaica-raised illustrator paid a small one-time fee and no royalties, whose name was left off the deck she made for a century. (Her sexuality is often described as queer in popular writing; the documented record — mostly her lifelong companionship with the spiritualist Nora Lake — is suggestive but genuinely contested, so I'll flag rather than assert it.) Calling the deck "Rider-Waite-Smith," which this course tries to do, is a small act of returning a name.

The clearest documented thread is queer use. Cassandra Snow's *[Queering the Tarot](https://redwheelweiser.com/book/queering-the-tarot-9781578636488/)* (2019) reads all seventy-eight cards against the heteronormative default, explicitly, card by card. And a 2022 ethnography by Sydney Sheedy, following thirty queer occult practitioners in Montréal — [published in the *European Journal of Cultural Studies*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11893267/) — found practitioners describing a felt kinship with the occult through *shared abjection*: the cards and the people who read them occupy the same cast-out status, and that shared exile is part of the draw. It's not a coincidence that the raw material here has always lived outside the academy's approval — Wouter Hanegraaff, the scholar most associated with the field, calls the whole esoteric tradition *["rejected knowledge"](https://books.google.com/books/about/Esotericism_and_the_Academy.html?id=48Lgg5fa78oC)*, excluded from the respectable canon by design. A practice with no clergy and no ordination is a natural home for people the clergy-and-ordination version of religion has already excluded.

A second, distinct thread runs through diasporic and Black-feminist practice, and it should not be folded into the first — the people doing this work say clearly that it isn't primarily about play. Luisah Teish's *Jambalaya* (1985) was already writing about "reclaiming our magic" — a phrase that predates the current wave by decades — as part of Yoruba, Ifá, and New Orleans Vodou practice reframed through a Black feminist lens. Rachel True's *[True Heart Intuitive Tarot](https://truehearttarot.com/)* (2020) pairs a deck illustrated with explicitly multicultural figures to twenty-two memoir essays. Marcelitte Failla, a scholar of African-heritage religions [who has spoken at Harvard's Center for the Study of World Religions](https://cswr.hds.harvard.edu/news/2022/12/5/video-black-tarot-african-american-women-and-divine-processes-resilience), frames tarot and adjacent practices like Ifá, Vodou, and Hoodoo as tools of *collective healing*. Junauda Petrus's essay *[Explorative Black Tarot](https://lambdaliterary.org/2023/02/explorative-black-tarot-a-black-queer-feminist-practice-in-creative-writing-and-oracle/)* (2023) works the same ground from a Black queer angle. None of that reads as playful in the way "camp" or "as-if" usually mean it — it reads as sincere, ancestral, and in earnest.

So which is it — is tarot's pull a *playful* affordance, or a *sincere* one? Johan Huizinga's *Homo Ludens* argues play is a human universal, present in every culture and every serious institution (law, war, religion all have ludic roots), which already tells you play can't be the *unique* marker of anything. And the sources above make the same point from the other direction: much of the richest work being done with these cards right now isn't playful at all. Here is where I want to be explicit that I'm reaching past what any single source claims: taken together, what these two strands might be showing is that tarot's real affordance is neither play nor sincerity alone, but the *mobility between them* — the capacity to move from the playful-subjunctive register to the sincere-devotional one and back, inside the same deck, at will. That mobility, if it's real, is exactly what someone burned by an institution that demanded doctrinal commitment would need: a way to engage without being captured, and a way to be sincere without submitting to a gatekeeper who could revoke your standing. I think that's the most interesting sentence in this whole course, and I hold it as a hypothesis, not a finding — nobody I've cited says it in these words.

## A practice without a church

The last piece is the least surprising and the shortest to tell. Organized religious affiliation in the U.S. has been declining for decades, while — as the belief numbers above already showed — the underlying rate of supernatural or spiritual belief has stayed roughly flat. What's shrinking is institutional membership; what isn't shrinking is the human appetite for practice, ritual, and a vocabulary for the parts of life that don't reduce to reasoning. Tarot slots neatly into the room that leaves behind: no membership, no clergy, no creed to sign, just a deck and an intention. It isn't converting anyone to anything. It's giving people who've stepped back from a church something to do on a Tuesday night instead.

## What's still thin

Being honest about the gaps, in the spirit this course was built on:

- **Industry-wide dollar totals** — for tarot's market size and for the Kickstarter aggregate figures above — disagree by an order of magnitude across vendor reports and trace to low-quality syndication. Direction is reliable; specific numbers are not.
- **Pamela Colman Smith's sexuality and the exact shape of her heritage** are popularly asserted with more confidence than the documented record supports. Flagged above, flagged again here.

## The invitation

Here's where the "license to create" this course keeps returning to actually comes from, and it isn't unique to tarot. J.R.R. Tolkien, in his essay *On Fairy-Stories*, argued that making fiction — building a "Secondary World" with its own consistent logic — isn't decoration on top of being human; it's close to the center of it. He called the act **sub-creation**: we don't invent from nothing, we take what's given — a medieval allegory, a scrap of folklore, a deck of cards someone else already drew — and remake it into something new. Discovery and invention, on this view, were never really opposites; every act of making starts by first finding something already there to work with.

Tarot is a small, unusually legible case of exactly that process. The images on the Major Arcana were *found* — drawn from medieval allegory that already existed before anyone shuffled a deck. The divinatory *meaning* was *made* — invented from whole cloth in 1781 by a man who admitted he had no evidence for the Egyptian pedigree he claimed. And every deck since — Smith's narrative pips, Teish's Ifá-inflected reclaiming, True's memoir cards, Snow's queered readings — is a further round of the same move: finding what's already in the cards, and making something new from it. Art theory has a name for this too — the reader or viewer completes the meaning of a work, doesn't just receive it — and Jung made the same point about symbols specifically: a symbol is never fully spelled out, which is exactly what leaves room for someone to finish it. A map is not the territory, and there is, as far as anyone can tell, an invisible world all around us — the part of the electromagnetic spectrum we can't see, the atomic structure of the chair you're sitting on, and very possibly more than that — so even our maps of the *visible* world are still just maps. You don't need to believe the occult origin stories some tarot books still repeat to find seventy-eight images beautiful and build your own meaning from them; the actual, documented history is one door over, in [The History of Tarot](course-viewer.html?course=history-of-tarot).

Read this way, the whole surge this course has been describing is the same thing happening at scale: a lot of people, at once, reclaiming the plain human need to be sub-creators — makers of meaning, not just recipients of someone else's. That's the whole reason recursive.eco exists: not to hand you a finished meaning, but to hand you the tools and get out of the way, so making meaning together stays something everyone gets to do, not something only a priesthood is licensed for.
