# Intention Setting

I came to tarot the way a lot of people do — half-hoping it could tell me something the world wouldn't. It didn't. Some of its promises broke in my hands: the predictions that were going to be true mostly weren't, and the ones that "came true" I could see, later, I had quietly arranged. I could have thrown the deck away. I didn't — and this short page is my account of why.

What I kept is not the fortune-telling. It is the deck as a **[grammar](https://recursive.eco)** — a shared symbolic language — held as a **gate, not a fate**: a card, drawn at random, that starts a conversation with what is already alive in me and hands the choice back to me. Held that way the cards are still — maybe more — worth keeping, and the one intention under everything else here is simple: *read to know yourself, not to be told your fate; relate to the card, never obey it.* (The longer version of that story — the disappointment and the turn — is its own essay, [Tarot & the Crack](course-viewer.html?course=tarot-and-the-crack).)

What follows next is the short history that shaped this stance — the game, the crystal ball, the gate — and why it turns out to matter well past the table. The **lenses** I read through — the voices you can pause and consult on the [Caster](../viewers/caster.html) before you draw — are gathered at the end of this page, each in its own subsection, so you can jump straight there if that's what brought you here.

## Game, crystal ball, gate

There is a short history folded inside the way a single person learns to hold these cards, and it runs along the same arch the cards themselves travelled.

First the **game**. For three centuries the tarot asked nothing of you but to play it well — trumps and tricks, skill and company, no claim on the truth at all. I have never wanted to lose that innocence; the play is still half of why I shuffle.

Then the **crystal ball**. In 1780s Paris a man calling himself Etteilla turned the deck into an oracle — reversed meanings, a *Book of Thot*, a fortune for a fee. This is the stance most people still picture: the card as a window onto fate, the reader as someone who *knows what is coming.* It is the chapter I came in through, and the chapter that broke in my hands. The crystal ball asks you to **obey** — to take the slice it shows you for the whole, and hand it your will.

And then, if you are lucky, the **gate**. Not a window that announces your fate but a threshold you choose to cross with your judgment intact — a card that reflects what is already in you and lets you *imagine* the step you have not yet taken. *Gate, not fate.* The [Golden Dawn](course-viewer.html?course=walking-the-golden-dawn-path) already walked the trumps this way, as doorways on the Tree; the whole of this course is the long argument for holding even a reading as a gate rather than a verdict.

Game, crystal ball, gate. I do not despise any of the three — but I have learned which one keeps me free.

## Why this matters off the table

Here is what surprised me, and why I think this is more than a hobby.

The thing that pulled me out of the crystal ball was not skepticism about *tarot*. It was the **practice of history** — the plain discipline this whole site is built on: where the documents are silent, do not fill the gap with the story you wish were true. The cards are 15th-century Italian; the Egyptian wisdom was invented in 1781; no secret society drew the trumps by candlelight. Learning to *want the evidence more than the story* is a muscle, and once you have built it on the cards you cannot put it down. It is the same muscle that meets a headline and asks: *who is showing me this, and what are they not showing me?*

That muscle has never mattered more, because we now live inside an industrial-scale crystal ball — a feed, an algorithm, an AI that answers in a fluent and confident voice, every one of them very good at showing you true-seeming things arranged to land. Tolkien had a name for exactly this object: the **palantír**, the seeing-stone that never lies and still destroys whoever obeys it — because a stronger will curates the truths it shows until you despair, or comply. (A surveillance company took the name for a banner, and missed — or did not care — that Tolkien wrote the thing as a *warning.*) The discipline against the palantír is the same discipline against the crystal ball, and against a card read as fate: **keep your will in front of the sight; read the slice as a slice; relate to it, and never obey.**

So the table turns out to be practice. Held the right way, a card is a small, safe rehearsal of the one move a person most needs in a world full of oracles: to take in everything a powerful, suggestive system shows you — and stay the author of what you do next. That is why I still shuffle, and why I think you might. *(The same vow holds the AI side of [recursive.eco](https://recursive.eco) — see its **AI Risks & Safer Containers**.)*

## The lenses — an ecosystem of conscience

What follows are the **lenses** I read through — the voices you can pause and consult on the [Caster](../viewers/caster.html) before you draw. Each is a different honest answer to "how should I hold this card?" None is the law; together they are an ecosystem of conscience. Tap a voice on the Caster and it brings you here, to its subsection.

*On these voices — a word of honesty.* Most are named for a **school**, not a person, on purpose. DBT, Non-Dual Tantra, Post-Activism and Hospicing Modernity are lenses I was *inspired by* — drawn from the work of living teachers (Marsha Linehan, Christopher Wallis, Bayo Akomolafe, Vanessa Andreotti) who have never heard of this deck and have endorsed nothing here. Kant and Jung name their own traditions, but even there I am not reporting their scholarship — what follows is *my own reading of* each of them, my interpretive use of a dead thinker's ideas, not a claim about what they actually taught. The Ecosystem speaks for no one in particular — it is this project's own voice. Each reading below is the most faithful version I could manage, and still my **interpretation**; where a line rings false, the fault is mine. And because these voices are, by my own admission, a bit less central to this project than the rest of this course, I have kept each one short on purpose — a compact reading, not a treatise.

### Kant

My reading of Kant turns on a single test: is your will *free* — self-legislating, autonomous — or has something outside reason decided for it: appetite, another person's authority, or fate? Kant's word for that second condition is *heteronomy*, and his whole ethics is built to get us out of it. Applied to a deck of cards, the result is clean: **the cards are never what's being judged. The maxim is.** The same draw, in the same hands, earns opposite verdicts depending only on the rule you act under — read as a message from destiny, it's a surrender; read as a prompt you author and revise, it's the same discipline Kant called *sapere aude*, daring to use your own understanding. Even the con — a stranger selling you your fate for a fee — is wrong on Kantian grounds only because it uses your reason against you and asks you to hand over your judgment; do the same thing to yourself, alone, and you have simply run the con on your own reason. Kant even hands me a motive for opening the deck at all: among the duties we owe ourselves, the first he names is *moral self-knowledge* — "know, scrutinize, fathom yourself." A card can be a disciplined instrument of exactly that duty, if I keep it a prompt and never a prophecy. (An honest aside: Kant is also a man whose anthropological writings are shot through with racism. I keep the instrument — autonomy — and name the hand that forged it.)

### DBT

My reading of Marsha Linehan, who built Dialectical Behavior Therapy out of surviving her own psychiatric hospitalization, is the counterweight to Kant's austerity. She draws three circles: *reasonable mind* (facts, logic — nearly all Kant trusts), *emotion mind* (how you feel), and where they overlap, *wise mind* — "that part of each person that can know and experience truth… almost always quiet, with a certain peace." A card read from wise mind isn't decoded by reason alone; I notice the feeling and the facts, and wait for the quieter thing under both. Her central move is the *dialectic*: two things that seem to contradict are both true at once, and a good card holds exactly that tension — the Tower is catastrophe *and* clearing; Death is loss *and* release. Read this way, a hard card isn't a verdict; it's an invitation to *radical acceptance* — stop arguing with what's already true. Before I trust a reading, I run it through her three filters: is it useful, does it fit the actual facts of my life, and is it compassionate? A reading that fails those is dread or wishful thinking wearing wisdom's coat. Inspired by Marsha Linehan's Dialectical Behavior Therapy — a lens, not her words.

### The Ecosystem

The ground. Read humbly: there is more than one healthy way to be, so tend rather than legislate, and let your reading answer only to whether it helps the whole of your life flourish. See [Morality Is an Ecosystem](course-viewer.html?course=morality-is-an-ecosystem).

### The Golden Dawn

The ascent. Read as the Order did — *pathworking*, the trump a gate on the Tree of Life, a step in the soul's climb toward the light. Rendered faithfully, and held — as everything here is — as a gate to cross, not a fate to obey: [Walking the Golden Dawn Path](course-viewer.html?course=walking-the-golden-dawn-path).

### Jung

My reading of Jung goes where Kant's autonomy leaves off: not toward the world, but down. Jung's quarrel with his century was that it mistook the conscious ego for the whole of a person; beneath it he places a far larger house — memories, disowned instincts, energies that didn't fit the story you tell about yourself — and that house speaks in images. A painted card is one more channel. What I take from him first is the *shadow*: the card I flinch from — the Tower, the Devil, whatever I instantly want to reshuffle away — is often the one worth staying with, because the disowned thing presses to be seen precisely by disguising itself as the card that unsettles me. Second is *active imagination*: don't analyze the figure from a safe distance — step inside the image, ask it who it is and what it wants, and let it answer in words I didn't plan. Third, more carefully: the uncanny timing of a card landing on the exact thing I'd been avoiding is what Jung called *synchronicity* — I take this as permission to pay attention, not as proof of a hidden mechanism routing the right card to my hand. The meaning is made by me, in the noticing; I try not to mistake the prompt for a message from outside.

### Non-Dual Tantra

My reading of the non-dual Śaiva Tantra taught today by Christopher Wallis asks me to loosen my grip entirely. Its wager is *līlā* — reality as play, the free, self-delighting activity of consciousness meeting itself — and if that's even a useful stance, it dissolves the one thing that makes tarot dangerous: the fear that drives a person to obey. I draw the Tower and a part of me flinches, as though the card knew something from outside. Non-dually, there's no outside: the meaning is arising in my own awareness, which makes it un-outsourceable — I cannot be told my fate by a thing that, on inspection, is not separate from the one doing the telling. The chill I feel when a card lands too well is *spanda*, the pulse of that same awareness stirring, not a ghost in the deck. And because it's play, I'm never its victim — a hard card isn't a sentence passed on me; it's a move in a game I'm co-authoring, and I have the next one. Inspired by the non-dual Śaiva Tantra taught by Christopher Wallis — a lens, not his words.

### Post-Activism

My reading of Bayo Akomolafe's post-activism is for when the rush to fix a card is itself the problem. "The times are urgent; let us slow down" — not slower action, a different quality of attention, available only once I stop trying to arrive. Most readings extract a meaning, name an action, close the loop; this one asks me to let the card become a *sanctuary* instead — a room I build for whatever I'd otherwise rush to manage: a grief that won't behave, a stuckness I keep trying to optimize away. Host it rather than fix it or hide it. And when the reading cracks — refuses to mean anything tidy — I try not to seal it back up. The crack is where the new gets in; closure is often where thought goes to die. Inspired by the post-activism of Bayo Akomolafe — a lens, not his words.

### Hospicing Modernity

My reading of Vanessa Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* asks for a harder thing than holding two true things at once: holding what is dying and what is being born *in the same image*, without being sure which is which. Something in the situation a card shows is already ending — a role, a certainty, a story I keep trying to revive — and deserves to be accompanied, not abandoned: that's hospicing. Something else is arriving, unformed, and deserves room I haven't yet made: that's midwifing. The harder discipline underneath is complicity — I'm not a clean observer studying the card's trouble from the shore; where it shows me something I'd rather condemn, the honest move is to ask where that same pattern lives in me. This voice won't forecast and won't instruct; if I came for an answer, it hands me a better question and asks me to keep it warm. Inspired by Vanessa Andreotti and the GTDF collective — a lens, not her words.
